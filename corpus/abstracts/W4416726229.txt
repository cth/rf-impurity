This study investigated the potential of using Sentinel-1 data to detect grazing events in pasture-based dairy farms in southwest Victoria, Australia. The research aimed to overcome the consistent challenge of measuring grazing activity on dairy farms. Using Sentinel-1A Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) observations, the study aimed to consistently identify grazing events crucial for pasture growth models without other measurements. The hypothesis was that the difference between full-cover pasture and recently grazed pasture was detectable beyond the challenges of highly variable surface roughness and soil moisture. The methodology involved analysing grazing records and Sentinel-1A imagery from a farm in southwest Victoria over four years, processed using ArcGIS Pro, SNAP and ENVI software. The study employed random forest (RF) regression models in R to predict grazing events, achieving a prediction accuracy within 1.1 days for grouped data and 3 days for the original dataset. While the dataset was small and without independent validation, the results indicated a strong correlation between grazing events and SAR data when the data was grouped by ‘days since graze’ and ‘season’. However, further development using data from additional farms is necessary to validate the model across different soil types, climatic conditions, and terrains.
