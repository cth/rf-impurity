Abstract Background COVID-19 vaccination has prevented substantial morbidity and mortality in the United States, yet its uptake remains an issue, with similar challenges persisting for seasonal flu vaccination. Public trust in federal statistics and federal institutions may influence vaccination behavior, but its role in predicting COVID-19 vaccine uptake has not been extensively evaluated. Methods We analyzed data from three waves of the United States Census Bureau Household Pulse Survey. The outcome was COVID-19 vaccine uptake for the 2024 to 2025 season. Predictors included sociodemographic characteristics, health insurance, flu vaccine uptake, and three measures of trust in federal statistics. Missing data were addressed using multiple imputations. Data from October to December 2024 were used for training, and data from February to March 2025 served as the test set. Five supervised learning models were tuned using cross validation to maximize the area under the curve. Model performance and calibration were evaluated on the test set, and feature importance was assessed using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values. Results Models performed overall with area under the curve values between 0.867 and 0.895. The model achieved discrimination. SHAP analyses identified flu vaccination uptake, age, number of children, and trust in federal statistics as the strongest predictors of COVID-19 vaccine uptake. Conclusion Machine learning models predicted COVID-19 vaccine uptake in a national sample. Flu vaccination behavior, age, number of children, and trust in federal statistics emerged as key predictors. These findings may help inform population-level outreach strategies, while recognizing that the results reflect associations rather than causal effects. Introduction In the United States (US), COVID-19 vaccination averted more than 2.5 million deaths globally from 2020 to 2024 [ 1 ]. Despite this success, vaccine uptake remains an ongoing public health challenge, with an estimated 240,797 COVID-19 deaths between December 2020 and June 2021 potentially preventable through vaccination [ 2 – 4 ]. For the 2024–2025 season, public health guidance recommended that everyone six months and older receive both the flu and COVID-19 vaccines, with the option to receive them during the same visit. However, by late December, a notable gap had emerged: approximately 42% of people had received the flu vaccine while only about 21% had received the COVID-19 vaccine, a pattern shaped by individual characteristics, perceptions of each vaccine, workplace and geographic context, and levels of institutional trust [ 5 ]. Although COVID-19 vaccination rates have shown some improvement, similar challenges in uptake persist for other vaccine-preventable diseases; among adults 18 years and older, flu vaccination coverage was 45%, 2% points lower than the previous season [ 6 ]. Taken together, these disparities highlight that the effectiveness of federal, state, and local efforts to reduce uptake depends heavily on public trust [ 7 ]. Such trust is vital for encouraging the public to adopt protective measures that limit disease transmission and mitigate negative health impacts [ 7 ]. However, sociodemographic factors such as age, race, and educational attainment significantly affect the trust required for vaccine uptake [ 8 – 12 ]. Racial/ethnic differences are evident, with African American and Hispanic populations demonstrating higher levels of vaccine uptake compared to their White counterparts [ 11 – 12 ]. This uptake often reflects distrust in government, shaped by historical discrimination [ 8 – 14 ]. Although many studies have examined demographic and attitudinal factors linked to COVID-19 vaccine uptake, their ability to predict COVID-19 uptake using modern machine learning methods (ML) remains largely untested. Prior work has relied mostly on traditional regression models, leaving a gap in evaluating how contemporary ML algorithms perform in identifying individuals at highest risk of COVID-19 uptake [ 15 – 17 ]. Given the widespread distrust in federal statistics, additional research is warranted to better understand how trust dynamics relate to vaccine uptake [ 13 – 17 ]. Using data from the Household Pulse Survey, supervised learning methods were employed to identify sociodemographic and attitudinal characteristics most predictive of COVID-19 vaccine uptake. The analytic framework was designed to inform policy by emphasizing feature importance rather than causal inference. Although multiple supervised learning algorithms were tuned for predictive accuracy, the primary goal was to interpret the resulting feature importance patterns to gain insight into the demographic and perceptual factors most associated with COVID-19 vaccine uptake behaviors. Methods Study design and setting We used cross-sectional data from three waves of the Household Pulse Survey [ 18 ], a national survey conducted by the US Census Bureau collected on October 15–29, 2024; December 17, 2024–January 3, 2025; and February 21–March 7, 2025. This 20-minute survey included various topics, including mental health, social isolation, and sociodemographic factors. This study followed the Strengthening of the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology guidelines [ 19 ]. Analytic sample Between October 15, 2024, and March 7, 2025, participants from three waves of the Household Pulse Survey were analyzed: 9,355 individuals in October 2024, 9,404 in December 2024–January 2025, and 6,740 in February–March 2025. Across these waves, the data exhibited 1.5–1.9% missingness for COVID-19 or flu vaccination status, 1.2–1.3% for trust or confidence measures, and 0.8–1.0% for covariate information (marital status, number of children, and employment status). The corresponding unweighted sample sizes were 9,022 (wave 1), 9,034 (wave 2), and 6,463 (wave 3) participants, respectively ( n = 24,519; see Table S1). COVID-19 and flu vaccine uptake We assessed COVID-19 and flu vaccination uptake using one item: Have you received the following vaccines this fall (i.e., since August 2024)? This item was asked separately for COVID-19 and flu vaccination. We derived each outcome as a binary variable, denoting ‘1’ for vaccine uptake and ‘0’ for non-vaccine uptake. Trust in federal statistical agencies The Household Pulse Survey included questions about trust in federal statistics, confidence in the US Census Bureau, and confidence in US statistical agencies. Although the Household Pulse Survey did not define “statistical agencies,” this study used the term to refer to federal partners that collaborate with the U.S. Census Bureau to collect and produce the Household Pulse Survey data, such as the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and the National Center for Health Statistics [ 20 ]. For trust in federal statistics, participants were asked, “The population count, the crime rate, and the unemployment rate are examples of statistics produced by the federal government. Personally, how much trust do you have in federal statistics in the United States? Would you say that you tend to trust federal statistics, or you tend not to trust them?” Response options included “Tend to trust federal statistics” or “Tend not to trust federal statistics.” We treated this as a binary variable in the analysis. For confidence in the US Census Bureau, US statistical agencies, and Congress, participants were asked, “Please indicate how much confidence you, yourself, have in the US Census Bureau” and “Please indicate how much confidence you, yourself, have in the US statistical agencies.” Response options included “A great deal,” “Quite a lot,” “Some, ” and “Very little.” Covariates We controlled for the following sociodemographic factors: age (measured continuously in years); sex (male, female); race/ethnicity (non-Hispanic White, non-Hispanic Black, Hispanic, non-Hispanic Asian, non-Hispanic Other/Multiracial); educational attainment (bachelor’s degree or higher, some college education/associate’s degree, high school or less); employment status (yes, no); marital status (married, not married but in a domestic partnership, divorced/separated/widowed, never married); number of children (measured ordinally); and health insurance coverage (private, Medicare, Medicaid, other, unknown). Access to transportation during the past four weeks was assessed by asking respondents which statement best described their situation: enough transportation to meet their needs; enough transportation, but not always the kinds they wanted to use; sometimes not enough transportation; often not enough transportation; or always not enough transportation. Statistical analysis All analyses were conducted in R using the tidymodels framework [ 21 ]. Descriptive statistics were generated with gtsummary [ 22 ]. The primary outcome was COVID-19 vaccine uptake. Predictors included demographic, socioeconomic, and attitudinal factors: age, gender, race, education, employment, transportation, marital status, number of children, health insurance type, flu non-uptake, and three trust measures reflecting confidence in federal statistics, the Census Bureau, and statistical agencies. Data from October–December 2024 were used for training, and data from February–March 2025 served as the held-out test set. Missing data were handled with multiple imputations by chained equations (m = 5, maxit = 20) using mice [ 23 ], with a fixed random seed for reproducibility. A wave indicator preserved differences across survey periods. Continuous variables used predictive mean matching; categorical and ordered variables used logistic, multinomial, or proportional-odds models. Survey weights and wave indicators were treated as auxiliary but not imputed. Convergence was evaluated through trace plots and R-hat diagnostics from miceadds [ 24 ]. Parameter estimates and performance metrics were pooled across imputations using Rubin’s rules to obtain point estimates, standard errors, 95% CIs, and fractions of missing information. Before modeling, categorical variables were one-hot encoded, numeric variables normalized, and zero-variance predictors removed. Five classifiers predicted COVID-19 uptake: penalized logistic regression (glmnet [ 25 ]), random forest (ranger [ 26 ]), linear discriminant analysis (MASS [ 26 ]), multilayer perceptron (nnet [ 27 ]), and extreme gradient boosting (xgboost [ 28 ]). These models were selected to represent several commonly used algorithmic families in predictive modeling, including linear models, tree-based ensemble methods, neural networks, and discriminant classifiers, allowing comparison across approaches with varying levels of flexibility and interpretability. Hyperparameters were optimized via ten-fold stratified cross-validation with space-filling grids, using AUC as the tuning metric. Cross-validation was done using the R packages rsample [ 29 ], dials [ 30 ], tune [ 31 ], and workflows [ 32 ]; models and recipes were defined through parsnip [ 33 ] and recipes [ 34 ]. Performance metrics were computed with yardstick [ 35 ]. Classification thresholds maximizing Youden’s J were selected on the training data with a target sensitivity ≥ 0.90; if unmet, the unconstrained maximizer was used. Final performance was evaluated on the test wave using ROC AUC, sensitivity, specificity, accuracy, positive predictive value, F1 score, and precision–recall AUC. Calibration was assessed via decile-based curves and linear slopes and intercepts. Feature importance was quantified with SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values from fastshap [ 36 ]. Visualizations used ggplot2 [ 37 ] and patchwork [ 38 ]. Pooled results were summarized with dplyr [ 39 ], tidyr [ 40 ], and tibble [ 41 ]; data imports used readr [ 42 ], haven [ 43 ], and readxl [ 44 ]. Iteration and string/factor handling employed purrr [ 45 ], stringr [ 46 ], and forcats [ 47 ]. Multicollinearity was checked with car [ 48 ], and parallel processing with doParallel [ 49 ]. Sensitivity analysis To evaluate the extent to which model performance was driven by highly influential predictors, we conducted a sensitivity (ablation) analysis by re-estimating the models under three predictor specifications. The first specification included the full set of predictors. The second excluded flu vaccination behavior, identified as the most influential variable in SHAP analyses. The third excluded both flu vaccination behavior and age, representing the two most dominant predictors. All models were trained and evaluated using the same imputed datasets, preprocessing steps, cross-validation procedures, and held-out test set as the primary analysis to ensure comparability. Performance metrics were computed and pooled across imputations using Rubin’s rules, allowing direct comparison of discrimination and classification performance across model specifications. Multiple imputation diagnostics In Table S2, the R hat statistics show that all imputed variables achieved values very close to 1, indicating strong agreement across chains and stable behavior of the imputation process. Variables with substantial missing data, including flu uptake and the trust measures, also remained within acceptable ranges, which supports the overall stability of the imputations. In Figure S1, the trace plots display well mixed chains with stable means and standard deviations across all twenty iterations. The lines fluctuate around steady central values with no visible drift or separation between chains, providing clear visual evidence that the imputation models converged properly. Model discrimination and calibration In Tables S1-S3 and Figures S1-S5, several diagnostic visualizations support the robustness of the modeling framework. As shown in the SHAP summary plot and the AUC curve, the random forest model demonstrated strong discriminative ability, and both the training and test ROC curves indicated consistent separation between classes across probability thresholds. In addition, the multiple imputation procedure performed reliably. The R hat statistics were close to one and the trace plots showed stable mixing across chains, suggesting that convergence was achieved and that the imputed datasets were adequate for downstream analyses. Together, these diagnostics provide reassurance that the modeling process was well calibrated and that the resulting estimates are based on stable and coherent imputations. Across all models, performance metrics were generally strong (Table 4 ), with the Random Forest and Extreme Gradient Boosting models showing the highest overall accuracy and discrimination. Accuracy was similar for Linear Discriminant Analysis and Logistic Regression at about 0.769 with narrow confidence intervals, while the Neural Network reached 0.773, Random Forest reached 0.816, and Extreme Gradient Boosting reached 0.805. The area under the curve showed a similar pattern. Linear Discriminant Analysis, Logistic Regression, and the Neural Network each achieved values around 0.868, while Random Forest reached 0.895 and Extreme Gradient Boosting reached 0.885. Sensitivity remained high across all models, ranging from 0.826 for Random Forest to 0.871 for Logistic Regression. Specificity varied more widely, with Linear Discriminant Analysis and Logistic Regression showing lower values near 0.608, the Neural Network modestly higher at 0.624, and Random Forest performing strongest at 0.800 followed by Extreme Gradient Boosting at 0.734. Positive predictive values ranged from 0.781 to 0.871, with Random Forest again performing best. F scores were consistent across models, ranging from about 0.823 for Linear Discriminant Analysis and Logistic Regression to 0.848 for Random Forest and 0.843 for Extreme Gradient Boosting. Sensitivity analysis In Table S4, sensitivity analyses demonstrated that model performance was partially driven by dominant predictors, particularly flu vaccination behavior. In the random forest model, the area under the curve decreased from 0.895 (95% CI: 0.883–0.907) in the full model to 0.795 (95% CI: 0.794–0.796) after excluding flu vaccination. When both flu vaccination and age were excluded, performance further declined to 0.728 (95% CI: 0.726–0.730). Despite these reductions, the model retained moderate discriminatory ability, indicating that additional predictors contributed meaningful incremental value beyond the most influential variables. Discussion Principal’s findings and broader implication After fine-tuning, the Random Forest model provided the best predictive performance from the data. Given the modeling context, where a true counterfactual for vaccine behavior cannot be observed, these results provide an informative comparison of factors that are strongly associated with COVID-19 vaccine non-uptake. The patterns in the data show meaningful structure that helps us understand how different characteristics relate to vaccination decisions, even when a true counterfactual is not available. Importantly, these results reflect predictive associations rather than causal effects, and they should be interpreted as indicators of patterns in the data rather than evidence of direct influence. For example, trust in federal statistics, age, and flu uptake emerged as the strongest predictors. Although classification thresholds were selected during training to prioritize a target sensitivity of at least 0.90, the final test set sensitivity for some models was lower, reflecting expected performance attenuation when applying a training-optimized threshold to independent data. This gap highlights the tradeoff between sensitivity and specificity in predictive modeling and underscores that threshold calibration on training data does not guarantee identical performance in external evaluation. Although logistic regression achieved predictive performance comparable to several of the more complex machine learning models, its inclusion served as an important benchmark within the analytic framework. Logistic regression offers interpretability and the ability to estimate confidence intervals and conduct statistical inference for individual predictors, which remain valuable in epidemiologic research. In this setting, the modest performance gains observed for more flexible algorithms suggest that much of the predictive signal may be captured through relatively stable linear or moderately structured relationships. Similarly, the neural network demonstrated performance comparable to simpler models despite its greater complexity, indicating that additional nonlinear modeling capacity did not substantially improve discrimination. These findings reinforce that increased model complexity does not automatically yield superior predictive performance and that simpler, interpretable models may perform competitively when underlying relationships are not strongly nonlinear. An important consideration in interpreting these findings is that predictive performance may be driven disproportionately by a small subset of highly informative variables, particularly prior flu vaccination behavior. The strong separation observed in descriptive analyses suggests that flu uptake alone captures a substantial portion of the predictive signal. While SHAP values provide a useful ranking of predictors, they do not directly assess whether additional variables contribute meaningful incremental predictive value beyond the most dominant features. As a result, the apparent importance of multiple predictors in SHAP summaries may overstate their independent contribution to model performance. These findings suggest that while prior flu vaccination behavior is a dominant predictor, model performance is not solely dependent on a single variable, and a broader set of demographic and attitudinal factors contributes to prediction. Consistent with this, the comparable performance of simpler models such as logistic regression suggests that much of the predictive signal may be captured by a limited number of variables with relatively stable relationships. Therefore, these findings should not be interpreted as evidence that a broad set of factors is required to achieve strong prediction. Instead, they highlight that a small number of key predictors, particularly prior vaccination behavior, may account for a large share of model performance, with additional variables providing more modest incremental value. Policymakers should continue developing age-specific and flu-specific campaigns to encourage vaccine uptake. In addition, continued efforts are needed to strengthen public trust in United States federal statistics by increasing transparency around data collection, validation and dissemination processes. Although some predictors identified in the model, such as trust in federal statistics or prior flu vaccination behavior, may not be directly observable at the individual level in real-world outreach settings, these variables are routinely measured in national and state surveys and can inform population-level communication strategies. Rather than serving as tools for identifying specific individuals, these predictors may help guide resource allocation, messaging design, and geographic targeting at the population level in communities where similar patterns are observed. Previous research consistently identifies prior flu vaccination as a positive predictor of COVID-19 vaccine uptake [ 15 ]. Trust in government or statistical agencies similarly predicts uptake in multiple settings [ 50 ]. In the U.S., machine learning studies show that demographic variables including number of children and prior vaccine behavior improve model discrimination (Mewhirter et al.) [ 51 ]. These findings align with our results, where flu vaccine history, age, number of children, and trust emerged as top predictors in the Random Forest model. Additionally, a higher number of children was associated with lower predicted COVID-19 vaccine uptake, consistent with patterns observed in the descriptive analyses. Prior simulation work by Orsini et al. demonstrated that SHAP values can recover meaningful epidemiologic patterns, including interaction effects, in both simulated and real prospective cohort settings [ 52 ]. In related methodological work, Svensson et al. evaluated SHAP based interpretations across a range of causal effect models and provided practical recommendations for using SHAP when true counterfactuals are unobserved. Several public health applications have similarly leveraged SHAP informed machine learning models to inform decision making when randomized trials are infeasible, including policy evaluation (Lyu et al.), targeted maternal health interventions (Sani et al.), and resource allocation in neurological care (Jin et al.) [ 53 – 54 ]. More general guidance from Feretzakis et al. and Ponce Bobadilla et al. emphasizes that SHAP supports evidence-based practice by providing transparent global and local explanations, even when the underlying data is observational. Consistent with this literature, we interpret our SHAP analyses as hypothesis generating tools that highlight groups and attributes associated with high predicted risk of COVID-19 vaccine uptake, informing outreach and policy discussions without asserting causal effects. Together, these findings further illustrate that prediction-oriented methods such as SHAP can extract stable structure from observational data, offering insight into likely patterns of vaccine uptake even when direct experimentation or true counterfactual evaluation is not possible. Strengths, limitations, and future research directions A strength of the Household Pulse Survey is that it is nationally representative and conducted by the US Census Bureau. This recent data provides a unique perspective over other studies that relied on data from 2020 to 2021 [ 18 ]. However, the limitations of self-reported data in vaccination studies are well documented in the literature [ 55 – 57 ]. Self-reported data may introduce information bias because participants may report false information on their vaccination status. This possibility of bias may affect the accuracy of findings and should be carefully considered when interpreting results. In addition, several predictors exhibited substantial variable-level missingness and were addressed through multiple imputation; although convergence diagnostics supported stable imputation, the results should be interpreted with appropriate caution. Furthermore, due to its cross-sectional design, this study provides only a snapshot in time, which limits causal inference and restricts assessment of how institutional trust and vaccination behavior change over time [ 58 ]. Conclusions Using nationally representative data from the Household Pulse Survey, we demonstrated that modern machine learning methods can accurately predict COVID-19 vaccine uptake. The random forest classifier achieved the strongest overall performance, and SHAP based feature importance showed that flu vaccination uptake, age, trust in federal statistics, and number of children were the most influential predictors of non-uptake. These variables had the largest SHAP values across imputations, indicating differences in recent flu vaccination behavior, variation in age, and reported trust in federal statistics contributed most to the predicted probabilities generated by the model. Because the analytic framework is predictive rather than causal, these results should be interpreted as empirical patterns in the data and not as estimates of intervention effects. Nevertheless, the findings highlight key domains that may inform future public health strategy, including efforts to strengthen trust in federal data systems and to coordinate effective and efficient communication around flu and COVID-19 vaccination.
