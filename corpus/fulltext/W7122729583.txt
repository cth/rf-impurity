Published 2026 by American Chemical Society This article is licensed under CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 Abstract Gaps remain in the interpretability and real-world applicability of children’s elevated blood lead level (EBLL) predictive models, which are important for fostering efficient health intervention. We produced predictive statistical models using publicly available population data and children’s EBLL rates (≥5 or ≥3.5 μg/dL) at the United States (U.S.) census tract resolution from Michigan (2014–2016), Ohio (2014–2016), California (2018–2022), Minnesota (2017–2021), and Wisconsin (2018–2021). Spatial and nonspatial random forest and logistic regression models were developed for each state to predict whether a census tract was above or below the 75th percentile EBLL rate. Spatial logistic regression models performed the best based on overall predictive performance, evaluated through a 10-fold cross-validation approach. Predictive accuracy ranged from 82% to 90%. The most influential final predictive variables for each optimal model varied, but there were consistencies across states. For example, the percent of homes built before 1940 was significant ( p &lt; 0.05) across all models, increasing the odds of a tract having higher EBLL rates (between 1 and 7%). By accounting for spatial autocorrelation, our modeling approach improves predictions by capturing local clustering effects. This approach can be applied elsewhere across the U.S. to inform prevention and mitigation actions and make predictions where measured EBLLs are sparse. Introduction Lead (Pb) is a known neurotoxicant that is particularly harmful to young children, with its health effects and the societal ramifications of exposure well understood for decades. − The widespread dispersal of Pb in both the outdoor and indoor environment in the 20th century resulted in blood Pb levels (BLLs) that were quite high in children, well above what would be considered “natural.” Two of the main Pb sources, which are now outlawed for sale in most countries throughout the world, were leaded gasoline and leaded paint. , Unfortunately, both sources can persist in the environment for extended time scales (i.e., hundreds of years) under typical environmental conditions, providing a legacy source of contamination long after their initial use. Thus, exposure to Pb continues, although exposure has declined in most middle- and high-income countries. Paint and legacy leaded gasoline often co-occur in the environment. , This is particularly true in urban environments as older homes often have leaded paint and are located near roadways where leaded gasoline was emitted and deposited in residential soilsalthough there is often heterogeneity in Pb soil concentrations within household yard soils. , While previous research indicates that older housing age is one of the main drivers behind both higher BLLs and elevated levels of Pb in house dust and soil, − heterogeneity in population characteristics and environmental Pb distribution can complicate the prediction of children BLLs and elevated BLL (EBLL) rates. This has been demonstrated when statistical models effectively predict BLLs in one state and not as well in another. Previous research has modeled childhood BLLs at national and state levels across the U.S. with varying successranging from only accounting for 20% of the variance in state-level BLL concentration predictive models (e.g., Texas in one study) to substantial agreement between geospatial EBLL hotpots and Pb exposure indices and models across multiple states (i.e., 78% agreement between EBLL hotspots and Pb high-risk areas). − A main challenge is data consistency and availability across states, particularly at the census tract geography or smaller. , This makes data interpretability and health intervention challenging, particularly in data-sparse areas where children’s BLL testing rates may be low. Additionally, only one state-level BLL predictive model within the U.S. (for Maryland) has been published to date that uses spatially explicit predictive BLL models, where a random effect is present in the model to incorporate spatial autocorrelation. , That BLL predictive model was a Bayesian binomial regression model and found spatial structure (autocorrelation) in EBLL probabilities and a positive association between the neighborhood deprivation index and EBLL risk. Incorporating spatial autocorrelation can significantly improve predictive modelseven complex machine learning models such as random forest models at a national scale. Thus, our work across five different U.S. states sought to address current gaps in the literature regarding predicting children’s EBLL rates in two ways: (1) to develop accurate, parsimonious statistical models that effectively predict where children’s EBLL rates are the highest at the census tract level using the same initial public data (American Community 5-YR Survey data from the U.S. Census Bureau) as predictor variables and (2) to generate a robust, novel statistical modeling framework for EBLL rates that incorporates spatial autocorrelation. With the creation and validation of this approach, it can be applied to inform Pb interventions via practitioners (i.e., public health agencies at state or local levels) in additional states within the U.S. that collect BLL surveillance data for public health protection. Materials and Methods Data Selection and Processing We used state-provided children EBLL rates at the census tract level (small geographic unit within a U.S. county, designed to contain around 4000 people) from Michigan (MI) (2014–2016), Ohio (OH) (2014–2016), California (CA) (2018–2022), Minnesota (MN) (2017–2021), and Wisconsin (WI) (2018–2021). These data were selected based on public availability and geographic spread as it allowed for the comparison of different regions in the U.S. (the Midwest and West). Children EBLL rate data for each state are available on our public GitHub project page ( https://github.com/USEPA/BLL-Predictive-Modeling ), where links to the initial data sets and the processed data are provided. Original BLL data from OH and MI at the individual child level were obtained through data use agreements following institutional review board approval, described in Xue et al. and Stanek et al. In general, deduplicated BLL test data for each state were used, where an individual child’s BLL test was included (one per child per year), and represented either an initial screening capillary test or a venous sample confirmation test. EBLL counts were determined based on single test BLLs of children (&lt;6 years old) that were ≥5 μg/dL in each state, except CA, which was ≥3.5 μg/dL. This difference in the EBLL threshold level is due to different state guidelines for intervention and data reporting, where CA has implemented a lower value that is based on the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) 2021 reference value of 3.5 μg/dL. It is noted though that over the past several years, MN, WI, OH, and MI have also been moving toward using the 3.5 μg/dL threshold for child EBLLs, which has been or will be reflected in newer public EBLL data. EBLL rates were calculated based on dividing the number of EBLL tests by the total number of children tested, with the data aggregated from all the years of testing. Measured EBLL rates varied throughout each state, oftentimes with high EBLL rates near urban centers ( Figure ). Tracts with &lt;50 children tested were omitted. For WI and MN, tracts with ≥50 children tested and a suppressed EBLL rate had EBLL counts randomly imputed ( Table ). Because all WI and MI census tracts with suppressed EBLL data contained between 1 and 4 children with an EBLL, , a random integer value between 1 and 4 was imputed for each census tract with suppressed data. The EBLL rate was then calculated by dividing the imputed EBLL integer by the total number of children tested in each census tract. Predictive Variables for Modeling Census tract level population data from the American Community 5-Year Survey (ACS) were included as predictive variables, paired by year with the EBLL data and chosen a priori based on previously published associations with EBLLs (19 initial variables, Table S1 ). ,,,,, In pairing EBLL and ACS data together by year, the last year of the ACS 5-Year was matched with the last year of the EBLL data. For example, ACS 5-Year data from 2018 to 2022 was paired with CA (2018–2022) EBLL data, ACS 5-Year data from 2012 to 2016 was paired with OH (2014–2016) EBLL data, etc. Census tract geographical boundaries were also paired according to the time periods of the data, with data from CA, MN, and WI all paired with 2020 census tract boundaries, while MI and OH data were paired with 2010 census tract boundaries. All ACS data were pulled directly from the U.S. Census Bureau API using the tidycensus package in R version 4.4.2 and merged based on GEOID with census tract EBLL data. ACS data were originally pulled as estimated count data for each census tract and then normalized to a percent (except for median household income) through dividing by the total count for the selected variable type. While not normalizing the median household income should not affect random forest models, it may affect logistic regression models. However, when comparing log 10 normalized and non-normalized median household incomes within models where it was a significant variable, the final models with normalized values performed slightly worse (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) values decreasing between 0.004 and 0.01). The merged EBLL rates and predictive variable data sets were saved as “sf” (simple features) objects in R based on census tract geographies to preserve the spatial attributes of the data, which were used directly in spatially explicit statistical modeling. Statistical Modeling Approach and Model Refinement Spatial random forest, nonspatial random forest, spatial logistic regression (binomial generalized linear model), and nonspatial logistic regression models were developed for each state. All of the spatial models incorporate spatial autocorrelation as a random effect, using an exponential spatial covariance function. Details of the random forest and spatial model structures are available in Dietrich et al. and Dumelle et al., but in general, all spatial models included a random effect to account for spatial dependence within the data, estimated through an exponential spatial covariance function ( eq ). 1 R = exp ( − M / φ ) where R is the resulting matrix, M is the matrix of the Euclidean distances among samples (census tracts), and φ represents the range parameter, which influences the distance at which the effects of spatial autocorrelation are estimated to occur. The general spatial linear model equation used for the spatial statistical models is based on eq 2 y = X β + τ + ϵ where y is the response variable, X is the matrix of explanatory (predictive) variables, β is a vector of fixed effects, τ is the spatial random effect, and ϵ represents a vector of random errors. The dependent variable was a binary factor of above (value of 1) or below (value of 0) the 75th percentile EBLL rates for each individual state model (actual children EBLL rates and percentiles in Table ), while the independent variables (predictive variables) were the percent-normalized ACS 5-Year data ( Table S1 ). Statistical models were developed in R through the ranger package (random forest), spmodel package (spatial models), stats package as part of base R (nonspatial logistic regression), and MASS package (stepwise nonspatial logistic regression). Initially, all 19 predictive variables were included in stepwise logistic regression models (both directions) that used Akaike information criterion (AIC) to select the best models (stepwise logistic regression models that contained the lowest AIC scores), based on 80/20 random training/testing data splits of the individual state data. Following that, four different types of models for each state were developeda random forest model (default settings in the ranger package, such as 500 trees and unlimited maximal tree depth), a spatial (residual) random forest model, a logistic regression model, and a spatial logistic regression modelfollowing a similar approach to Dietrich et al. Details on the structure of the random forest spatial residual model are available in Dietrich et al., but in short, a spatial linear model was fit to the residuals of a random forest model. Specifically, our approach involved independently evaluating each type of model (for each state) for predictive accuracy using the same explanatory variables for each model as previously selected through stepwise regression based on AIC values. After running each type of model for each state, explanatory variables were dropped that had p values &gt;0.05, and these initial predictive models for each state were tested for performance accuracy based on 10-fold cross-validation. Final parsimonious models were then developed with the goal of maintaining (or improving) predictive model performance while reducing the number of variables needed. Variables were assessed and then dropped from models if high collinearity was observed (variance inflation factor &gt;5 ( Table S2 ) or spearman rank correlation coefficient &gt;0.7 ( Figure S1 )). Additionally, uncertainty (percent margin of error) in ACS racial variables (such as those known from the scientific literature to be strong predictors of BLLs) can be especially large due to the survey nature of the ACS and particularly when group population size is low. This, in addition to the minimal effect on the overall models’ performance (see the Results section), led to all racial variables being dropped from the predictive models to help improve the reliability of the predictive variables and contribute further to the overarching modeling goal of parsimony. The resulting final parsimonious predictive statistical models were then assessed for model accuracy/performance based on 10-fold cross-validation ( Table )four different types of models for each state, as conducted before. The primary metrics used to assess model performance were mean squared error (MSE), median absolute error (MEDAE), AUC, and model predictive accuracy. When comparing the different types of initial predictive models within each state, the spatial logistic regression models consistently performed the best overall (based on the combined assessment of model cross-validation MSE, MEDAE, AUC, and accuracy) across 10-fold cross-validation ( Table ). Generally, spatial models performed better than nonspatial models. However, model performance varied between states, with CA experiencing the greatest improvements in model performance when adding a spatial random effect (AUC increased by 0.09 or 0.12 for random forest and logistic regression models, respectively), while WI experienced the smallest gains (AUC increased 0.01 for both random forest and logistic regression models; Table ). AUC values varied between 0.79 and 0.92 for the best individual state models, with predictive accuracy between 80% and 90% ( Table ). OH had the best overall performing state-level model with an AUC of 0.92 and a predictive accuracy of 90% for its spatial logistic regression model. MN had the worst overall model performance, with its spatial logistic regression model containing an AUC of 0.79 and a predictive accuracy of 80%. Final Parsimonious Model Results and Comparison After the removal of highly collinear variables and variables associated with race, spatial logistic regression models continued to perform the best overall within each state ( Table ). The CA data-based model still had the greatest improvement in model performance from the nonspatial to spatial models ( Table ) and the greatest spatially dependent random error variance (partial sill) ( Table ). In general, the final parsimonious models either performed similarly or slightly better than the models that included racial variables and collinear variables. For example, the AUC for the CA final parsimonious spatial logistic regression model remained essentially unchanged despite dropping the percent Asian and percent Hispanic predictive variables ( Table ). AUC for MI increased in the parsimonious model from 0.90 to 0.91, for WI from 0.84 to 0.87, and for MN from 0.79 to 0.83. The OH spatial logistic regression model was the only example where the AUC did not increase, decreasing slightly from 0.92 to 0.91. When applying the final state-level parsimonious spatial logistic regression models ( Table ) to make predictions of whether unmeasured census tracts have “high” or “low” EBLL rates, these predictions were primarily for more rural census tracts, such as northern MI, WI, and MN, or the “border” census tracts of CA not centrally located within the state ( Figure ). CA and WI tended to have greater overall probabilities of containing high EBLL rates in census tracts with no measured EBLL values, while MI and MN tended to have lower overall probabilities. Influence of Individual Predictive Model Variables The structure of logistic regression models allows for the computation of odds ratios (ORs), which quantify the association between the outcome variable (in this case, the dependent variable of high or low EBLL rates) and a predictive variable. Most variables across all final parsimonious state-level models had an OR &gt; 1, primarily between 1.01 and 1.08. This means that for each one unit increase in the predictive variable (i.e., every percent increase in homes built before 1940), there is a 1% to 8% increase in the odds of a high EBLL rate outcome within a census tract. Older housing age (percentage of homes built pre-1980 or pre-1940) was significant for every state, with estimated mean ORs between 1.01 and 1.07, except for WI, where the OR for percentage pre-1980 homes was just under 1, with the 95% confidence interval overlapping with 1 ( Figure ). The percentage of homes built pre-1940 was the most consistently statistically significant variable for all states and was included in all five state-level final parsimonious models ( Figure ). The percent of renters within a census tract was the next most common variable, included in the majority of state models except for MI ( Figure ). ORs ranged between 1.01 and 1.03 for the percent of renters ( Figure ). Median household income and the percent of naturalized U.S. citizens were the only variables with ORs at or below 1 ( Figure ). Discussion Differences and Similarities between State-Level Predictive Models Differences were observed between state-level predictive models regarding both relative predictive accuracy and the main predictive variables influencing the models. MI and OH final parsimonious models yielded the highest AUC values and predictive model accuracy across 10-fold cross-validation ( Table ). Both states also had the oldest BLL data relative to the other states, covering the years 2014–2016. This may point to potential factors related to EBLLs, such as median household income and older housing, being more related to children’s EBLL rates in the past. This relationship between predictive variables such as housing age and median household income with children’s EBLL rates may be weaker in recent years due to Pb exposure intervention actions being effectively targeted toward these risk factors. Alternatively, the variables most associated with EBLLs in MI and OH are perhaps more aligned with existing knowledge on Pb exposure and coincide better with the initial ACS variable list ( Table S1 ). The lack of imputed data for MI and OH may also contribute to greater overall model performance, although the CA model did not have any imputed data and was a larger data set, yet had less predictive accuracy. Based on the data available for our study and the project scope, we cannot pinpoint the exact reason(s) why the OH and MI predictive statistical models performed the best, other than the initial subset of variables from the ACS explains more variance in the OH and MI models relative to CA, WI, and MN. Previous research on older BLL data (i.e., collection years ranging from 1999 to 2018) and EBLL risk factors in other states and across the U.S. found similar variables to our study that were significantly associated directly or indirectly with EBLLs or EBLL risk, including proportions of pre-1960 housing and poverty, vacant housing and homes built before 1950 or 1940, or homes built before 1940 and median home value. Unfortunately, detailed analysis of more recent BLL data is lacking in the literature (i.e., post-2020) as there is often a lag between BLL data collection, data availability, and detailed statistical analysis. CA, WI, and MN final parsimonious models each had AUCs between 0.83 and 0.87, demonstrating high values for predictive models yet lower than the AUCs for MI and OH ( Table ). CA, WI, and MN have different development histories than MI and OH, although in general, CA has a relatively unique infrastructure compared to all four other states due to differences in population growth, economy, and regulation. These differences may drive some of the nuance between predictive model performance and the significant predictive variables included in the final state models, as may the included years of EBLL rate data. The fact that the CA spatial models improved the most in predictive performance from the nonspatial models relative to the other states ( Table ) points to the spatial autocorrelation of children EBLL rates being greater in CA than in MI, OH, MN, and WI (at least at the census tract geographical level). However, additional investigation is needed to probe these differences. Schultz et al. also reported differences between predictive BLL regression models when comparing across different states. They developed a regression model to estimate BLLs based on 543,295 individual records in MI from 1999 to 2009. While the model worked well for MI census tracts (predicting geometric mean BLLs), with an R 2 of 0.69, the model performance decreased substantially in Massachusetts (MA) and Texas (TX) with R 2 values of 0.28 and 0.20, respectively. The combination of these prior findings with our results demonstrates the uniqueness of state-level factors and variables that may influence children BLLs. There were many similarities in the predictive variables across the final models such as the percentage of renters in a census tract, the percentage of vacant homes, and the percentage of homes built prior to 1940 ( Figure ). Each of these variables was significant in three or more of the final state models. For example, the percentage of homes built pre-1940 within a census tract was a positively associated significant predictive variable within all five final state parsimonious models (as the percentage of homes built pre-1940 in a census tract went up, so did the odds of a census tract having high EBLL rates). This supports previous findings of BLL predictive modeling efforts (for EBLL risk or EBLL values) across the U.S., ,, and makes sense, as homes built before 1940 in the U.S. are most likely to contain lead-based paint hazards. The percentage of renters was significant across all final parsimonious state models except for MI ( Figure ). This supports previous research as well as rental status was important in EBLL risk indices in Minnesota (MN) and Maryland (MD) and for the increased likelihood of children having higher BLLs in Rochester, New York (NY). The percentage of renters sometimes covaried slightly with other predictive variables in our analysis, such as median household income and age of housing ( Figure S1 ), but not enough that it lost its significance within models ( Table ). Rental status can be related to the building condition, which has been shown to be an important factor associated with child EBLLs. However, we do not have paired rental status and building condition data at the census tract level to test this hypothesis. All other final parsimonious model variables (percent homes vacant, median household income, percent homes built pre-1980, and the percent of naturalized U.S. citizens) may also covary with factors such as average housing condition within a census tract or environmental data such as soil, dust, or drinking water Pb concentrations. Collection of additional data would help refine these predictive models and aid in model interpretation, as discussed more below. Implications and Real-World Applicability of a Statistical Modeling Approach Parsimonious models are useful in simplifying interpretations and preventing overfitting of data, which can be helpful for applications such as environmental management (i.e., managing lake eutrophication) or, in our case, primary prevention at the state or local level to reduce children’s BLLs. Previous research compared binary (high vs low) childhood EBLL predictions using a parsimonious logistic regression model and a more complex random forest model and found random forest to perform better overall. However, the random forest model contained over 1000 predictor variables (after various data aggregations). While the list of important predictors in the random forest model (based on a scaled importance value between 0 and 1 for each variable) was much smaller at 25 variables, interpretability and application of a random forest model with this number of variables can still be challenging. However, it is noted that if the main objective is to produce the most accurate model, then variable parsimony is less important. Lobo et al. also made predictions of children EBLL rates, with random forest models performing the best (although at larger spatial scaleszip code or city/town level), but included between 35 and 47 features in their predictive models, and very subtle differences were observed in most of their top 15 features for each model as ranked by Gini importance (mean decrease in impurity). Our final parsimonious models each contained between two and five predictive variables, and spatial logistic regression outperformed spatial random forest models. Adding a spatial random effect to account for spatial autocorrelation was particularly important for improving both types of models. Although our models made predictions at the census tract scale and not for individual samples (as was done by Potash et al.), the fact that spatial logistic regression models performed the best across five different states implies that sometimes, simpler may be better. We recognize, however, that a random forest model with more predictive variables would likely outperform our final parsimonious spatial logistic regression models. However, such a model would have less real-world interpretability, and improvement would likely be minimal based on the already high AUCs of our final parsimonious spatial logistic regression models. Model interpretability is particularly important for primary prevention of children EBLLs based on associations between predictive variables (i.e., old housing stock, percent vacant homes, etc.) and EBLL rates, which enables closer examination of these variables to determine what exposure determinants or sources are ultimately driving the higher children EBLL rates. A goal of this work was to provide a statistical modeling example for elucidating the primary variables associated with the highest EBLL rates (i.e., &gt;75th percentile) within a state, so that any given state could determine the population characteristics that are most important for primary intervention. There are characteristics that are likely important across all U.S. states, such as the percent of pre-1940 homes, but there are also more nuanced characteristics that likely vary from state-to-state, such as the percent of vacant homes or median household income. Unlike national Pb exposure risk indices (i.e., those mentioned in Zartarian et al.), this state-by-state modeling approach is based on making predictions of actual EBLL rate thresholds through training models on publicly available data (ACS U.S. Census Bureau variables) that any state can access. While states must have at least some BLL testing data to run the models employed here, they do not need comprehensive BLL surveillance across the entire state. Furthermore, all 19 original 19 predictive variables used in this modeling effort should capture most of the variance explained by EBLLs across all U.S. states. Additional variables from the ACS survey can always be included, though, if local knowledge of possible childhood Pb exposure points to their importance. While these are statistical, probabilistic models rather than physical, process-based modelswhich makes verifying causation challengingthey nonetheless provide a starting point for more detailed regional-based assessments and linkages to the likely determinants of childhood Pb exposure. Some examples of specific determinants may be degraded housing conditions (i.e., peeling paint or corroding Pb pipes), the Pb concentrations in residential media (i.e., water, soil, dust, food, air, consumer products), and environmental media in schools or daycare centers where children spend a large portion of their time. Study Limitations As with most analyses of secondary health data, there are several limitations in this study. The first is the lack of universal blood Pb testing during the time periods our EBLL data were collected in each of the states analyzed, meaning that testing was likely biased to include children deemed most at risk of Pb exposure, such as households on Medicaid or those in housing built pre-1978. However, this potential testing bias is common in most states and acknowledged in previous BLL modeling efforts. ,, Based on an analysis of children’s EBLL data from 1999 to 2010, most states that we modeled (WI, OH, MI) tended to report nearly the expected number of EBLL cases to the CDC, although the cumulative ascertainment ratio for MN and CA were notably lower. We are confident, though, that the EBLL data in our study still included the majority of the child EBLLs measured within each state (OH, MI, WI, MN, and CA) during the specified time periods, although future analyses of who is missed during childhood BLL surveillance would be beneficial. Data imputation was substantially used for census tracts with suppressed data in MN, 605 out of 1104 tracts or 54.8% ( Table ), and this may have affected modeling results. However, because we only included census tracts with 50 or more children tested and conducted random imputation within a small range of values (between 1 and 4 child EBLL counts, as integers), this should inject minimal additional variance. When omitting census tracts that contain suppressed EBLL data and then conducting the exact same modeling methodology with the same parsimonious predictor variables for WI (noting, though, that the 75th percentile cutoff EBLL rate value decreased from 3.75% to 3.40%), the AUC for the WI optimal spatial logistic regression model increased slightly from 0.870 to 0.899 ( Table S3 ). Unfortunately, it was not possible to conduct comparative models for MN on the nonimputed data as there were simply too many census tracts with EBLL rates of 0 (75th percentile cutoff EBLL rate for MN was 0 when looking at only nonimputed census tracts). Balanced accuracy [average of the recall (true positive rate) for each EBLL rate classification] was also the lowest for MN relative to the other four states (0.61 vs 0.70–0.79; Table S4 ), implying that classification with the 75th percentile EBLL rate cutoff may particularly imbalance predictive accuracy for MN. Thus, better data resolution in MN and not relying as heavily on data imputation will likely increase model predictive performance and reduce predictive accuracy imbalance. There were also distinct differences in blood Pb testing procedures and reporting between state data sets. As noted before, in the provided EBLL rate data, all states but CA utilized a threshold of 5 μg/dL to identify a child with an EBLL, while CA used a threshold of 3.5 μg/dL. This prevents aggregation of all state EBLL rate data together, although each individual state predictive model still achieves the same goal of identifying where children are most likely to have the highest EBLL rates within a given state. The actual blood Pb testing procedures and reporting also varied between states or even within states. For example, in both MN and WI, although data were deduplicated, there was no distinction between capillary or venous samples. The changing blood Pb level thresholds that triggered confirmatory venous sampling likely influenced the EBLL data between states. Additionally, MN and WI suppressed some of their data, while the other states did not. This introduces some inconsistencies when trying to compare models between states, but we contend that keeping our statistical predictive models state-specific helps reduce the variability associated with these inconsistencies. Lastly, it is important to note the limitations in the U.S. Census Bureau ACS 5-Year data used as predictive variables in this study as these are data based on a population survey approach and include inherited uncertainty. Future research on sensitivity analyses of the ACS predictor variables and the subsequent impact on the EBLL rate binary dependent variable may be useful, particularly for the ACS variables with the greatest margins of error. A Path Forward and Opportunities to Build on the Predictive Models The models tested here on large, state-level data sets at the census tract geographical scale provide a useful foundation for states and local agencies to begin focused, more efficient, BLL reduction efforts. Testing the generalizability and differences of the models, methods, and predictive variables from the ACS survey in other regions of the U.S. would be worthwhile. Our same modeling methodology can also be applied at finer spatial scales in states and municipalities that have access to any point-level (spatial) child EBLL data, which may reveal any EBLL heterogeneity present within census tracts. Those data can still be paired with publicly available ACS data as needed (i.e., census block or census tract scale) to assist with model refinement, although any individually paired lifestyle data, such as housing information for the child, would be ideal to reduce predictive variable uncertainty and any required assumptions. Moving forward, more state- or local-level data at the census tract scale or smaller can help researchers and practitioners better understand the true drivers behind EBLLs, particularly now that exposure is often chronic and difficult to attribute to a source. Additional publicly available environmental data such as water, air, and soil chemistry at greater spatial resolutions would be particularly useful for expanding upon local research efforts of paired children BLLs and environmental Pb data. − However, our modeling efforts demonstrate that publicly available data such as the ACS 5-Year data set can still provide highly accurate predictive models at the census tract scale, helping focus children EBLL intervention efforts. Comparison of children’s BLL data between states with and without universal testing requirements would also provide additional insights into the potential nuance between EBLL associated variables in predictive models. Ideally, detailed long-term analyses of BLL data in states that have switched from targeted testing to universal testing (i.e., Maryland) would provide the most insight into potential testing bias. Additionally, detailed modeling of BLLs over extended time scales (i.e., decadal) to assess changes in predictive variable importance over time would be particularly useful in determining how human and environmental factors that potentially influence child Pb exposure changed through time. While this can be a challenge due to changing data collection methods and data availability, increasing technological advancements and data access over the last several years point to this being possible. While widespread child Pb exposure has been an issue for nearly a century now, the number of children adversely affected in the U.S. is orders of magnitude less than it was several decades ago. Policy (i.e., banning of Pb in paint and on-road vehicle gasoline), families, health practitioners, and scientists have made significant strides in reducing children’s BLLs, and with increased access to data and more advanced computational modeling techniques, we can collectively further minimalize children’s exposure to Pb.
