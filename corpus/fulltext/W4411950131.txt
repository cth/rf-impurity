Small lakes are suffocating as winters warm 122 32 4 8 2025 e2514621122 Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America 10.1073/pnas.2514621122 40758898 Significance Climate change causes rapid ice loss from freshwater lakes, but overall water quality implications remain poorly understood amid a lack of large-scale studies. Using a 63-y dataset of chemical and physical measurements in thousands of lakes, we tracked long-term trends in the mean dissolved oxygen content under ice, a key metric of aquatic ecosystem health. We found that in general, a shorter ice cover increased oxygen concentrations by lengthening the fall ventilation period and shortening the duration of oxygen consumption in winter. However in small lakes, the most abundant size class, carry-over of increasingly oxygen-depleted conditions in summer led to an expansion of bottom-water hypoxia under ice. Winter fish kills may decline with ice loss only in larger northern lakes. Dissolved oxygen (DO) is an essential resource in ice-covered lakes, regulating water quality and biodiversity, including the survival of economically important fish species. Most of the world’s lakes seasonally freeze, often resulting in oxygen depletion as ice cover inhibits water column ventilation and snow cover limits photosynthesis while respiration continues. Widespread shortening of ice-cover duration in a warmer world might improve winter oxygenation, but this hypothesis remains untested. Here, we performed a systematic analysis of 6.6 million physical and chemical observations from 19,645 lakes in the Northern Hemisphere during 1960 to 2022. Contrary to expectations, under-ice DO trends ranged from significantly negative in small lakes (A surf &lt;10 ha) (−0.14 ± 0.05 mg L −1 decade −1 ) to significantly positive in large lakes (≥10 4 ha) (0.11 ± 0.03 mg L −1 decade −1 ). This morphometric scaling emerged partly because ice-cover periods have shortened 2.2 times faster in large lakes compared to small lakes. Hierarchical modeling revealed that in smaller lakes, increasingly oxygen-depleted conditions in summer carried over to the ice-cover season, because fetch size limited wind-driven aeration in fall. As a result of this cross-seasonal ecological memory, under-ice hypoxic zones have expanded. Oxygen trended most negative in small eutrophic and humic lakes with high seasonal oxygen depletion rates. In larger lakes (≥10 3 ha), negligible summer deoxygenation, prolonged ventilation in fall, and shortening of the oxygen drawdown period in winter explained positive DO trends. Ice and snow cover prevent DO replenishment from the atmosphere (ventilation) and limit photosynthetic oxygenation, while DO consumption via respiration and nitrification gradually depletes oxygen until increases in light or meltwater inputs enable reoxygenation of the water column ( 2 – 5 ). Hypoxia (DO &lt; 2 mg L −1 ) diminishes the habitat of overwintering aquatic organisms including economically important fish species and can lead to mass mortality events (i.e. winter fish kills) if persistent ( 6 ). Anoxia (DO &lt; 0.5 mg L −1 ) promotes accumulation of methane, a potent greenhouse gas ( 7 ), as well as nutrients which can fuel harmful algal blooms ( 8 , 9 ). The vast majority of the world’s lakes are seasonally ice-covered ( 10 ) and are located in arctic and boreal regions ( 11 ). Here, climate warming has occurred at two to four times the global rate ( 12 ), resulting in higher lake surface water temperatures ( 13 ) and overall shorter ice cover ( 14 , 15 ). Global analyses of monitoring data from thousands of lakes have uncovered widespread deoxygenation in summer, linked primarily to warming-induced declines in DO solubility and prolongation of thermal stratification ( 16 , 17 ). However, no such systematic study has been conducted for the winter season, and the decadal trajectory of oxygen under ice remains unclear. Prediction of under-ice DO trends is complicated by a multitude of drivers ( 18 ). In seasonally ice-covered lakes that stratify (intermittently) in summer, we can characterize these drivers as long-term shifts in oxygen dynamics during four distinct annual phases, here termed “summer” (oxygen depletion), “fall” (water column ventilation), “winter I” (ice growth, light-limited photosynthesis, oxygen depletion) and “winter II” (ice- and snowmelt, reoxygenation) ( 19 ) ( Fig. 1 ). Widespread delay of ice-on dates ( 20 ) may allow for longer mixing and improved ventilation in fall ( 21 ), increasing DO at ice-on. Model studies suggest that shorter ice-cover duration can also increase under-ice DO by reducing the time over which hypoxia can develop during winter I ( 22 , 23 ), particularly in lakes with high oxygen depletion rates, such as small or eutrophic lakes ( 3 ) ( Fig. 1 B ). Earlier onset of snowmelt runoff ( 24 , 25 ) or photosynthesis ( 26 ) (i.e. the winter II phase), which oxygenates the upper water column below ice ( 27 ), can further shorten the oxygen depletion period. Conversely, oxygen-depleted conditions that increasingly develop in warmer summers ( 16 , 17 ) might carry over to the ice-cover season in lakes where fall ventilation is incomplete ( 28 , 29 ), a phenomenon more broadly defined as “ecological memory” ( 30 ). Decadal declines in under-ice DO may also result from net increases in oxygen consumption rates via enhanced loading of terrestrial organic carbon (browning) ( 31 ) or nutrients (eutrophication). Few of the drivers of under-ice DO trends have been quantified in the field or at regional scales. In order to quantify and understand DO trends in seasonally ice-covered lakes, we conducted a large-scale systematic analysis of under-ice and ice-free water quality monitoring data from 19,645 lakes in Fennoscandia and North America from 1960 to 2022, comprising over 6.6 million observations ( SI Appendix , Fig. S1 ) ( 32 ). We hypothesized that shortening of ice-cover duration over the last six decades has resulted in long-term increases in oxygen concentrations under ice and that under-ice DO trends vary predictably with lake size, depth, trophic state, and humic content (terrestrial organic carbon concentrations). To test these hypotheses, we compiled, harmonized, and quality-checked measurements of DO, temperature, chlorophyll a , total phosphorus (TP) (indicating trophic state), water color (indicating humic content), and lake surface area and maximum depth, from governmental databases and nonprofit organizations. To examine the impact of ice-cover duration shifts on under-ice DO, we additionally compiled 56,928 ice-on and ice-off dates from 1,107 lakes in the same regions. We focused on oxygen trends in January–March (winter I and early winter II), the period when most lakes in our dataset were sampled. Results Under-Ice Oxygen Trends Diverged with Lake Size. We examined decadal patterns in volume-weighted mean DO concentrations (DO vw ) under ice between 1960 and 2022 across discrete classes of lake size, depth, trophic state, and humic content ( Fig. 2 ). DO vw was lowest in small, shallow, eutrophic, and polyhumic (very brown) lakes, in accordance with known indicators of hypoxia ( 33 ). To quantify the under-ice DO vw trends we fitted hierarchical linear models (HLMs) for each lake class with random intercepts for each lake ( SI Appendix , Table S1 ). This approach allowed us to utilize the full dataset rather than a limited subset of lakes with long-term winter timeseries. We found that DO vw trends diverged with lake size, ranging from −0.14 ± 0.05 mg L −1 decade −1 (n obs = 6,618, trend ± 95% CI) in lakes &lt;10 ha to 0.11 ± 0.03 mg L −1 decade −1 (n obs = 2241) in lakes ≥10 4 ha ( SI Appendix , Table S2 ). A comparable pattern emerged across lake maximum depths, with deoxygenation in shallower lakes (z max &lt; 8 m) and oxygenation in deeper lakes (z max ≥ 14 m) ( Fig. 2 B ). These morphometric differences amplified with trophic state and humic content ( Fig. 2 C and D ). Under-ice DO vw severely declined in small eutrophic lakes (−0.18 ± 0.09 mg L −1 decade −1 , n obs = 2,129) and greatly increased in large eutrophic lakes (0.16 ± 0.06 mg L −1 decade −1 , n obs = 762), while in oligotrophic lakes, DO vw trended weakly positive across size classes (0.03 ± 0.02 mg L −1 decade −1 , n obs = 11,778) ( SI Appendix , Table S3 ). Similarly, under-ice DO vw trended more negative in humic (brown) than in clear lakes &lt;10 ha and more positive in humic than in clear lakes ≥10 4 ha, though trends in polyhumic lakes had large CIs and were mostly nonsignificant ( SI Appendix , Table S4 ). Because water bodies smaller than 10 ha are estimated to make up over 76% of the ∼1.4 million freshwater lakes globally ( 11 ), our results imply that most ice-covered lakes have experienced a rapid decline in under-ice DO over the last 60 y. Weighting class-median DO vw ( Fig. 2 , dots) by a representative lake size distribution, we were able to confirm that under-ice oxygen indeed trended negatively in the vast majority of lakes in North America and Fennoscandia ( Fig. 2 A , black line). We next examined decadal changes in the extent of hypoxia in winter ( Fig. 3 and SI Appendix , Fig. S2 ) by bin-averaging DO observations into relative depth categories defined for each lake (z sample /z max = 0 to 0.2, 0.2 to 0.4, etc.). Under-ice DO decreased toward the bottom, following the typical profile in inversely stratified lakes ( 1 ). A majority of the small lakes in our dataset (A surf &lt; 10 ha) showed a vertical expansion of the hypoxic zone to shallower depths, as well as bottom-water anoxia ( Fig. 3 ). A similar expansion of the hypoxic zone developed in shallow lakes (z max &lt; 8 m) ( SI Appendix , Fig. S2 ). Anoxia occurs naturally in ice-covered lakes, but has recently become widespread. The percentage of small lakes in our dataset affected by anoxia averaged 13% prior to 1970 and exceeded 50% for the first time in 2003. Fig. 3. Dashed and dotted lines mark hypoxia (DO &lt; 2 mg L −1 ) and anoxia (DO &lt; 0.5 mg L −1 ), respectively. Under-Ice Oxygen Trends Scale with Shortening Ice-Cover Duration. The lake size dependence of under-ice oxygen trends ( Fig. 2 ) was linked to morphometric scaling of ice phenology trends ( SI Appendix , Table S2 ). We found significant shortening of ice-cover duration in lakes across Fennoscandia and North America (HLMs, SI Appendix , Table S2 ), with similar contributions from later ice-on and earlier ice-off. Ice loss accelerated with increasing lake size, from −2.35 ± 1.32 d decade −1 in lakes &lt;10 ha (n obs = 412), to −5.19 ± 0.45 d decade −1 in lakes ≥10 4 ha (n obs = 4,000). Ice duration trends correlated significantly with under-ice DO vw trends across size classes (GLM, r 2 = 0.81, P = 0.038, df = 3). These results may explain more positive DO vw trends in larger lakes, but not the deoxygenation of small lakes. No Significant Trend in Fall Ventilation or Winter Oxygen Depletion Rates. We next assessed whether long-term changes in the rates of fall ventilation or winter oxygen depletion, for example, due to long-term shifts in wind speed ( 34 ) or organic matter loading ( 31 ), could contribute to the observed under-ice DO vw trends. We computed daily rates of change in DO vw (in mg L −1 d −1 ) in fall (∂DO vw /∂t fall ; 1 September – ice-on) and winter I (∂DO vw /∂t winter ; ice-on – 15 March) for each lake-year. Mean ∂DO vw /∂t fall was significantly positive (one-sided Wilcoxon test, P ≪ 0.001, n lakes = 270) while mean ∂DO vw /∂t winter was significantly negative ( P ≪ 0.001, n lakes = 260), indicating seasons were demarcated correctly. Next, we estimated trends of the ∂DO vw /∂t rates via HLMs with random intercepts for each lake ( SI Appendix , Table S5 ). We found a small, nonsignificant ∂DO vw /∂t fall decline of −0.28 ± 0.69 µg L −1 d −1 decade −1 (n obs = 1713, trend ± 95% CI), or 2% of the mean rate. Similarly, the trend of ∂DO vw /∂t winter was negligible and not significant (−0.03 ± 1.45 µg L −1 d −1 decade −1 , n obs = 811). Ecological Memory Drives Winter Deoxygenation of Small Lakes. We fitted a hierarchical generalized additive model (HGAM) of DO vw as a function of time and lake morphometry, with clustered location parameters as random effects to examine how DO vw trends in different seasons are linked ( Fig. 4 ). As we might expect from the effects of shortening ice cover, long-term DO vw changes were more positive in the ice-covered- compared to the ice-free season in each size class. Importantly, the HGAM evidenced the existence of ecological memory from ice-free to ice-covered seasons. In small lakes (A surf &lt; 10 ha), DO vw declined from 1970 to 2010 in summer, through fall and ice-on, and into the ice-cover period. DO vw did not reach saturation concentrations at ice-on ( Fig. 4 , dots), challenging the idea of a ubiquitous oxygen “reset” in fall. We confirmed this result with two separate analyses of DO saturation (DO sat ) during periods of full water column mixing prior to ice-on (surface-bottom-water density difference &lt;0.05 g L −1 ). First, mean DO sat, vw during fall overturns ranged from 77.4% in lakes &lt;10 ha to 90.1% in lakes ≥10 4 ha ( SI Appendix , Table S6 ). Time between these observations and ice-on was insufficient to reach 100% DO saturation. For example, in lakes &lt;10 ha, ventilation at 0.02 mg L −1 d −1 (mean ∂DO vw /∂t fall ) for 35.2 d (mean period between sampling and ice-on) would offset the 22.6% DO sat deficit (3.3 mg L −1 at 0 °C) by only 0.7 mg L −1 . Second, continuous high-frequency DO sat observations from lakes Erssjön (A surf = 6 ha), Feresjön (52 ha), and Erken (2,400 ha) from the Swedish Infrastructure for Ecosystem Science also show oxygen undersaturation during fall overturns, and confirm that DO sat declines with decreasing lake size ( SI Appendix , Fig. S5 ). In Erssjön, where measurements continued year-round, DO undersaturation in fall (∼80%) was maintained into the ice-cover season. Minor interannual variations in DO that constitute long-term trends (&lt;0.1 mg L −1 y −1 ) were thus not erased during overturns. Fig. 4. Balance of Drivers Explains Lake Size Dependency of Under-Ice DO Trends. We used the HGAM ( Fig. 4 ) to understand how different drivers (Eq. 1 ) contributed to the divergence of winter DO vw trends with lake size. The model revealed that the seasonal DO vw cycle has shifted significantly between 1970 and 2010 (nonoverlapping 95% confidence bands). The timing of the fall oxygen maximum has advanced with later ice-on, and the duration of fall ventilation has generally increased. This is most evident in lakes ≥10 3 ha with minimal summer deoxygenation as an increase in DO vw at ice-on. Conversely, the spring oxygen maximum and ice-off occur earlier in the year. Consequently, the duration of under-ice oxygen consumption has shortened, particularly in large lakes experiencing rapid ice loss. This effect is highly visible during the transition from winter I to winter II phases, when earlier onset of under-ice oxygenation ( Fig. 4 , dashed lines) has halted oxygen depletion at an earlier (higher) point. In summary, the HGAM shows how divergent under-ice DO vw trends emerged from a balance of drivers (Eq. 1 ). In small lakes (A surf &lt;10 ha), significant summer deoxygenation persisted at ice-on and was only partly offset by longer fall ventilation and shorter winter oxygen depletion, resulting in negative DO vw trends under ice. In larger lakes (10 3 to 10 4 ha) with negligible summer DO vw trends, prolonged fall ventilation increased DO vw at ice-on which in turn contributed to positive DO vw trends in winter. In mid-sized lakes (100 ha) the three drivers canceled out, resulting in a negligible winter DO vw trend ( Fig. 2 ). Lake Size and Trophic State Modulate Sensitivity of Under-Ice Oxygen to Warming. Our deductive approach ( Fig. 1 ) implies that the impact of ice-cover shortening on under-ice DO vw is greatest in lakes with high rates of fall ventilation or winter oxygen depletion. To identify these climate-sensitive lakes, we conducted random forest analyses of ∂DO vw /∂t fall and ∂DO vw /∂t winter ( SI Appendix , Fig. S3 ). Partial dependence plots ( Fig. 5 ) show that fall ventilation rates increased with volume-weighted cooling rates—the most important predictor variable—and toward large and deep lakes. Under-ice DO depletion rates increased with surface mean TP—the most important predictor variable—toward shallow lakes and in polyhumic lakes. Water temperature, a key regulator of aquatic respiration rates and oxygen solubility, was not identified as an important predictor. The random forest model indicates that under-ice DO vw in eutrophic, polyhumic, and small lakes is sensitive to ice duration trends. Amplification of Winter Oxygen Trends in Eutrophic Lakes. We quantified the component drivers of under-ice DO vw trends (Eq. 1 ) to find out why the divergence of DO vw trends with lake size was amplified in eutrophic lakes ( Fig. 2 C ). Summer DO vw trends (June–August) were estimated with HLMs. The contribution of longer fall ventilation was estimated by multiplying fall ventilation rates with ice-on trends, and that of shorter winter oxygen depletion periods by multiplying winter oxygen depletion rates with ice duration trends. The sums of the driver trends generally matched winter DO vw trends (overlapping 95% CIs) and showed the same patterns ( Fig. 6 ). In small lakes (&lt;10 ha), trophic state intensified long-term DO vw decline in summer. Summer deoxygenation was exceptionally severe in meso- and eutrophic small lakes ( Fig. 6 , red bars; &lt;−0.2 mg L −1 decade −1 ), where the effect of high ∂DO/∂t winter was offset by modest ice loss ( Fig. 6 , teal bars; 0.1 mg L −1 decade −1 ). In larger lakes (≥10 3 ha), summer DO vw trends were minimal, while the effect of the shortening DO depletion period, bolstered by substantial ice loss, increased with trophic state. Positive trend components were greatest in eutrophic large lakes ( Fig. 6 ; 0.15 mg L −1 decade −1 ). Summer and winter DO vw trends were subdued in oligotrophic (nutrient-poor) lakes of all sizes. Prolongation of fall ventilation ( Fig. 6 , orange bars) was more important in large lakes on account of their high ∂DO vw /∂t fall rates ( Fig. 5 C ) but made a relatively minor contribution to under-ice DO vw trends. Discussion Over the past decades, under-ice oxygen trends diverged with lake size ( Fig. 2 and SI Appendix , Table S2 ). We explain this pattern via lake size-dependent scaling of both the climatic drivers of DO vw trends and lakes’ physical and biological responses to climate forcing. In small lakes, the long-term decline of bottom-water DO in summer was the strongest correlate of winter DO vw trends ( Fig. 6 and SI Appendix , Fig. S4 ). Based on previous work, we attribute the exacerbated deoxygenation of small lakes to severe prolongation of summer stratification, as their small fetch limits wind mixing, and to high oxygen demand enhancing the impact of longer stratification ( 17 , 35 ). Minimal surface DO trends in summer ( SI Appendix , Fig. S4 ) suggest that direct effects of lake surface warming, such as a reduction in DO solubility, were less important. At the same time, small lakes also showed significant positive trend components ( Fig. 6 ) as high under-ice DO depletion rates ( Fig. 5 B ) compensated for modest declines in ice-cover duration. High oxygen depletion rates in small lakes ( Fig. 5 B ) have been attributed to their greater sediment surface area, where most oxygen is consumed, relative to the total water volume ( 2 , 36 , 37 ), and a larger littoral area fraction supplying fresh organic matter toward respiration ( 22 , 38 , 39 ). A greater sediment surface area also increases the capacity for heat accumulation and release, generating downslope flows of denser water that accelerate sediment-water oxygen exchange ( 40 ). In larger lakes, positive under-ice DO vw trends ( Fig. 6 ) were linked to severe shortening of ice-cover duration ( Fig. 4 ), a consequence of their formidable heat storage capacity and slow release of excess summer heat ( 41 , 42 ). Summer deoxygenation was minimal in large and deep lakes, likely because their greater volume limited bottom-water DO depletion ( 2 , 36 , 37 ), and greater surface area enhanced wind exposure, mixing, and ventilation ( 43 ). Lake trophic state and humic content modulated under-ice oxygen trends ( Fig. 2 C and D ). The contribution of shortening winter DO depletion periods to under-ice DO vw trends ( Fig. 6 , teal bars) was greatest in eutrophic lakes as under-ice oxygen depletion rates were positively correlated with TP ( Fig. 5 E ). From early winter limnology studies, we know that nutrients can enhance primary production in the growing season, which in turn fuels oxygen consumption via microbial decomposition in winter ( 38 , 44 , 45 ). Recent work revealed that nitrification and methanotrophy, which can account for a majority of DO loss under ice, also increase with trophic state ( 4 , 46 , 47 ). Similarly, stimulation of aerobic respiration via terrestrially derived carbon ( 48 , 49 ) could explain high oxygen depletion rates in polyhumic lakes ( Fig. D ), though overall, color was a weak predictor of ∂DO vw /∂t winter ( SI Appendix , Fig. S3 ). Thus, trophic state appears to be a more reliable correlate of under-ice metabolism than color-based indices of dissolved organic matter. The negative component of under-ice DO vw trends (summer deoxygenation) also increased in magnitude with trophic state ( Fig. 6 ) likely through stimulation of bottom-water oxygen depletion ( 16 , 17 ). Importantly, the anoxic conditions which now develop in small meso- and eutrophic lakes ( Fig. 3 and SI Appendix , Fig. S4 ) can drive a positive feedback of internal loading of nutrients, enhanced biomass production, and further DO depletion ( 50 ), which may accelerate oxygen loss as northern regions continue to warm. Significant long-term deoxygenation of ice-covered lakes smaller than 10 ha ( Fig. 2 ) has not been identified in previous studies informed by process-based models ( 3 , 22 , 23 , 39 , 51 ). The HGAM showed that deoxygenation persisted from summer, over ice-on and into winter ( Fig. 4 ), and we did not detect a long-term increase in the under-ice oxygen depletion rate. Therefore, the most likely explanation for the negative under-ice oxygen trends is cross-seasonal persistence (memory) of increasingly oxygen-depleted conditions in summer. Ecological memory has been attributed to incomplete mixing of the water column during particularly short mixing periods in spring ( 30 ). However, oxygen undersaturation has also been observed during complete fall overturns prior to ice-on, both in our study ( SI Appendix , Fig. S5 and Table S6 ) and in the literature ( 28 , 29 , 31 , 40 , 52 ). This suggests a predominance of redistribution over resupply of DO in fall, which is consistent with convective mixing driving bottom-water entrainment and ventilation ( 53 , 54 ) and the clear dependence of fall ventilation rates on water column cooling rates ( Fig. 5 A ). Lower DO saturation at ice-on in smaller lakes ( Fig. 4 ) is consistent with reduced fall ventilation rates ( Fig. 5 B ) as their limited fetch subdues wind-driven air–water gas exchange ( 43 ), and their overall earlier freeze-up dates ( Fig. 4 ), both of which would have constrained the uptake of atmospheric oxygen in fall. Undersaturation ensured that interannual shifts in DO vw were not erased by complete oxygen “resets” during overturns. The HGAM ( Fig. 4 ) shows that the transition from winter I to winter II phases—from oxygen minimum to onset of reoxygenation—occurred progressively earlier. The onset of reoxygenation in ice-covered lakes can be driven by the intrusion of oxygen-rich meltwater and by photosynthesis ( 27 , 55 ). Stream gauge readings from hydrometric monitoring networks across North America and Fennoscandia indicate that the timing of peak discharge in winter/spring has receded, with regional mean trends ranging from 1 to 5 d decade −1 ( 24 , 25 , 56 ). This range overlaps with our estimated recession in the timing of oxygen minima of 0.9 to 3.6 d decade −1 , scaling inversely with lake size ( Fig. 4 , dashed lines). We hypothesize that discharge regime shifts most affected lakes with short water residence times, but this remains a topic for future study. Observation-based estimates of primary production trends in ice-covered lakes remain, to the authors’ knowledge, elusive, due to the complexity and cost of sampling in winter ( 57 ). We did not observe a significant trend in the winter oxygen depletion rate ( SI Appendix , Table S5 ). Previous studies similarly report no trends in summer oxygen depletion rates in bottom waters of stratified lakes ( 17 , 58 ). At the same time, it is established knowledge that anoxia can increase internal loading of nutrients, promoting primary production and availability of labile organic matter for aerobic respiration the following season or year ( 50 , 59 ). Indeed, DO concentrations were lower in eutrophic than in oligotrophic lakes ( Fig. 2 C ). So why has summer deoxygenation not caused widespread increases in oxygen demand? In northern lakes, TP has declined significantly in bottom and surface waters despite the proliferation of anoxia, likely as a result of reduced external loading ( 17 , 60 , 61 ). The modest ice-free volume-weighted mean TP decline in our study lakes in 1960 to 2022 (−0.74 ± 0.08 µg L −1 decade −1 , n obs = 49,514) appears insufficient to have had a significant impact on oxygen depletion rates ( Fig. 5 E ). Conversely, prolonged summer anoxia has likely exacerbated the dissolution of iron-bound organic matter from lake sediments, which could fuel respiration if retained in winter ( 17 , 31 ). While lake water under ice has indeed darkened (4.1 ± 0.2 mg Pt L −1 decade −1 , n obs = 46,590), indicating a slight increase in colored dissolved organic matter, winter oxygen depletion rates appeared largely insensitive to water color ( Fig. 5 D ). Finally, it is possible that trends in the drivers of winter oxygen depletion rates counteracted one another. Our findings have implications for the identification of lakes at risk of future winter hypoxia. Shortening of winter ice cover has been purported to improve oxygen availability in ice-covered lakes ( 3 , 22 , 23 ) ( Fig. 1 B ), but we found positive and negative DO vw trends emerging from a balance of drivers ( Fig. 6 ). While significant increases in under-ice DO vw in large and deep lakes can be attributed to ice loss, trophic state, and humic content modulated DO vw trends. Like an early model study ( 3 ) we found that well-known indicators of seasonal hypoxia, such as elevated TP ( 2 , 33 ) ( Fig. 5 E ), are associated with more positive under-ice DO vw trends in lakes ≥10 3 ha. Most importantly, our results emphasize that long-term winter deoxygenation is directly linked to summer deoxygenation ( Fig. 4 ). Models used to predict the future trajectory of DO in ice-covered lakes should account for cross-seasonal ecological memory, and recognize the importance of watershed characteristics that influence nutrient loading and ice-cover dynamics. Nutrient-rich lakes smaller than 10 ha remain at greatest risk of winter hypoxia due to high oxygen depletion rates ( 33 , 36 ) ( Fig. 5 ) as well as long ice-cover periods ( Fig. 4 ). Small lakes are the most common and also generally the most vulnerable to anthropogenic pressures ( 62 ), and require better representation in lake monitoring and management programs ( 63 ). The ecological consequences of the divergent trends in under-ice oxygen in small and large lakes remain largely unexplored. Expectations were that shorter ice-cover seasons would expand the oxythermal habitat and alleviate winterkills of fish ( 64 ). While our results and previous modeling ( 3 ) imply that this is true for large and mid-sized lakes, we found that hypoxia has increased in small lakes already prone to mass mortality events ( 6 ). In addition, the proliferation of anoxia could increase the buildup and release of methane from small lakes ( 7 ), which are already an important source of greenhouse gases to the atmosphere ( 65 ). Anoxia would also be expected to increase internal P loading, but under-ice TP vw decreased (A surf &lt;10 ha, −0.62 ± 0.37 µg L −1 decade −1 , n obs = 8,468) implying the widespread decline of external P loading continues to dominate TP trends ( 60 , 61 ). Lake ice loss is projected to continue at its current pace of 3 to 5 d decade −1 during the 21st century under the SSP3-7.0 climate warming scenario ( 66 ). Together with further lake surface water warming and longer summer stratification projected for the coming decades ( 67 ), ice-cover decline is likely to increase seasonal and morphometric contrasts of oxygen availability across northern lakes. Materials and Methods In this section we describe the lake water chemistry and morphometry dataset, quality control steps, and statistical analyses. All data processing and analysis was done in R 4.2.2 ( 68 ). The overarching objectives of our study were to 1) quantify the sign and magnitude of long-term trends in under-ice dissolved oxygen across lake classes (e.g., surface area, trophic state) and 2) identify the most important drivers of these trends. Data Compilation. In this study, we focused on seasonally ice-covered lakes in the northern regions of North America (&gt;43 °N) and Northern Europe (&gt;55 °N), where the vast majority of the world’s lakes are located ( 11 ) and the climate is warming significantly faster than the global mean rate ( 12 ). We compiled lake water quality measurements from a wide variety of governmental and not-for-profit organizations in the United States (Alaska, Wisconsin), Canada (British Columbia, Québec, Ontario, Saskatchewan, Alberta, Manitoba, Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Prince Edward Island, and the Northwest Territories), Finland, Sweden, and Norway ( SI Appendix , Table S8 ). Data were accessed directly through online data portals (19,084 lakes) or obtained via data requests (561 lakes). Lake morphometry information was similarly collected from public repositories, scientific literature, and data requests for 34,955 lakes ( SI Appendix , Table S9 ) and matched with the water quality data through common identifiers or manual colocation. Lakes without maximum depth or surface area information were excluded from the study. The synthesized water quality dataset is composed of 6.67 million measurements from 19,645 lakes, of which 17,428 lakes had under-ice oxygen observations ( 32 ). In addition, we collected observations of ice-on (n = 27,153) and ice-off dates (n = 29,775) from 1,107 lakes across Fennoscandia and North America from open-source data repositories ( SI Appendix , Table S10 ) ( 32 ). A map of the lakes in the dataset is included with SI Appendix , Fig. S1 . Site Selection. We included freshwater lakes and ponds in the dataset, but excluded reservoirs as well as lakes undergoing active management against deoxygenation and eutrophication, such as artificial aeration, dredging, hypolimnetic withdrawal, and the addition of aluminum sulfate. Since measures to improve water quality are often costly and disruptive, they are generally well documented. With help from the data providers, through news media, reports and published research, and via regional authorities and local conservation organizations, we identified 197 managed lakes and ponds in our dataset. Basins of large lakes that were sampled separately were treated as distinct water bodies. Each lake was assigned a new identifier to replace originals which could overlap between datasets, and to merge observations from water bodies sampled across borders or by multiple organizations. Variable Selection and Computation. Physical and chemical variables were selected based on previously reported (proxy) relations with oxygen concentrations ( 18 , 33 ) and availability in the monitoring datasets. Here, we included the following variables: water temperature, true water color, total phosphorus (TP), chlorophyll a , and ice thickness. TP was computed as the sum of particulate and dissolved fractions (i.e. unfiltered or coarse-filtered samples). For a subset of Swedish and US lakes, color on the platinum-cobalt scale (mg Pt L −1 ) was computed from absorbance at 420 nm following ISO 7887:2011. The dataset also features sampling coordinates, sampling date and sampling depth (z sample ), as well as information about each lake including altitude, surface area (A surf ), mean depth (z mean ) and maximum depth (z max ). In order to estimate oxygen concentrations representative of the whole lake, we computed the volume-weighted DO concentration for each profile with at least one surface (&lt;0.5 × z max ) and one bottom (≥0.5 × z max ) measurement. The median number of observations per profile was 4. We applied the “approx.bathy” function rLakeAnalyzer R package ( 69 ) to compute hypsographic curves based on lake surface area, mean depth, and maximum depth with the volume-development parameter, or, if mean depth was unavailable, assuming a cone shape (Hakanson, 1981, Johansson et al., 2007). Lake surface area classes were assigned according to a base-10 logarithmic scale, and lake maximum depth classes were divided by 20th percentiles of z max . Trophic state classes were assigned based on surface water chlorophyll a concentrations or, if unavailable, TP concentrations as oligotrophic (Chl a ≤ 2.6 µg L −1 , TP ≤ 12 µg L −1 ), mesotrophic (Chl a = 2.6 to 7.3 µg L −1 , TP = 12 to 24 µg L −1 ), and eutrophic (Chl a &gt; 7.3 µg L −1 , TP &gt; 24 µg L −1 ) ( 70 ). Humic state was divided into three categories based on surface water color: clear (&lt;30 mg Pt L −1 ), dark or humic (30 to 90 mg Pt L −1 ), and very dark or polyhumic (&gt;90 mg Pt L −1 ) ( 71 ). We computed DO saturation concentrations with a temperature function ( 72 ). Oxygen saturation (%) was computed as oxygen concentration divided by the saturation concentration. Filtering and Quality Control. Quality control consisted of a multistep process. First, we removed observations without a sampling date or (plausible) sampling depth (0 – lake maximum depth). Because bathymetric surveys based on transects can be incomplete, we adjusted z max to the maximum sampling depth in water bodies where the sampling depth consistently (in &gt; 5 profiles) exceeded the reported z max . We standardized units of measurement across the dataset, and only kept measurements with clearly defined fractions (“total” or “dissolved”) and unambiguous units (either molar quantities or concentrations “as P”). We subsequently removed measurements outside a predefined plausibility range, generally excluding the top 0.1% quantile: dissolved oxygen (0 to 20 mg L −1 ), water temperature (0 to 40 °C), TP (0 to 3 mg L −1 ), chlorophyll a (0 to 1,000 µg L −1 ), and water color (0 to 800 mg Pt L −1 ). The range filter was applied to the raw data prior to the computation of derived quantities (e.g. trophic state index, humic content). Lake Ice Phenology. We compiled lake ice phenology data from 1107 lakes across Canada, the United States, Sweden, Finland, and Norway ( 73 , 74 ) ( SI Appendix , Table S10 ). Ice-on dates (n = 26,855) and ice-off dates (n = 29,361) were available for all lake size and depth classes ( SI Appendix , Fig. S6 and Table S1 ). In lake-years with multiple entries, either as incremental observations (e.g. percentages ice cover) or because a lake was monitored at multiple locations (e.g. large lakes), we selected the earliest ice-on date and latest ice-off date. Only observations within the water quality monitoring timeframe (1960 to 2022) were included. Time scaling prior to fitting the HGAM ( Fig. 4 ) required ice phenology observations paired with each DO vw estimate, but these were not available for the vast majority of lakes. We therefore estimated ice phenology for each lake and year from the “lake ice depth” product in the ERA5 Land reanalysis dataset ( 75 ). The product is based on the 0.1° × 0.1° ECMWF weather model coupled with the 1D hydrodynamic Freshwater Lake model (FLake) ( 76 ). We identified ice-on and ice-off dates for each lake and year as the first day prior to 15 August and last day after 15 August, respectively, where the modeled ice thickness was 0. The ERA5-FLake ice phenology estimates were calibrated with observations. Offsets (observations−ERA5) were fitted to polynomial functions of log 10 (A surf ) and log 10 (z max ) and applied to ERA5 ice phenology for each lake ( SI Appendix , Fig. S6 ). The interquartile range of the offsets spanned −3.1 to 15.6 d for ice-on and −5.5 to 7.0 d for ice-off. Time Series Analyses Across Lake Classes and Depth Zones. We examined the interannual variation of under-ice dissolved oxygen across classes of lake surface area, maximum depth, trophic state, and humic content. First, we averaged volume-weighted mean DO concentrations for each lake-year in January–March (if ice-covered). This period was chosen because most (73.4%) under-ice DO vw observations fell within this timeframe. We then computed median values for each class and year (dots in Fig. 2 ) and removed medians with fewer than 10 lake-year observations. In addition, we estimated how long-term DO trends varied across depth zones in order to assess potential change in the habitat of aquatic organisms. We first normalized sampling depth by the maximum depth (z sample /z max ) for each lake, and then computed lake-year mean DO concentrations for each 20th percentile of the normalized depth. Finally, we computed median values for each depth zone and class-year and removed medians with fewer than 10 observations. The results of this analysis are shown in Fig. 3 and SI Appendix , Fig. S2 . For visualization, we fitted GAMs to the class-median values and applied inverse variance weights to account for higher uncertainty in years with fewer samples. Determination of Long-Term Trends. Long-term trends in volume-weighted mean dissolved oxygen concentrations, as well as in ice-on and ice-off day-of-year were estimated using HLMs ( 77 ). The HLM approach enabled us to estimate long-term mean trends using information from all measurements, not just the subset of lakes with long-term monitoring data, while accounting for unbalanced properties of the dataset, particularly changes in the lake population over time. For ice phenology trends, ice-on and ice-off day-of-year were shifted into unimodal distributions (i.e. without a break at DoY 365). For DO vw trends, separate models were fitted for surface area, maximum depth, trophic state, and humic content classes. Prior to trend analyses, we removed extreme outliers (&gt;3 s.d. from the mean) which comprised &lt;2% of values. HLMs were fitted using the “lme4” package in R ( 78 ), with year as fixed effect and random intercepts for each lake. P -values for the slopes were determined with “lmerTest” using Satterthwaite’s method to estimate degrees of freedom ( 79 ). We compared trends within each class category using ANOVA (lmerTest), followed by a post hoc Tukey’s HSD test using the “emmeans” R package ( 80 ). Results of the trend analyses are shown in SI Appendix , Tables S1–S4 and S6 . As an additional check, we also computed under-ice DO vw trends using more traditional Theil-Sen estimators for each lake with ≥15 y of observations ( 16 , 17 ) ( SI Appendix , Table S7 ). The mean trends agree well with those computed with HLMs, but the CI are larger, particularly for small and shallow lake classes comprising few lakes. Determination of Seasonal Rates of Change. We estimated seasonal rates of change in volume-weighted mean dissolved oxygen concentrations (∂DO vw /∂t) and water temperature (∂T vw /∂t), during fall ventilation (1 September – ice-on) and in winter (ice-on – 15 March). For each lake-year and season, we computed rates for each time period with more than 3 DO vw observations per season using the Theil-Sen estimator. The median number of observations per rate estimate was three in fall and four in winter. We used Sen’s slope rather than HLMs (for class-mean slopes) because the random forest analyses required rate estimates in individual lakes. Random Forest Analysis of ∂DO vw /∂t Rates in Fall and Winter. The sensitivity of under-ice DO to shortening ice-cover duration increases with the (absolute) rates of ∂DO vw /∂t fall and ∂DO vw /∂t winter (Eq. 1 ). In order to identify climate-sensitive lakes in which these seasonal rates are highest, we performed a random forest (RF) analysis ( 81 ). Random forests are based on an ensemble of regression trees, where each tree is trained on a bootstrap sample of data and a random subset of the independent variables splits each node. Model performance is evaluated as the error of its predictions for a subset of data not used to train the model (33% of the dataset). The importance of each variable corresponds to the decrease in model performance upon shuffling of its values (permutation). Predictor variables included known drivers of both ventilation and oxygen depletion in lakes ( 2 , 33 ): the volume-weighted water temperature rate of change (∂T vw /∂t), the DO vw concentration at the start of the rate estimate (DO vw, init ), surface mean total phosphorus, surface mean water color, water temperature, lake surface area, and lake maximum depth. DO vw, init was included because the fall ventilation rate can be expected to scale with the air–water concentration difference which diminishes with increasing DO, and because winter oxygen depletion via sediment respiration depends in part on the ambient oxygen concentration ( 18 ). We included water temperature as a predictor because of the temperature dependence of aquatic respiration in winter ( 82 ) and because temperature-dependent solubility of oxygen ( 72 ) can alter the concentration gradient between the equilibrated diffusive sublayer and the bulk liquid, potentially affecting the oxygen influx from the atmosphere in fall. For TP and color we used lake-mean surface water measurements to avoid confounding effects of anoxia on internal loading of P and CDOM ( 83 ). Because RF cannot easily distinguish the importance of highly correlated variables (collinearity), we computed Kendall’s τ for each independent variable pair but found none that exceeded the recommended threshold of |τ| &gt; 0.7 ( 84 ). RF models were fitted using the “Ranger” package ( 85 ). The number of trees was set at 1,500 and the optimal number of splitting variables was determined using tuneMtryFast. The RF models explained 67 and 43% of the variance in ∂DO/∂t in fall and winter, respectively ( SI Appendix , Fig. S3 ). We created partial dependence plots with the R package “pdp” ( 86 ) to visualize the marginal effect of each predictor variable ( Fig. 5 ). Quantitative Comparison of the Drivers of Under-Ice Oxygen Trends. Following the deductive approach of Livingstone and Imboden (1996), the winter DO vw trends may be driven by changes in the oxygen concentration during summer, changes in the maximum DO concentration attained during ventilation in fall, and a shift in the duration of winter oxygen depletion (right-hand terms in Eq. 1 ). Long-term trends in the fall ventilation and winter oxygen depletion rates were negligible in this study ( Results ). Summer DO vw trends (June–August) were estimated with HLMs. The effect of later ice-on prolonging fall ventilation was estimated by multiplying the mean fall ventilation rates (∂DO vw /∂t) with ice-on trends ( SI Appendix , Table S1 ). The effect of shorter ice-cover duration on the oxygen depletion period was estimated by multiplying the mean winter oxygen depletion rates (∂DO vw /∂t) with ice duration trends. Ice phenology trends were assumed to be a function of lake morphology only. Mean ∂DO/∂t rates for each class were estimated with random forest models (previous section) to quantify partial dependencies, using class-median predictor values. In Fig. 6 we compared the sum of the right-hand terms of Eq. 1 ;with the winter (January–March) DO vw trends, estimated with HLMs. CI for the second and third terms of Eq. 1 ;were propagated from the SE of the ice phenology trends and mean ∂DO vw /∂t rates, assuming independence of the two variables. Sampling Bias Correction. In water quality datasets, small lakes and ponds are typically under-represented relative to their natural abundance. Lakes with surface areas &lt; 10 ha comprise over three quarters of global lakes ( 11 ), but only 39% of our dataset. We therefore computed size-class abundance weighted medians of annual lake-mean oxygen concentrations (black line in Fig. 2 ). Abundance fractions were derived from the Swedish national lake registry, which lists the size class of over 100,000 water bodies (Svensk Vattenarkiv (SVAR) version 2016_3; SI Appendix , Table S9 ). The census is based on a compilation of maps and is considered to be a complete record of Swedish lakes ≥ 1 ha ( 87 ). Resolving Interannual Changes in the Seasonal Oxygen Cycle (HGAM). We fitted a HGAM to evaluate how the seasonal cycle of volume-weighted DO varied between years and across lake morphometric gradients. Generalized additive models are a class of linear models, in which the response variable depends linearly on unknown smooth functions of the predictor variable(s) ( 88 ). Hierarchical models contain fixed and random effects to explicitly model variance–covariance structures of nonindependent, longitudinal data, such as repeat DO measurements in separate lakes ( 77 ). The hierarchical modeling approach enabled us to incorporate information from the full dataset, rather than a subset of lakes with long-term, high-resolution time series. The HGAM was fitted with the “mgcv” package ( 88 ) in R 4.2 compiled with OpenBLAS 0.3.15, on a computing cluster hosted by the National Academic Infrastructure for Supercomputing in Sweden. As fixed effects we included smooth terms for day-of-year, year, log(surface area) and log(maximum depth), and their two- and three-way interactions. We modeled the effects as smooth functions that were represented in the model via cyclic (day-of-year) and cubic (all other variables) spline basis expansions of the covariates. In order to compute mean seasonal cycles across lakes with differing ice phenology, we scaled day-of-year to long-term (1960 to 2022) lake-mean ice-on and ice-off dates. We modeled the conditional errors as a Gaussian distribution. Including random slopes and intercepts for each lake (n = 15,035) resulted in a model that was too large to fit, so we created lake clusters within which we could reasonably expect highly synchronous oxygen cycles. To this end, the lakes were divided into groups by standardized (zero mean and unit variance) latitude, longitude, annual mean air temperature, surface area, and maximum depth via k-means clustering. The number of groups (n = 1,000) was limited by the memory capacity of the computing cluster. Seasonal cycles were predicted for years 1970 and 2010 for five lake surface areas (1 to 10 4 ha) with corresponding maximum depths ( SI Appendix , Fig. S7 ), using the “predict” function in mgcv. CI of predictions were computed from SE based on the Bayesian posterior covariance matrix of the parameters. We estimated the long-term change in the start of the ice-off period by tracking the timing of the end-of-winter oxygen minimum. Mean trough locations and 95% credible intervals were computed from predictions based on repeated draws from the posterior distribution of model parameters (n = 1,000) using the “fitted_samples” function in the “gratia” R package ( 89 ). For visualization of the predictions, we scaled the relative time ( x -axis) back to absolute DoY values using the mean ice-on and ice-off dates for each surface area class ( Fig. 4 ), such that the long-term mean timing of annual DO vw maxima coincided with that of ice-on. Because x -axis rescaling was the same for both predict years (1970, 2010) in each size class, it did not alter the modeled shifts in the seasonal cycle of DO vw .
