Significance Current radon maps of the United States lack the detailed up-to-date information. We compiled a national database of over 6 million radon tests during 2001 to 2021. A machine learning model was used to estimate monthly community-level concentrations based on geological, meteorological, architectural, and socioeconomical factors. Our findings indicate slightly decreasing concentrations in high-radon areas. Nonetheless, nearly 25% of the population faces exposure above the action level of 148 Bq/m 3 (4 pCi/L). Most of these residences are in low-radon regions, underscoring the need for comprehensive surveys. Our high-resolution radon maps aim to refine regulations and the understanding of radon's risks. Keywords: radon, exposure assessment, indoor environment, geospatial model Abstract Radon, a common radioactive indoor air pollutant, is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States. Knowledge about its distribution is essential for risk assessment and designing efficient protective regulations. However, the three current radon maps for the United States are unable to provide the up-to-date, high-resolution, and time-varying radon concentrations. Tens of millions of radon measurements have been conducted as parts of property inspections in the past two decades, making it possible for us to improve the national radon map. We compiled a national database of over 6 million radon measurements conducted by independent laboratories during 2001 to 2021. A random forest model was built to predict monthly community-level radon concentrations based on nearly 200 geological, meteorological, architectural, and socioeconomical factors. Our radon map can accurately show the distribution of radon at higher spatial and temporal resolutions. Radon, a common indoor air pollutant, is the radioactive decay product of Uranium-238 in the soil. Before further decaying, radon gas can migrate into residential and industrial environments through cracks in foundations and accumulate to harmful concentrations in poorly ventilated spaces. As the second leading cause of lung cancer, radon is responsible for over 220,000 lung cancer death globally every year ( 1 , 2 ) including approximately 21,000 in the United States. The US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) recommends installing a radon mitigation system when the concentration in living space reaches or exceeds 148 Bq/m 3 (4 pCi/L) ( 3 ). Recent health studies have associated residential radon exposure with multiple other negative outcomes, such as breast cancer ( 4 ), stroke ( 5 ), stomach cancer ( 6 , 7 ), and mortality ( 8 , 9 ), underscoring the urgency of improving our understanding of its effects. Granular information regarding radon’s spatiotemporal distribution is crucial for policymakers and health scientists. High-resolution indoor radon maps can help regulators determine local priorities, refine building codes, and allocate mitigation resources. Health scientists can use radon maps to assess radon exposure, and then investigate the health effects of radon. However, current national indoor radon models in the United States are lacking spatially and temporally resolved details. Three US-based national radon models have been developed respectively by Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (referred to as the LBNL model, published in 1993) ( 10 , 11 ) and the US Geological Survey (referred to as the USGS model, published in 1993) ( 12 ) and US EPA (referred to as EPA model, published in 1993) ( 13 ). All these maps estimate radon levels for large areas like counties or geological provinces, missing finer, within-unit variations. These three models rely on radon surveys conducted in the 1980s ( 14 , 15 ), prior to the 1987 mitigation recommendation and the 1993 publication of measurement protocols ( 16 ), underscoring the urgency for up-to-date radon distribution data. Finally, no temporal variations are shown in any of these maps. Significant improvements in radon maps are needed to account for the recent trends due to the expanding installation of mitigation systems ( 17 ), the improved building code regarding energy efficiency in new constructions ( 18 ), and the changing climate ( 19 ). Here, we developed a national model to predict monthly community-level radon concentrations from 2001 to 2021. The model is based on over 6 million radon measurements, mostly conducted during property transactions by inspectors. We obtained these data from independent analytic laboratories and then assimilated them into a database after removing sensitive information according to privacy policies. These extensive measurements allowed us to investigate the trends in radon concentrations driven jointly by widespread mitigations, improved energy efficiency, increased adoption of air conditioning, and a changing climate. The classic random forest algorithm was used to learn the complex interactions between geological, meteorological, architectural, and socioeconomic radon predictors. Our model was developed using a larger, more recent dataset collected with improved detectors and more standardized protocol. We also applied advanced statistical learning techniques to estimate radon concentrations with unprecedented spatial and temporal granularity. The results provide up-to-date distributions of radon, which will assist national, state, and local organizations in targeting resources for testing, mitigating, and implementing radon-resistant building construction. Our national model can also provide detailed exposure assessments for population-based health studies, aimed at understanding the health effects of radon. Results Spatial Distribution of Predicted Radon Concentrations. According to our model, the population-weighted average of community-level radon concentration at screening floor is 53.3 Bq/m 3 , higher than EPA’s estimated concentration of 48.1 Bq/m 3 . As shown in Fig. 1 , the South region has the lowest average radon concentrations while the Midwest region has the greatest average radon concentrations. South Dakota has the greatest population-weighted average radon concentration at screening floor (128.3 Bq/m 3 ), followed by the state of Nebraska (119.2 Bq/m 3 ). Louisiana has the lowest population-weighted average radon concentration at screening floor (24.7 Bq/m 3 ), followed by Texas (29.7 Bq/m 3 ). Population Exposed to Radon Concentration above Action Level. We found right-skewed distribution in the radon measurements of each radon zone ( Fig. 2 ), suggesting that people living in each zone are likely to be exposed to dangerous concentrations of radon. From Zone 5 to Zone 1, the center of the zone-specific distribution gradually shifts lower, and the kurtosis increases. Out of the 110,697 measurements in Zone 5, 76,343 (70.1%) are above 148 Bq/m 3 . Meanwhile, only 7.5% of measurements in Zone 1 are above 148 Bq/m 3 . A total of 83.8 million (26.8% of the population) residents in the contiguous United States, according to our model, are exposed to radon concentrations above 148 Bq/m 3 at screening floor. This estimate is larger than the previous estimation by Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry and Centers for Disease Control ( 20 ). Respectively, 33.4 million residents in Zone 2 and 20.0 million residents in Zone 3 are exposed to radon concentrations above 148 Bq/m 3 , accounting for 63.7% of all the residents exposed to the action level. Meanwhile, the average prediction is 20.5 Bq/m 3 higher than the LBNL model in the West, where radon concentrations are low. Performance of Model. We used two cross-validation (CV) methods: repeated ten-fold CV (10-fold CV) and leave-one-location-out CV (LOLO CV) to evaluate the performance of our model. The CV-based Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Mean Error (ME) between the log-transformed predictions and observations are reported as the metric for bias. The uncertainty of ZCTA-level averages is a function of sample size: A larger sample size generally leads to smaller uncertainty. We therefore stratified the comparisons by sample size and calculated size-specific MAEs. As visualized in Fig. 6 , MAE of 10-fold CV drops quickly as the sample size of ZCTA-level observations increases from 4 to 10, then declines with smaller and inconsistent gradients. The MAEs of predictions against ZCTA-level observations based on small samples (&lt;10) are subject to the uncertainty of ZCTA-level observations themselves. We therefore only used the MAE of predictions against ZCTA-level observations based on large samples as a metric of accuracy. A similar decreasing trend can be observed in the MAEs of LOLO CV, as shown in SI Appendix , Fig. S9 . Fig. 6. Trend of MAE against sample size according to the results of 10-fold CV. When sample size is over 10, the MAE of 10-fold CV for log-transformed concentrations is 0.22 (22.6 Bq/m 3 on the original scale). The ME for these log-transformed concentrations is 1.9 × 10 −3 (1.3 Bq/m 3 on the original scale). Under the same condition, the MAE of the LOLO CV for log-transformed concentrations is 0.35 (35.9 Bq/m 3 in original scale), with a ME of 0.05 (3.7 Bq/m 3 in original scale). No concentration-dependent bias is observed ( SI Appendix , Fig. S10 ). In basement-majority ZCTA-level observations, which consist of ≥75% measurements in the basement, the MAE of 10-fold CV is 0.23 with an ME of 7.5 × 10 −4 . In aboveground-majority observations, which consist of ≥75% measurements in aboveground floors, the average MAE of 10-fold CV is 0.23 with an ME of 5 × 10 −3 . The LOLO CV-based MAEs and MEs follow a similar trend. The MAE of LOLO CV is 0.33 for basement-majority observations and 0.39 for aboveground-majority observations. We observed regional differences in predicting accuracy and bias. As shown in SI Appendix , Fig. S11 , four census regions have similar 10-fold CV MAEs and MEs for basement-majority observations. However, the 10-fold CV MAEs and MEs for aboveground-majority observations vary considerably. West United States has the smallest 10-fold CV MAE for aboveground-majority observations, while Midwest region has the largest 10-fold CV MAE for aboveground-majority observations. The Northeast region has the most positive 10-fold CV ME for aboveground-majority observations, suggesting overestimations. This pattern is likely caused by a regional rarity of radon measurements in aboveground floors, which also leads to wide CI. We further divided census regions into census divisions ( SI Appendix , Fig. S12 ). Middle Atlantic division, consisting of New York, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania, has the largest 10-fold CV MAEs for aboveground majority and basement-majority observations. The New England division, consisting of Maine, New Hampshire, Vermont, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, and Connecticut, has the second most positive ME in aboveground-majority observations. Pacific division, consisting of Washington, Oregon, and California, has the most negative ME in basement-majority observations, suggesting local underestimations in basements. These local biases are also likely caused by the local lack of specific buildings, thus the rarity of radon measurements. The LOLO CV-based MAEs and MEs follow a similar spatial distribution, as shown in SI Appendix , Fig. S13 . Meanwhile, we did not observe trends in MAE and ME against the community-level socioeconomic status, which is proxies by median household income ( SI Appendix , Table S1 ). Important Radon Predictors. We evaluated the importance of each radon predictor by calculating the relative increase in MAE when an alternative random forest model was fitted using the predictor randomly permuted. Predictors that produced a significant increase in error were deemed more important than those that did not. The ten most important radon predictors, as well as their normalized importance scores, are average geological radon potential (100), percent of measurements with detectors by Alpha Energy (70.6), elevation (69.2), ambient temperature (64.6), percent of measurements in the basement (61.1), barometric pressure (54.9), longitude (51.7), soil temperature (47.8), latitude (41.6), percent of measurements with detectors by AirChek (40.8), population density (38.9), and concentration of Uranium-238 in surficial materials (33.7). These factors indicate that spatiotemporal variation of radon is a joint function of detector-related, geological, meteorological, and architectural factors. More importantly, a significant part of the variation in the observed radon concentrations can be explained by the percent of detectors manufactured by each laboratory, highlighting the necessity of accounting for the regional differences in market share ( SI Appendix , Figs. S14–S17 ). We assessed the importance of each predictor category by measuring the increase in MAE after randomly permuting all radon predictors within each category, as shown in SI Appendix , Table S2 . Geological factors, including the geological potential index, Uranium-238 concentration, and gravity anomaly, contributed the most to the increase in MAE (72.3%). This was followed by meteorological factors (51.6%), architectural factors (38.9%), soil factors (22.5%), bedrock-related factors (20.8%), surficial materials (16.6%), detector-related factors (14.9%), topographic factors (13.7%), socioeconomic factors (11.8%), spatial trend (6.9%), temporal trend (5.9%), and seismic factors (5.0%). The ranking of categorical importance differs from the importance of individual radon predictors, likely due to correlations within categories. For example, soil temperature and ambient temperature are highly correlated (Spearman correlation = 0.97), which leads to an underestimating their individual importance, as the information in one can be mostly inferred from the other. By randomly permuting all variables within a category, we gain a clearer understanding of the true contribution of each category. Thus, variation in radon is mostly driven by geological, meteorological, and architectural factors in the United States. Partial dependence plots (PDPs) were used to investigate the relationships between radon predictors and radon concentrations. A two-dimensional PDP shows the marginal effects of a radon predictor on the predictions. Higher percentages of radon measurements in the basement are associated with higher average radon concentrations ( SI Appendix , Fig. S18 E ). Variation in strong geological radon predictors, such as geological radon potential, elevation, and concentration of Uranium-238 in surficial materials can cause large variations in community-level radon concentrations ( SI Appendix , Fig. S18 A , B , and H ). Both ambient temperature and soil temperature are negatively associated with radon concentrations, in agreement with the seasonal variation in ventilation ( SI Appendix , Fig. S18 C and F ). Barometric pressure negatively influences the exhalation of radon, as shown in SI Appendix , Fig. S18 D . Communities with higher population densities tend to have lower radon concentrations, likely due to reduced earth-contact areas in densely populated environments ( SI Appendix , Fig. S18 G ). The proportions of detectors from different manufacturers influence radon predictions in distinct ways. All four types of detectors met the annual accuracy standards during the study period, indicating that their relative measurement errors were below 20% in the controlled chamber environment. However, as shown in SI Appendix , Fig. S19 , detectors manufactured and analyzed by Alpha Laboratory tend to produce higher measurements compared with the other three types, even after adjusting for related factors. This variation in performance in real-world conditions, as previously noted by Sun et al. ( 23 , 24 ), can be attributed to lab-specific protocols that address factors not controlled in the chamber, such as fluctuations in radon concentrations, airflow, and barometric pressure. This heterogeneity underscores the importance of recognizing these patterns in the data and mitigating prediction biases by integrating measurements from different laboratories. Discussion Our model provides a nationwide community-level prediction of radon in 30 y. The high-resolution maps are based on a resourceful, most of which was conducted for mandatory disclosure during property transactions. These extensive measurements, along with the cutting-edge machine learning techniques and improved knowledge about driving factors of radon, enabled us to achieve an unprecedented resolution and accuracy. Our results revealed that approximately one-quarter of residents live in buildings with radon concentrations above 148 Bq/m 3 on the screening floor. Most of these residents live in zones with medium predicted radon concentrations, highlighting the importance of comprehensive radon surveys regardless of the background concentrations. The predictions of our model were significantly higher than those of the LBNL model, which also uses geometric mean concentration as the major metric. Because of the small observed temporal variations during the study period, it is highly unlikely that this difference is solely caused by a drastic rise in radon during the 1990s. Differences in measurement protocols, measurement devices, and data analysis methods jointly lead to the discrepancy. For instance, the US EPA did not implement the current short-term radon testing protocol, which requires close-building conditions ( 16 ), until the completion of two national radon surveys ahead of the LBNL model. Another likely cause of the mismatch is the different spatial resolution, as a coarse spatial unit tends to smooth out the extreme values. The predictions of our model were meanwhile lower than those of the USGS and US EPA model. These differences are primarily caused by the usage of geometric means in our model. Arithmetic means, previously used in USGS and EPA model, are unable to reflect the general radon concentrations in the community due to the highly right-skewed distribution of radon measurements ( Fig. 2 ). We observed slight decreases in radon concentrations during the study period in Zone 4 and Zone 5, regions with high average radon levels ( Fig. 3 ). These trends were jointly driven by the increased adoption of radon mitigation systems, and changes in natural and building environments. Since 1987, US EPA has recommended installing an active mitigation system if the indoor radon concentration is above 148 Bq/m 3 ( 3 ). Passive mitigation systems are more commonly required for new constructions in regions with high background radon levels ( 25 ). The higher precipitation and humidity, associated with climate change, can also impede the natural emanation of radon, directing it toward the unsaturated soil underneath the buildings ( 26 ). Meanwhile, the increased usage of air conditioning systems and improved energy efficiency lower the exchange between indoor air and outside, thereby elevating radon concentrations ( 19 ). The influences of these environmental and behavioral factors are tightly intertwined, thus difficult to quantify independently. The observed correlations between radon predictors and community-level radon concentrations are mostly consistent with previous studies. For instance, it has been known that buildings over shales and limestones are more likely to have a high risk of radon, compared to buildings over other types of bedrocks ( 27 , 28 ). Residents of higher socioeconomic status are more aware of radon’s health risk and therefore less likely to ignore or delay addressing the known radon problems ( 29 ). The negative correlation with Bouguer gravity anomaly, though was never studied before, was theoretically reasonable because the fractures and openness in low-density geological formations can expedite the underground migration of radon ( 27 ). However, we observed that distance to the nearest geological fault is negatively correlated with radon concentrations, contradicting the previous findings ( 30 ). The discrepancy is likely due to the heterogeneity of geological faults and study scale. We observed that most of geological faults in the lower 48 states are in the West, where radon concentrations are generally lower than the Midwest and Northeast. It is likely that proximity to some specific geological faults is correlated with higher radon concentrations. We incorporated features of faults, including angles of fault and direction of slip, to investigate the distinct effects on radon. However, none of these features are strong predictors of radon. Our national radon model is a significant improvement over our previous regional radon model, which covers the Northeast and Midwest ( 31 , 32 ). We collected over 6 million radon measurements from a larger network of radon analytic laboratories across the nation. A resampling method was applied to learn the patterns in radon concentrations in relation to detector-related factors. Factors, such as gravity anomaly, types of bedrock, and features of geological faults, were incorporated into a database consisting of 186 radon predictors. These improvements jointly enlarge the volume of our training dataset over 100 times larger than the previous model, enabling us to investigate the small-scale variations in radon concentrations. We used the classic random forest model, rather than the spatial random forest model ( 33 ), to predict ZIP Code-level radon concentrations due to its superior performance. Our previous study showed that spatial random forest model worked well in the Northeast and Midwest, a densely sampled region. In our study, we initially trained over 300 base models using random forest, gradient boosting, and neural network methods, with various combinations of hyperparameters ( SI Appendix , Table S3 ). However, the ensemble model did not outperform the single best model without allowing the negative contribution of base learning models. Consequently, we selected the best-performing base model as the final model. Limitations exist in our study. First, building-level radon concentrations cannot be predicted with our model due to a lack of building-specific geological and architectural information in the training dataset. As a result, our community-level predicted concentrations should not be generalized to individual buildings within the community as the geological and architectural factors vary. Second, aboveground floor-dependent gradients in multifamily buildings were not investigated. Multifamily buildings, such as high-rise residential buildings, are becoming increasingly popular in densely populated areas. However, floor information (basement or aboveground) is only available for single-family buildings in our data. Previous studies have found that variation of radon among aboveground floors is generally smaller than the difference between basement and aboveground floors ( 34 ). As a result, aggregating aboveground measurements regardless of building type carries a low risk to seriously biasing the ZCTA-level observations. Third, property inspections, which consist of radon measurement and other essential evaluations of the building, are likely to be skipped by buyers in a highly competitive property market. Without a data source covering all the transactions, it is challenging to estimate the influence of these practices on our estimations. Finally, the EPA protocol requires inspectors to place the detector on the lowest livable floor, such as a finished basement, rather than the lowest occupied space. Li et al. ( 34 ) quantified the basement-to-aboveground gradients in radon concentrations, showing that radon levels tend to be higher in basements than in aboveground floors. Consequently, relying solely on the model without accounting for these gradients may result in an overestimation of radon exposure dose. In this national study, we took advantage of the extensive short-term radon measurements to update our knowledge in a cost-effective way. While individual short-term radon measurements may not be as accurate as long-term measurements ( 35 ), their large sample size and the mandatory disclosure during property transactions make them more representative of the population than voluntary radon surveys. Our study presents a workflow to filter and aggregate measurements, which will facilitate future efforts to generate updated radon maps with finer spatial and temporal resolutions. The updated community-level estimates are important for policymakers to determine the extent of regulations, such as mandatory passive mitigation in new multifamily residence construction. Previous regulations based on county-level estimations likely diffuse the resources because of the strong community-to-community variations in radon levels. Our model can be used to design community-specific building codes, which are cost-effective ways to address the issue in new constructions. Our model can also be used to assess residential exposure to radon in population health studies aimed at improving understanding of radon’s health effects. The monthly estimations will facilitate health scientists in investigating the short-term or prenatal health effects of radon. Materials and Methods National Database of Radon Measurements. Tens of millions of radon measurements have been conducted in the past decades because radon disclosure is required during property transactions in 35 of the lower 48 states ( 36 ). Most of these measurements were conducted with short-term detectors as part of property inspection. The de facto mandatory nature of disclosure, rather than voluntary participation, helps guarantee the representativeness of the samples. We gained access to 6.23 million radon measurements from Spruce Environmental Technologies, LLC (Haverhill, Massachusetts) and Alpha Energy Labs, Inc (Carrollton, Texas), two major radon analytical laboratories in the United States. These measurements were conducted with four different types of detectors approved by both National Radon Safety Board (NRSB) and National Radon Proficiency Program (NRPP): the Air Chek foil bag charcoal kit (NRSB #10333, NRPP #AC-8200), AccuStar charcoal canister (AccuStar PicoCan-400; NRSB #10320; NRPP #AC-1159), liquid scintillation vial (AccuStar CLS-2; NRSB#12193; NRPP# LS-8088), and Alpha Energy tray (Alpha Energy Labs RD-1; NESB #10325, NRPP #AC-8202). All four types of detectors use activated charcoal, contained in four different kinds of containers, to passively absorb the radon in the surrounding environment during a 2 to 7-d period. The integral radon concentration during the period can be subsequently estimated either with gamma spectrometry (Air Chek, PicoCan-400, and Alpha Energy RD-1) or a scintillation counter (CLS-2) shortly after the completion of measurement in the laboratories. The relative errors of all four short-term passive detectors were below 0.2 in NRSB- and NRPP-accredited chambers. Li et al. (2023) reported a strong correlation (nearly 0.8) between a short-term measurement and a collocated follow-up 90-d radon measurement in a US-based study, suggesting that short-term measurements can serve as a good proxy for the average radon concentration in the following months ( 35 ). The spatial density of radon measurements ( SI Appendix , Fig. S20 ) agrees with the population distribution of the United States., indicating coverage of most population in the study region. Highly right-skewed distributions were observed at both national and community levels. Under these conditions, the arithmetic mean concentration, used in the EPA and USGS model, is vulnerable to being biased by a few outlier concentrations and therefore tends to overestimate the exposure for normal residents in a community. Consequently, we calculated median value, rather than arithmetic mean value, as a proxy for the general exposure level. The protocol of radon measurement requires short-term detectors to be placed in the lowest livable space ( 16 ). As a result, 73.6% of the measurements were conducted in the basement. In northern areas, where the required depth of foundation is greater, most measurements were conducted in the basement ( SI Appendix , Fig. S21 ). Radon measurements in southern areas were predominantly conducted in aboveground floors due to the rarity of basements in residential buildings. The majority of the radon measurements were conducted with AirChek (45.7%) and Alpha Energy (37.8%), which respectively have a greater share in the northern and southern states ( SI Appendix , Figs. S14 and S15 ). Contributions of AccuStar Liquid Scintillation (10.6%) and AccuStar Canister (6.0%) were smaller and more localized ( SI Appendix , Figs. S16 and S17 ). These patterns highlight the importance of controlling for the regional distinctions in implementation that lead to the difference between the observed and “true” concentrations. We collaborated closely with Spruce Environmental Technologies, Inc. and Alpha Energy Laboratories to design the workflow for filtering and processing the original dataset of 6,060,050 rows of measurements. To ensure the validity of the measurements, we excluded readings that did not adhere to the prescribed measurement duration instructions. These instructions specified a duration of 3 to 7 d for AirChek, 2 to 4 d for both AccuStar PicoCan and AccuStar CLS, and 2 to 7 d for Alpha Energy. We then averaged concurrent measurements conducted in the same building on the same floor during the same period, a practice recommended in regions such as New Jersey. Measurements conducted before 2001 were excluded from the analysis due to a lack of matching radon predictors. We removed radon measurements that were conducted within three months after the previous measurements in the same buildings. These follow-up measurements were either conducted to confirm the necessity of mitigation or to evaluate the effectiveness of the mitigation. As a result, these follow-up measurements were no longer conducted in a random and representative sample of buildings in a ZCTA. Including these measurements in the model will bias the exposure assessment, as well as the subsequent health analysis. Measurements over 3,700 Bq/m 3 (99.95 th percentile) were excluded due to the rarity of the measurements and their overshadowing impacts on the following model training. Measurements under the lower detection limit (LDL) of detectors were imputed with random numbers uniformly distributed between 0.01 Bq/m 3 and the detector-specific LDLs (AirChek: 3.7 Bq/m 3 ; AccuStar PicoCan and CLS: 14.8 Bq/m 3 ; Alpha Energy: 19 Bq/m 3 ). We subsequently grouped the measurements according to the 5-digit ZIP Code Tabular Area (ZCTA) and the calendar month of measurement. ZCTA, developed by the US Census Bureau for areal statistics, generally covers a zone much smaller than a county. If radon measurements were conducted in over three residences in the same ZCTA and month, we calculated the geometric mean of radon levels. Besides the average, we calculated associated factors consisting of the proportions of measurements in the basement versus in aboveground floors, and the proportions of the four types of detectors. Furthermore, we increased the size of the training dataset by hierarchically resampling the radon observations. For a ZCTA-level observation based on N (N &gt; 20) measurements, we randomly sampled ⌊N/2⌋ measurements twice and ⌊N/4⌋ measurements four times, then calculated the corresponding geometric mean and the associated detector-related factors. The resamples allowed us to extend the limited training dataset to understand the floor-dependent and detector-independent variations. We log-transformed these ZCTA-level observations to address their right-skewed distribution. At the end of data processing, we obtained 4,477,070 observed monthly ZCTA-level radon concentrations (13.2% from original measurements and 86.8% from resampling), which were subsequently log-transformed and used in the model. It is important to note that we did not have access to building-specific mitigation records. As a result, we included all radon measurements, regardless of mitigation status, as long as they were taken from a representative sample of buildings in a ZCTA. This included radon concentrations from mitigated buildings if they were measured during property transactions. The impact of an increasing proportion of mitigated residences was reflected in the ZCTA-level observations, although the presence of a specific mitigation system in a building could not be identified. Radon Predictors. We compiled a database of 186 community-level predictors to estimate monthly ZCTA-level radon concentrations. These radon predictors were categorized into five classes: detector-related, geological, architectural, socioeconomic, and meteorological factors. Some of these covariates were used in our previous study to estimate monthly ZCTA-level radon concentrations in the Northeast and Midwest ( 31 ). We added more geological factors to model the spatial patterns related to bedrocks and geological faults. The sources and resolutions of the 186 predictors are summarized in SI Appendix , Table S2 . Grid-based radon predictors were aggregated into community boundaries using population-weighted methods. Detector-related factors control for differences among ZCTA-level observations due solely to the types or positions of detectors, independent of other covariates. For each ZCTA-level observation, we calculated the proportions of measurements conducted in basements, in aboveground spaces, and with four types of detectors. Incorporating the proportions of measuring floors enabled the model to detect floor-dependent gradients in concentrations and to subsequently predict radon concentrations in basements and aboveground floors, respectively. Similarly, the proportions of the four types of detectors allowed us to model the patterns in radon concentrations caused jointly by the systematic differences among detectors and regional market shares, thereby removing these artificial trends from the predicted ZCTA-level radon concentrations. Geological factors govern the generation, emanation, and underground movement of radon and, therefore, determine the radon concentration in soil gas ( 28 ). These factors include the ground surface concentration of Uranium-238, the parent element of radon, to account for radon generation ( 27 ); geological radon potential, which accounts for the abundance of radon in soil gas; the age and type of bedrock underneath the community, which also influences the abundance of radon in soil gas ( 12 ); the age and granularity of surficial materials, which affect the generation and emanation of radon in soil ( 37 ); the density of multiple types of geological faults, which facilitate the vertical movement of radon via fractures ( 30 ); magnetic anomalies and gravity field, which indicate underground structures ( 38 ); and soil parameters. These include available water capacity, percent of organic matter, saturated hydraulic conductivity, vertical permeability, bulk density, field capacity, porosity, erodibility, depth, and the percent of soil components of different granularities (gravel, sand, and clay) ( 37 ). Architectural factors influence the infiltration of radon from soil into the indoor environment ( 39 ). We also incorporated covariates related to other domestic radon sources, such as construction materials, drinking water, and natural gas used as cooking or heating fuel. The four included community-level factors are building size, as a proxy for foundation depth, which governs the pressure difference between soil and the domestic environment; building density, as a proxy for whether the dwelling has access to a municipal water supply, which typically has a lower radon level, or relies on a private well, which generally has a higher radon concentration ( 40 ); average building age, which reflects the prevailing building structure, the construction materials used, and the condition of the basement ( 41 ); and the type of fuel, which accounts for the potential impact of heating or cooking with natural gas, which may have an elevated concentration of radon ( 42 ). Socioeconomic factors likely influence energy efficiency and the prevalence of radon mitigation ( 21 ). The covariates in our model are median property value, mean household annual income, percent of residents living below poverty line, and percent of properties occupied by owner. Meteorological factors influence the exhalation of radon gas from the soil to the atmosphere, underground radon movement, and atmospheric radon concentrations. The included factors are snow depth and accumulated precipitation, which reduce radon exhalation ( 43 ); barometric pressure, which is negatively related to radon exhalation; soil temperature, which affects radon concentration in soil gas via a temperature-sensitive gaseous/aqueous partitioning process ( 44 ); soil moisture content, which can slow the subterranean flow of soil gas and affect the emanation rate of radon ( 37 ); and gross beta radiation measurements at nearby RadNet monitors operated by the US EPA ( 45 ). Gross beta radiation is the sum of beta radiation from beta-emitting radionuclides that are bound to ambient particulates. Most beta radiation is emitted by Pb-210, a decay product of radon gas. Therefore, gross beta radiation can be used as a proxy for atmospheric radon concentration, which has not been widely measured ( 46 , 47 ). We included the calendar year to account for long-term trends and month of the year to account for seasonality that has not been fully captured by meteorological factors. Data Analysis. We utilized the classic random forest method to predict monthly ZCTA-level radon concentrations. The random forest is a widely used machine learning algorithm that combines the predictions of multiple decision trees, each trained on different subsets of the training dataset ( 48 ). Known for its high accuracy and the ability to learn nonlinear decision boundaries, the random forest method has been successfully applied in modeling the spatiotemporal distributions of various environmental pollutants, such as fine particulate matter ( 49 ), nitrogen dioxide ( 50 ), and ozone ( 51 ). We trained 353 machine learning models using various methods—random forest, gradient boosting, and neural networks—under different hyperparameter settings ( SI Appendix , Table S3 ), after preprocessing the radon predictors by centering and rescaling. The random forest model, with ntree =300, mtry =80, max.depth =60, and min.node.size =1, outperformed all other models in accuracy, including the ensemble model with non-negative coefficient constraints. The parallel implementation of random forest made it particularly well-suited for our large dataset. Additionally, we used variable importance metrics and PDPs generated by the random forest model to gain insights into the influence of radon predictors. These analyses allowed us to compare our findings with existing knowledge on the factors affecting radon concentrations. We fit classic random forest model using the “ranger” package (version 0.13.1) ( 52 ) in R (version 4.1.3) ( 53 ). All analyses were conducted on the Cannon cluster, which is supported by the Research Computing Group at Harvard University, Faculty of Arts and Sciences. The study is part of an IRB-approved project to reassess national residential radon exposure based in Harvard T H Chan School of Public Health (IRB21-0056). Model Evaluation. To evaluate the performance of the model, we employed the repeated ten-fold CV (10-fold CV) method and the LOLO CV (LOLO CV) method, converting the predicted log-transformed concentrations back to their original form afterward. In the 10-fold CV, we randomly split the entire training dataset into ten nonoverlapping subsets. Nine of these subsets were used to train the random forest model, while the remaining one was used to evaluate the model’s performance. This split-train-test process was repeated three times to ensure robust evaluation. In LOLO (LOLO) CV, we iteratively evaluated the model’s performance using data from a specific ZIP code, while the model was trained on all measurements except those from that ZIP code. As a classic spatial CV method, LOLO provides an estimate of the model’s accuracy for predicting radon concentrations in ZIP codes where no radon measurements are available. The CV-based MAE between the log-transformed predictions and observations is reported as the metric for accuracy. We assessed the model’s performance by calculating two metrics: MAE and ME. Additionally, we calculated these metrics for each census region, division, season, and 5 y period, aiming to identify any spatial and temporal trends in the model’s performance. The difference between ZCTA-level prediction ( C p ) and ZCTA-level observation ( C o ) was divided into two components: the difference between C p and the actual concentration ( C a ) and the difference between C a and C o . The discrepancy between C a and C o is a joint function of C o ‘s sample size and the within-ZCTA variation in unit-specific radon concentrations. A C o derived from a larger sample is more likely to accurately represent C a than a similar C o derived from a smaller sample. Therefore, C o can be considered a reliable estimation of C a when it is based on a large sample. Consequently, we restricted our evaluation of C p against C o to instances where C o ‘s sample size exceeded a predetermined cutoff value (n). This cutoff was determined by stratifying the pairwise differences between C p and C o according to N, analyzing the trend of differences relative to N, and identifying a value above which the difference between C p and C o did not significantly decrease ( Fig. 6 ). Furthermore, we fitted simple linear regression models to explore Pearson’s correlation between C p and C o (r 2 ). The R 2 of these linear models was used to assess the extent to which C p could explain the variance of C o . Prediction. We utilized the model to predict the monthly average radon concentration of a ZCTA based on radon predictors. First, we assumed that the four types of detectors had equal proportions to eliminate spatial trends caused by regional differences in their market shares and systematic differences in performance. Second, we set the proportion of measurements in the basement to 100% and 0% to generate two distinct prediction values for basement and aboveground floors, respectively ( SI Appendix , Figs. S22 and S23 ). Third, we calculated the ZCTA-level radon concentration at screening floor by taking the weighted average of the predictions for basement and aboveground spaces, where the weight was determined by the percentage of measurements in the basement ( SI Appendix , Fig. S21 ). Finally, we employed nonparametric bootstrapping to evaluate the uncertainty and the potential risk of overfitting for each predicted concentration ( C p ), as shown in SI Appendix , Fig. S24 .
