Accurate cell-type deconvolution is critical for correct interpretation of Epigenome-Wide Association Studies. For all cell-type deconvolution tasks, it is necessary to estimate underlying cell-type fractions in a sample, which is usually accomplished using a DNA methylation reference panel built from sorted or single-cell DNAm data. Two competing approaches have emerged to build such reference panels, one which uses machine-learning, and another based on optimizing effect size and cell-type specificity. Here we demonstrate that the latter approach is preferable, because, owing to the relatively small number of sorted samples used in building panels, standard machine learning does not optimize effect size and cell-type specificity, causing the model to overfit and underperform when tested in independent data. Furthermore, adult blood panels built from cell-type specific hypomethylated markers improves estimation of cell-type fractions when compared to panels built from hypermethylated ones. These insights provide important guidelines for optimizing the construction of future DNAm reference panels. To aid this task, we have added a function for building an optimized DNAm reference panel to our EpiDISH R-package. A comparative analysis of methods for building DNA methylation reference panels for cell-type deconvolution highlight important guidelines for optimizing their construction. Because DNAm is highly cell-type-specific 2 , 3 , measuring it in bulk tissue, as is most often done in DNA methylome studies, can cause statistical confounding 4 . This is because DNAm changes in bulk-tissue can arise from shifts in cell-type composition and not because of underlying DNAm changes in individual cell-types 3 – 6 . This challenge can be addressed by estimating the actual cell-type fractions in the samples, which could be achieved either experimentally (e.g., blood cell-counts) or statistically 4 , 7 – 9 , the latter being more feasible and cheaper. Statistically, the procedure involves using a cell-type deconvolution algorithm that first estimates the underlying cell-type proportions in a bulk-tissue sample, subsequently using these estimated fractions as covariates in linear models to identify differentially methylated cytosines (DMCs) that are not driven by changes in cell-type composition. The estimation of these cell-type fractions requires the construction of a DNAm reference panel, which contains representative DNAm profiles for all main underlying cell-types in the tissue of interest, and which is normally built by processing genome-wide DNAm profiles of sorted cell-types 9 – 12 or single-cells 13 , 14 . Although studies have shown that the inference of cell-type fractions from such a DNAm reference panel is a relatively robust procedure 15 , it is nevertheless important to ensure that DNAm reference panels are built in the most optimal way. This is particularly important in view of the growing need to build such panels for a wider range of different tissue-types at increased cellular resolution 14 , 16 – 18 . In this regard, a key challenge is posed by the relatively small number of sorted samples available for each cell-type (typically less than 10) 10 , 11 , as generating larger numbers of sorted samples (say 25) for each of 10–20 cell types would require the generation of several hundred samples. Similar limitations apply to single-cell DNAm (scDNAm) data, which is plagued by high sparsity and not yet widely available, with existing scDNAm datasets only profiling samples from a small number of independent donors 13 , 19 , 20 . Thus, it is important to set up guidelines for the optimized construction of DNAm reference panels from what are typically small DNAm reference datasets. Ideally, a DNAm reference panel should comprise DNAm markers that are (i) highly discriminatory between different cell types, (ii) stable within each cell type, and (iii) robust to variable factors such as age, race or genotype of the donors. As the deconvolution process essentially projects the methylation information of a bulk-tissue sample onto a feature space defined by the reference markers, if this space is poorly defined (i.e., markers lack true cell-type specificity or discriminatory power), the accuracy of the projection and estimation of cell-type fractions will be compromised. Traditionally, the selection of cell-type-specific markers for a reference panel is based on identifying methylation sites with significant statistical differences between the various cell types. The first approach, due to Houseman et al. 9 , independently fitted one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) models to each CpG to test for differences in mean methylation levels across K purified cell types, selecting CpGs with the largest F -statistics to constitute the reference library/panel. However, the F-statistic prioritizes sites with large overall DNAm differences across all cell types, which can lead to the over-selection of CpGs that strongly differentiate one or a few cell types while neglecting others, resulting in imbalanced discriminatory power within the panel. To address the global limitation of the ANOVA method, subsequent studies shifted to using t-statistics (now called “Legacy method”, implemented in the Bioconductor package minfi using the function pickCompProbes ) to select CpGs that are significantly hyper- or hypo-methylated in a specific cell type compared to all others 21 . Subsequent optimized feature selection frameworks have largely built upon the library generated by this “Legacy method” as their initial candidate pool of marker CpGs. Examples include iterative refinement strategies like IDOL and RESET 22 , 23 , as well as machine learning approaches such as Elastic Net and Random Forest 24 . However, marker CpGs selected purely on statistical significance ranking and machine learning may potentially overlook the magnitude of the effect size, as a small effect can still be very informative if standard deviations are small. We reasoned that such small effect size CpGs are more likely to represent false positives (i.e., features that do not validate in independent data), because based on the available evidence from large WGBS atlases 25 , true cell-type specific marker CpGs have large or reasonably large effect sizes. Moreover, selecting candidate marker CpGs using t -statistics does not guarantee maximum specificity for a given cell-type, especially if the number of sorted samples per cell-type varies significantly between cell-types. To overcome these limitations, we recently introduced a novel marker selection strategy based on a joint t -statistic and “gap specificity score” (GSS) 26 , 27 . This GSS metric is designed to directly prioritize the magnitude of the methylation difference—the ‘gap’—between a target cell type and all other cell types, thus maximizing effect size and specificity, while also ensuring that markers are selected based on statistical significance and hence a high within-cell-type stability. Here, we directly compare this GSS-based approach to the competing method described above based on using IDOL, as well as other machine-learning methods, including Elastic Net and Random Forests. We demonstrate that the GSS-based approach builds a more accurate and robust deconvolution reference panel. We further validate this approach by demonstrating its enhanced ability to detect known age-related changes in immune cells and to yield more biologically meaningful or significant findings in epigenome-wide association studies (EWAS) of schizophrenia and type 1 diabetes. As such, the insights gained from our comparative analysis may serve as useful guidelines for the construction of future DNAm reference panels. Results Contamination of low-effect-size DMCs The construction of a DNAm reference panel according to the IDOL algorithm begins with a feature selection strategy followed by an optimization designed to select an optimal subset. Here, we demonstrate that the use of t -statistics in the feature selection step can lead to contamination of undesirable low-effect size DMCs that are retained after optimization. To show this, we utilized the sorted immune-cell library generated by Salas et al., which encompasses EPICv1 DNAm profiles of 12 sorted immune cell types 10 (Methods). Initially, for each CpG site, a two-sample t -test was performed to compare the mean DNAm of a given cell type against the remaining 11 cell types. For each cell type, CpGs exhibiting a statistically significant difference, defined by a false discovery rate (FDR) less than 0.05, were identified as cell type-specific DMCs. These DMCs were then ranked, followed by the selection of N marker CpGs per cell type, comprising the N/2 DMCs with the largest positive t -statistics and the N/2 DMCs with the largest negative t -statistics. Using N = 100 and following application of IDOL, we observed that there were many low effect size DMCs among the selected markers (Fig. 1a ), thus highlighting a potential problem with using t -statistics to build DNAm reference panels. A novel gap-specificity-score (GSS) to improve DNAm reference panels To address the above problem, we devised a novel approach to rank DMCs by a gap-specificity score (GSS) (Fig. 1b ). This score quantifies the methylation ‘gap’: for hypermethylated markers, it is the target cell type’s minimum methylation value minus the maximum methylation value taken over all other samples representing the other cell types; for hypomethylated markers, it is defined by the minimum methylation value over all other cell types minus the target cell type’s maximum methylation value (Fig. 1b ). Thus, in both cases, the GSS should ideally be positive and as large as possible, as this ensures specificity of the marker to the one given cell-type. GSS is designed to identify robust cell type-specific markers by prioritizing this ‘gap’ (reflecting effect size) alongside the marker’s cross-sample stability within the target cell type. In order to objectively compare GSS to the previous t -statistic-based (‘legacy’) approach, we used the same cell-type-specific DMCs identified earlier using t -statistics, but now ranking hypermethylated and hypomethylated DMCs separately by the GSS and subsequently selecting the top N /2 from each category (Fig. 1b ). The effect size of each DMC was defined as the absolute difference between the mean beta value of its target cell type and the mean beta value of the remaining 11 cell types. As required, top DMCs ranked by the GSS displayed significantly larger effect sizes than those ranked purely by t -statistics, and this was stable for variable choices of N (Fig. 1c , Supplementary Data 1 ). Fixing N = 100, we next compared the effect sizes from the two methods for each cell type separately. We observed that ranking based only on t -statistics often included DMCs with notably low effect sizes, an issue especially prominent for lymphocyte subtypes (Fig. 1d , Supplementary Data 1 ). To further demonstrate the value of the GSS, we compared it to standard effect size measures, including Cohen’s F and Cohen’s D 28 . Using Cohen’s F , which is a global effect size measure across all cell-types, top-ranked markers could only distinguish between the myeloid and lymphoid lineages, as the variation between these lineages is much larger than the variation within the myeloid and lymphoid lineages, hence failing to discriminate more similar cell-types from each other (e.g., naïve vs memory CD4+ T-cells) (SI Fig. S1a ). Cohen’s D , which estimates effect sizes by comparing one cell-type to all others, also proved suboptimal as it fails to guarantee specificity, i.e., it frequently selected probes with high effect size but negative GSS (SI Fig. S1b , Supplementary Data 1 ). In contrast, ranking features by positive GSS values ensures specificity (SI Fig. S1c , Supplementary Data 1 ). A concrete example of a marker for memory CD4+ T-cells selected using Cohen’s D illustrates how its GSS is negative, since T-regulatory cells display even lower DNAm levels than memory CD4+ T-cells (SI Fig. S1d ). Low-effect-size DMCs can negatively impact cell-type deconvolution accuracy To confirm that low effect size DMCs can negatively impact downstream cell-type deconvolution, we built DNAm reference matrices using the two different approaches (Fig. 1b ), subsequently applying them to deconvolve twelve experimental mixtures, each being a mixture of 12 immune-cell-types with known cell type proportions 10 (Supplementary Data 2 ). We used the robust partial correlations (RPC) method within EpiDISH 29 , 30 to estimate the underlying cell-type fractions, which were then compared to the true fractions by calculating the Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC) and root mean square error (RMSE) (Methods, Fig. 2a , Supplementary Data 1 ). While both ranking strategies yielded similar PCCs, the GSS-strategy consistently achieved a lower global RMSEs (Fig. 2a, b , Supplementary Data 1 ). Furthermore, this improvement was not dependent on the optimization algorithm, as it consistently outperformed the t -statistic-ranked panel when fractions were evaluated using two other optimization methods (Constrained Projection 9 and Support Vector Regression 31 ) (SI Fig. S2 , Methods, Supplementary Data 1 ). Notably, the RMSE for memory CD4 T cells was markedly higher than that of other cell types (Fig. 2a , Supplementary Data 1 ), consistent with their DMCs exhibiting the smallest effect sizes relative to other cell types (Fig. 1d , Supplementary Data 1 ). To further investigate the variable performance across cell types, we calculated the mean t -statistic (representing average significance) and the mean gap specificity score (representing average effect size) for the top 100 marker DMCs selected (by t -statistics) for each cell type. This revealed that deconvolution performance (i.e., a low RMSE, high PCC) of each cell-type was strongly and significantly correlated with the cell-type’s mean gap specificity score, while the correlation with the mean t -statistic used to select them was weaker and not statistically significant (Fig. 2c , Supplementary Data 1 ). This suggests that the accuracy of a deconvolution reference panel is determined by the effect size of inter-cell-type differences (i.e., gap specificity score) rather than their statistical significance (i.e., t -statistic). To further confirm this, we devised a sliding window approach, where we compared deconvolution accuracies for a total of 46 windows of size 50 DMCs, with DMCs ranked either by t -statistic or GSS (Methods). References ranked by GSS demonstrated a strong performance dependence on marker rank for both PCC and RMSE, while references ranked by t-statistics exhibited only a weak correlation for PCC and showed no significant association with RMSE (Fig. 2d , Supplementary Data 1 ). Hypomethylated DMCs improve cell-type deconvolution accuracy While the legacy method selected both hyper- and hypomethylated DMCs impartially, hypomethylated DMCs are more likely to be true cell-type-specific markers, since markers that are uniquely hypomethylated for a given cell-type are much more frequent than uniquely hypermethylated ones 25 . To further refine the GSS-based strategy, we thus asked if a reference constructed solely from top-ranked hypomethylated DMCs could outperform a reference combining both hyper- and hypomethylated DMCs, and a reference constructed using only top-ranked hypermethylated DMCs. Using a range of N values, the three distinct reference matrices were then compared in terms of their accuracy to deconvolve the twelve experimental 12-cell-type mixtures (Fig. 3a , Supplementary Data 1 ). The reference constructed solely from hypermethylated DMCs yielded the lowest PCC values across all N values and the highest RMSE values when N &gt; 100, compared to the other two reference types, indicating that only a small proportion of hypermethylated DMCs are effective for cell-type discrimination (Fig. 3b , Supplementary Data 1 ). Conversely, using only top hypomethylated DMCs at N = 600 emerged as the best-performing reference, achieving the highest PCC and lowest RMSE (Fig. 3b , Supplementary Data 1 ). Compared to the combined reference type, this ‘hypomethylated-only’ strategy resulted in a modest but clear reduction in RMSE, and notably improved deconvolution accuracy for memory CD4 T cells, which had previously exhibited poorer deconvolution accuracy (Fig. 3a , Supplementary Data 1 ). While the ‘hypomethylated-only’ strategy performed best, the underlying combined reference type also achieved excellent performance (PCC ~ 1, RMSE ~ 0.01), demonstrating how the inclusion of many accurate markers can offset the presence of low effect size markers (Fig. 3b , Supplementary Data 1 ). ML-optimization for marker selection can overfit to small training data Next, we sought to determine if the IDOL algorithm, which is often used to construct and optimize a DNAm reference panel 10 – 12 , 23 , could further improve the performance of a reference matrix built exclusively from top hypomethylated DMCs ranked by the GSS. Since both the legacy method and the GSS-strategy reached their best performance at N = 600 (Fig. 2b , Fig. 3b , Supplementary Data 1 ), we applied IDOL to the two reference matrices defined at this N -value, labeling them as ‘T-Both’ and ‘GSS-Hypo’, respectively. We note that IDOL also requires as input a training set comprising experimental mixtures generated from sorted immune cell types. Using the leave-one-out method, the probability of each CpG being selected is then updated based on whether the average R ² and average RMSE of the estimated proportions for all cell types improve, continuing this process until convergence 23 . Thus, we applied the IDOL algorithm to optimize the T-Both and GSS-Hypo references, adopting the same training set and libSize = 1200 parameter as used by Salas et al. 10 (Methods), resulting in refined T-Both-IDOL and GSS-IDOL DNAm reference panels. Surprisingly, comparing the effect sizes of markers before and after IDOL optimization showed that IDOL has a tendency to select DMCs with lower effect sizes (SI Fig. S3a, b , Supplementary Data 1 ), irrespective of whether we started out using the T-Both or GSS-Hypo reference panel. To explore this further, we assessed the performance of the four reference matrices in the experimental mixtures from the training set as well as those of an independent validation set 10 (Methods, see Supplementary Data 2 for known cell-type proportions). Interestingly, the IDOL-optimized references demonstrated comparable PCCs in the training set but somewhat higher RMSEs (SI Fig. S3c , Supplementary Data 1 ). Moreover, IDOL-optimized references performed worse in terms of both PCC and RMSE in the independent validation set (SI Fig. S3d , Supplementary Data 1 ). Overall, ‘GSS-Hypo’ retained its superior deconvolution performance, even when compared to its IDOL-optimized version, indicating that IDOL may be overfitting slightly due to the relatively small number of experimental mixtures used in training. To demonstrate that this result is robust to different library sizes and generalizes to other machine-learning algorithms, we performed a similar analysis, but now using Elastic Net (EN) 32 and Random Forests (RF) 33 instead of IDOL (Methods). As before, effect sizes of selected features using EN or RF were substantially smaller compared to GSS-Hypo (Fig. 4a, b , Supplementary Data 1 ), translating into a lower accuracy for the cell-type deconvolution on experimental mixtures (Fig. 4c, d , Supplementary Data 1 ), as well as on an independent DNAm dataset with matched FACS counts for 4 cell-types (Methods, Fig. 4e, f , Supplementary Data 1 and 2 ). Because the improvement observed with GSS-Hypo could be driven by the selection of hypomethylated markers, we also benchmarked GSS-Hypo against an analogous approach that uses hypomethylated markers selected using t -statistics (T-hypo) (Methods). GSS-hypo generally outperformed T-hypo across the experimental mixture datasets and the dataset with matched FACS counts (Fig. 4c–f , Supplementary Data 1 ). Collectively, these results support the view that the improvements seen are not only due to the selection of hypomethylated markers but also due to improved specificity, and that, given the relatively small number of sorted samples, any machine learning method is likely to overfit to random variation. GSS-reference detects the known age-related increase in NK cells The immune cell composition of peripheral blood undergoes significant changes with age, characterized by shifts in the proportions of various subgroups, including the ratios of myeloid to lymphoid cells and memory to naive T cells 34 – 36 . To further compare the GSS-Hypo and t -statistic-based reference panels, we examined their ability to capture known age-related changes in immune-cell type composition. Performing a meta-analysis across 15 adult blood cohorts, while adjusting for sex, we observed that for all 3 reference panels (i.e., GSS-hypo, T-hypo and T-both), naive B-cell, naïve CD8+ T-cell and naïve CD4+ T-cell fractions decreased with age, whilst corresponding memory fractions increased (Fig. 5a , Methods, Supplementary Data 3 ). These results align with findings from previous flow cytometric studies, particularly the pronounced decline of naive CD8⁺ T cells with age 34 , 35 . Less trivial is the positive association of NK fractions with age 30 . Only the GSS-Hypo and T-Hypo panels identified a significant age-associated increase in NK-cells (Fig. 5a, b ), consistent with previous reports 35 , 37 . Thus, in this context, the GSS-Hypo panel is more accurate in the estimation of cell-type fractions, but only because of its exclusivity for hypomethylated markers. GSS-reference panel improves interpretability of EWAS findings Small differences in estimated cell-type fractions could also impact EWAS findings 29 . To test this, we first analyzed two independent schizophrenia (SZ) case-control EWAS whole blood datasets from Hannon et al. 38 , 39 , aiming to determine which reference matrix yielded more consistent disease-associated signals across cohorts (Methods). In both the Hannon1 (discovery) and Hannon2 (validation) cohorts, we applied a multivariate linear regression model, adjusting for covariates (e.g., age and sex) and cell-type fractions estimated by one of the 3 reference panels. In the discovery cohort, we obtained 328, 352, and 351 SZ-DMCs (FDR &lt; 0.05) using T-Both, T-Hypo, and GSS-hypo references, respectively. This result was robust to additional adjustment for chip and position, as well as top principal methylation components (SI Fig. S4 , Supplementary Data 1 ). In Hannon2, we observed a strong batch effect, which we successfully adjusted for using Combat 40 (SI Fig. S5 , Supplementary Data 1 ). A total of 260 (T-Both), 274 (T-Hypo) and 271 (GSS-hypo), replicated in the validation cohort (Fig. 6a ). Because there was substantial overlap between the SZ-DMCs from each of the 3 panels (Fig. 6b , Supplementary Data 1 ), any downstream differences in GSEA would be relatively minor. Indeed, in all 3 cases, we observed enrichment for the steroid biosynthesis pathway, which has been implicated in SZ (SI Fig. S6 , Methods, Supplementary Data 1 ) 41 , 42 . Thus, we focused on those SZ-DMCs exclusively replicated by each of the 3 panels, i.e., a total of 16 (T-Both), 18 (T-Hypo) and 20 (GSS-hypo) SZ-DMCs, respectively (Fig. 6b , Supplementary Data 1 ). Interestingly, in the case of GSS-hypo, the 20 SZ-DMCs were enriched for inflammation-related pathways, whilst this enrichment was absent or less evident for T-Both or T-Hypo (Fig. 6c , Supplementary Data 4 , Supplementary Data 1 ). Given that inflammation is well-known to be associated with SZ 43 , this suggests that the 20 SZ-DMCs identified using GSS-hypo are likely to be true positives. Supporting their biological significance, among the 20 SZ-DMCs, were CpGs mapping to the chemokine CCL18 , consistent with reports of elevated CCL18 chemokine levels in the serum of SZ patients 44 , and to the chemokine receptor gene CXCR5 , a gene implicated in neuroinflammation and schizophrenia 45 , 46 (Fig. 6d , Supplementary Data 4 , Supplementary Data 1 ). Another CpG mapped to the KDM2B gene (Fig. 6d , Supplementary Data 4 , Supplementary Data 1 ), which is known to promote IL-6 production and inflammatory responses through chromatin remodeling 47 . Notably, mutations in KDM2B have also been directly linked to schizophrenia and are associated with neurodevelopmental disorders 48 , 49 . Thus, overall, these results support the view that the GSS-hypo panel improves inference. As a second validation, we analyzed a longitudinal DNAm dataset (Illumina 450k + EPICv1) representing a prospective study of type 1 diabetes (T1D) 50 (Methods). This dataset, which has also been previously analyzed by us 27 , comprises 395 blood samples collected from infants and children at multiple timepoints during the progression of islet autoimmunity (IA) prior to T1D diagnosis. Following a similar analytical approach as the SZ study, we used linear models to identify T1D-associated DMCs, adjusting for confounders including age, sex, timepoint, technical factors (chip and position), and the 12 immune cell fractions derived from either the T-Both, T-Hypo or GSS-Hypo reference. Analysis using T-Both, T-Hypo and GSS-hypo identified 11,264, 10,012 DMCs, and 8020 T1D-DMCs, respectively (FDR &lt; 0.05). To avoid bias and ensure a fair downstream comparison, we restricted the T-Both and T-Hypo lists to their respective top 8020 DMCs (ranked by P -value), subsequently comparing their enrichment for KEGG pathways using missMethyl 51 . This revealed that while all three lists showed highly significant enrichment for the ‘Type I diabetes mellitus’ pathway, the DMC list derived from the GSS-Hypo reference was more enriched for T1D-related genes (Fig. 6e ). Additionally, as T1D is an autoimmune disease, the ‘Antigen processing and presentation’ pathway should also be enriched: notably, GSS-Hypo also displayed the most significant enrichment for this pathway and contained the most genes (Fig. 6e ). Overall, these data indicate that using the GSS-Hypo reference, we can obtain improvements in the enrichment of biologically relevant pathways. Discussion Our study highlights the importance of a long-standing question in omic data science, namely, when selecting features, do we rely on statistics only, or should we also take the magnitude of the effect into account? Generally speaking, using statistics and P -values to select features is of paramount importance. However, once features have been selected based on P -values, a less trivial question is whether significant features should be ranked by the statistic (or P -value) or whether they should be re-ranked according to effect size. The answer to this question largely depends on the biological problem and context. As demonstrated here, in the context of building DNAm reference panels for cell-type deconvolution, where one has to select features that are highly discriminatory of different cell-types, relying only on statistics and P -values to select and rank features is a suboptimal procedure, as it can inadvertently place low-effect size markers at the top of the ranked list, which, if subsequently not removed, can negatively impact downstream cell-type deconvolution. Indeed, our results clearly suggest that these low-effect-size markers are either false positives or lower-quality markers that arise because of the typically low number of sorted cell samples. The number of independent sorted samples is typically small because of the high cost and difficulty of generating such samples for a relatively large number of donors and cell types. Our GSS-strategy, which re-ranks features previously selected by statistics and P -values by their cell-type specific effect size, can efficiently remove these false positive or low-quality markers, improving the quality of the downstream cell-type deconvolution and statistical inference, as demonstrated here, not only in terms of the accuracy of the cell-type deconvolution itself, but also by improving the interpretability of EWAS findings. Interestingly, we found that the IDOL algorithm may not optimize DNAm reference panels. The reduced performance of the IDOL-derived reference panels was true irrespective of whether we re-ranked significant DMCs by the GSS or not, and that performance dropped further when tested in independent datasets. Thus, a likely explanation as to why IDOL may reduce performance is that it overfits to the experimental mixtures used in training due to their small sample size. In support of this, a reduced performance was also seen using other machine learning algorithms such as Elastic Net or Random Forests. Of note, this overfitting happens despite leave-one-out strategies, because the number of experimental mixtures ( n = 12) in the training set is too small. Thus, we envisage that IDOL would be more valuable if it could be trained on a much larger set of experimental mixtures. Another important insight from this work is that building DNAm reference panels using exclusively cell-type-specific hypomethylated DMCs may further improve inference. This is consistent with the observation that the most cell-type-specific CpGs are those that are unmethylated in the given cell type, as indeed cell-type-specific hypermethylated CpGs are relatively infrequent 25 . Thus, including hypermethylated markers may inadvertently increase the number of low-effect-size markers, compromising downstream inference. However, we caution that this likely applies only to adult tissue-types and terminally differentiated cell-types 14 , 25 , because for other tissue-types like cord-blood, the presence of immature and progenitor cell-types may require hypermethylated markers to improve discrimination, as demonstrated recently by us 27 . Indeed, DNAm-atlases like that of Loyfer et al. are focused on adult tissue and cell-types 25 , which may lead to the misleading impression that most cell-type specific markers are always unmethylated in the given cell-type. However, in early development, where DNAm undergoes substantial remodeling, comparing immature and progenitor/stem cell-types to each other, the amount of cell-type specific hypo and hypermethylation is much more comparable. One potential caveat to our GSS strategy is that we do not provide a concrete recommendation as to the number of markers that should enter a DNAm reference panel. This, however, is a complex question, as we now explain. There are 4 important criteria that will determine what a reasonable or optimal number of markers is. First, it is desirable to have a balanced number of markers for each cell type, as this ensures that the inference is not biased to the cell types with more markers. When building a panel, it is a priori not known if this is even possible, and may require using different GSS thresholds per cell-type. Second, the number of markers per cell-type should be large enough so that one can still reliably estimate fractions when, say, a proportion of the markers fail QC in independent datasets. Thus, redundancy in a panel is desirable as it confers robustness. On the other hand, too many markers will eventually also compromise their quality as measured by the GSS. Third, it is important to maximize the fraction of high GSS markers entering the panel (say, &gt;70%), because this circumvents the need for further optimization using machine learning. Indeed, the purpose of any subsequent optimization, say via cross-validation, would be to remove false positives or low-quality markers, yet if the original selection of markers is characterized by high GSS-values (say &gt;0.5), then these are extremely unlikely to be false positives, in which case any optimization may only be counterproductive, specially if the training or optimization is performed on small datasets. Although potential confounders like age, smoking, genotype or ethnicity generally only account for much smaller effect sizes compared to the DNAm differences between cell-types 27 , 52 , their effect may be non-negligible in the context of cross-validations performed on small datasets. To make this point clear, a method like IDOL would be advantageous only if the original marker selection contains a significant number of false positives driven by such confounders, yet as shown here, IDOL would also require a larger training set for this advantage to become apparent. Fourth, the number of markers per cell-type is also a function of the number of cell-types in the panel. The more cell-types we have, the more markers we may need, since distinguishing more similar cell-types from each other (say classical from non-classical monocytes) may require a larger number of lower GSS features. In this regard, it is worth pointing out that even the current highest resolution panels for blood, encompassing 12 immune cell-types, fall short, since the number of biologically or clinically relevant immune cell-types is thought to be around 50 or more 53 . Despite this clear limitation, performing an adjustment at the current resolution of 12 immune cell-types is better than no adjustment at all 54 . In summary, the number of markers per cell-type is a complex function of many factors, and not necessarily amenable to an optimization procedure, especially if the number of training set samples is small. However, based on our experience building and successfully validating DNAm reference panels 7 , 15 , 27 , 29 , 30 , we advise on the order of around 50 GSS &gt; 0.3 markers per cell-type, so ~600 markers for a 12-cell-type panel. Although our GSS-strategy led to improvements in statistical inference in the context of 3 different EWAS analyses (age, SZ, and T1D), we acknowledge that improvements in the case of SZ and T1D were marginal. That improvements are marginal is entirely expected, given that the cell-type deconvolution estimation procedure is relatively robust, as it can tolerate up to 30% of errors in the deconvolution panel 15 . Nevertheless, as future efforts will need to generate DNAm reference matrices for larger numbers of cell-types 18 using, say, sorted or single-cell DNAm data 14 , 19 , 20 , this will require accurate deconvolution for an increasing number of cell-types, which will place more stringent demands on the reliability and quality of a DNAm reference panel. Thus, the insights gained here can provide guidelines to help build future and more accurate DNAm reference panels. To aid the community, we now provide within our EpiDISH R-package 29 , an R-function to build DNAm reference panels from sorted or single-cell DNAm data using our GSS-strategy. In summary, sole reliance on statistics and P-values to select features for building DNAm reference panels can lead to suboptimal cell-type deconvolution, even if followed up with a machine-learning optimization strategy. This is because of the inclusion of low effect size markers, as well as overfitting to the inevitably small datasets comprising independent sorted or single-cell DNAm data. As shown here, the GSS-strategy, which selects a reasonable number of markers with high specificity and large effect size, optimizes cell-type deconvolution tasks and will prove valuable for future efforts to build higher-resolution deconvolution panels. Methods Statistics and reproducibility Many of the analyses in this study involve computation of root mean square errors (RMSE) and Pearson correlation coefficients (PCC) or R -values over mixtures, either experimentally generated ones or in-silico, in both cases, the mixing fractions are known. Robustness was addressed by running the deconvolution for different values of parameters. In the applications to EWAS data, we performed a random effects meta-analysis over 15 independent whole blood cohorts, a statistical procedure that directly addresses reproducibility. In the context of identifying SZ-DMCs, reproducibility was assessed by using two independent cohorts. The DNAm reference library for panel construction Salas et al. Cell mixture deconvolution Cell-mixture deconvolution refers to the process of inferring the cellular composition of a mixed sample, which is typically accomplished using a multivariate regression, either in constrained or unconstrained form 9 , 10 , 22 , 29 . Ridder et al. have demonstrated that the robust partial correlation (RPC) method of EpiDISH is currently among the best-performing algorithms among 16 tested deconvolution algorithms 55 . Therefore, we estimated corresponding cell-type fractions using the EpiDISH 29 , 56 . Specifically, we ran the epidish function with “RPC” as the method and maxit = 500. We also evaluated performance using the Constrained Projection (method = “CP”) and CIBERSORT (method = “CBS”) methods, both available in the same package. For Constrained Projection, we tested both the ‘equality’ and ‘inequality’ constraint parameters (constraint = “equality” and constraint = “inequality”). Sliding window analysis To assess the robustness of the t -statistic and gap specificity score ranking methods, a sliding window analysis was performed. This analysis was based on two distinct N = 1000 reference libraries. The first was the t -statistics library (500 hyper- and 500 hypo-methylated DMCs per cell type), and the second was the GSS library (500 hyper- and 500 hypo-methylated DMCs per cell type). For each of these two 12*1000 libraries, the hyper- and hypo-methylated DMC lists for each cell type were independently sorted in descending order according to their respective metric ( t -statistic or GSS). A sliding window was then applied to each sorted library to generate sequential reference matrices. The window size was set to 50 DMCs, composed of the top 25 hyper- and top 25 hypo-methylated DMCs from the current position. The window was moved with a step size of 10 DMCs (5 hyper- and 5 hypo-methylated DMCs). This process generated 46 sequential reference matrices for each of the two ranking methods, where each subsequent matrix was composed of progressively lower-ranked DMCs. Each of the resulting 92 (46 ×2) reference matrices was used to deconvolve the independent 12-mixture validation dataset using the RPC method. The mean PCC and mean RMSE across all 12 cell types were calculated for each window. Finally, Spearman’s rank correlation was used to quantify the association between the window index (1–46) and the mean deconvolution performance (PCC and RMSE). IDOL algorithm Koestler et al. have provided the source code for the core IDOL function, IDOLoptimize, and the IDOL R package in their GitHub repository https://github.com/immunomethylomics/IDOL 23 . As performed by Salas et al. 10 , we first used the legacy method to construct a feature pool containing 1,000 DMCs for each cell type using the sorted immune cell types from GSE167998 , then optimized them using the experimental mixtures from GSE167998 , and subsequently conducted independent validation using the experimental mixtures from GSE182379 . The library size parameter, libSize = 1200, was determined as the optimal size by Salas et al. based on testing ranging from 250 to 3000 CpGs 10 . Elastic net marker selection As a primary linear benchmark, we trained an Elastic Net (ELN) model using the 12 purified cell types from the GSE167998 dataset as the training set. To ensure a direct and fair comparison, the analysis was restricted to the identical pre-screened DMC feature pool (CpGs) also used by the IDOL method. We implemented a multinomial logistic regression model using the glmnet R package (family = “multinomial”) 32 , treating the 12 sorted immune cell types as the categorical response variable. Hyperparameter optimization was conducted to select both the elastic net mixing parameter ( α ) and the regularization strength ( λ ). We performed a grid search for α (testing values from 0.1 to 1.0 in 0.1 increments). For each α value in the grid, the optimal λ was determined using threefold cross-validation via the cv.glmnet function. This two-dimensional tuning process identified a global optimal α of 0.9. For this final model, we selected the lambda.min value, which represents the model with the minimum cross-validated error. Finally, we extracted all DMCs ( N = 348) that had non-zero coefficients from this optimized model to construct the final ELN-derived reference matrix. Random Forest marker selection To provide a robust non-linear benchmark, we implemented a Random Forest (RF) classifier 33 . To ensure a direct and fair comparison, this analysis utilized the same pre-screened DMC feature pool that was used for both the Elastic Net benchmark and as the input for IDOL. The analysis leveraged the ranger R package, which is optimized for high-dimensional data 57 . Hyperparameter tuning for the model was conducted using the caret R package 58 . We focused on optimizing the key hyperparameter, mtry, which defines the number of variables randomly sampled as candidates at each split. A pre-defined grid of mtry values was evaluated using threefold cross-validation. After identifying the optimal mtry value, a final native multiclass classification model was trained on the entire training dataset using the ranger package. This model was built using 500 trees and fixed the mtry parameter to the optimal value determined during tuning. The model treated the 12 sorted immune cell types as the categorical response variable. For this final model, feature importance was quantified using the Mean Decrease in Accuracy (MDA), calculated via permutation on the out-of-bag (OOB) samples. This process yielded a single, global ranking of all DMCs based on their contribution to the multiclass model’s accuracy. From this list, we selected the top 1200 features to construct the final RF-derived reference matrix. Meta-analyses In each cohort, we standardized the fractions of each cell type to unit variance. We then assessed associations between the standardized cell type fractions and chronological age using a multivariate linear regression. For specific cohorts where additional covariates were available, such as gender, disease and smoking status, the multivariate regression models included these factors as covariates. The specific covariates adjusted for in each of the 15 cohorts are detailed in Supplementary Data 3 . For each regression, we extracted the corresponding effect size, standard error, Student’s t test, and P -value. We then performed a fixed and random effect inverse variance meta-analysis using the metagen function implemented in the meta R-package (version 7.0-0) 71 . Analysis of the schizophrenia (SZ) EWAS datasets For the Epigenome-Wide Association Study (EWAS) of Schizophrenia (SZ), we utilized the Hannon1 ( GSE80417 ) and Hannon2 ( GSE84727 ) 38 , 39 datasets. These Illumina 450k DNAm datasets have previously been normalized by us 30 . Briefly, this processing and normalization involved probe filtering, which included the exclusion of 17,541 SNP-associated probes, 11,458 sex chromosome probes, and 26,569 cross-reactive probes. Only probes with detection P -values &lt; 0.05 across all samples were kept for further analysis. The filtered beta-valued data were subsequently normalized with BMIQ 72 . To systematically investigate major sources of variation within this dataset and identify potential confounding variables, we performed a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) analysis on the normalized beta-value matrix of the Hannon1 and Hannon2 datasets. The matrices were first mean-centered by subtracting the row-wise mean from each CpG. We then estimated the number of significant singular vectors using Random Matrix Theory, as implemented in the EstDimRMT function from the isva R package 73 . SVD was subsequently performed using the base svd() function in R. To identify which factors contributed to the principal axes of variation, we tested for associations between the top singular vectors (or PCs) and the recorded sample covariates. Specifically, linear regression (via the lm function) was used to assess the association between each SV (PC) and continuous variables (e.g., age, cell-type fractions), while analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used for categorical variables (e.g., disease status, sex, SentrixID, and SentrixPos). To identify DMCs associated with SZ, we employed the limma R package separately in the discovery (Hannon1) and validation (Hannon2) cohorts 74 . For each cohort, a design matrix was constructed modeling disease status while adjusting for age, sex, and the 12 estimated immune cell-type fractions. We fitted this model to the methylation data for every CpG site using the lmFit function. Subsequently, empirical Bayes moderation was applied using the eBayes function to compute moderated t-statistics, log-fold changes, and P -values. DMCs were identified in the discovery cohort (Hannon1) at an FDR &lt; 0.05 (derived from the eBayes P -values) and subsequently carried forward for replication in the validation cohort (Hannon2), with significance defined as a nominal P -value &lt; 0.05. Following the DMC analysis, we performed pathway enrichment analysis for KEGG pathways using the gometh function from the missMethyl R package 51 . Analysis of type-1 diabetes DNAm dataset DAISY study 50 : The Illumina DNAm dataset was downloaded from the GEO data repository (GEO accession GSE142512 ). This DNAm dataset was processed and normalized as described by us previously 27 , and consists of 395 samples from 174 individuals. The ages of the individuals range from 6 months to 22 years, with the majority being under 10 years old. A total of 211 samples were profiled using the EPICv1 array, and 184 samples were sequenced using the 450k array. Half of the individuals were eventually diagnosed with type 1 diabetes, but all samples were taken before diagnosis. For the T1D EWAS, we also applied the limma pipeline (lmFit and eBayes), fitting a multiple linear model that adjusted for multiple confounders, including age, sex, sample timepoint, technical factors such as chip and position, and the 12 estimated immune cell fractions, to identify T1D-associated DMCs. DMCs were considered significant at an FDR &lt; 0.05. Following the DMC analysis, we performed pathway enrichment analysis for KEGG pathways using the gometh function from the missMethyl R package 51 .
