Abstract The surge in high‐throughput technologies has empowered the acquisition of vast genomic datasets, prompting the search for genetic markers and biomarkers relevant to complex traits. However, grappling with the inherent complexities of high dimensionality and sparsity within these datasets poses formidable hurdles. The immense number of features and their potential redundancy demand efficient strategies for extracting pertinent information and identifying significant markers. Feature selection is important in large genomic data as it helps in enhancing interpretability and computational efficiency. This study focuses on addressing these challenges through a comprehensive investigation into genomic feature selection methodologies, employing a rich soybean ( Glycine max L. Merr.) dataset comprising 966 lines with over 5.5 million single nucleotide polymorphisms. Emphasizing the “ small n large p ” dilemma prevalent in contemporary genomic studies, we compared the efficacy of traditional genome‐wide association studies (GWAS) with two prominent machine learning tools, random forest and extreme gradient boosting, in pinpointing predictive features. Utilizing the expansive soybean dataset, we assessed the performance of these methodologies in selecting features that optimize predictive modeling for various phenotypes. By constructing predictive models based on the selected features, we ascertain the comparative prediction accuracies, thereby illuminating the strengths and limitations of these feature selection methodologies in the realm of genomic data analysis. Core Ideas High‐throughput genomic technologies produce vast datasets that reduce the efficiency of trait association. Genomic feature selection can increase the accuracy and computational efficiency of trait association. Our comparison of feature selection approaches can help readers optimize feature selection and trait association. Abbreviations GBLUP genomic best linear unbiased prediction GEBV genomic estimated breeding values GRM genomic relationship matrix GWAS genome‐wide association studies RF random forest SNP single nucleotide polymorphism SVM support vector machine XGBoost extreme gradient boosting 1 INTRODUCTION The advent of high‐throughput technologies has facilitated the acquisition of extensive genomic data in plants, holding promise for the discovery of genetic markers and biomarkers related to complex plant traits (Marsh et al., 2021 ; Sun et al., 2022 ). Genetic markers have significantly enhanced the efficiency and precision of crop improvement programs through marker‐assisted selection (MAS) (Collard &amp; Mackill, 2008 ). The discovery of quantitative trait loci (QTLs) has enabled the validation of candidate genes associated with specific traits and the application of MAS in crop breeding, marking a pivotal advancement in agricultural genomics (Kushanov et al., 2021 ). However, the analysis of high‐throughput genomic data poses significant challenges due to the high dimensionality and sparsity of the data, which can lead to overfitting, decreased model interpretability, spurious associations, and computational load difficulties (Singh et al., 2015 ). High dimensionality refers to the large number of variables or features in the dataset, which can make it computationally challenging and time consuming (Pires &amp; Branco, 2019 ). For example, the estimated size of the bread wheat ( Triticum aestivum L.) genome is around 15.5 billion base pairs (Aury et al., 2022 ), and high‐throughput sequencing technologies can generate millions of data points per sample, resulting in datasets with millions of features. Furthermore, many of these features may be redundant or irrelevant, which can make it difficult to extract meaningful information and identify important genetic markers or biomarkers. As a result, dimensionality reduction techniques, such as feature selection or feature extraction, are necessary to reduce the number of variables and improve the efficiency and interpretability of the analysis (Meng et al., 2016 ; Ray et al., 2021 ). Feature selection is an essential step in genomic data analysis. This is because genomic datasets often contain a vast number of variables or features, many of which may not contribute significantly to the prediction or classification of phenotypes of interest. Also, this can lead to computational challenges when using machine learning models. By selecting a subset of informative features, we can reduce the noise and the dimensionality of the dataset and improve the performance of machine learning models, while also gaining insights into the underlying biological processes that drive the observed data patterns. Moreover, feature selection plays a pivotal role in identifying potential biomarkers associated with economically valuable plant traits, enhancing the accuracy of trait prediction for crop yield (Fei et al., 2022 ), disease resistance (van Esse et al., 2020 ), and nutritional quality (Tong &amp; Nikoloski, 2021 ). As such, the selection of variables stands as an important stage in the analysis of genomic plant data, necessitating the adoption of suitable feature selection techniques to ensure precise and meaningful outcomes in agriculture and economic productivity. Besides the high dimensionality, sparsity is another common challenge in genomic datasets. Sparsity refers to the fact that many features in genomic datasets have a small or negligible effect on the outcome of interest. For example, in gene expression datasets, most genes are not differentially expressed between groups or conditions of interest. Sparse data can make it difficult to identify meaningful patterns or relationships between features and outcomes, as the noise‐to‐signal ratio can be high (Atla et al., 2011 ; Gupta &amp; Gupta, 2019 ). Additionally, sparsity can lead to model instability, making it challenging to generalize the results to new datasets or populations. Feature selection is a critical step in addressing the challenges of high dimensionality and sparsity in genomic data analysis and it can help to identify the genetic variants and pathways that are most relevant to a particular trait, disease, or biological process. Feature selection is an important step in the process of discovering QTLs, as it helps in identifying the most relevant genetic markers that are associated with the traits of interest (Hoff et al., 2019 ). In the context of genomic selection (GS), the application of feature selection has shown promising results in enhancing predictive performance. Montesinos‐Lopez et al. ( 2023 ) demonstrated this in their study involving six real datasets, where they found that performing variable selection significantly improved prediction accuracy in four out of the six datasets. This finding confirms the empirical evidence that feature selection can substantially enhance prediction accuracy. Machine learning methods have emerged as a powerful tool for feature selection in large genomic datasets. It not only allows researchers to identify relevant features and improve the performance of predictive models, but it also helps to interpret their biological relevance in the context of the disease or process of interest (Aono et al., 2020 ; Gill et al., 2022 ). Many existing studies on feature selection for genomic data have limitations, either working with a small number of single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) markers, which does not reflect the modern genomic data scenarios where millions of SNPs are available for each sample or by analyzing a limited number of samples. Additionally, much of the research has been confined to either human or animal datasets. For instance, Bermingham et al. ( 2015 ) assessed the impact of five feature selection methods on prediction using genotype data from 2186 Croatian and 810 UK individuals, housing 263,357 and 260,562 SNPs, respectively. B. Li et al. ( 2018 ) leveraged 38,082 SNP markers from 2093 Brahman cattle to evaluate the predictive potential of SNPs selected by random forest (RF), gradient boosting, and extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost). Another study by Y. Li et al. ( 2022 ) delved into feature selection for multi‐omics cancer data but was restricted to just over 100 individuals. In a study by Aono et al. ( 2020 ), a full‐sib progeny made up of 219 individuals resulting from a cross between elite sugarcane ( Saccharum officinarum L.) varieties was utilized. This progeny was characterized by 14,540 SNP markers. The research focused on the selection of features for sugarcane brown rust resistance. Through the testing of five distinct feature selection methods, the team achieved a significant improvement in prediction accuracy. Specifically, they elevated the accuracy from 50% to an impressive 95% using a mere 131 SNP markers, a stark improvement over using the entire set of 14,540 SNPs. Meanwhile, a more recent study by Xiang et al. ( 2023 ) compared the feature selection efficacy among various machine learning algorithms, namely, RF, support vector machine (SVM), XGBoost, and convolutional neural network. This comparison was conducted using two distinct pig datasets. The authors utilized pig data genotyped on 42k SNP chips and encompassed seven different pig phenotypes. The sample sizes ranged from 2691 to 6211 individuals, depending on the trait under investigation. The study revealed that feature selection techniques could reduce the number of genomic markers to 1 in 20, with some traits even exhibiting improved predictive performance compared to utilizing the entire genome dataset. Core Ideas High‐throughput genomic technologies produce vast datasets that reduce the efficiency of trait association. Genomic feature selection can increase the accuracy and computational efficiency of trait association. Our comparison of feature selection approaches can help readers optimize feature selection and trait association. While feature selection can significantly enhance prediction accuracy in genomic prediction models, several limiting factors must be considered before applying any feature selection method. First, the sample size needs to be sufficiently large to identify features that will generalize well to unseen data. If the training sample size is too small, there may be selection bias, leading to features that do not perform well in predicting new data. Additionally, a potential issue arises when researchers select a discriminative subset of features using the entire dataset and then estimate the prediction model's accuracy with this subset. This approach tests the discrimination rule on samples that were already used to determine the final subset of features, which can lead to an overestimation of the model's performance (Saeys et al., 2007 ). More importantly, the benefits of feature selection in genomic selection may diminish over time due to various factors. Changes in allele frequencies, driven by selection pressures and genetic drift, can alter the relevance of initially selected features. Recombination events, which occur during meiosis, can break up linked genetic markers, reducing the effectiveness of feature sets that rely on these linkages. Additionally, the inherent instability of the selected features, especially those with smaller effect sizes, can lead to a decline in prediction accuracy as the population evolves. In our research, we explore further genomic feature selection, comparing the predictive accuracies of features identified by genome‐wide association studies (GWAS) with those chosen by two leading machine learning tools, namely, RF (Breiman, 2001 ) and XGBoost (Chen &amp; Guestrin, 2016 ). We harnessed a comprehensive soybean dataset encompassing 966 lines with over 5.5 million SNPs (Bayer et al., 2022 ). This extensive dataset offers a prime opportunity to scrutinize contemporary challenges faced in genomic studies, notably the challenge of dealing with massive feature spaces with a limited sample count (often termed as “ small n large p ” dilemmas). Our article begins by offering a comprehensive overview of the various methodologies employed for genomic feature selection, emphasizing machine learning techniques. Subsequently, using genuine soybean data, we venture into an in‐depth analysis, evaluating how well different methods choose important features. By building predictive models based on the selected features, we were able to juxtapose the prediction accuracies for diverse phenotypes, thereby shedding light on the relative power of these feature selection methodologies. Finally, we utilized simulated data to investigate the impact of feature selection over time across future breeding cycles. 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS 2.1 Genomic data and phenotypes The genotype data for this study were sourced from a pool of 1110 soybean lines from the USDA Soybean Germplasm Collection, as previously examined in the soybean pangenome study conducted by Bayer and colleagues in 2022 (Bayer et al., 2022 ). Briefly, raw sequencing reads underwent a cleaning process where adapter sequences were removed using Trimmomatic v0.36 (Bolger et al., 2014 ). Following data cleaning, the resulting clean reads were aligned to the reference genome Wm82.a4.v1 (Valliyodan et al., 2019 ) using BWA‐MEM v0.7.17 with default settings (H. Li, 2013 ). Duplicate reads, which can skew results, were eliminated using Picard tools ( http://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/ ). Read realignment was performed using GATK v3.8–1‐0 RealignerTargetCreator and IndelRealigner. Subsequently, genetic variants were identified using GATK v3.8–1‐0 (McKenna et al., 2010 ) HaplotypeCaller. Indels and SNPs located on unassembled scaffolds were separated into distinct files to facilitate subsequent analysis. SNPs failing to meet quality criteria (quality by depth &lt; 2.0 || mapping quality &lt; 40.0 || Fisher strand bias &gt; 60.0 || overall quality score &lt; 60.0 || mapping quality rank sum &lt; −12.5|| read position rank sum &lt; −8.0) were removed. To streamline the dataset and focus on informative genetic variations, SNP loci with over 10% missing data were filtered out using VCFTools v3.0 (Danecek et al., 2011 ). Missing genotypes were inferred, and phasing was conducted using BEAGLE v5.1 (Browning &amp; Browning, 2007 ; Browning et al., 2018 ). Data about seed oil content, protein content, and seed weight phenotypes were acquired by downloading information from the USDA‐GRIN database ( https://npgsweb.ars‐grin.gov/ ) for those accessions that possessed accessible observational records. 2.2 A general overview of feature selection methods Feature selection methods for genomic prediction can be grouped into three main classes including filter methods, wrapper methods, and embedded methods (Pudjihartono et al., 2022 ). Filter methods utilize statistical measures and tests to rank and select the most relevant genomic features, such as genes or genetic markers, based on their associations with specific biological outcomes or phenotypes. By identifying features that exhibit strong correlations or statistical significance with the target variable, filter methods reduce the dimensionality of genomic data while retaining biologically meaningful information. It evaluates individual feature‐outcome relationships using tests such as chi‐squared and t ‐tests but may miss redundancy and interactions. For example, in gene expression analysis, filter methods can identify genes that are differentially expressed between experimental conditions or disease states, aiding in the discovery of potential biomarkers or key regulatory genes. One study demonstrating the application of filter methods in genomics is the research by Al‐Shahrour et al. ( 2004 ), which employed statistical techniques for feature selection in gene expression data analysis. Filter methods are model independent, avoiding bias and overfitting, but may result in a more generalized feature set and potentially lower predictive performance compared to wrapper or embedded methods. Multivariate techniques such as LASSO, Ridge, and Elastic Net Regression consider multiple features simultaneously. Several studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of LASSO regression in genomic data analysis, including the identification of biomarkers for cancer prognosis and prediction (Bovelstad et al., 2007 ; Tibshirani, 1997 ). Furthermore, LASSO regression has been shown to outperform other feature selection techniques, including principal component analysis and univariate feature selection, in terms of both predictive accuracy and model interpretability (Goeman &amp; Bühlmann, 2007 ; Ogutu et al., 2012 ). However, LASSO methods might be computationally demanding based on the size of the datasets (Bovelstad et al., 2007 ). Wrapper methods for feature selection rely on a chosen model's performance to select the most relevant feature subset, offering higher predictive power compared to filtering methods. They employ heuristic search strategies, such as recursive feature elimination or forward selection, to efficiently explore feature combinations while considering feature interactions. However, wrapper methods are computationally intensive (Y. Li et al., 2022 ), making them less suitable for high‐dimensional data. Additionally, their effectiveness depends on the specific model chosen, and the selected feature subset may not perform well with different models. Despite these limitations, studies such as Choi et al. ( 2011 ), Guyon et al. ( 2002 ), and Maldonado and Weber ( 2009 ) have demonstrated the effectiveness of wrapper methods in improving classification accuracy, making them a valuable tool for feature selection when optimizing model performance is a priority. Embedded methods seamlessly integrate feature selection into prediction or classifier models. During training, the model optimizes its internal parameters to weigh features and achieve accurate outcomes. This approach combines feature subset search and model construction. Examples include decision tree‐based machine‐learning algorithms (RF and XGBoost) and regularization models such as LASSO. Embedded methods balance features from filter and wrapper methods, offering computational efficiency and classifier interaction benefits. Several studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of LASSO in genomic data analysis, including the identification of biomarkers for cancer prognosis and prediction (Bovelstad et al., 2007 ; Tibshirani, 1997 ). Furthermore, LASSO regression has been shown to outperform other feature selection techniques, including principal component analysis and univariate feature selection, in terms of both predictive accuracy and model interpretability (Goeman &amp; Bühlmann, 2007 ; Ogutu et al., 2012 ). However, LASSO might be computationally demanding based on the size of the datasets (Bovelstad et al., 2007 ). Some embedded methods support feature interactions, particularly the decision tree‐based approaches RF and XGBoost. Díaz‐Uriarte and de Andrés ( 2006 ) described how the RF algorithm can be efficiently used for gene selection and classification of microarray data. In a study with Brahman cattle genomic data for genomic breeding value prediction, B. Li et al. ( 2018 ) found that RF and gradient boosting machine proved effective in selecting SNPs linked to growth‐related genes, with their top 3000 SNPs showing similar genomic prediction accuracy to using the entire SNP set and outperformed evenly spaced SNP subsets and XGBoost in terms of accuracy. More recently, Y. Li et al. ( 2022 ) found that the permutation importance of RF delivered strong predictive performance when considering only a few selected features. In the next sub‐section, we will provide a brief overview of the RF and XGBoost algorithms. 2.2.1 Random forest Leo Breiman first proposed RF algorithms, which are a type of ensemble learning method (Breiman, 2001 ). Ensemble learning combines multiple models or algorithms to improve prediction or classification performance beyond what could be achieved with individual models. In RF, multiple decision trees are generated based on m random samples selected by bootstrap sampling, where replacement is allowed. To reduce the correlation between features, only a random subset of features is considered at each node of the tree‐building process. For classification problems, majority voting is used to predict the class of the validation data, while for regression problems, the mean of the individual tree's prediction is used as the output of the model. The RF algorithm has two key parameters, the number of decision trees ( num.trees ) and the number of randomly selected features ( mtry ) used to determine the best split at each node. The attraction of the model is based on the following several attributes: a high degree of accuracy; a resistance to overfitting, including in the presence of large numbers of redundant variables; the provision of an out‐of‐bag error measure; and the provision of a variable ranking at a little extra computational cost. 2.2.2 Gradient boosting Gradient boosting are machine learning algorithms that combine multiple weak learning models to create a strong predictor. It works by training a sequence of decision trees, where each new tree attempts to correct the errors made by the previous tree. In other words, the algorithm focuses on the data points that were misclassified by the previous tree and tries to classify them correctly in the next tree. The algorithm uses a loss function to calculate the difference between the predicted and actual values and then updates the model by minimizing this loss function using gradient descent. By iterating this process, the algorithm gradually improves the accuracy of the model until the desired performance is achieved. XGBoost is an advanced implementation of the gradient boosting algorithm that uses a more regularized model formalization to control overfitting and improve generalization performance. XGBoost provides a built‐in feature importance ranking that can be used to identify the most relevant features. The algorithm calculates the contribution of each feature in the model based on the number of times it is used to split the data across all trees in the ensemble. The feature importance ranking can be used to select a subset of the most important features for training the model. This can be done by setting a threshold on the importance scores and selecting only the features that exceed the threshold. Alternatively, one can train multiple models using different subsets of features and compare their performance to select the best subset of features. 2.3 Experimental details We adopted two distinct approaches to assess prediction accuracies across GWAS, RF, and XGBoost‐selected SNPs. In the initial approach, we conducted SNP data filtration solely based on minor allele frequency (MAF), and subsequently, these MAF‐filtered datasets were employed for feature ranking in GWAS, RF, and XGBoost. In our second approach, we applied filtration based on MAF and linkage disequilibrium (LD) (at different levels r 2 &gt; 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7) to the available SNP data prior to utilizing them in GWAS, RF, and XGBoost for feature ranking. Furthermore, using simulation data, we examined how the features selected by these three methods hold up across different future generations. We used PLINK 1.9 (Chang et al., 2015 ; Purcell et al., 2007 ) to perform GWAS and LD pruning. To calculate associations between individual SNPs and phenotypes, we utilized the PLINK ‘–assoc’ command. Afterward, we arranged the SNPs according to their p ‐values, assigning higher ranks to those with lower p ‐values as obtained from the PLINK output. For LD pruning, we employed the ‘–indep‐pairwise’ command with specific parameters: 100, 10, and 0.3, 0.5, or 0.7. This LD pruning process entailed creating SNP windows of size 100 that did not overlap, shifting the window by 10 SNPs at each iteration, and subsequently removing one SNP from each pair that exceeded pairwise correlation thresholds of 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7, respectively. To get the feature importance (SNPs ranking) from RF and XGBoost, we used the R packages ranger and xgboost, respectively. ‘ranger’ (Wright &amp; Ziegler, 2017 ) is a high‐performance, scalable, and parallelized implementation for the RF algorithm for classification and regression. We used the importance =‘impurity’ mode in the ranger package to get the feature ranking. The ‘impurity’ metric corresponds to the Gini index in classification tasks and the variance of the responses in regression scenarios. The R package ‘xgboost’ (Chen &amp; Guestrin, 2016 ) offers a scalable tree boosting system that not only provides prediction models but also facilitates feature ranking based on model performance. In our analysis, we employed the “Gain” score from the feature importance output of the xgboost model to rank SNPs. The “Gain” score serves as a metric of each feature's relative contribution to the model's predictive accuracy, quantifying the enhancement in accuracy or reduction in loss achieved by including a specific feature when making decisions at each split within the XGBoost ensemble's decision trees. In our analysis, we applied MTG2 (Lee &amp; van der Werf, 2016 ) for genomic prediction. Following SNP ranking through GWAS, RF, or XGBoost, MTG2 was utilized to compute genomic estimated breeding values (GEBV). For each specific set of selected SNPs, we initially computed the genomic relationship matrix (GRM) using PLINK. Subsequently, this GRM, in conjunction with phenotype data, was employed to estimate breeding values within the MTG2 framework. We conducted a comparative analysis of SNP selection methods, including GWAS, RF, and XGBoost, using a 10‐fold cross‐validation approach. Initially, the available genotype and phenotype data were partitioned into 10 equal folds. Onefold served as the validation set, while the remaining data were utilized for ranking SNPs through GWAS, RF, or XGBoost. These SNP rankings were then employed to create diverse SNP subsets for GEBV prediction using MTG2 on the validation set. This process was repeated 10 times for each fold, and GEBV prediction accuracy was assessed using the Pearson correlation coefficient between predicted GEBV and actual phenotypes. Ultimately, we averaged the prediction accuracies across the 10 folds to determine the final prediction accuracy for SNP subsets selected by GWAS, RF, and XGBoost. 3 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION In this section, we present the outcomes of our benchmark study, which aims to evaluate the predictive accuracy of selected SNPs using three distinct methods: GWAS, RF, and XGBoost. The results of our study reveal a nuanced relationship between the number of SNPs and predictive accuracy. While increasing the number of SNPs leads to improved predictions for oil content, protein content, and seed weight phenotypes, this enhancement is not indefinite. The phenomenon of reaching a plateau in prediction accuracy indicates that there is an optimal point at which additional SNPs do not significantly contribute to model performance. These findings have important implications for the design of genetic and genomic studies. Researchers should carefully consider the balance between dataset size and predictive accuracy. Collecting an extensive number of SNPs is valuable, but it may not always lead to substantial improvements in predictive power. Consequently, selecting an optimal subset of informative SNPs can be a more efficient approach. 3.1.2 Prediction accuracy for SNPs selected by RF variable importance We performed a 10‐fold RF analysis for comparison with the 10‐fold GWAS described in the previous section. We used the same 80% of individuals as the previous section for running 10‐fold RF and then selected the top‐ranked n SNPs from the output of variable importance produced by the RF models. When we examined the variable importance scores produced by the RF models, we found that the variable importance scores are zero beyond 250,000 SNPs for almost all folds. For this reason, we used only SNPs up to 250,000 for genomic prediction. For each of the phenotypic traits, we evaluated the predictive accuracy while varying the number of SNPs. Beyond this point, the accuracy remained stable at 0.80 with higher SNP numbers. The protein content trait demonstrated a similar pattern. Starting at 0.43 with 100 SNPs, the accuracy increased to 0.56 with 100,000 SNPs, after which it plateaued. For the seed weight phenotype, the prediction accuracy began at 0.69 with 100 SNPs and steadily climbed to reach a plateau at 0.84 with 5000 or more SNPs. These observations align with the trends observed in GWAS‐selected SNPs, demonstrating that an optimal number of SNPs significantly enhances predictive accuracy for all three phenotypic traits. Beyond this optimal threshold, the addition of further SNPs does not yield significant improvements in predictive power. However, several notable distinctions exist between these two approaches. Primarily, the utilization of RF variable importance‐selected SNPs allows us to attain comparable levels of accuracy as GWAS but with a significantly reduced number of SNPs. When employing RF feature selection, the peak accuracy for oil content, protein content, and seed weight stood at 0.80, 0.56, and 0.84, and was achieved with just 10,000, 50,000, and 2,000 SNPs, respectively. In contrast, with GWAS, the peak accuracies were 0.79, 0.55, and 0.84, and these were achieved using 1,200,000, 800,000, and 1,400,000 SNPs, respectively. The second distinctive feature lies in the post‐peak accuracy behavior. In RF, there was a slight decrease in accuracy after reaching the peak before it eventually plateaued. In contrast, in GWAS, the prediction accuracies remained stable once the peak was attained. The difference in the number of SNPs required to attain similar levels of accuracy between GWAS‐selected SNPs and those chosen based on RF variable importance can be elucidated by multiple factors. Since we applied minimal filtration to the genotype data, except for MAF, the dataset contains numerous correlated SNPs. In GWAS, which concentrates on individual SNP‐trait associations, these correlated SNPs tend to rank similarly. Consequently, when selecting a subset of SNPs from the top‐ranked GWAS list, many of these SNPs may be correlated and point to the same QTL region. Consequently, choosing a limited number of top‐ranked GWAS SNPs may not fully capture the entirety of genomic variation within the dataset. As the number of SNPs increases, the dataset's variation is more comprehensively captured, leading to improved prediction accuracy. In contrast, RF employs a distinct feature importance metric based on the reduction of impurity in decision tree nodes. This metric identifies SNPs that wield a substantial influence on the model's performance. RF prioritizes SNPs that make the most significant contributions to predictive power, resulting in a more concise and informative subset. Moreover, RF is designed to detect interactions between SNPs and other features (Wright et al., 2016 ), enabling it to identify combinations of SNPs that collectively exert a potent influence on predictions. This capacity allows RF to achieve comparable accuracy with a smaller subset of SNPs. 3.1.3 Prediction accuracy for SNPs selected by XGBoost feature importance In alignment with our previous methods for GWAS and RF feature selection, we performed a 10‐fold XGBoost analysis on the genotype data to assess feature importance. We maintained consistency by utilizing the same set of individuals for training the XGBoost models, which enabled us to determine the top‐ranked SNPs. Subsequently, we executed genomic prediction on the remaining 20% of the data. It is important to note that XGBoost models generate feature importance scores exclusively for the features employed in constructing the decision trees. Consequently, the quantity of SNPs for which we obtained feature importance scores via XGBoost was limited. In our comprehensive 10‐fold XGBoost analysis, the count of SNPs featuring these importance scores ranged between 2200 and 2300. Due to this constraint, we opted to incorporate only those SNPs that were ranked within the top 2200 by XGBoost's feature ranking methodology for our genomic prediction benchmark. Given that our starting dataset has over 5.5 million SNPs, many of them are closely interrelated due to high LD. Such strong interrelations between features can pose challenges as they might influence the predictive performance of models, though the impact varies across different model types. After applying the chosen LD threshold to prune the initial dataset, we fed the pruned SNPs into the RF, XGBoost, or GWAS methods for ranking. The observed trends indicate that while RF is effective for short‐term predictions, the stability of feature importance over generations is a critical factor in maintaining high prediction accuracies. The rapid degradation of XGBoost‐selected features highlights the need for models that can identify a larger set of stable features to ensure long‐term predictive power. These findings underscore the importance of continuously updating feature selection strategies to maintain robust prediction accuracies in breeding programs. Future research should focus on developing methods that combine the strengths of different feature selection techniques to enhance the robustness and longevity of genomic predictions across multiple generations. 4 CONCLUSIONS Using a large soybean dataset, we compared the prediction accuracies of features selected by GWAS against two machine learning approaches: RF and XGBoost. We found that GWAS performance could be compromised by high LD in the feature sets, while machine learning‐based feature selection methods were less affected by LD. Therefore, LD pruning emerged as a crucial step in feature selection for GWAS. Among the two machine learning methods, RF proved to be more robust than XGBoost. XGBoost tends to produce a very small number of features, which perform well in the current generation but may perform poorly in future generations. Using simulated data, we further explored the effect of feature selection over future breeding cycles. We found that, over successive generations, the genomic landscape changes, and features that were once predictive may no longer capture genetic variation effectively. Consequently, it is crucial to continuously update and reassess feature selection strategies to maintain robust prediction accuracies in breeding programs. Future research should focus on developing adaptive methods that account for these changes and enhance the long‐term utility of genomic predictions. AUTHOR CONTRIBUTIONS Hawlader A.
