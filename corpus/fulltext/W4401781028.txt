Abstract Grassland conservation planning often focuses on high‐risk landscapes, but many grassland conversion models are not designed to optimize conservation planning because they lack multidimensional risk assessments and are misaligned with ecological and conservation delivery scales. To aid grassland conservation planning, we developed landscape‐scale models at relevant scales that predict future (2021–2031) total and proportional loss of unprotected grassland to cropland or development. We developed models for 20 ecoregions across the contiguous United States by relating past conversion (2011–2021) to a suite of covariates in random forest regression models and applying the models to contemporary covariates to predict future loss. Overall, grassland loss models performed well, and explanatory power varied spatially across ecoregions (total loss model: weighted group mean R 2 = 0.89 [range: 0.83–0.96], root mean squared error [RMSE] = 9.29 ha [range: 2.83–22.77 ha]; proportional loss model: weighted group mean R 2 = 0.74 [range: 0.64–0.87], RMSE = 0.03 [range: 0.02–0.06]). Amount of crop in the landscape and distance to cities, ethanol plants, and concentrated animal feeding operations had high variable importance in both models. Total grass loss was greater when there were moderate amounts of grass, crop, or development (∼50%) in the landscape. Proportional grass loss was greater when there was less grass (∼&lt;30%) and more crop or development (∼&gt;50%). Some variables had a large effect on only a subset of ecoregions, for example, grass loss was greater when ∼&gt;70% of the landscape was enrolled in the Conservation Reserve Program. Our methods provide a simple and flexible approach for developing risk layers well suited for conservation that can be extended globally. Our conversion models can support conservation planning by enabling prioritization as a function of risk that can be further optimized by incorporating biological value and cost. INTRODUCTION Grassland ecosystems have been extensively used to meet human needs for food, fiber, and fuel (Foley et al., 2005 ). As a result, the extent and ecological condition of grasslands have declined, and little is protected relative to other ecosystems (Askins et al., 2007 ; Hoekstra et al., 2004 ). Worldwide, the grassland biome has the largest ratio of converted to protected lands (i.e., 45.8% converted and 10.6% protected) (Hoekstra et al., 2004 ). In the North American Great Plains, this ratio is approximately 5 times greater (62% converted and 1.2% protected) (Comer et al., 2018 ). The leading causes of anthropogenic conversion are tillage agriculture and development (Homer et al., 2020 ). Grasslands are also being degraded by woody encroachment (Homer et al., 2020 ) and other stressors, all of which can have negative effects on wildlife populations and ecosystem services (Foley et al., 2005 ; Lark et al., 2020 ; Rosenberg et al., 2019 ). The imperiled state of grassland ecosystems coupled with pressure from a growing human population (Ray et al., 2013 ) has led to discussions on conservation strategies in the face of limited resources, including return on investment (Withey et al., 2012 ), land sharing versus sparing (Phallan et al., 2011 ), proactive versus reactive conservation (Dobrovolski et al., 2011 ), and vulnerability or irreplaceability assessments (Brooks et al., 2006 ; Wilson et al., 2005 ). The various strategies speak to the intricacies of trying to prioritize conservation actions given socioeconomic, biological, and land‐cover or land‐use dynamics and have bolstered the field of systematic conservation design (Margules &amp; Pressey, 2000 ). Ideally, systematic conservation optimizes the spatial targeting of a specific conservation action (e.g., protection, restoration, enhancement) (Tallis et al., 2021 ) as a function of relevant spatial variables, such as biological value, risk, and cost. Multiple approaches have been used to develop estimates of grassland risk, such as cropland suitability models or models of cropland expansion or grassland conversion. Cropland suitability models are generally based on known climatic, topographic, and edaphic thresholds that hinder cropland suitability (Eitelberg et al., 2015 ) or by developing models that relate cropped and uncropped landscapes to climatic and biophysical conditions at those locations (Olimb &amp; Robinson, 2019 ; Smith et al., 2016 ). Although these models are useful for conservation planning (e.g., Lipsey et al., 2015 ; Smith et al., 2016 ), they generally have a vague temporal resolution and lack important drivers of conversion. Cropland expansion or grassland conversion models incorporate cropland suitability but also include other drivers of conversion, such as socioeconomic factors. Ultimately, they predict where new cropland is likely to be developed in the future. Integrated assessment models predict future cropland extent under different shared socioeconomic pathways (Popp et al., 2017 ; Prestele et al., 2016 ; Wilson et al., 2021 ). These models integrate submodels from various disciplines (e.g., climate science, economics, land‐use change, etc.), are developed at coarse scales (10–55 km), and make predictions relatively far into the future (e.g., 2100). These models are suitable for informing climate change mitigation policy (Wilson et al., 2021 ) but less suitable for local conservation planning and targeting due to their coarse resolution and large prediction uncertainty (Eitelberg et al., 2015 ; Prestele et al., 2016 ; van Asselt &amp; Rotmans, 2002 ). Some regional grassland conversion models have been developed specifically for landscape‐scale conservation planning. These are often developed at finer spatial resolutions for relatively shorter periods at relevant ecological and conservation delivery scales. For example, Stephens et al. ( 2008 ) related a binary indicator of conversion (i.e., landscape with &lt;35% grass) to landscape‐scale estimates of land‐cover and protection status, topographic and climatic variables, and crop price in a logistic regression model. Conversion probabilities were overlaid with models of biological value (i.e., duck pair density) (Reynolds et al., 2006 ) to aid conservation planning. Niemuth et al. ( 2022 ) modeled grassland conversion by identifying and summarizing the total amount and proportion of grassland pixels converted to crop (2001–2016) at a landscape scale and related these metrics to landscape‐scale summaries of 2001 grass amount in generalized additive models. Results from this simple analysis demonstrated the utility of including landscape context in the models as a representation of the economic margin to predict which landscapes are at the greatest risk of conversion based on 2 loss metrics. Risk layers are best suited for conservation planning when developed at ecologically relevant scales that also align with the scale of conservation delivery. This is almost invariably less than a 0.5‐degree pixel (∼55 km), which is a common resolution for coarse‐scale conversion predictions (Oakleaf et al., 2015 ; Prestele et al., 2016 ). In addition, model predictions are most compatible with conservation planning if they can express multiple dimensions of a threatening process to better define landscape vulnerability and the payoff of conservation actions (Tallis et al., 2021 ; Wilson et al., 2005 ). For these reasons, we developed a landscape‐scale approach based on fine‐resolution land‐cover data to estimate grassland conversion to cropland or development in the near future at a scale that is ecologically and socially relevant. We applied the approach to 2 conversion metrics, total and proportional grass loss, which can be used to define vulnerable landscapes that align with conservation goals (Appendix S1 ). This approach includes mechanistic drivers of grassland conversion and limits the uncertainty that may exist with long‐term predictions of conversion given uncertainty in various climatic or socioeconomic drivers. These short‐term predictions also align with conservation funding cycles that target high‐risk landscapes (e.g., US Farm Bill programs). The landscape scale we used aligns with species distribution models used to guide conservation in the Great Plains (Fink et al., 2019 ; Niemuth et al., 2017 ; Reynolds et al., 2006 ) and conservation programs that protect land parcels rather than individual data pixels. We provided an example of how the grassland conversion models can be paired with publicly available spatial data on cost and biological value to target landscapes that meet conservation goals under a systematic conservation framework. METHODS Response variables We derived grassland loss data from the National Land Cover Database (NLCD) (Dewitz &amp; USGS, 2021 ; Yang et al., 2018 ) by identifying unprotected grassland pixels in 2011 that were converted to cropland or development in 2021 and then summarized their conversion at a landscape scale with a moving window analysis (ESRI, 2023 ). We processed all NLCD and protected lands data in Environmental Systems Research Institute ArcGIS (ESRI, 2020 ). We defined grass as a combination of NLCD's grass or herb, pasture or hay, shrub, and emergent herbaceous wetland cover classes. We included emergent herbaceous wetlands and shrubs because these are components of grassland systems also at risk of conversion and used by many species (Homer et al., 2020 ; Lark et al., 2015 , 2020 ; Niemuth et al., 2017 ; Wright &amp; Wimberly, 2013 ). We identified unprotected grass pixels based on whether they were outside protected areas designated GAP status code 1–3 in the Protected Area Database of the United States 2.1 (PAD‐US) (USGS‐GAP, 2020 ). These protected lands prohibit tillage agriculture and urban development. In addition, we removed U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service wetland easements from PAD‐US based on information in the FWSInterests database (USFWS, 2020 ) because their protected boundaries represent the entire land parcel, whereas only the wetland basins within are protected. Henceforth, we refer to unprotected grass cover simply as grass cover or grassland. To identify grassland loss pixels, we identified any 2011 grassland pixels that were classified as cropland or development in 2021 NLCD. This binary indicator of conversion does not differentiate between the 2 transition states we considered (i.e., crop or development). We focused on conversion to cropland and development given their high accuracy in NLCD and high rates of conversion (Lark et al., 2020 ; Wickham et al., 2023 ). In addition, both types of conversion are ultimately anthropogenically driven and would be targeted for the same conservation action (i.e., protection). We used the focal statistics tool (ESRI, 2023 ) to summarize grassland loss with a moving window analysis. The analysis was conducted at NLCD's native resolution (30 m) with a 3.6 × 3.6‐km moving window, resulting in a spatial layer in which each pixel's value represents a summary statistic calculated from all the pixels in the window around it. We resampled the resolution of the resulting layer to 270 m with the nearest neighbor method. The window size was selected to approximate a 1600‐m area around a 16‐ha land parcel (i.e., quarter–quarter section), a common agricultural land unit in the Public Land Survey System, which is used across most of the study region. The window size is large enough to sample landscape‐scale conditions that can represent landscape context, such as the ratio of grass to crop, which can act as a proxy for the socioeconomic conditions that influence land‐use decisions (Niemuth et al., 2022 ). We used a moving window analysis to sum 2011 grass and 2011–2021 grass loss (i.e., 30‐m pixel count). We derived proportional grass loss by dividing the amount of grass lost from 2011 to 2021 within each window by the amount of grass available to be lost in 2011; all null values resultant from zero division were set to zero. Because the response variables do not distinguish between transition states and because there could be interest for focusing conservation planning on either conversion to cropland or development, we used the same methods to separately develop models for grass loss to crop and grass loss to development. These models were developed with the same predictor variables and modeling methods and were summarized with the same metrics described in the following sections and are available at https://ecos.fws.gov/ServCat/Reference/Profile/157174 . Predictor variables We generated 25 spatial predictors for model training, validation, and spatial application (Table 1 ; Appendix S2 ). To characterize landscape context, we included landscape‐scale estimates of 7 land‐cover classes that reflected the likelihood and amount of land available for conversion. For example, conversion may be hindered by large amounts of forested land, open water, and bare ground, and future conversion may be more likely in landscapes when there is a mosaic of grassland, cropland, development, and land enrolled in the Conservation Reserve Program (CRP). We reclassified all cover classes to binary rasters and calculated their coverage (percent) with moving window analyses. For NLCD‐derived covariates, we used 2011 cover for model training and validation and 2021 cover for spatial application of the models to predict future loss. The amount of land enrolled in CRP was derived from a programmatic data set maintained by the Farm Service Agency that reflects boundaries of land parcels enrolled in CRP and includes attributes such as conservation practice and expiration date. Producers enrolled in CRP generally receive rental payments from the Farm Service Agency to restore environmentally sensitive croplands (i.e., prone to erosion) to grassland for 10–15 years. We included these data because federal program support has a demonstrated influence on land‐use decisions (GAO, 2007 ; Rashford et al., 2010 ; Stephens et al., 2008 ; Sylvester et al., 2013 ), and model results could lead to a better understanding of how postcontract land‐use decisions are related to landscape context. The earliest CRP data set we could obtain was from 2012. We calculated percent cover of CRP with moving window analyses as previously described. We generated cover estimates with 2012 parcels enrolled in a grassland conservation practice that had an expiration date prior to 2021 for model training and validation and 2021 parcels enrolled in a grassland conservation practice that had an expiration date prior to 2031 for spatial application of the model to predict future loss. Because of their influence on cropland suitability (Eitelberg et al., 2015 ) and to a lesser degree development, we summarized 10 topographic, edaphic, and climatic variables with a moving window analysis (Table 1 ). We processed topographic and edaphic data in Google Earth Engine (GEE) (Gorelick et al., 2017 ) and climatic data in ArcGIS. Topographic data were derived from the National Elevation Dataset (Gesch et al., 2002 ) and edaphic data from OpenLandMap (Hengl, 2018a , 2018b ). Edaphic data were provided at 6 depths from 0 to 200 cm. We processed the mean of the 6 depth values with moving window analysis. The same topographic and edaphic data were used for model training and generating future predictions, whereas climatic data included the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6 20‐year mean projections from the shared socioeconomic pathway 3–7.0 for 2001–2020 for model training and 2021–2040 for generating future predictions (AdaptWest Project, 2021 ; Wang et al., 2016 ). To capture socioeconomic drivers of grassland conversion, we summarized 9 variables with Euclidean distance calculations and moving window analyses in ArcGIS (Table 1 ). These variables were intended to characterize demand and distance to market, which influence land‐use change (Li et al., 2016 ; Miralha et al., 2021 ; Sylvester et al., 2013 ; Wright et al., 2017 ). We used 2021 concentrated animal feeding operation (CAFO) point locations from the Enforcement and Compliance History Online database (ECHO, 2021 ) to create Euclidean distance rasters. CAFOs are regulated through federal permitting for pollution by programs such as the National Pollution Discharge Elimination System that stem from the Clean Water Act (Section 502 [14]) (CWA, 1988 ; ECHO, 2021 ). We removed records that had coordinates with an unknown provenance or were attributed to general locations, such as county centroids. We did not filter by date of establishment due to differences in state and program regulations. Facilities mostly represented large (e.g., &gt;1000 head of cattle) to medium (e.g., 300–1000 head of cattle) operations (EPA, 1995 ). We used 2020 ethanol plant point locations from 2020 Homeland Infrastructure Foundation‐level Data (HIFLD, 2020 ) to create Euclidean distance rasters. We could not filter by date of establishment. We included only plants that produce ethanol from agricultural feedstocks for model training and added additional plants being built that will produce ethanol from agricultural feedstocks for generating future predictions. We used the same distance to roads, railroads, and cities layers for model training and future predictions (ESRI, 2021a , 2021b , 2021c ). We used population density estimates from the year 2000 for model training and 2020 for future predictions from Gridded Population of the World 4, which are based on census data (CIESIN, 2018 ). All our response and predictor variables were resampled to a final resolution of 270 m to enable and expedite model application and validation. We used a nearest neighbor resampling technique to limit changes to pixel values. Stratified sampling We developed models for 20 ecoregions across the conterminous United States (USDA‐NRCS, 2006 ) (Figure 1 ) to enable and expedite processing and to account for regional variation in grassland conversion and drivers of conversion. To better understand observed spatial trends of grass loss across ecoregions and to aid interpretation of model associations or variable importance, we summarized observed total and proportional grassland loss to crop and development (2011–2021) within ecoregion boundaries. FIGURE 1 Twenty ecoregions across the contiguous United States (labeled with their ecoregion code) used to spatially constrain model training and application to predict future total and proportional grass loss (2021–2031). We developed model training and validation data sets with stratified sampling within each ecoregion to ensure we sampled across the range of conditions represented by response and predictor data (Hirzel &amp; Guisan, 2002 ). To reduce pseudoreplication and spatial autocorrelation, we made one third of the pixels available for sampling by generating a grid of points spaced 810 m apart and extracted dependent and independent covariate data at point locations (Hirzel &amp; Guisan, 2002 ). This distance aligns approximately with a quarter section in the Public Land Survey System, and a window shifting 810 m results in approximately 4 new quarter sections being evaluated. For each ecoregion, we used the prcomp function from the stats package in R (R Core Team, 2021 ) to conduct principal component analysis to reduce the dimensionality of predictor variables and to identify main spatial patterns and trends. We stratified each ecoregion into 200 strata with the kmeans function from the stats package to conduct unsupervised classification in which training data included the response variable and the first 3 principal components. We set a target sample size of 40% of the available data for each ecoregion, and for each stratum, we randomly sampled up to what would be a balanced distribution of data points across strata (i.e., target sample size divided by 200). We split the data set by ecoregion stratum: 80% for training and 20% for validation. This resulted in a maximum of ∼10.6% of an ecoregion's area sampled for training the model and ∼2.6% for validating the model. Overall, the total grass loss models were trained with 3,638,258 data points, and the proportional grass loss models were trained with 3,696,636 data points. Model and summary statistics We used R to train random forest regression models (Breiman, 2001 ) to predict total and proportional grassland loss to crop or development (2011–2021). We used the ranger package (Wright &amp; Ziegler, 2017 ) to train models for each ecoregion. Model settings included 500 trees, 5 variables per split, and a minimum node size of 10, and the variable importance mode was set to use an impurity metric. We graphically assessed model residuals to check model fit. We used a spline correlogram function from the ncf package (Bjornstad, 2022 ) to assess spatial autocorrelation by calculating spatial dependence (i.e., covariance) as a continuous function of distance up to 100 km. We plotted absolute residuals versus predicted values, residuals versus row identity, and fitted trend lines with generalized additive models. Last, we plotted a histogram of residuals and inspected the figures for deviations from a normal distribution centered at zero. We assessed model performance based on observed versus fitted values from 20% of the data that were withheld to calculate R 2 and root mean squared error (RMSE) per ecoregion. We also calculated these metrics for the contiguous United States based on all data points (i.e., unstratified estimate) and summarized performance across ecoregions by calculating group means and weighted group means. Prior to performance calculations, we truncated any predictions that were greater than what was possible based on the amount of grass available to be lost. We plotted observed versus fitted values for all data points and fitted trend lines from generalized additive models for all data and for each ecoregion, including a reference line of perfect fit. We assessed variable importance based on 3 methods. We used a model‐generated impurity metric that summarized the variance reduction of the response variable for each split when the variable of interest was used across the ensemble of decision trees. We used the DALEX package (Biecek, 2018 ), which is designed to provide model‐agnostic methods and metrics for model exploration and explanation, to calculate 2 measures of variable importance. For each ecoregion's predictor variables, we calculated the standard deviation of accumulated local effects of the response variable (Apley &amp; Zhu, 2020 ) and a permuted loss function by randomizing the values of the target predictor variable, reapplying the model, and computing the change in performance (i.e., RMSE) (Fisher et al., 2019 ). We summarized variable importance results across ecoregions in box plots. To understand associations between loss and predictor variables, we used the DALEX package to calculate and plot accumulated local effects, which are similar to partial dependence or marginal effects, for each variable and ecoregion. To help compare effects across ecoregions, we centered the response curves by subtracting the mean response for each ecoregion. Plot lines represent the average difference from mean loss for each value across the range of predictor values. We summarized these relationships across all ecoregions by calculating a fitted line from a generalized additive model. We spatially applied the models to the extent of their respective ecoregion and created a mosaic of the outputs for the contiguous United States. We applied the model to covariate raster data from ∼2011 that were used to train the model to predict past loss (2011–2021) and then applied it to updated surface data (∼2021) to predict future loss over a similar time frame (2021–2031). We truncated predictions that were greater than what was possible based on the amount of grass available to be lost. We subtracted spatially applied models of past loss from future loss to better understand spatiotemporal trends of grass loss. To better understand model performance across landscape conditions, we extracted observed and predicted loss values for the universe of sample points (i.e., every third pixel) and summarized observed, predicted, and RMSE total and proportional grass loss within bivariate plots by relating each variable to the amount of grass in the landscape and calculating associated mean loss within 10% bin‐width tiles. Finally, we compared performance based on observed versus fitted values between the proportional loss model and a derived proportional loss model that was created by dividing the total grass loss prediction (2011–2021) by the amount of grass available to be lost (2011). This analysis helped determine whether the spatial differences between the total and proportional grass loss models were due simply to the denominator (amount of grass available to be lost) changing across the landscape or whether modeled associations provided a better predictor of proportional loss. Conservation prioritization example We provided a simple illustrative example of how these layers could contribute to conservation planning with the R package prioritizr (Hanson et al., 2023 ) and the optimizer provided by the highs package (Huangfu &amp; Hall, 2018 ; Schwendinger &amp; Schumacher, 2023 ). The prioritizr package allows users to construct conservation prioritization scenarios by specifying conservation objectives, constraints, and penalties, and the highs optimizer uses an exact algorithm to find optimal solutions. We developed conservation prioritization scenarios based on the following layers: total and proportional grassland loss models, chestnut‐collared longspur ( Calcarius ornatus ) abundance model from eBird (Fink et al., 2019 ; Strimas‐Mackey et al., 2022 ), and fair market value model for vacant land sales in the contiguous United States (Nolte, 2020 ). We restricted the prioritization analyses to the boundaries of the Prairie Pothole and Northern Great Plains migratory bird joint ventures (North American Migratory Bird Joint Ventures, 2022 ). All models were resampled to a 3.6‐km resolution with nearest‐neighbor resampling technique, which matches the grassland loss models’ moving window size. To focus on landscapes experiencing cropland expansion, we used the product of the 2 conversion models, which will have larger values in landscapes that have greater total and proportional grassland loss (Appendix S1 ). To speed up processing, we removed pixels from the analysis that were &gt;95% protected or &lt;5% unprotected grass. We developed 2 scenarios that each spatially prioritized a conservation goal of 10% of the population. The first scenario simply used highest bird density estimates to prioritize landscapes representing the top 10% of the population. The second scenario used a 2‐step process that allowed pixels of lower bird density to be prioritized if they had lower cost and higher risk. In the first step, we prioritized 25% of the population that minimized cost and maximized bird density. In the second step, we evaluated this area and prioritized pixels that had the highest risk values until 10% of the population was prioritized. We calculated summary statistics for both prioritizations, including mean bird density, cost, predicted total and proportional grass loss, total cost, and the percentage of the study area prioritized. In the data repository ( https://ecos.fws.gov/ServCat/Reference/Profile/157174 ), we provided the raster layers and R code used to construct and solve these problems. In summary, we used raster layers in which pixels represented planning units, and we defined problems with minimum set objectives, relative targets, and binary outcome decisions. DISCUSSION Model evaluation Our models offer a simple, transparent, quantitative, and defensible approach for assessing risk of grassland conversion across the contiguous United States. These models can serve as tools for conservation planning and guide the strategic protection of grasslands to prevent conversion to crop or development in the near future. Our models incorporated fine‐scale mechanistic drivers, allowing for a deeper understanding of risk across marginal lands compared to simple cropland suitability models. By considering grassland conversion to crop or development, we offer a more holistic measure of conversion that, when combined with information on biological value and cost, can inform strategic allocation of conservation investments that optimize ecological outcomes that align with conservation strategies. Our results provide guidance for grassland conservation that accounts for multiple drivers of grassland conversion across the conterminous United States. These findings are consistent with previous research on cropland suitability (Eitelberg et al., 2015 ) and conversion to agriculture or development (Appendix S5 ) (Golding &amp; Winkler, 2020 ; Lark et al., 2015 ; Wimberly et al., 2017 ; Wright &amp; Wimberly, 2013 ). From 2011 to 2021, several factors influenced land‐use change that differed from historic patterns. Record high crop prices incentivized grassland conversion, and high biofuel and crop insurance subsidies offset the risk of converting marginal lands (Aragon, 2019 ; Lark et al., 2015 ; Miao, Hennessy, et al., 2016 ; Miao, Khanna, et al., 2016 ; Wright et al., 2017 ). New crop varieties, such as herbicide‐tolerant corn ( Zea mays ) and soybeans ( Glycine max ), facilitated conversion (Benbrook, 2016 ). Demand for and production of ethanol, meat, eggs, and dairy increased (Carrie et al., 2011 ; Henchion et al., 2021 ; Mussatto et al., 2010 ). Numerous CRP contracts expired during this period of agricultural profitability (FSA, 2021 ). There was an expansion of human development, primarily in exurban and suburban areas (Golding &amp; Winkler, 2020 ; Vogler &amp; Vukomanovic, 2021 ), and increases in impervious surfaces outpaced population growth across all population density classifications (i.e., rural, exurban, suburban, and urban) (Vogler &amp; Vukomanovic, 2021 ). These conditions were reflected in the models in which total and proportional loss were greater given the presence of cropland and development, proximity to corn‐based industries and cities, and greater population density and amount of landscape enrolled in CRP. Although some data sets we used were specific to the United States, the modeling approach could be extended globally. The roads, railroads, cities, soils, and gridded population data that we used are all available globally. The same landscape‐scale analysis could be conducted with the World Database on Protected Areas (UNEP‐WCMW &amp; IUCN, 2024 ) and fine‐scale landcover data, such as GlobeLand30 (Jun et al., 2014 ). The data sets Geomorpho90m (Amatulli et al., 2020 ) and NASA Earth Exchange Global Daily Downscaled Projections (NEX‐GDDP‐CIMP6; Thrasher et al., 2022 ) could be used for topographic and climatic data. Although CRP, ethanol plant, and CAFO data sets were unique to the United States, there are similar global data sets that can represent socioeconomic drivers of grass conversion, such as gridded livestock density (Robinson et al., 2007 ) and locations of power plants (WRI, 2019 ), wind and solar farms (Dunnett et al., 2020 ), and oil and gas infrastructure (Omar et al., 2023 ). Limitations and uncertainty We encountered several spatial and temporal limitations associated with the data sets we used for modeling grassland conversion. For example, grassland conversions occurring at a scale smaller than a 30‐m pixel could not be identified and were therefore beyond the scope of our models, and the time‐series land‐cover data we used were temporally restricted due to the availability of remote‐sensing missions (e.g., NLCD covers the period from 2001 to 2021). Similarly, programmatic data sets, such as the CRP data set, had even greater temporal limitations, with the earliest national spatial representation dating back to 2012. Furthermore, the uncertainty surrounding future climatic or socioeconomic pathways introduces a significant factor that could affect the future extent of cropland and development. Although grassland loss models (2011–2021) benefited from the inclusion of current CAFO and ethanol data, these layers did not provide updated information on where new industries will develop. Grassland conservationists should be cognizant of how future development of these industries may influence local land‐use decisions. Both industries use agricultural feedstocks, primarily corn, and new facilities may create a market for feedstocks that, because of reduced transportation costs, is most competitively met by local producers, thereby increasing likelihood of conversion. Bivariate and accumulated local effects plots also indicated there was greater loss at distances farther from industry or infrastructure (e.g., 500–800 km from ethanol plants or beef CAFO), which was likely a spurious correlation with other factors driving loss. Our results are consistent with previous findings, but caution should be used interpreting importance metrics for these variables. Federal agricultural program support has a positive influence on conversion (GAO, 2007 ; Rashford et al., 2010 ; Stephens et al., 2008 ; Sylvester et al., 2013 ). Agricultural programs can work against each other; CRP provides incentive to restore cropland to grassland, whereas other factors, such as crop and insurance subsidies, provide incentive to convert grass to cropland. Therefore, future models may benefit by incorporating fine spatial data that represent subsides and programmatic drivers of conservation as well. However, these fine‐scale data are not publicly available, to our knowledge. Land enrolled in the CRP declined by 36,472 km 2 from 2012 to 2021 (FSA, 2021 ), and models reflected this by having high variable importance and positive effect size of CRP on grassland loss. This likely reflects the ease of converting these previously cultivated lands back to cropland or a leakage effect (Meyfroidt et al., 2020 ), when nearby grasslands are converted to cropland to offset the loss of cropland payments due to CRP enrollment. Training a model and making future predictions over shorter periods may have greater precision compared to long‐term predictions given the uncertainty of climatic and socioeconomic conditions in the future. Our models were based on the assumption that estimated associations between covariates and grassland loss in the past will remain similar in the future, which seems reasonable, but even in these short periods, factors that influence land‐use decisions can change. For example, development was stunted by a collapse in the housing market during the start of the analysis training period (Golding &amp; Winkler, 2020 ), and in the later period, a pandemic provided many people with a hybrid work lifestyle that has been associated with increases in suburban sprawl (Ramani &amp; Bloom, 2021 ). In addition, our methods do not allow the model to learn from variation in conditions during the training period, for example, annual changes to crop price and grass extent. Future cropland area, food production, and grassland loss are difficult to predict given the uncertainty in future technological, social, and climatic factors (FAO, 2018 ; Jägermeyr et al., 2021 ; Lawler et al., 2014 ; Ray et al., 2013 ). Despite this uncertainty, identifying current vulnerable grasslands of high biological value and high risk of conversion will provide objective targets for programs that want to safeguard grassland resources and ecosystem functions while identifying grasslands of low conservation value and high risk of conversion that could be set apart for development or sustainable agricultural practices. This is especially important for grasslands because conversion can happen rapidly but recovery to old‐growth characteristics takes centuries, if it happens at all (Nerlekar &amp; Veldman, 2020 ). Conservation implications Given that grassland conversion outpaces conservation (Doherty et al., 2013 ; Fields &amp; Barnes, 2019 ) and resources for conservation are limited, incorporating estimates of risk provided by our models can increase the efficiency of strategic grassland conservation efforts by agencies such as the U.S. Department of Agriculture and the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. The systematic conservation design example we provided illustrates how landscape‐scale prioritization can be optimized for a specific conservation strategy and provide understanding of trade‐offs and efficiencies beyond biological value. Results will vary depending on the specified conservation priorities, such as optimizations based on budget, different biological metrics (e.g., species richness), and connectivity. Although we targeted high‐risk landscapes, alternative prioritizations could target high biological value and low‐risk landscapes for protection. Although we prioritized bird abundance, other biological value layers could be considered, such as grass quality (Field &amp; Barnes, 2019 ; Jones et al., 2021 ). Risk layers could also be used to prioritize lands for conservation actions other than protection. For example, prioritization of landscapes for short‐term restoration easements could target croplands within relatively low‐risk landscapes, which could indicate poorer conditions for economically viable tillage agriculture and a greater likelihood of retention after contract expiration (Sullins et al., 2021 ). There are several broadscale grassland conservation programs operating in the United States that could maximize the effect of their limited resources by incorporating risk estimates provided by our models when ranking competing enrollment or targeting landscapes, such as the Farm Service Agency's CRP program and easement and private lands programs delivered by the Natural Resources Conservation Service or the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (Briske et al., 2017 ; Niemuth et al., 2014 ; USDA‐CRP, 2023 ; USDA‐NRCS, 2023 ; USFWS, 2017 ). For example, the goal of the Grassland CRP program is to protect high‐quality grasslands at the greatest risk of conversion (USDA‐FSA, 2022 ), and models of biological value and risk of conversion could help evaluate candidate parcels enrolling in the program. Although some of these programs use spatial tools to help define risk and prioritize conservation delivery, they often rely on simple heuristics or cropland suitability metrics (e.g., NRCS Working Land for Wildlife) (USDA‐NRCS, 2021 ) rather than grassland conversion models, which have clearer temporal resolutions and consider additional important drivers of conversion. Integrating our grassland conversion models into the decision‐making process of broadscale grassland conservation programs can enhance the precision of their conservation actions and yield more effective outcomes. By protecting landscapes of high biological value and at high risk of conversion, important natural resources can be safeguarded and a prograssland culture can be fostered. This can limit conversion cascades (Arora et al., 2021 ) and help ensure a sustainable future for these vital ecosystems, the services they provide, and the pastoral communities that depend on them.
