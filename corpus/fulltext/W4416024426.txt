Highlights • Nutritional geometry showed carb-rich diets but distinct nutrient targets. • Both species maintained nutrient balance through dietary composition. • Red deer maximized energy via fats and energy-associated gut microbes. • Gazelles prioritized protein with microbes aiding digestion and immunity. • Red deer hosted microbes for fiber breakdown and toxin detoxification. Keywords: Jarman–Bell principle (JBP), Gut microbiota, Nutritional strategies, Herbivore ecology, Symbiotic adaptation Abstract Body size is a key determinant of nutritional strategies in herbivores, yet the role of gut microbiota in mediating these strategies remains insufficiently explored. To address this gap, we examined two sympatric ungulates of contrasting body sizes in an arid ecosystem—red deer ( Cervus elaphus , large-bodied) and goitered gazelle ( Gazella subgutturosa , small-bodied)—to test three predictions derived from the Jarman–Bell principle (JBP). We applied dietary DNA metabarcoding, plant nutritional profiling, and 16S rRNA sequencing of gut microbiota to assess how body size shapes macronutrient intake, microbial composition, and functional adaptations. Both species exhibited similar macronutrient ratios dominated by carbohydrates (∼88–90 %), supporting the nutritional balancing hypothesis. However, they differed in nutrient targets and microbial profiles: red deer consumed more non-structural carbohydrates and fats, with enriched gut microbial taxa ( Paludibacter, Turicibacter ) linked to energy metabolism, reflecting an energy maximization strategy. In contrast, goitered gazelles ingested more crude protein and harbored microbial taxa ( Prevotella) associated with protein digestion, alongside immune-supporting microbes ( Butyricicoccus, Coprococcus , and Victivallis ), consistent with a protein maximization strategy. Red deer also demonstrated a greater microbial capacity for fiber degradation ( Fibrobacter, Ruminococcus albus ) and detoxification of plant secondary metabolites ( Clostridium, Desulfovibrio, Prevotella, Variovorax ). Functional pathway analysis revealed enrichment in lignocellulose and terpenoid metabolism, indicating an adaptation to low-quality forage. In contrast, goitered gazelles showed limited microbial associations with fiber or phytotoxin regulation, suggesting weaker microbial-mediated adaptation to low-quality diets. These results demonstrate that body size governs distinct nutritional strategies in sympatric herbivores, mediated through differential gut microbiota composition and function. Larger species, such as red deer, exhibit enhanced microbial capacity for fiber and toxin regulation, allowing them to utilize lower-quality forage more efficiently. Introduction The Jarman–Bell principle (JBP) has long been foundational in herbivore nutritional ecology ( Bell, 1971 ; Jarman, 1968 , 1974 ), offering a size-based framework to explain dietary differentiation ( du Toit and Olff, 2014 ). It posits a central trade-off: larger-bodied herbivores exploit abundant, low-quality forage, while smaller-bodied species select high-quality, nutrient-rich plants. This size-dependent foraging strategy is well supported by empirical links between body mass and dietary niche breadth ( Gordon and Illius, 1994 ; Pérez-Barbería and Gordon, 2001 ). Larger herbivores, with lower mass-specific metabolic rates and higher gut capacity, can tolerate fibrous diets through microbial fermentation. In contrast, smaller species—with limited digestive capacity and higher energy demands—must selectively forage on protein- or energy-dense resources ( du Toit and Olff, 2014 ; Robbins et al., 1995 ). Beyond food selection, JBP implies divergent nutritional strategies shaped by body size. Larger herbivores may rely on digestive efficiency and microbial symbiosis to extract nutrients from low-quality or chemically defended forage ( Müller et al., 2013 ; Sensenig et al., 2010 ). Smaller herbivores, under tighter metabolic constraints, are expected to optimize nutrient acquisition through selectivity and physiological specialization. Recent advances in nutritional ecology propose five core strategies employed by herbivores to meet nutritional demands, shaped by both physiological traits and ecological conditions: 1.1. Nutritional balance strategy (NBS) Herbivores regulate nutrient intake to maintain a balanced supply of macronutrients (e.g., proteins, carbohydrates, lipids), as captured by geometric models ( Felton et al., 2021 ; Westoby, 1974 ). Evidence from both captive and wild populations—such as Iberian red deer ( Cervus elaphus hispanicus ) and white-tailed deer ( Odocoileus virginianus )—shows selective foraging aimed at maintaining nutrient homeostasis under varying resource conditions ( Ceacero et al., 2009 ; Dykes et al., 2020 ). Across body sizes, this strategy reflects the capacity for dynamic diet adjustment to metabolic needs ( Spitzer et al., 2023 ). 1.2. Energy maximization strategy (EMS) Rooted in optimal foraging theory ( Pyke et al., 1977 ), this strategy assumes herbivores seek to maximize net energy intake. According to JBP, larger herbivores compensate for low diet quality through slow digestion and microbial fermentation, facilitated by specialized anatomy ( Clauss et al., 2003 ; Demment and Van Soest, 1985 ). In contrast, smaller herbivores focus on food quality over quantity, using flexible and selective foraging to meet high energy demands ( Simpson and Raubenheimer, 2012 ). Thus, energy maximization varies with body size—via efficiency in large species, and precision in small ones. 1.3. Protein maximization strategy (PMS) Under JBP, small-bodied herbivores prioritize protein-rich forage to meet intense metabolic requirements ( Clauss et al., 2013 ). This is observed in species like Aepyceros melampus and Gazella dorcas , which consistently target high-protein plants even in resource-scarce environments ( Cromsigt et al., 2009 ; Henley and Ward, 2006 ). Larger herbivores, with greater total intake, may be less protein-selective and rely more on carbohydrates or lipids to fulfill energy needs, aligning with the protein maximization hypothesis ( Simpson and Raubenheimer, 2005 ). 1.4. Dietary fiber regulation strategy (DFRS) Fiber digestion capacity scales with body size and digestive complexity. Large-bodied ruminants possess specialized gut structures for prolonged microbial fermentation, enabling efficient extraction of energy from fibrous plant material ( Clauss et al., 2003 ; Hungate, 1975 ). In contrast, smaller herbivores with shorter gut transit times favor low-fiber, high-digestible food ( Karasov and del Rio, 2020 ). Thus, fiber regulation emerges as a strategy predominantly used by large-bodied species, consistent with JBP predictions. 1.5. Plant secondary metabolite (PSM) regulation strategy (PMSRS) Detoxification of PSMs requires both physiological and microbial adaptations. Large herbivores—via longer retention times and diverse microbiota—tolerate higher PSM intake ( Loh et al., 2020 ; Omondi et al., 2024 ). Symbiotic microbes facilitate detoxification, expanding dietary breadth to include chemically defended plants ( Schön et al., 2023 ). Small herbivores, constrained in detox capacity, minimize PSM exposure through selective avoidance ( Devi et al., 2022 ). Hence, PSM regulation is a body size-dependent strategy, favoring larger herbivores. To integrate these strategies across species and ecosystems, the trophic niche model ( Machovsky-Capuska et al., 2016 ) links nutrient intake, food traits, and foraging behavior along a generalist–specialized continuum. This framework complements JBP by capturing how herbivores modulate their strategies across environmental and seasonal gradients, aligning digestive physiology, microbial ecology, and foraging dynamics. In this study, we investigate two sympatric ungulates with contrasting body sizes—the red deer ( Cervus elaphus ) and the goitered gazelle ( Gazella subgutturosa )—inhabiting the arid Junggar Basin. By linking dietary nutrient composition and gut microbial profiles, we test how the JBP informs species-specific nutritional strategies. (1) According to JBP, wehypothesize that sympatric red deer and goitered gazelles exhibit nutritional strategies consistent with NBS, whereby both species maintain stable macronutrient intake ratios despite differences in body size (2) The JBP further suggests that large-bodied herbivores tend to follow EMS, while small-bodied herbivores prioritize PMS. We hypothesize that red deer, as large-bodied herbivores, follow EMS, while goitered gazelles, being smaller, adopt PMS to meet their higher mass-specific metabolic demands. (3) Given the superior digestive capacity associated with larger body size, the JBP implies that large-bodied herbivores are better suited to fiber-based and phytotoxin-regulating foraging strategies. We thus predict that the red deer adopt DFRS and PMSRS, supported by a gut microbiota enriched in fiber-degrading and detoxifying taxa, enabling them to utilize low-quality forage more effectively 2. Materials and methods 2.1. Study area The Ebinur Lake National Wetland Reserve (44°31′5″N–45°9′35″N, 82°33′47″E–83°53′21″E) is located in Bortala Mongol Autonomous Prefecture, Xinjiang, in northwestern China. It represents the lowest elevation and the main terminal site for water and salt accumulation within the western Junggar Basin ( Li et al., 2006 ). The reserve spans 2 670.85 km 2 and comprises two principal ecosystems: wetlands surrounding Ebinur Lake and adjacent forest–grassland zones. Ebinur Lake forms the ecological core of the reserve, encompassing its shorelines and associated floodplains. The landscape is geomorphologically diverse, featuring mudflats, gravel and stone deserts, arid belts, salt crusts, marshes, and saline lakes. The wetland system is characterized by high spatial isolation, hydrological variability, eutrophication, and elevated salinity, forming a structurally complex inland wetland ecosystem with heterogeneous environmental conditions and high biodiversity ( Li et al., 2010 ; Zhang et al., 2008 ). The reserve supports rich vascular plant diversity, with 385 species representing 53 families and 191 genera. Gymnosperms account for 1 family, 1 genus, and 7 species (1.8 % of the total), whereas angiosperms comprise 52 families, 190 genera, and 378 species (98.2 %) ( Liu, 2017 ). The region is recognized as one of the most species-rich areas for desert flora in China, harboring approximately 64 % of the country’s documented desert plant species. Dominant woody taxa include Tamarix chinensis, Salix babylonica , and Populus euphratica . Salt- and alkali-tolerant shrubs such as Nitraria tangutorum and Alhagi sparsifolia are also widespread. Herbaceous species are particularly abundant and include halophytic and xerophytic taxa such as Kalidium foliatum , Halocnemum strobilaceum, Kali collinum, Phragmites australis, Karelinia caspia, Suaeda glauca, Aeluropus pungens, Lycium ruthenicum, Scirpus triqueter , and Anabasis aphylla ( Li et al., 2016 ). 2.2. Fecal and plant sample collection In the Ebinur Lake Wetland Reserve, the goitred gazelle primarily inhabits the southern and northern regions of the reserve, where it is located at the interface of various vegetation types, resulting in higher environmental heterogeneity compared to other areas. This edge effect creates a rich vegetative diversity, providing more ecological niches for the gazelle ( Ezizjan et al., 2015 ). Conversely, the red deer predominantly concentrates in the northern part of the reserve, particularly near the Kuigutun River, in shrubland and forested areas ( Shamshinur et al., 2016 ). Between February and March 2023, we collected fecal and plant samples from areas where these two ungulate species coexisted in the northern section of the Ebinur Lake Wetland Reserve. Fresh fecal piles were identified based on track chains or direct field observations of red deer and goitred gazelle, with efforts made to avoid repeated sampling from the same individual.Each fecal pile was at least 100 m apart from the others. A sample was collected from each fresh fecal pile. To prevent contamination, sterile polyethylene gloves were changed between different fecal piles, and samples were placed in 10 mL cryovials containing 95 % ethanol. To minimize individual variation, fecal samples of the same species were thoroughly mixed within the cryovials (16 samples for red deer and 13 samples for goitred gazelle), and subsequently stored at –20 °C upon returning to the laboratory ( Tende et al., 2014 ; Zheng et al., 2024 ). In total, 16 mixed samples of red deer and 13 mixed samples of goitred gazelle were collected. From each fresh mixed sample, 0.2 g was used for DNA extraction employing the QIAamp® Fast DNA Stool Mini Kit (50, QIAgen GmbH) for dietary and gut microbiome analysis (Supplementary Tables S1, S2). Plant samples were collected near fecal samples and track chains, totaling 20 samples. Upon identification, these samples included: Apocynum venetum, Populus euphratica, Alhagi camelorum, Calligonum mongolicum, Halocnemum strobilaceum, Karelinia caspia, Tamarix chinensis, Haloxylon ammodendron, Phragmites australis, Reaumuria songarica, Ephedra sinica, Nitraria sibirica, Caragana halodendron, Seriphidium kaschgaricum, Lepidium perfoliatum, Halogeton arachnoideus, Halostachys capsica, Kalidium foliatum, Salix wilhelmsiana , and Poa pratensis. The samples were oven-dried to a constant weight at 65 °C, ground to a fine powder, and stored at room temperature for subsequent nutritional composition analysis (Supplementary Table S3). 2.3. Diet DNA metabarcoding The P6 loop of the chloroplast trnL (UAA) region was targeted for DNA metabarcoding using the trnL (UAA) g and trnL (UAA) h primers ( De Barba et al., 2014 ; Guo et al., 2021 ; Kartzinel et al., 2015 ; Taberlet et al., 2007 ; Willerslev et al., 2014 ). Diet DNA was extracted from fecal samples using the E.Z.N.A.® Soil DNA Kit (Omega Biotek, Norcross, GA, USA) following the manufacturer’s protocol. PCR amplification was performed with an initial denaturation at 95 °C for 5 min, followed by 32 cycles at 95 °C for 30 s, 55 °C for 30 s, and 72 °C for 30 s, with a final extension at 72 × for 10 min, using primers trnL (UAA) g (5′-GGGCAATCCTGAGCCAA-3′) and trnL (UAA) h (5′-CCATTGAGTCTCTGCACCTATC-3′). The barcode was an eight-base sequence unique to each sample. PCR reactions were conducted in triplicate using a 20 μL mixture containing 4 μL of 5 × FastPfu Buffer, 2 μL of 2.5 mM dNTPs, 0.8 μL of each primer (5 μM), 0.4 μL of FastPfu Polymerase, and 10 ng of template DNA. Amplicons were excised from 2 % agarose gels and purified with the AxyPrep DNA Gel Extraction Kit (Axygen Biosciences, Union City, CA, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Purified PCR products were quantified using a Qubit® 3.0 Fluorometer (Life Technologies). Twenty-four amplicons with distinct barcodes were pooled in equimolar amounts. The pooled DNA product was used to construct an Illumina paired-end library following Illumina’s genomic DNA library preparation protocol, and sequencing was performed using a paired-end 2 × 300 bp run on an NGS platform (Shanghai BIOZERON Biotech. Co., Ltd) following standard procedures. 2.4. Regarding the relationship between dietary and nutritional data, we first determined the dietary composition of both species using DNA metabarcoding. The V1–V9 region of the bacterial 16S rRNA gene was amplified via PCR with an initial denaturation at 95 °C for 2 min, followed by 27 cycles at 95 °C for 30 s, 55 °C for 30 s, and 72 °C for 60 s, with a final extension at 72 °C for 5 min, using the primers 27F (5′-AGRGTTYGATYMTGGCTCAG-3′) and 1492R (5′-RGYTACCTTGTTACGACTT-3′). The barcode was an eight-base sequence unique to each sample. PCR was performed in triplicate with 20 μL reaction mixtures, each containing 4 μL of 5 × FastPfu Buffer, 2 μL of 2.5 mM dNTPs, 0.8 μL of each primer (5 μM), 0.4 μL of FastPfu Polymerase, and 10 ng of template DNA. Amplicons were excised using 2 % agarose gels and purified with an AxyPrep DNA Gel Extraction Kit (Axygen Biosciences, Union City, CA, USA) as per the manufacturer’s instructions. SMRTbell libraries were prepared using the SMRTbell Prep Kit 3.0 via blunt-end ligation following the manufacturer’s protocol (Pacific Biosciences). Purified SMRTbell libraries from the Zymo and HMP mock communities were sequenced on dedicated PacBio Sequel cells using S/P1-C1.2 sequencing chemistry, while libraries from pooled and barcoded samples were sequenced on a single PacBio Sequel cell. Replicate 1 samples were sequenced using S/P2-C2/5.0 sequencing chemistry, and Replicate 2 samples used a pre-release version of S/P3-C3/5.0 sequencing chemistry. Amplicon sequencing was performed by Shanghai Biozeron Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). 2.6. DNA metabarcoding sequence analysis Barcode sequence data from each sample were processed using Trimmomatic ( Bolger et al., 2014 ) through an internal Perl script with the following criteria: (i) sequences shorter than 10 bp or with an average Illumina FASTQ quality score below 20 (USEARCH11 ( Edgar, 2013 ) were filtered out); (ii) sequences with perfect barcode matches, up to two nucleotide mismatches in primer regions, or containing ambiguous characters were discarded; and (iii) only sequences with an overlap of &gt;10 bp were assembled, while non-overlapping reads were excluded. The remaining sequences were analyzed with DADA2 (Divisive Amplicon Denoising Algorithm) to remove amplification and sequencing errors as well as chimeric reads. Unlike the UPARSE clustering approach used in QIIME, DADA2 performs denoising and chimera detection independently of similarity-based clustering, providing high-resolution output akin to 100 % similarity OTU tables from QIIME-era analyses. Insertions, deletions, and substitutions were identified with DADA2 ( Park and Won, 2018 ), with a confidence threshold set at 0.8. BLAST alignment was performed with a threshold of 1e-10, and the UCLUST algorithm ( Prasad et al., 2015 ) was applied. Sequences were first aligned to the NCBI nucleotide database (NT database). The aligned results were cross-referenced with our locally established reference database containing 205 plant sequences to filter out plant species not present in the region, thereby enabling more accurate determination of the winter dietary composition of both species. Approximately 87 % of ASVs in our dataset were reliably assigned to the species level, Such resolution (70–90 % species-level annotation) is consistent with other metabarcoding studies on herbivore diet analysis ( Kartzinel et al., 2015 ) 2.7. Microbiota sequence analysis PacBio raw reads were processed using SMRT Link Analysis software v9.0 to generate circular consensus sequence (CCS) reads with a minimum of three passes and a predicted accuracy of 99 %. Reads were further refined using SMRT Portal, with length filtering (1200–1800 bp) and quality control. Additional filtering removed barcodes, primers, chimeras, and sequences with homopolymer runs of 10 or more identical bases. Clustering of operational taxonomic units (OTUs) was performed using UPARSE v10.0 with a similarity threshold of 98.65 %, and UCHIME was used for chimera detection and removal. Phylogenetic relationships were determined using the RDP Classifier against the Silva SSU132 16S rRNA database, with a confidence threshold of 80 % ( Amato et al., 2013 ). 3. Data analysis 3.1. Nutrition The geometric framework (GF) is a multidimensional approach for evaluating nutritional strategies by placing different nutrients within a coordinate system, thereby illustrating relationships among nutrients through geometric principles ( Machovsky-Capuska et al., 2016 ; Simpson and Raubenheimer, 2012 ). The right-angled mixture triangle (RMT) model is a geometric tool for assessing nutrient complementarity. In this study, the RMT model was applied to evaluate macronutrient balance in red deer and goitered gazelle across two sets of nutrient axes: (1) carbohydrates, ether extract, and crude protein (CP + EE + C = 100 %); and (2) crude protein, non-structural carbohydrates plus ether extract, and crude fiber (NSC + EE + CP + CF = 100 %) ( Raubenheimer and Simpson, 2022 ). For each sample, macronutrient intake was expressed as proportional values based on the relative contribution of each nutrient to total intake (e.g., CP % = CP / [CP + EE + C ] × 100). Nutrient composition was obtained from representative plant samples, and each animal’s nutrient intake was estimated according to its observed diet composition. These data were then plotted within the RMT model to visualize the distribution of dietary macronutrient balance across species. Visualization was conducted using the ggplot2, dplyr, plyr , and viridis packages in R.4.1. LOESS smoothing was used to explore how gut microbial diversity varies across nutritional space ( Gomez-Rubio, 2018 ). Additionally, random forest modeling ( P &lt; 0.05) was employed to predict the distribution of gut microbial diversity in response to variations in specific nutrients ( Wright and Ziegler, 2017 ). By integrating local plant nutritional data (Supplementary Table S3), the nutrient composition of each fecal sample was calculated by multiplying the proportional abundance of plant species by their respective nutritional content. Independent samples t -test( P &lt; 0.05) was then applied to analyze differences in nutritional intake levels between the two species during winter. 3.2. Associations between protein, energy, and gut microbial communities Crude protein, non-structural carbohydrates, and crude lipids were selected as key nutritional indicators of dietary protein and energy content ( Potter and Pringle, 2023 ). Given the potential for multicollinearity among these nutrients, a partial least squares (PLS) model was used to identify gut microbial genera associated with each nutritional variable ( Becker et al., 2023 ). The relationships between the selected microbial genera and key nutritional indicators were then examined using structural equation modeling (SEM) to explore their underlying interactions ( Becker et al., 2023 ). 3.3. Associations between cellulose, plant secondary metabolites, and gut microbial communities Initially, interspecific differences in gut microbial taxa known to be associated with cellulose and plant secondary metabolites were assessed based on published literature. A partial least squares (PLS) model was then applied to identify microbial genera correlated with acid detergent fiber (ADF) ( Becker et al., 2023 ). Functional predictions of microbial communities across samples were performed using the Tax4Fun R package, based on the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database, to infer potential functional shifts. Significantly different functional pathways were identified using STAMP 2.1.3 ( Parks et al., 2014 ), and group-level differences in KEGG levels 1, 2, and 3 pathway abundances were compared using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test. Pathways with significantly different abundances were used as response variables in a random forest model to identify microbial genera associated with plant secondary metabolite-related pathways ( R &gt; 0.85, P &lt; 0.05). The top 20 most important genera were used to construct a network of associations between gut microbiota and metabolic pathways in red deer ( Sochacka et al., 2024 ; Wright and Ziegler, 2017 ). 4. Results 4.1. Nutritional strategies of sympatric ruminants with contrasting body sizes Nutritional balance among crude protein (CP), Ether extract (EE), and carbohydrates (C) was assessed for red deer and goitered gazelles using the right-angled mixture triangle (RMT) model, with the constraint CP + EE + C = 100 %. Both species displayed a relatively consistent intake composition within a narrow range ( Fig. 1 A), with carbohydrates contributing approximately 88–90 %, crude protein 7–10 %, and Ether extract around 2 %. The nutritional centroids of both species clustered within the same region of the nutrient space ( Fig. 1 A). Gut microbial diversity was negatively associated with dietary protein and positively associated with Ether extract content ( Fig. 1 A). This pattern was further supported by predictions from the random forest model, which indicated that microbial diversity increased with higher carbohydrate and fat intake, but decreased with increased protein consumption in both species ( Fig. 1 B). Nutritional relationships among crude protein, crude fiber, non-structural carbohydrates, and Ether extract were evaluated for red deer and goitered gazelles using the RMT model. The two species exhibited distinct distributions in nutrient space, suggesting ecological differences in food selection and nutritional adaptation. Specifically, goitered gazelles consumed higher levels of crude protein and fiber, while red deer had a greater intake of non-structural carbohydrates and fat ( Fig. 2 A). Differences in Shannon diversity indices may reflect variation in environmental adaptability between species. Further analysis using random forest models revealed that gut microbial diversity decreased with increased intake of crude protein and fiber, but increased with higher consumption of non-structural carbohydrates and fat in both species ( Fig. 2 B). Hypothesis 1: sympatric red deer and goitered gazelles exhibit nutritional strategies consistent with the nutritional balancing hypothesis Foraging behavior in herbivores is inherently dynamic, involving the regulation of multiple nutrients and antinutritional compounds to meet changing physiological demands across temporal scales ( Simpson and Raubenheimer, 2012 ). The nutritional balancing hypothesis posits that animals select foods to achieve a balance of required nutrients rather than maximizing any single component ( Dykes et al., 2020 ; Felton et al., 2016 , 2021 ). For example, captive Iberian red deer ( Cervus elaphus hispanicus ) have been shown to preferentially select foods rich in sodium (Na) and cobalt (Co) under nutrient-deficient conditions ( Ceacero et al., 2009 ). Wild ungulates exhibit similar compensatory behaviors, opting for phosphorus-rich ( Dykes et al., 2018 ; Lashley et al., 2015 ), protein-rich ( Dostaler et al., 2011 ), or energy-dense plants ( Berteaux et al., 1998 ) depending on resource availability and environmental context, such as drought-induced shifts in vegetation ( Cain et al., 2017 ; Lashley and Harper, 2012 ). In winter, white-tailed deer ( Odocoileus virginianus ) increase their consumption of carbohydrate-rich plants to meet elevated energetic demands ( Felton et al., 2016 , 2021 ), and a similar pattern has been documented in red deer and sika deer, whose winter diets are composed of 84–87 % carbohydrates ( Zhong et al., 2020 ). Our results are consistent with these findings: sympatric red deer and goitered gazelles consumed diets with 88–90 % carbohydrates during winter food shortages ( Fig. 1 A), while the contributions of crude protein (7–10 %) and crude fat (2–3 %) remained relatively stable. These patterns suggest both species experienced similar nutritional constraints during winter, leading to consistent macronutrient compositions despite low forage quality. Notably, goitered gazelles occupied a broader nutritional niche than red deer, which exhibited a more clustered distribution in nutrient space ( Fig. 1 A). This supports the idea of dietary convergence in response to shared environmental pressures, wherein a broader range of plant species results in a wider nutrient intake profile ( Machovsky-Capuska et al., 2016 ). We also found similar trends in gut microbiota diversity: for both species, microbial diversity declined with increasing dietary protein, but increased with higher proportions of carbohydrates and fat ( Fig. 1 B). Although proteins serve as energy sources for many microbes, their fermentation byproducts (e.g., ammonia, hydrogen sulfide) can exert toxic or inhibitory effects on beneficial taxa, thereby reducing microbial diversity ( Liu et al., 2024 ). In contrast, dietary fibers provide fermentable substrates that promote microbial growth and community stability ( Chen et al., 2024 ). These mechanisms may underlie observed differences in Shannon diversity indices between species and reflect their differential capacities for microbial-mediated adaptation. Importantly, despite comparable carbohydrate levels in their diets, red deer tended to ingest more non-structural carbohydrates and lipids, whereas goitered gazelles increased protein intake as a compensatory strategy during winter ( Fig. 1 A, B; Fig. 2 A, B). This divergence suggests species-specific pathways to nutritional regulation. Overall, the consistent macronutrient intake ratios observed in both species support Hypothesis 1—that sympatric red deer and goitered gazelles adopt nutritional strategies aligned with the nutritional balancing hypothesis. 5.2. Hypothesis 2: red deer follow an energy-maximizing strategy, while goitered gazelles follow a protein-maximizing strategy The Jarman-Bell principle (JBP) highlights the relationship between plant quality and body size, stating that larger herbivores consume greater quantities of lower-quality plants, whereas smaller herbivores tend to select higher-quality plants ( Bell, 1971 ; Jarman, 1968 , 1974 ). Quantitative analyses of the relationship between food quality (e.g., crude protein) and body size provide strong evidence supporting the JBP effect. For instance, studies on African ungulates demonstrate that the crude protein content of ingested plants generally decreases with increasing body mass ( Kleynhans et al., 2011 ; Owen-Smith, 1988 ). In the present study, Since the primary diet of herbivores is largely composed of carbohydrates, this premise must be considered when discussing their nutritional strategies. On this basis, red deer were found to consume plants with significantly lower crude protein content compared to goitered gazelles ( Fig. 3 A), while the intake of non-structural carbohydrates plus crude fat was significantly higher ( Fig. 3 B). These results are consistent with the JBP framework, during winter, when plant crude protein content typically declines, larger-bodied animals, due to their lower metabolic rates and longer digestive tracts and retention times, are able to consume lower-quality forage ( Demment and Van Soest, 1985 ). In African herbivores dietary protein concentration decreases with increasing body mass among congeneric antelopes, whereas digestible energy scales positively ( Daskin et al., 2023 ). Such contrasting allometries reflect the fundamental trade-off between nutrient concentration and intake capacity: smaller-bodied species prioritize protein-rich, high-quality forage, while larger-bodied species maximize energy intake from abundant, lower-quality resources ( Bell, 1971 ; Jarman, 1968 ). Similarly, in our study, goitered gazelles selected high-protein legumes, whereas red deer consumed more carbohydrate- and fat-rich forage, consistent with the predicted body-size–dependent nutritional strategies ( Daskin et al., 2023 ). However, this imposes challenges on digestive efficiency, an issue previously studied mainly from the perspective of gut morphology, with less attention paid to the role of gut microbiota. Analysis based on PLS and SEM models revealed that the abundance of Paludibacter, Turicibacter, Lentimicrobium , and Candidatus Soleaferrea in the gut of red deer was significantly associated with the intake of non-structural carbohydrates and crude fat ( Fig. 5 B). Among these, Paludibacter is associated with cellulose hydrolysis and can form mutualistic relationships with Clostridium to enhance the synthesis of glucosidase enzymes within the gut ( Cui et al., 2019 ). Turicibacter , an important member of the mammalian gut microbiome, is linked to dietary fat intake and body weight regulation; different strains can differentially influence host bile acid profiles and lipid metabolism ( Lynch et al., 2023 ). Lentimicrobium is associated with selenium (Se) availability in the diet; under low-Se conditions, its abundance declines significantly, accompanied by increased levels of ruminal citrulline, ornithine, and glutathione, ultimately impacting amino acid and glutathione metabolic pathways ( Meng et al., 2024 ). Candidatus Soleaferrea is known for its role in maintaining intestinal homeostasis and exerting anti-inflammatory effects ( Zhang et al., 2015 ). These microbial taxa likely contribute to enhancing energy extraction from low-quality forage. Thus, the nutritional strategy of red deer aligns with an energy-maximization strategy. For small-bodied animals, higher metabolic rates and shorter digestive tracts and retention times make plant quality a higher priority ( Demment and Van Soest, 1985 ; Nagy, 2005 ). This distinction arises from the JBP, which predicts that the limiting nutrient shifts from protein to energy with increasing body size ( Bell, 1971 ; Jarman, 1968 , 1974 ). Small-bodied herbivores have high mass-specific metabolic rates and short gut retention times, which constrain their ability to process large volumes of low-quality forage. ( Demment and Van Soest, 1985 ; Nagy, 2005 ). Consequently, they must prioritize protein-rich, easily digestible plants to meet their metabolic nitrogen requirements. In contrast, larger-bodied herbivores possess greater absolute energy demands but lower mass-specific metabolic rates and longer retention times, enabling them to extract energy efficiently from high-fiber, low-protein diets ( Pérez-Barbería et al., 2004 ). The present results are consistent with this principle: goitered gazelles preferentially consumed higher-protein plant species, particularly legumes such as Alhagi sparsifolia . Analysis using PLS and SEM models revealed that the abundance of Prevotella, Butyricicoccus, Coprococcus , and Victivallis in the gut of goitered gazelles was significantly and directly associated with the intake of crude protein ( Fig. 5 A). Among these, Prevotella is known to degrade complex carbohydrates and is involved in peptide breakdown during protein metabolism ( Walker et al., 2005 ). Diets low in protein have been shown to alter the gut microbiota composition, leading to a reduction in Prevotella abundance ( Zhu et al., 2022 ). Both Butyricicoccus and Coprococcus are associated with butyrate production; they generate short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) through the fermentation of complex substrates, which can influence host metabolism and immune homeostasis directly or indirectly ( Liu et al., 2023 ; Notting et al., 2023 ). Victivallis shows a positive correlation with volatile fatty acids (VFAs) in the gut ( Ahmad et al., 2020 ).The enrichment of these microbial taxa suggests that the gut microbiota of goitered gazelles facilitates both the digestion of high-protein forage and the maintenance of intestinal immune function, potentially reducing gut inflammation. Therefore, the nutritional strategy of goitered gazelles aligns with a protein-maximization strategy. 5.3. Hypothesis 3: red deer adopt a dietary fiber-regulation strategy and a secondary plant metabolite-regulation strategy Large and small herbivores exhibit notable differences in fiber intake and processing, primarily due to structural differences in their digestive systems ( Müller et al., 2013 ). Across herbivores, the difference in mean retention times (MRT) between particles and fluids—represented by the selectivity factor (SF = MRT_particle/MRT_fluid)—increases significantly with the proportion of fibrous forage in the diet ( Steuer et al., 2010 ). Compared to small herbivores, large ruminants possess more voluminous gastrointestinal tracts, with longer particle MRTs. As the intake of fibrous forage increases, the proportion of fluids to particles in the reticulorumen increases, and the fluid turnover rate accelerates significantly, suggesting that large ruminants have a greater capacity to digest fiber ( Pérez-Barbería et al., 2004 ). Differences in selectivity factors (SF)—reflecting the differential movement of particles and fluids—may lead to the removal of certain microbial populations from the rumen ( Müller et al., 2011 ). In theory, this process promotes microbial washout into the abomasum and small intestine ( Dittmann et al., 2015 ; Hummel et al., 2015 ), enhancing microbial turnover and fermentation efficiency ( Clauss and Hummel, 2017 ). In the present study, the relative abundances of common cellulolytic rumen bacteria—including Fibrobacter, Prevotella, Treponema, Butyrivibrio, Ruminococcus albus , and Pseudomonas —were significantly higher in red deer than in goitered gazelles ( Fig. 6 ). Among these, Fibrobacter and Ruminococcus albus are recognized as key fiber-degrading bacteria in ruminants ( Mizrahi et al., 2021 ). Prevotella, Treponema , and Butyrivibrio are efficient degraders of hemicellulose ( Mizrahi et al., 2021 ; Tokuda et al., 2018 ; Jensen et al., 2021 ), while Pseudomonas is known for its lignocellulose degradation capacity, targeting cellulose (35–50 %), hemicellulose (30–35 %), and lignin (10–40 %) ( Di Donato et al., 2019 ; Xu et al., 2018 ). PLS regression analysis indicated significant correlations between red deer gut microbiota and the intake of acid detergent fiber (ADF). Notably, Acetivibrio , a bacterium capable of degrading lignin, was significantly associated with ADF intake. This taxon produces a suite of cellulolytic enzymes—β-glucosidase, exoglucanase, and endoglucanases—that act on different sites along the cellulose chain to facilitate hydrolysis ( Saddler and Khan, 1981 ; Shahid et al., 2018 ). Herbivorax , an anaerobe that degrades both cellulose and xylan, was also identified as an efficient degrader of crystalline cellulose ( Aikawa et al., 2018 ; Pechtl et al., 2018 ). These results support the interpretation that red deer adopt a fiber-regulation strategy mediated by gut microbes, enabling digestion of fibrous, low-quality forage. The microbial profiles also suggest potential roles in mitigating secondary plant metabolites, though further verification is needed. In goitered gazelles, PLS regression also revealed significant correlations between gut microbiota and ADF intake. Among associated taxa, Prevotella —a common cellulolytic bacterium in ruminants ( Mizrahi et al., 2021 )—was present, though its relative abundance was lower than in red deer ( Fig. 6 ). Other correlated genera, including Lachnoclostridium and Victivallis , were linked to short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production ( Ahmad et al., 2020 ; Vacca et al., 2020 ). SCFAs, especially butyrate, enhance intestinal barrier function and exert anti-inflammatory effects ( Melbye et al., 2019 ). Together, these findings provide microbial-level evidence that red deer possess a greater fiber-processing capacity than goitered gazelles, supporting the hypothesis that red deer follow a dietary fiber-regulation strategy. On the other hand, plant secondary metabolites (PSMs) can reduce digestibility in herbivores and may even cause toxicity ( Loh et al., 2020 ; Rogowska‐van der Molen et al., 2023 ). According to the JBP, animals of different body sizes adopt distinct strategies for coping with low-quality or toxic plants due to differences in gut morphology and food retention time ( Devi et al., 2022 ; Lambert, 2002 ). Our results showed that the relative abundances of Klebsiella, Firmicutes -unclassified, and Bacillus were significantly higher in the gut microbiota of goitered gazelles compared to red deer ( Fig. 7 ). Klebsiella and Firmicutes -unclassified are associated with diaminopropionic acid (DAP), a metabolite involved in the detoxification of harmful compounds and nitrogen cycling ( McSweeney et al., 2005 ). Bacillus has been linked to the degradation of mycotoxins such as ochratoxins and penicillins, which are known for their nephrotoxicity, hepatotoxicity, immunosuppressive effects, and potential carcinogenicity ( Schatzmayr et al., 2006 ). conversely, the relative abundances of M. elsdenii, Clostridium, Pigmentiphaga, Desulfovibrio, Prevotella, Streptococcus , and Lactobacillus were significantly higher in red deer ( Fig. 7 ). Among these, M. elsdenii and Clostridium are associated with β-N-oxalyl-l-α,β-diaminopropionic acid (β-ODAP), a naturally occurring non-protein amino acid with neurotoxic properties ( Marichamy et al., 2005 ). Pigmentiphaga is linked to fluoroacetate, a toxic compound found in certain woody plants ( Camboim et al., 2012 ). Desulfovibrio and Prevotella are associated with pyrrolizidine alkaloids (PAs), hepatotoxic and carcinogenic compounds found in plant families such as Asteraceae and Boraginaceae, including herbs like Senecio and Symphytum ( Craig et al., 1992 ; Molyneux et al., 1988 ; Rattray and Craig, 2007 ). Additionally, Streptococcus and Lactobacillus are linked to aflatoxin B1, the most toxic and carcinogenic member of the aflatoxin family, commonly present in mold-contaminated feed ( Verheecke et al., 2016 ). The relationship between gut microbiota and PSMs in the two species is likely linked to differences in plant consumption. Compared to goitered gazelles, red deer preferentially feed on woody plants such as Populus euphratica and Salix wilhelmsiana , and occasionally consume toxic plants like Senecio jacobaea . Based on Tax4Fun functional predictions, we found that red deer exhibited significantly higher abundances in PSM-related metabolic pathways, including xylene degradation, ubiquinone and other terpenoid-quinone biosynthesis, and terpenoid backbone biosynthesis, compared to goitered gazelles ( Fig. 4 , Fig. 5 , Fig. 6 , Fig. 7 , Fig. 8 ). Random forest analysis further indicated that these enriched pathways were significantly associated with specific microbial genera, including Intestinimonas, Proteiniphilum, Paludibacter, Variovorax, Romboutsia, Campylobacter, Trichococcus , and Prevotella . Among these, Proteiniphilum is a hydrolytic and fermentative bacterium possessing lignocellulolytic enzymes such as cellulases, hemicellulases, and ligninases, with demonstrated lignin-degrading potential ( Thongbunrod and Chaiprasert, 2021 ). Paludibacter abundance increases with a higher intake of plant flavonoids ( Hnokaew et al., 2024 ). Variovorax is associated with terpenoid biosynthesis, and antioxidant activity, and has the capacity to degrade various pollutants while exhibiting resistance to heavy metals such as cadmium and mercury ( Han et al., 2011 ; Woo and Kim, 2022 ). Alkaloid levels significantly affect the abundance of Romboutsia ( Ijoma et al., 2024 ). Prevotella can detoxify pyrrolizidine alkaloids from plants, such as heliotrine, by converting them into less toxic compounds like 7α‑hydroxy-1-methylene-8α-pyrrolizidine and heliotric acid ( Rattray and Craig, 2007 ). In contrast, fewer associations were observed between microbial taxa and metabolic pathways in goitered gazelles; only glutathione metabolism was significantly correlated with Papillibacter , suggesting that detoxification in this species is likely less microbiota-mediated and more dependent on enzymatic processes. These results further support the prediction that red deer are more tolerant of low-quality, toxic plants and that their nutritional strategy aligns with DFRS and PSMRS. 6. Conclusion This study investigated two sympatrically distributed ungulate species of contrasting body sizes—red deer ( C. elaphus ) and goitered gazelle ( G. subgutturosa )—in an arid ecosystem, to evaluate three ecological hypotheses through analyses of nutrient composition and gut microbiota: (1) Both species conform to the nutritional balancing hypothesis. Despite differences in body size, both red deer and goitered gazelles maintained relatively stable macronutrient intake ratios under sympatric conditions, with carbohydrates serving as the dominant nutritional component. (2) Red deer follow EMS, while goitered gazelles follow a PMS. This is supported by gut microbiota composition: red deer harbored a higher abundance of energy-related microbial taxa such as Paludibacter and Turicibacter , which facilitate the conversion of low-quality plant materials into energy. In contrast, goitered gazelles exhibited enrichment of microbial taxa associated with high-crude-protein plant digestion ( Prevotella ) and intestinal immune regulation ( Butyricicoccus, Coprococcus , and Victivallis ). (3) Red deer conform to both DFRS and PSMRS. Compared to goitered gazelles, red deer exhibited significantly greater microbial enrichment in taxa associated with the degradation of cellulose ( Fibrobacter, Ruminococcus albus ), hemicellulose ( Prevotella, Treponema , and Butyrivibrio ), and lignin ( Pseudomonas ), as well as taxa involved in PSM detoxification ( M. elsdenii, Clostridium, Pigmentiphaga, Desulfovibrio, Prevotella, Streptococcus, Lactobacillus, Paludibacter, Variovorax , and Romboutsia ). In contrast, goitered gazelles showed limited microbial capacity for fiber degradation and PSM regulation, suggesting they do not adopt these nutritional strategies. Ethics approval Not applicable.
