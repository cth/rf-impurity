ABSTRACT Background and Aims Detecting gastroesophageal varices with a high risk of bleeding (HRV) in children with biliary atresia requires endoscopies from an early age to apply primary prophylaxis of bleeding. However, repeated endoscopies are invasive and costly. This study was performed to define tools to rule out HRV, with the objective of reducing the number of endoscopies. Methods Children with biliary atresia and portal hypertension were prospectively enrolled. Laboratory tests, elastography, echocardiography, and endoscopy were performed. Variables were subjected to principal component analysis, followed by multivariate logistic regression analyses for HRV prediction. The effectiveness of composite models and criteria based on liver and spleen stiffness measurements was evaluated using the missed HRV ratio and the spared endoscopy rate. Results Among the 100 children enrolled, a higher indexed right atrium area, higher left ventricle end‐diastolic diameter, younger age, higher serum bile acid concentration, and higher spleen stiffness were independently associated with HRV. In children under 3 years old, a model combining indexed right atrium area from echocardiography and serum bile acid concentration achieved a 37% spared endoscopy rate with no missed HRV. For children over 3 years old, spleen stiffness of ≤ 30 kPa was associated with a 41% spared endoscopy rate and no missed HRV. Conclusion Combining an echocardiography‐based model for children under 3 years old with spleen stiffness criteria for older children may reduce the need for screening endoscopies by 40% in children with biliary atresia and portal hypertension. Introduction Biliary atresia is the leading cause of cirrhosis and portal hypertension in children, accounting for 56% of all cases of portal hypertension, according to a recent report [ 1 ]. Portal hypertension develops early in children with biliary atresia; one study found that 90% of children showed clinical and/or ultrasonographic signs of portal hypertension by a median age of 13 months; 70% of these children had gastroesophageal varices detected on upper gastrointestinal endoscopy, with 30% presenting with variceal bleeding at a median age of 17 months [ 2 ]. Bleeding can occur as early as the first months of life and may result in death or necessitate urgent liver transplantation [ 1 ]. Primary prophylaxis of bleeding—commonly performed in adults with cirrhosis to prevent a first episode of spontaneous bleeding—remains a topic of debate in paediatric patients. This is particularly true because endoscopic screening for varices which have a high risk of bleeding (high‐risk varices, HRV) is invasive, expensive, and may require repeated general anaesthesia, along with its potential side effects [ 3 , 4 ]. In adults, non‐invasive criteria, such as platelet count and liver or spleen elastography, are used to identify cirrhotic patients at low risk of HRV, enabling them to safely avoid screening endoscopy [ 5 , 6 ]. However, the application of these criteria in children with biliary atresia has been limited and has yielded uncertain results [ 7 , 8 ]. Echocardiography, performed at regular intervals, is an integral part of managing children with portal hypertension, primarily to detect early signs of portopulmonary hypertension [ 9 ]. However, little is known about the broader findings of echocardiography in an unselected population of children with biliary atresia and portal hypertension. Furthermore, no studies have explored a potential relationship between echocardiographic results and the severity of gastroesophageal varices. The objective of this study was to evaluate the utility of a model incorporating echocardiography, alongside criteria based on liver or spleen elastography, in ruling out HRV in children with biliary atresia and portal hypertension. 2. Patients and Methods This was an observational, monocentric cohort study of children with portal hypertension secondary to biliary atresia. Children with biliary atresia who had undergone a Kasai operation and presented with palpable splenomegaly and/or ultrasonographic signs of portal hypertension were prospectively enrolled [ 10 , 11 ]. Children who had experienced gastrointestinal bleeding or undergone previous endoscopic primary prophylaxis of bleeding were excluded. As part of a scheduled follow‐up surveillance, each child underwent a comprehensive evaluation over a 3‐day period. This included successively a clinical examination, blood tests (total serum bilirubin, total serum bile acids and serum albumin concentration, platelet count, and prothrombin ratio), liver and spleen stiffness measurements, transthoracic echocardiography, and upper gastrointestinal endoscopy as the final procedure. Liver and spleen stiffness measurements were performed using the Aixplorer ultrasound platform (Supersonic Imagine; Hologic, Aix‐en‐Provence, France). A complete evaluation included B‐mode and Doppler imaging. Supersonic shear‐wave elastography was conducted on the same platform using a high‐frequency linear probe SL 10–2 (2–10 MHz). Liver and spleen measurements were obtained by positioning the transducer in a right intercostal space for the liver and in a left intercostal space or subcostal area for the spleen. Stiffness values were measured using a stiffness colour map with a fixed size (2.0 × 2.5 cm), placing the region of interest within the map while avoiding artefacts and vessels. Three to five measurements of liver and spleen stiffness were performed, and the medians of their mean values were calculated [ 12 ]. Two elastography‐based criteria were used to rule out HRV: the Baveno VI criteria (liver stiffness of &lt; 20 kPa and platelet count of &gt; 150 000/mm 3 ), developed for adult patients with compensated advanced chronic liver disease [ 13 ], and a second criterion based on spleen stiffness of ≤ 30 kPa. This spleen stiffness threshold was selected because it represents an intermediate value between two previously reported optimal cut‐offs for detecting HRV in children with chronic liver disease: 27 and 38 kPa [ 14 , 15 ]. Standard transthoracic echocardiography was performed by a single paediatric cardiologist (V.L.) using the Toshiba Aplio 500 ultrasound system. Recorded cardiac parameters included M‐mode, two‐dimensional imaging, conventional pulse/continuous Doppler, and tissue Doppler imaging [ 16 , 17 ]. Details on these measurements are provided in the supplementary methods (Data S1 ). A comprehensive examination was conducted, which included an evaluation of signs potentially related to the effects of portal hypertension. These included indicators of myocardial remodelling (e.g., atrial and ventricular dimensions, wall thickness and left ventricular mass index), cardiac hyperkinesia such as cardiac index, left ventricle (LV) shortening and ejection fractions and raised pulmonary artery pressure [ 18 , 19 ] To account for variations in weight and age, some values were expressed as Z‐scores calculated using the Cardio Z reference site (UBQO Limited) [ 20 ] or indexed by body surface area ( Supporting Information ). Children with congenital heart diseases were excluded from the study. Upper gastrointestinal endoscopies were performed or supervised by the same endoscopist (M.D.), and findings were recorded as follows [ 1 ]: the grade of oesophageal varices was categorised as Grade 1 when the varices were flattened by insufflation, Grade 2 when the varices were not flattened but were separated by healthy mucosa, and Grade 3 when the varices were not flattened by insufflation and appeared confluent or pseudotumoral, obstructing the lumen. Red wale markings on the mucosal wall were also noted. Gastric varices were recorded by retroflexion and described as gastroesophageal varices 1 (GOV1, extending down to the cardia and lesser curve) or gastroesophageal varices 2 (GOV2, extending to the fundus), as well as fundal isolated gastric varices 1 (IGV1). Based on previous experience, the endoscopic patterns considered to carry a high risk of bleeding (i.e., HRV) were Grade 3 oesophageal varices and Grade 2 oesophageal varices with red wale markings and/or GOV1 [ 1 ]. 3. Statistical Analyses A univariate analysis was performed to identify clinical, laboratory, elastographic, and echocardiographic variables that differed significantly between children with and without HRV. Pearson's chi‐squared test (or Fisher's exact test, where appropriate) was used for categorical variables, while Student's t ‐test (or Satterthwaite's approximation for unequal variances) was applied to quantitative variables. Missing data due to technical difficulties (child's restlessness, poor echogenicity) represented less than 5% of all studied parameters. To reduce the impact of missing values when applying the multivariate model, multiple imputations on mixed data were calculated using the missRanger package in R [ 21 ]. The quantitative variables were subjected to a principal component analysis (PCA) to reduce the number of variables. Based on the variables identified through PCA, several multivariate logistic regression models were developed for predicting HRV. For the final model, a receiver operating characteristic curve was generated, and the area under the curve (AUC) was calculated. Youden's J statistic was used to determine the optimal threshold for HRV detection. Performance metrics, including sensitivity, specificity, accuracy, and predictive values, were also calculated. The performance of the composite model and the elastography‐based criteria for ruling out HRV was evaluated by calculating two metrics for each: the missed HRV ratio (number of missed HRV cases/total number of HRV cases) and the spared endoscopy rate (the ratio between the number of patients who underwent an endoscopy that could be avoided based on the model or the criterion and the total number of patients) [ 22 ]. All legally authorised representatives of participants were informed of their inclusion in the study. Data collection was conducted in compliance with the recommendations of the French Data Protection Authority (“Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés”) and in accordance with the General Data Protection Regulation (agreement BPD 2018DIA008). 4. Results From 2015 to 2022, 100 children with portal hypertension secondary to biliary atresia were included in the study at a mean age of 6 years (range: 6 months to 17 years), with 37 of them under the age of 3 years. Total serum bilirubin concentration was &lt; 20 μmol/L in 54 children, between 20 and 100 in 19 and &gt; 100 μmol/L in 27. Oesophageal varices were identified in 69 children, including HRV in 26 children (detailed in Table S1 ). No patient was on beta‐blocking agents. Results from the clinical, laboratory, endoscopic, elastographic, and echocardiographic investigations, along with their distribution into two age groups (younger and older than 3 years), are provided in Tables S2 and S3 . 4.1. Models Based on Echocardiography As shown in Tables 1 and 2 among 38 variables across clinical, laboratory, echocardiographic, and elastographic data, 25 differed significantly on univariate analysis between children with and without HRV. Children with HRV were younger and exhibited higher liver and spleen stiffness, higher total bilirubin and bile acid serum concentrations, and lower serum albumin levels and prothrombin ratios. Among the 23 echocardiographic parameters tested, 14 differed significantly between the two groups (Table 2 ). Children with HRV had greater left and right heart dimensions, including indexed left and right atrial area, LV diameter, right ventricular area, wall thickness, mass index, cardiac index, S′ wave velocity, and pulmonary artery pressure. The PCA method (Figure S1 ) selected 5 variables most strongly associated with HRV: age, total serum bile acid concentration, spleen stiffness, indexed right atrium area, and LV end‐diastolic diameter. In the multivariate logistic regression analysis (Table S4a ), a higher indexed right atrium area, higher LV end‐diastolic diameter, younger age, higher serum bile acid concentration, and higher spleen stiffness were independently associated with HRV. Similar results were obtained when using only the 89 children with complete data (without imputation) for the model (Table S4b ). The chosen model, named Echocardiography‐based Model 1, was characterised by an AUC of 0.91 and a Youden index of 72 for a cut‐off value of 0.43. This model demonstrated an accuracy of 88%, sensitivity of 81%, and specificity of 91% for predicting HRV (Figure S2 ). When applied to the entire population of 100 children, regardless of age, Model 1 achieved a spared endoscopy rate of 73% and a missed HRV ratio of 19% (Table 3 ). However, in children under the age of 3 years, the model performed better, with a spared endoscopy rate of 46% and a missed HRV ratio of only 6%, whereas in older children, the missed HRV ratio was 50%. To study specifically children under 3 years, a new logistic regression was conducted in this stratified population of 37 children, identifying the indexed right atrium area and serum bile acid concentration as the two most predictive parameters for HRV (Table S5 ). This second model, named Echocardiography‐based Model 2, was characterised by an AUC of 0.88 and a Youden index of 74 for a cut‐off value of 0.37. Model 2 demonstrated an accuracy of 87%, sensitivity of 100%, and specificity of 74%. When applied to children under 36 months of age, Model 2 achieved a spared endoscopy rate of 37% and a missed HRV ratio of 0% (Table 4 ). Elastography‐Based Criteria In the entire population of 100 children, regardless of age, the Baveno VI criteria were associated with a 20% spared endoscopy rate and a 0% missed HRV ratio (Table 3 ). Spleen stiffness of ≤ 30 kPa was associated with a 27% spared endoscopy rate and a 4% missed HRV ratio. Among children under the age of 36 months, both criteria performed poorly in terms of spared endoscopy rate. Conversely, in older children, the spared endoscopy rates were 30% and 41% for the Baveno VI criteria and spleen stiffness of ≤ 30 kPa, respectively, with a 0% missed HRV ratio for both criteria. To determine the optimal cut‐off for spleen stiffness in children aged ≥ 36 months old, a ROC analysis was carried out in this population: for a sensitivity of 100%, the maximal value of Youden index showed that the 30 kPa threshold was indeed the optimal cut‐off (Figure S3 ). As shown in Table 4 , combining a model based on the indexed right atrium area and serum bile acids for children under the age of 3 years with spleen stiffness for older children resulted in a 40% spared endoscopy rate and a 0% missed HRV ratio for the entire population. 5. Discussion This study of a population of children with biliary atresia and portal hypertension, encompassing various ages and outcomes of the Kasai operation, provides two key insights: first, an assessment of the condition of the heart, and second, a practical guide to reducing the number of screening endoscopies during the monitoring of portal hypertension. Assessment of the cardiac condition in children with cirrhosis secondary to biliary atresia has primarily focused on younger children evaluated prior to liver transplantation [ 23 , 24 ]. Previous studies have reported cardiac anomalies, primarily ventricular hypertrophy, with a prevalence ranging from 16% to 70%. By contrast, the current study found LV hypertrophy in only 6% of children, with normal relative wall thickness in all cases. This difference may reflect the less severe liver condition and the higher mean age of the population in our study compared with previous reports. Notably, while the mean LV free wall and septal thicknesses were within normal ranges for most children, they were higher in children under 3 years of age than in older children. Conversely, a significant proportion of children in our study exhibited increased diastolic cavity dimensions: one‐third had dilated left and right atria, and half had a dilated right ventricle that could be attributed to chronic volumetric overload in the absence of elevated pulmonary artery pressure. The high cardiac index observed in two‐thirds of children in this study aligns with a hyperdynamic circulatory syndrome that occurs early in portal hypertension regardless of the cause [ 25 ]. Having previously observed several instances of right cardiac cavity dilation in children with HRV on endoscopy, we hypothesized that certain echocardiographic indices might be associated with the severity of varices. The present study confirms this hypothesis, demonstrating significant differences between children with and without HRV on univariate analysis of echocardiographic data. These differences include indicators of hyperkinetic circulation, such as the cardiac index, and signs of volumetric overload, such as the right and left heart dimensions. Multivariate analysis further confirmed a significant relationship between the presence of HRV and an increase in the indexed right atrial area, which could be attributed to increased cardiac preload secondary to elevated venous return. Additionally, an increase in LV end‐diastolic diameter was found, likely reflecting hyperkinetic circulation. Changes in these two parameters indicate myocardial remodelling, an adaptive response to the effects of peripheral vasodilation and hyperdynamic circulation, which occurs later in the progression of portal hypertension, as described in adults [ 26 ]. With the growing trend toward using endoscopic primary prophylaxis of bleeding in children with portal hypertension and gastroesophageal varices, screening endoscopies are increasingly performed to detect HRV. In children with biliary atresia, portal hypertension develops early in life, with gastroesophageal varices sometimes present at the time of the Kasai operation and HRV potentially developing within a few months or years [ 2 , 27 , 28 , 29 ]. To apply primary prophylaxis effectively, screening endoscopies must be performed early and repeated as needed. A model has been proposed for detecting HRV in children whose previous endoscopy did not reveal HRV [ 28 ]. Additionally, several non‐invasive methods have been suggested for predicting HRV at the first endoscopy; however, their effectiveness in children with biliary atresia remains uncertain [ 30 ]. In adults, the current trend is to identify criteria that can rule out HRV, thereby limiting endoscopy to patients who do not meet these criteria [ 22 ]. These criteria, primarily based on liver stiffness and platelet count (Baveno criteria) or spleen stiffness, assess the proportion of spared endoscopies that maintain a risk of missed HRV below 5% [ 5 , 6 ]. Two recent studies have attempted to apply this strategy to children with biliary atresia. Both reports studied children whose Kasai operation was associated with a restoration of bile flow. Kang et al. [ 7 ] studied children with a mean age of 2.5 years and a mean total serum bilirubin concentration of 13 mg/L; using Baveno VI criteria, 30% of screening endoscopies could be spared, but the missed HRV ratio was high at 13%. Yokoyama et al. [ 8 ] investigated children with a median age of 4 years with a median total bilirubin concentration of 9 mg/L, including six infants under 12 months. Depending on the combination of variables, spared endoscopy rates ranged from 30% to 41%, while missed HRV ratios varied between 4% and 20%. Conversely, our study included children with various outcomes of the Kasai operation including failure of the procedure, only partial or temporary restoration of bile flow, as well as (relatively) satisfactory bile flow as shown by the wide range of total serum bilirubin concentration. The results of the current study suggest that non‐invasive tests for ruling out HRV should be tailored to the age of the child. As shown recently, the rate of variceal progression in children with biliary atresia and the cumulative risk of HRV emergence are higher in infants than in older children [ 29 ]. This rapid progression in younger children with biliary atresia is likely due to the severity of cholestasis and its impact on portal hypertension. In infancy, after a Kasai operation, cholestasis often remains substantial, either because the operation fails, the operation restores bile flow only partially or temporarily, or, in jaundice‐free children, the serum bile acid concentrations remain very high. Cholestasis contributes to portal hypertension by promoting liver fibrosis. Elevated serum bile acid levels may also play a role in the development of hyperdynamic circulation and are associated with pathological structural and functional remodelling of the heart in infants with biliary atresia [ 31 , 32 ]. This may explain why, in children under the age of 36 months, the model combining the indexed right atrium area—an echocardiographic indicator of increased cardiac preload—and the serum bile acid concentration was effective in ruling out HRV in 37% of cases with no missed HRV. The poor performance of elastography‐based criteria at this early age can be attributed to the very high values of liver and spleen stiffness, as well as the normal platelet counts observed in a large proportion of children with HRV under 36 months old [ 28 , 29 ]. Conversely, in children over the age of 3 years for whom the Kasai operation has restored bile flow, the hyperdynamic circulation is less marked, the serum bile acid concentrations are not as elevated, liver and spleen stiffness values are lower, and thrombocytopenia is more common. This may explain the better performance of elastography‐based criteria, including those incorporating platelet count, in older children. In conclusion, this study indicates that signs of cardiac overload, rather than ventricular hypertrophy, are observed in children with biliary atresia outside the pretransplant period. The combination of a model based on the indexed right atrium area and serum bile acids for children under 3 years of age, and spleen stiffness for children over 3 years, has the potential to reduce the need for screening endoscopy by 40% in children with biliary atresia and portal hypertension.
