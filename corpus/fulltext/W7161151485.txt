See: https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc/4.0/ . Abstract Objective Inadequate bowel preparation impairs the accuracy of colonoscopy and increases the burden on patients and healthcare systems. Consequently, the quality of bowel preparation is an important quality indicator. We aim to develop and validate multivariable prognostic models for the identification of patients at risk of inadequate bowel preparation using machine learning (ML). Methods Demographic and clinical data from consecutive patients ≥18 years of age who underwent colonoscopy at six centres in Germany were prospectively collected. Adequate bowel preparation was defined as Boston Bowel Preparation Scale ≥6 with a value ≥2 in each colonic segment. We used statistical and ML methods to build prognostic models and to compare them to published models. Results Overall, we analysed 2652 patients, including 699 (26.4%) inpatient procedures. The mean patient age was 57.6 years (SD 16 years), and 48.9% were women. In 1401 (52.8%) patients, the indication was screening or surveillance, and 1035 (39%) patients had a first-time colonoscopy. The rate of inadequate bowel preparation was 16%. Sensitivities, specificities and areas under the curve of predictive models obtained by generalised boosting models, Ranger, support vector machine (radial), CatBoost and Net Regularised Generalised Linear Models were 0.51–0.86, 0.52–0.83 and 0.71–0.74, respectively. They were only marginally superior to a logistic regression model. All models had high negative predictive values &gt;0.9 for inadequate bowel preparation. To detect one patient with inadequate bowel preparation, 8–10 patients need to be evaluated. Conclusions Predictive models to identify patients at risk for inadequate bowel preparation obtained by ML showed comparable results compared with a logistic regression model. WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC A considerable proportion of patients still have inadequate bowel preparation. Prognostic models for identifying patients with inadequate bowel preparation are not routinely used to date. WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS Modern machine learning models were prospectively studied in a multicentre cohort from various levels of care with a balanced ratio of outpatient/inpatient procedures, first-time/re-examination, and screening and surveillance/diagnostic. Machine learning models delivered high negative predictive values for inadequate bowel preparation, but low positive predictive values, and did not produce better results than logistic regression models. HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY This study supports clinicians in the risk stratification of patients before bowel preparation. Introduction A sufficient bowel preparation is essential for detecting and evaluating abnormalities of the colonic mucosa, including colorectal polyps during colonoscopy and, consequently, for ensuring the effectiveness and safety of the procedure. Poor bowel preparation is associated with reduced adenoma detection rates, an increase in incomplete examinations, prolonged procedure durations and the necessity to repeat the procedures. 1 Subsequently, it leads to higher costs and to decreased efficient utilisation of endoscopic resources. 2 3 Therefore, the European Society of Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ESGE) emphasises the quality of bowel preparation as an important quality indicator for colonoscopy. 4 Several tools have been published to systematically evaluate the quality of bowel preparation, of which the Boston Bowel Preparation Scale (BBPS) is the most validated and commonly used instrument. 5 Regardless of the type and dose of the cleansing agent, split dosing provides better cleaning results than the day-before bowel preparation. 3 6 7 Therefore, the current ESGE guideline recommends split-dose bowel preparation for elective colonoscopy and a same-day bowel preparation for patients undergoing afternoon colonoscopy as an acceptable alternative to split dosing. 4 Furthermore, at least 90% of individuals undergoing colonoscopy should present with adequate bowel preparation. 4 However, the frequency of suboptimal bowel preparation remains substantial. According to a recent systematic review and meta-analysis, it ranges from 5% to 67% with a median of 26%. 8 In recent years, several studies aimed to develop tools to identify patients at risk for inadequate bowel preparation. 9 , 24 Due to suboptimal accuracy, none of these tools have been established in clinical practice. 25 , 27 Machine learning (ML), a subset of artificial intelligence, focuses on developing algorithms and statistical models that enable computers to perform tasks without being explicitly programmed for each task. In ML, computers learn from data and iteratively improve their performance over time. ML is currently used in various domains, for example, natural language processing, computer vision, healthcare, finance, recommendation systems and autonomous vehicles. For instance, in healthcare, ML algorithms are used for disease diagnosis, drug discovery and personalised treatment recommendation. 28 In addition, ML is applied in healthcare management systems for tasks such as patient triage, predicting hospital readmissions, optimising resource allocation and enabling the analysis of large-scale patient data, including genomic, clinical and lifestyle information, to tailor treatment plans for individual patients. 29 , 32 The objective of our study was to develop and validate a multivariable prognostic model by utilising ML methods to predict quality of bowel preparation in patients undergoing elective colonoscopy in a prospective, multicentre cohort that includes inpatient and outpatient procedures for screening/surveillance as well as diagnostic procedures performed across different levels of the healthcare system, and to compare this model to already published models. With this work, we aim to support physicians in the planning and implementation of bowel preparation for colonoscopies. Methods Study design, setting and participants This prospective, multicentre study was conducted at three academic hospitals with open-access endoscopy units (two university hospitals, one general hospital) and three medical practices specialised in gastroenterology in Germany from 1 March 2020 to 1 December 2022. The study cohort consisted of all consecutive patients aged ≥18 years who underwent elective colonoscopy at one of the study centres. In the case of repetitive colonoscopies in the same patient during the study period, only the first colonoscopy was included in our analysis. Procedures Bowel preparation was conducted following local protocols at the participating centres, of which all used 2–4 L of polyethylene glycol-based bowel preparation according to the summary of product characteristics, and recommended split-dose bowel cleaning regimen starting the first half of bowel preparation the afternoon before the procedure day and taking the second half of bowel preparation the morning of the procedure day. For patients undergoing afternoon colonoscopy, a same-day bowel preparation regime was offered. Patients who deviated from the standard bowel preparation protocol—for example, due to previously inadequate bowel cleansing—were excluded, as this would have constituted a highly heterogenous patient population. Furthermore, the inclusion of varying and potentially non-standardised bowel preparation regimes would have complicated the development of a reliable predictive model. A patient questionnaire designed to assess adherence to bowel preparation was introduced during the course of the study. Self-reported intake of bowel preparation was categorised in &lt;50%, 50%–75% and ≥75% of the recommended amount. However, information obtained from the survey was not used for the ML models, as it became available only after the initiation of bowel preparation. We used the BBPS to evaluate the quality of bowel preparation as previously published. 5 Adequate bowel preparation was defined as a BBPS ≥6 with values ≥2 in each colonic segment. In the case of partial resection of the colon or incomplete colonoscopy, BBPS of the adjacent colonic segment was carried forward to calculate total score. The BBPS was assessed by the endoscopists performing the colonoscopy. No central or blinded assessment of the BBPS was performed. All participating endoscopists were trained in the use of BBPS previously to study start. Information on medical history and co-medication was collected prior to colonoscopy by the investigator using a structured case report form. Education was categorised from no formal education to college/university degree and then dichotomised into ‘low education attainment’ (no formal qualification or lower secondary school) versus ‘medium/high education attainment’ (intermediate secondary school or higher). American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) classification was applied as previously published. 33 The variable previous inadequate bowel preparation was not applicable to patients undergoing first-time colonoscopy. In cases of repeat colonoscopy, the category unknown was assigned if the findings of the prior colonoscopy were unavailable or if patients were unable to recall the quality of bowel preparation during the previous examination. We evaluated two coding strategies: (1) ‘not applicable’ was classified as indicating no previous inadequate bowel preparation, resulting in three distinct categories for the variable representing prior inadequate bowel preparation and (2) ‘not applicable’ was classified as indicating no previous inadequate bowel preparation, while ‘unknown’ was classified as indicating previous inadequate bowel preparation, resulting in two distinct categories. The latter option was used for the final ML models. The indication for colonoscopy distinguished screening or surveillance for colorectal polyps and cancer in asymptomatic individuals from diagnostic procedures for the evaluation of signs and symptoms. The ward’s specialisation in gastroenterology versus all other medical fields was used for inpatient procedures only. Polyp detection rate (PDR) was calculated as number of colonoscopies with the detection of any polyps divided by total number of colonoscopies. Statistical analysis Statistical analysis was performed using Statistical Package for Social Sciences V.29 (IBM), R V.4.4.3 32 and Microsoft Excel (Microsoft, Richmond, USA). Categorical variables were presented as absolute numbers and relative frequencies, continuous variables as mean with SD or median with IQR. Categorical variables were compared by χ² test or Fisher’s exact test, when indicated. A p&lt;0.05 was considered statistically significant. The required sample size was estimated using the framework for sample size calculation in prediction model development described by. 34 The calculation was performed using the pmsampsize (R-package) approach, which accounts for the expected event rate, number of candidate predictor parameters and anticipated model performance to minimise overfitting and ensure reliable estimation of predictor effects. For the final model including eight predictor variables, a minimum sample size of 829 participants was estimated. The final study cohort substantially exceeded this requirement. There were no missing values. Potential features for the ML models were selected based on published models from literature, univariable OR and frequencies in the cohort. Various feature subsets have been tested with fivefold cross-validation on the training set, suggesting no improvement on larger feature subsets. Therefore, the final model does not include all significant variables. Variables, which were finally integrated into the ML models are provided in table 1 . Many comorbidities that are highly associated with poor bowel preparation are relatively rare in the cohort. Therefore, an aggregated comorbidity score was developed as an equally weighted feature from ASA ≥3, diabetes, chronic kidney disease, dialysis, cirrhosis, coronary heart disease and history of liver resection, pancreatic resection, gastrectomy or colon resection. It was then used as a dichotomous variable for a comorbidity score ≥1. The ML models used were inspired by previous publications on predicting poor bowel preparation and recent developments in ML for structured data: Ranger, an optimised version of the Random Forest model. 35 36 CatBoost and generalised boosting models (GBM3) as modern versions of sequentially boosted decision trees. 37 38 A support vector machine (SVM) with radial basis kernel. 39 Logistic regression model and Lasso and Elastic-Net Regularised Generalised Linear Models (GLMNet). The dataset was divided into training and independent test sets with a stratified 80/20 split. Meta-optimisation of the parameters was performed with fivefold cross-validation on the training set to avoid information leakage. The cut point for prediction from the probability score of the ML models was selected using the Youden index. Performance was reported with area under the receiver operating characteristics curve (AUC), detection rate, number needed to treat, positive predictive value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV), sensitivity and specificity. Besides ML models, a novel predictive score was implemented using the binary variables gender, intake of concomitant medication &gt;4 per day, history of poor bowel preparation, education level, specialisation and the aggregated comorbidity score. For comparison, the Dik Score and a modified PREPA-CO Score have been implemented. 9 10 The modified PREPA-CO Score omitted the Physical activity, as it was not available in this study. Reporting The study was registered in the German Clinical Trials Register (DRKS) prior to the commencement of the clinical study phase (DRKS00018878). The reporting of the study results is in accordance with the recommendations of the TRIPOD+AI checklist ( online supplemental file 1 ). 40 Patient and public involvement There was no involvement of patients or public during the design, conduct, reporting, interpretation or dissemination of the study. Results We included 2652 patients in our analysis. The mean age of patients was 57.6 years (SD 16 years), 51.1% were men and 48.9% were women. Colonoscopy was performed as an inpatient procedure in 699 (26.4%) patients and as an outpatient procedure in 1953 (73.6%) patients. A first-time colonoscopy was reported in 1035 (39%) patients. The indication for colonoscopy was screening or surveillance in 1401 (52.8%) asymptomatic individuals, whereas 1251 (47.2%) patients had diagnostic colonoscopy for the evaluation of signs and symptoms. The PDR was 43%, which was significantly higher in procedures with adequate bowel preparation compared with those with inadequate bowel preparation (44% vs 37%, p=0.024) and in complete versus incomplete procedures (44% vs 22%, p&lt;0.001). Complete procedures were significantly more common in patients with adequate bowel preparation compared with patients with inadequate bowel preparation (98% vs 84%, p&lt;0.001). An inadequate bowel preparation was documented in 430 (16.2%) patients. The proportion of patients with a BBPS &lt;2 in a colonic segment was higher in the right colon (13.2%) than in the left colon (10.1%) and the transverse colon (8.3%). In patients with a BBPS ≥6, bowel preparation was classified as inadequate due to a value &lt;2 in at least one colonic segment in 41 (1.8%) patients, which was mainly the case in the right colon (n=28, 1.2%) as compared with the left (n=12, 0.5%) and the transverse colon (n=1, 0.0%). Information on self-reported intake of bowel preparation was available in 1321 (49.8%) patients. Most patients (85%) reported having taken &gt;75% of bowel preparation (&lt;50% of bowel preparation in 1%, 50%–75% of bowel preparation in 14%). The amount of bowel preparation taken by patients was significantly associated with the quality of bowel preparation (adequate bowel preparation in 0.3%, 11.4% and 88.3% in patients taking &lt;50%, 50%–75% and &gt;75% of bowel preparation, respectively, p&lt;0.001). Risk factors, which were associated with more than a threefold increase in the odds for inadequate bowel preparation in univariable analysis, were previous inadequate bowel preparation, renal replacement therapy, history of liver or pancreatic resection, specialisation of the ward other than gastroenterology for inpatient procedures, chronic kidney disease and ASA classification ≥3, whereas female gender (OR 0.73, 95% CI 0.59 to 0.9) and first-time colonoscopy (OR 0.65, 95% CI 0.52 to 0.81) significantly reduced the risk. In multivariable analysis, specialisation other than gastroenterology in inpatient procedures (adjusted OR (aOR) 2.51, 95% CI (1.67 to 3.77), p&lt;0.001), previous inadequate bowel preparation (aOR 2.27, 95% CI (1.74 to 2.96), p&lt;0.001), an aggregated comorbidity score ≥1 (aOR 1.58, 95% CI (1.18 to 2.11), p=0.002), number of concomitant medication &gt;4 per day (aOR 1.56, 95% CI (1.15 to 2.12), p=0.004) and low education attainment (aOR 1.39, 95% CI (1.06 to 1.82), p=0.017) remained significantly associated with inadequate bowel preparation. Discussion In this prospective, multicentre study, in which we aimed to build a model to predict the quality of bowel preparation prior to colonoscopy and which comprised 2652 elective colonoscopies for various indications, ML models reached acceptable values for sensitivity and AUC as well as high NPVs, without superiority of a specific model. However, ML models did not demonstrate a substantial benefit over conventionally derived predictive score in identifying patients at risk for poor bowel preparation. Inadequate bowel preparation is a clinically relevant problem in diagnostic and therapeutic colonoscopies affecting relevant outcomes of the procedure, for example, completeness of the procedure, complication rate and patient satisfaction, but most importantly detection rates of neoplastic lesions. 1 31 41 Current guidelines recommend adjustment of surveillance intervals in cases of inadequate bowel preparation; consequently, shorter examination intervals are frequently implemented compared with those applied after adequate preparation, resulting in increased utilisation of healthcare resources and associated costs. 2 42 43 Intensified bowel preparation or combined cleansing regimens have been suggested to increase the rates of adequate bowel preparation. 44 45 Although effective, these measures may be burdensome for the patients and increase procedural costs and the incidence of adverse events. Therefore, they should, ideally, be applied only to patients at risk for inadequate bowel preparation. Potential risk factors for inadequate bowel preparation have been extensively investigated over the past years. Among others, age, male sex, inpatient status, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, cirrhosis, narcotic use, constipation, stroke and tricyclic antidepressant use were identified as strong risk factors in most studies. 46 Further studies developed prediction models for the quality of bowel preparation which incorporated these and other risk factors and applied different methods of model building. 9 , 24 In these studies, which were described in detail in a recent review, AUC, PPV and NPV ranged from 0.62 to 0.77, 0.20–0.76 and 0.64–0.95, respectively. 47 In a previous evaluation that was limited to a subset of the prediction models currently available, it was concluded that the models published up to that time had not been implemented in clinical practice owing to their suboptimal predictive performance. 25 Advancements in artificial intelligence, including ML, have generated impressive results in various areas of medicine regarding diagnostic and therapeutic applications. Therefore, we hypothesised that the use of novel ML methods could enhance the diagnostic performance of predictive models for quality of bowel preparation. In fact, ML methods have only been applied in the most recently published studies on this topic so far. 14 17 21 23 24 The main findings of our study were that the various ML methods applied yielded comparable results in terms of sensitivities, specificities, AUC, PPV and NPV, and that they did not exceed a predictive model obtained from conventional score building. This is consistent with the previously published studies, as those using ML did not produce numerically better results compared with studies using logistic regression and other conventional methods of model building ( table 5 ). In the further analysis of these results, we found low prevalences of individual risk factors in our, but also in other published study cohorts. Furthermore, a high proportion of the data points were similar in terms of the distribution of the variables considered for the analysis, with many patients having no risk factor. In addition, the rate of inadequate bowel preparation, which ranged from 12.9% to 33% in previously published studies, was only 16% in our study. 9 , 1315 23 These factors resulted in a limitation of the data points for the development and training of predictive models using ML, although we already considered a large number of patients for our study. However, the sole increase in the number of cases would not automatically lead to a substantial improvement in the results, based on the findings described above. The predictive models we developed were characterised by particularly high specificities and NPVs, which was in accordance with previously published studies. Therefore, a primary application area for the predictive models may involve the reliable identification of patients with a high likelihood of adequate bowel preparation. This, in turn, would lead to an increased pretest probability of inadequate bowel preparation in the remaining cohort, where measures to improve bowel preparation could then be implemented more effectively. Prospective and adequately powered studies are necessary to evaluate this assumption. The strengths of our study include the prospective and multicentric study design, with data collection across different levels of the healthcare system, the consideration of both outpatient and inpatient examinations as well as balanced ratios of patients undergoing their first versus repeated colonoscopy, and patients undergoing the procedure for screening/surveillance versus diagnostic. Together with the substantial number of included patients with no missing values, we would assume good internal validity of the findings obtained in our study. In contrast, a relevant number of studies on this topic have focused on specific patient groups, for example, outpatients or inpatients, elderly patients or patients with screening and surveillance colonoscopy. 11 , 1317 21 23 24 Although the proportion of examinations with adequate bowel preparation in our study was below the minimum of ≥90% recommended by ESGE and the target value of ≥95%, the rate of inadequate bowel preparation at 16% was low compared with other published studies on this topic, which reported rates between 6% and 33%. 9 , 25 As in most, we used the BBPS to assess the quality of bowel preparation, as its use is recommended by ESGE despite the inherent subjectivity of the assessment system. 4 48 To minimise interobserver variability regarding the BBPS in our study, training of the participating investigators was conducted before the start of the study. 5 25 49 However, because the BBPS has already demonstrated sufficient validity and reliability and observers have been trained on the method, no independent assessment of interobserver variability was performed within the framework of our study. 48 It should also be noted that in our assessment of bowel preparation, we followed a strict application of the BBPS. The selection of variables in our study was based on the available evidence. 46 However, we focused on variables that could already be collected before the start of bowel preparation. Additionally, certain variables, such as patients’ level of physical activity and dietary habits in the period preceding colonoscopy, were not considered for reasons of feasibility. The predictive models by Fuccio et al , which outperformed the models by Dik et al and Gimeno-García et al in a comparative post hoc analysis, as well as by Chen et al , incorporated variables which became available during the ongoing preparation process. 10 , 1223 These variables included the volume and mode of administration of the bowel preparation solution, a drinking-to-stool interval exceeding 3 hours, the presence of brown liquid or solid stool at the last rectal evacuation, and a preparation-to-colonoscopy interval greater than 6 hours. Although these variables were significantly associated with bowel preparation quality, the opportunity to influence the bowel preparation based on these variables is limited. Some of the variables analysed in our study did not show a significant association with the quality of bowel preparation, contrary to expectations based on the current literature. We primarily attributed these differences to the fact that the variables were based on patients’ anamnesis, especially when no corresponding documentation was available in the patient records. Additionally, the variable constipation did not require the fulfilment of the current Rome IV criteria, which may have led to the inclusion of less severe and non-chronic forms of constipation, thereby potentially weakening the observed influence of this variable on the endpoint. 50 Also, the medication groups of antidepressants and neuroleptics are heterogeneous and include agents with both constipating and laxative effects, which could have altered the association with these variables. This approach, in which the necessary information is regularly available through the patients’ reports only, however, reflects everyday practice and could potentially have a positive impact on the practical application of these predictive models. In conclusion, predictive models to identify patients at risk for inadequate bowel preparation obtained by novel ML methods show a good overall performance but did not perform superior to a conventional predictive scoring. Prospective studies on the application of these predictive models should be conducted.
