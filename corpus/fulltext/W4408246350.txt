Abstract BACKGROUND While natural forests have decreased globally, forest plantations have expanded highlighting the need for studies on plantations’ capacity to sustain biodiversity and provide ecosystem services within agroforestry mosaics. This study examined the breeding habitat preferences of European honey buzzards Pernis apivorus (a native predator of the invasive yellow‐legged hornet) and their relationship with reproductive performance in exotic Eucalyptus plantations in southwestern Europe. We expected better reproductive performance in preferred habitats. RESULTS Breeding habitats ( n = 57 nesting locations) and randomly selected control points ( n = 77) were compared across spatial scales. We analyzed the relative importance of vegetation and topographic features, intraspecific territorial behavior, and interspecific interactions within the diurnal raptor guild. Two complementary approaches were employed: binomial generalized linear models for predictive habitat models, and random forest classification models to assess the relative importance of habitat factors. We examined the relationship between habitat preferences and reproductive performance based on laying phenology and fledgling success. Vegetation and topography were the most influential factors affecting habitat preferences, especially around nests (a 200 m‐radius circle). Honey‐buzzards preferred large nest trees within forest patches exhibiting high structural complexity and maturity. Preferred habitats were negatively associated with fledgling success, and no significant relationship was detected with laying phenology. CONCLUSIONS Our results suggest that Eucalyptus plantations can offer suitable breeding habitats for honey‐buzzards if managed appropriately. This finding is crucial for guiding forest management decisions aimed at enhancing high‐quality breeding habitats for honey‐buzzards and encouraging their regulatory services against yellow‐legged hornets. © 2025 The Author(s). Keywords: biological control, regulatory ecosystem services, Eucalyptus globulus , forest plantations, smallholding forestry, Vespa velutina Our study revealed a positive relationship between the structural complexity of forest plantations and their capacity to deliver regulating ecosystem services. The likelihood of occurrence and the density of breeding pairs of European honey buzzards increased in proportion to the structural complexity of Eucalyptus forest plantations: large trees, multiple canopy layers, high tree species richness, and high tree density. INTRODUCTION Achieving sustainable agricultural production requires the integration of biodiversity services, such as pest control, into agroecosystem management. 1 Crop fields should be adjacent to natural or semi‐natural vegetation that supports pest‐controlling biodiversity. Designing effective agroforestry mosaics requires characterizing high‐quality habitats capable of sustaining biodiversity in the long‐term. This knowledge should guide the conservation, management, and restoration of sustainable agroforestry mosaics that enhance agricultural productivity, functional biodiversity, and associated ecosystem services. The invasive yellow‐legged hornet Vespa velutina nigrithorax causes significant ecological and economic problems to both natural and human‐managed ecosystems, with its impact continuing to escalate. 2 Yellow‐legged hornets are the second most important prey of the forest‐dwelling European honey buzzard Pernis apivorus L. 1758 (hereafter honey‐buzzard) in south‐western Europe 3 , 4 and they reduce yellow‐legged hornet worker abundance within, at least, a 1000‐m radius around raptor nests. 5 Therefore, promoting breeding populations of honey‐buzzards could serve as an effective biocontrol measure within integrated pest management programs targeting yellow‐legged hornets. 4 , 5 Maximizing pest regulation in agroforestry systems requires integrating natural and semi‐natural habitats that are essential for maintaining stable populations of pest controllers at the landscape scale. 6 Breeding habitats are particularly crucial for the long‐term viability of local populations, 7 especially for migratory species like honey‐buzzards, with a brief reproductive period in Europe from May to August. 8 Factors influencing the spatial distribution and habitat preferences of forest‐dwelling raptors are classified into first, second, and third‐order factors. 9 , 10 First‐order factors include habitat features related to landscape heterogeneity, such as land uses and topography, influencing at multiple spatial scales. Second‐order factors involve intraspecific interactions, leading to aversion between conspecific pairs in territorial species. Third‐order factors pertain to interspecific interactions within the forest raptor guild, where dominant species influence the spatial distribution of subordinate ones. 10 In Europe, Northern goshawks ( Accipiter gentilis , hereafter goshawks) prey on honey‐buzzards, and chick mortality is highly correlated with the distance to the nearest active goshawk nest. 11 , 12 , 13 Third‐order factors may be especially significant for migratory raptors arriving at breeding grounds after resident raptors have established territories, requiring migratory raptors to locate vacant areas. 12 Few studies have explored the relative importance of these factors in raptors’ habitat preferences. 9 A simultaneous assessment of these factors offers a comprehensive understanding of the hierarchical and complex decision‐making in habitat selection of pest regulators. Identifying habitat characteristics most closely linked to reproductive performance is essential, as they serve as key indicators of habitat quality for forest‐dwelling raptors. 13 , 14 Habitat quality links specific habitat features to species performance. 15 Understanding habitat quality provides a solid basis for managing pest regulators effectively, focusing on promoting high‐quality breeding habitats that support long‐term population viability. This study examined the breeding habitat preferences of European honey‐buzzards and their relationship with reproductive performance in exotic Eucalyptus globulus plantations in southwestern Europe. As natural forests decline globally and plantations expand, it is crucial to assess the plantations' ability to support biodiversity and ecosystem services. 9 , 16 We hypothesized that first‐, second‐ and third‐order factors would influence breeding habitat preferences, including an aversion to active honey‐buzzard and goshawk nests, with first‐order factors operating across multiple spatial scales. We assessed whether these preferences had adaptive value by examining their relationship with reproductive performance, hypothesizing that preferred habitats would have better reproductive performance, indicated by earlier laying dates and higher fledgling production. Understanding honey‐buzzard breeding habitat preferences and the hierarchical habitat selection process can guide forest management to promote high‐quality breeding habitats, support stable raptor populations, and enhance their role in controlling invasive yellow‐legged hornets within agroforestry mosaics. 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS 2.1. Study area The study area is in northwestern Spain and covers 747 km 2 with approximately 326 km 2 of forested land (42°20′ N, long. 8°47′ W; Supporting Information, Fig. S1 ). The terrain is predominantly hilly, with an average elevation of 213 m, ranging from sea level to 646 m. The area is densely populated, with around 480 inhabitants per km 2 . 10 Forests dominate the higher elevations and steep hillsides, forming a contiguous expanse, while the more fertile lower hillsides and valleys have been developed for agriculture and residential uses. Forests cover 51% of the total area, nowadays primarily consisting of exotic Eucalyptus spp . plantations (85% of the total forest area). 9 , 17 In terms of number of trees, Eucalyptus (61.2%) and native species such as the maritime pine Pinus pinaster (24%), Quercus spp . (9.7%) and other less abundant species (5.1%) such as Acacia melanoxylon , Castanea sativa or Laurus nobilis ( unpubl. data ). 2.2. Study species The European honey buzzard has the widest distribution among species in the genus Pernis , breeding across most of Europe and parts of Asia, 8 with wintering grounds in sub‐Saharan Africa. Pre‐nuptial migration typically begins in early May and post‐nuptial migration in late August. Although adults usually return to the same region each breeding season, they rarely reuse the same nest or pair with the same mate. 18 In the Iberian Peninsula, honey‐buzzards breed at low densities, primarily in forested areas of northern Spain and Portugal. 4 , 19 Estimated a density of 1.64 ± 0.41 pairs per 100 km 2 in the study area. Nest building or reusing begins in early May 3 , 4 with two eggs usually laid on alternating days. 3 , 20 The study area supports a high density of resident diurnal raptors, including goshawks, common buzzards Buteo buteo , and sparrowhawks Accipiter nisus . 21 These species differ in breeding phenology: common‐buzzards lay eggs on average by March 30, goshawks by April 11, and sparrowhawks by May 24. 10 Honey‐buzzard breeding phenology is less well‐studied but egg‐laying typically occurs between May 18 and 30. 3 All four species exhibit intraspecific territoriality during the breeding season 21 , 22 and often nest in proximity within the same forest patch, either during the same or alternating breeding seasons. 23 Competition for optimal nesting sites can occur, with goshawks often displaying dominance and displacing other species from their nests. 10 , 12 , 24 , 25 2.3. Nest searches As part of a long‐term ecological study on raptors, 4 , 10 an extensive search for nests of the four raptor species was conducted in the study area from 2014 to 2023. Nest searches began in different years depending on the subzone (Supporting Information, Table S1 ). Each nest was regularly monitored during the breeding season to determine its builder and occupancy status, including the presence of eggs (indicating an incubating female) and nestlings. To accurately estimate breeding populations and reproductive success, only pairs that laid eggs were classified as active nests and included in the calculations. 2.4. Breeding phenology and reproductive success We estimated reproductive success (number of fledglings) and goshawk predation of nestlings using camera traps installed in the honey‐buzzard nests (32 nests) or telescopic monitoring (25 nests). Fledgling success was categorized as null (no fledglings), low (one fledgling), or high (two fledglings). To assess the breeding phenology of each active nest, we accessed the nests to weight and measure the wing length of the chicks. The nestling age was estimated by interpolating these variables according to 26 tables. Laying dates were calculated by subtracting 35 days, the typical incubation period for a single egg, from the hatching date of the oldest nestling in each nest. 27 Laying phenology was recorded in Julian days, with January 1st defined as day 1. 2.5. Breeding habitat selection We compared the breeding habitats of honey‐buzzards, based on all active nests recorded between 2014 and 2023 ( n = 57), with randomly selected control points ( n = 77) representing available forest habitat. Control points were established by generating random coordinates within the forested area. 9 , 28 At each control point, the nearest suitable tree for nesting was identified. For a tree to be considered suitable, it had to meet specific criteria: a diameter at breast height (DBH) of ≥50 cm for eucalypts or ≥ 30 cm for oaks and pines. The average distance from each random control point to the selected tree was 25.7 ± 1.9 m (mean ± SE). Habitat selection based on first‐order factors occurs across multiple spatial scales, each potentially influencing fitness. 29 , 30 We evaluated five specific spatial scales in the nesting and control points (Fig. 1 ): Nest tree: the tree used for nesting or considered suitable for nesting (control tree). Nest site: a 10 m radius circular area surrounding nest and control trees. Nest stand: the habitat within a 50 m radius of nest and control trees, sampled using four 10‐m radius circular plots positioned to the N, S, E and W, with habitat variables averaged across these plots. Post‐fledging family area (PFA): the area used by the juveniles from fledging until the young are independent. 30 Based on a satellite‐tagged fledgling in the study area, we estimated the PFA size as the radius of a circle encompassing 95% of the fledgling's positions (Kernel 95%) from the day it left the nest until the start of its post‐nuptial migration. We rounded the estimated radius to 200 m for the PFA of nesting and control points. Nesting territory: the defended area around the nest where the breeding pair deters other conspecifics and minimizes overlap with neighboring territories. 31 We defined the nesting territories as circular areas with a 1700 m radius around the nest and control trees, based on half of the most common distance (mode) between neighboring active honey‐buzzard nests in the study area (3384 m, Supporting Information, Fig. S2 ). Figure 1 Five spatial scales of breeding habitat preferences and forest structure analyzed in the nesting and control points: (i) nest tree, (ii) nest site (a circle with a radius of 10 m), (iii) nest stand (radius of 50 m), (iv) post‐fledging family area (PFA, radius of 200 m), and (v) nesting territory (radius of 1700 m). We assessed intraspecific nearest‐neighbor distance (NND) as an indicator of intraspecific territorial behaviour. 10 For nesting points, intraspecific NND was calculated as the distance to the nearest active honey‐buzzard nest during the same breeding season. For control points, it was determined as the most frequent nearest‐neighbor distance to an active honey‐buzzard nest over the years of study of each subzone (Supporting Information, Table S1 ). Interspecific interactions were measured using interspecific NNDs, representing the distances between honey‐buzzard nests and the nearest active nests of goshawks, common buzzards, and sparrowhawks. For nesting points, interspecific NNDs were calculated based on the nearest active nest of each raptor species within the same breeding season. For control points, they were determined using the most frequent nearest‐neighbor distances to active nest of the three raptors species over the 10‐year study period. For nests active in multiple years, average intraspecific and interspecific NNDs were estimated. We characterized raptor breeding habitats using 118 variables across first‐order (forest composition and structure, canopy height, topography, and land cover types), second‐order (intraspecific NND), and third‐order (interspecific NNDs) factors. These were assessed at both raptor nesting and control points (Supporting Information, Tables S2 and S3 ). Forest sampling occurred in the post‐breeding season (November–December 2022 and 2023) to avoid disturbing raptors. Variables at the PFA and nesting territory scales, along with other landscape variables, were obtained using QGIS 32 with cartographic information from the SIOSE AR project of Land Uses of Spain, 33 a Digital Elevation Model of Spain, 34 and a high‐resolution canopy height model. 35 Mean values (± SE) for each habitat variable were compared using the Mann–Whitney U test (Supporting Information, Table S4 ). 2.6. Statistical analyses Honey‐buzzard habitat preferences were assessed using two complementary approaches: binomial generalized linear models (GLMs) to identify predictive models, and random forest classification models to determine the relative importance of habitat factors. Both approaches involved a binary classification analysis to distinguish between nesting and control points. The response variable was the presence of active nests versus randomly selected control points. In both approaches a cross‐scale analysis was performed using the 118 first‐, second‐, and third‐order predictor variables simultaneously. In the first approach, we followed the methodology of García‐Salgado et al ., 28 using binomial GLMs with a logit link function to examine breeding habitat selection. Predictor variables were selected through a three‐step reduction process: In the first step, the mean of each habitat variable was compared between nesting points and control points using the Mann–Whitney U test with a significance cut‐off of P ≤ 0.05. In the second step, variables satisfying this cut‐off were included in the principal component analysis (PCA). This early filtering of predictor variables was performed to avoid negligible information about nest location in the landscape. We considered a variable to be summarized by a PCA component if the correlation between the two was associated with r ≥ 0.5. In subsequent analyses, the first two PCA components were used as predictors in binomial Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). In the third step of variable reduction, Pearson's correlation analyses were performed with variables not summarized by the PCA components. In the case of strongly correlated pairs of variables (r &gt; 0.6), both variables were considered to estimate a single underlying factor, 36 so only the variable that showed greater significance in the Mann–Whitney U test of the first step was incorporated into subsequent analyses. Model selection was driven by the Akaike Information Criterion corrected for small sample sizes (AICc), using a forward selection process. We considered a cut‐off of ΔAICc &gt;2 units to distinguish models with a better fit. 37 Data analysis was performed in R 3.0.3 38 using the packages ‘lme4’, 39 ‘nlme’ 40 and ‘MuMIn’. 41 In the second approach, following Monteagudo et al ., 9 habitat preferences were analyzed using the random forest classification models, a machine learning technique that builds multiple decision trees based on random samples of the data. 42 , 43 The final prediction is determined by the majority vote from all the trees. 44 To evaluate model performance, the data were randomly split into a training set (70%) and a test set (30%). The test set was used to calculate the F1 score, which combines precision (the ratio of true positives to all predicted positives) and recall (the ratio of true positives to false positives), along with accuracy, derived from a confusion matrix. 45 The relative importance of predictors was determined by the mean decrease in node impurity, using the Gini index as the splitting rule within each decision tree. 44 , 46 Partial dependence plots were generated to illustrate the marginal effect of each habitat factor on nest presence, keeping all other variables at their average values. 47 These plots help visualize how changes in specific factors influence nest presence probability and identify threshold values where this probability increases or peaks. Model building and statistical analyses were performed using the ‘ranger’, 48 ‘caret’, 49 and ‘pdp’ 50 R packages. To examine the relationship between habitat preferences and reproductive performance, we applied linear models (LMs) and generalized linear models (GLMs) using habitat variables identified in the first and second approaches of the habitat selection analyses. LMs were employed to assess laying dates. GLMs with a binomial error distribution were used to analyze fledgling success rates, categorized as 0% (null, zero fledglings), 50% (low, one fledgling), or 100% (high, two fledglings). In both LMs and GLMs, we employed a forward selection model process, based on the variation of AICc values. All mean values reported in this study are accompanied by standard error (SE) to indicate the data variability. 3. RESULTS 3.1. Breeding biology and pair density In the period 2014–2023, honey‐buzzards utilized 57 different nests for egg‐laying: 21 were originally constructed by honey‐buzzards, while others were built by common‐buzzards (eight nests), goshawks (six nests), sparrowhawks (five nests), carrion crows Corvus corone (one nest), and 16 were of unknown builders. Most nests (47 nests, 82.5%) were used once, with the remaining nests being used twice (eight nests, 14.0%) or three times (two nests, 3.5%). Across these nests, in the period 2014–2023 we recorded a total of 69 reproductive events involving egg‐laying. The average distance between active nests in the same breeding season was 5777 ± 658.8 m, ranging from 1699 to 22 603 m ( n = 69). The mean egg‐laying date was May 18th ± 1.15 days with a range from May 9th to June 3rd ( n = 39). Average fledging success was 1.2 fledglings ±0.1 (range 0–2, n = 68 reproductive events), with fledgling production being zero in 21.1% of cases, one fledgling in 32.3%, and two fledglings in 45.6%. Among successful pairs, the average fledging success was 1.58 fledglings ±0.07 (range 1–2, n = 53 reproductive events). In 14 cases (20.3%) of the 69 reproductive events, we documented confirmed or probable instances of nestling or fledgling predation by goshawks. During the period when the four subzones were sampled simultaneously (2020–2023, Supporting Information, Table S1 ), there was an average of 1.41 egg‐laying pairs per 100 km 2 , or 3.22 egg‐laying pairs per 100 km 2 when considering only forested areas. In the subzones I and II, surveyed between 2015 and 2023 (see Supporting Information, Table S1 ), the number of egg‐laying pairs significantly increased over this period (r = 0.87, P &lt; 0.001, r 2 = 0.76, n = 9 years). Based on the 40 variables that showed significant differences between nesting and control points (Mann–Whitney U tests, Supporting Information, Table S4 ), we provided a detailed description of the honey‐buzzard breeding habitat in Supporting Information, Text S1 . 3.2. First approach: habitats preferences based on the GLM procedure 3.2.1. Principal component analysis The first principal component (PC1) accounted for 21.2% of the variance and represented a gradient of forest structure complexity at the nest tree and its surroundings. This included nest tree DBH and height, characteristics of the nest site and nest stand (e.g., maximum tree height, number of forest strata, density of extra mature total and Eucalyptus trees, and species richness of extra mature trees), and mean canopy height at the PFA scale. We designated this first gradient as ‘complexity‐maturity of forest in the nest surroundings’ (Table 1 ). To facilitate understanding, we multiplied the PC1 coordinates by −1, so that negative values in PC1 correspond to lower complexities of forest structure, while positive values indicate higher complexities. The second principal component (PC2) accounted for 12.6% of the variance and represented a gradient of land use types at the nesting territory scale. One end of this gradient is characterized by nesting territories with greater forest coverage, while the opposite end featured territories at lower altitudes, exhibiting more diverse land use types, increased residential area coverage, and longer ecotones between forested land and anthropogenic areas. We referred to this second gradient as ‘anthropization‐productivity of the nesting territory’ (Table 1 ). Second approach: habitat preferences according to the random forest algorithm The F1 Score of 87.5% and Accuracy of 85% demonstrated that the random forest model effectively discriminated between nesting and control points. A primary group of seven influential habitat factors was identified (black bars in Fig. 3 , Table 3 ). This group highlighted that, compared to control points, nesting points exhibited greater structural complexity at the nest stand scale (e.g., higher densities of total and Eucalyptus trees, taller trees, and a greater number of forest strata). Additionally, nesting points showed an increased length of ecotones between forest and anthropogenic land uses (fields and buildings) at the nesting territory scale, along with longer intraspecific NNDs among honey‐buzzard pairs. A secondary group of 10 influential habitat factors was also identified (grey bars in Fig. 3 , Table 3 ). This group emphasized higher structural complexity at the nest tree scale and its surroundings compared to control points (e.g., taller trunks, larger crown areas of the nest tree, and a higher number of extra mature trees at the nest site), as well as a greater presence of anthropogenic land uses in nesting territories (e.g., higher field cover and shorter distances from the nest tree to fields and buildings). Additionally, honey‐buzzard nests were situated at longer distances from active goshawk and sparrowhawk nests than control points. Partial dependence plots, illustrating the marginal effects and trends of the 17 most influential factors, are shown in Supporting Information, Figs S4 and S5 . Relationship between breeding habitat preferences and reproductive performance We examined the relationships between reproductive performance and the two most important predictors identified in the first approach, as well as the 17 most influential predictors from the second approach. Overall, these relationships were weak and non‐significant (Supporting Information, Table S5 ). The only significant model was the one linking fledgling success to PC1 (complexity‐maturity of forest in the nest surroundings), which explained 14.1% of the deviance. A negative relationship was observed between fledgling success and forest structure complexity in the nest surroundings, where positive PC1 values correspond to greater forest complexity (Fig. 4 ). Positive PC1 values indicate greater complexity of the forest structure. 4. DISCUSSION 4.1. Breeding population and reproductive success of honey‐buzzards The density of egg‐laying pairs (1.41 per 100 km 2 , rising to 3.22 pairs considering only woodland areas) was low compared to other European regions. In The Netherlands, 11–12 pairs per 100 km 2 were reported and 15–25 pairs per 100 km 2 if only woodland areas were considered. 22 , 51 , 52 In our study area, density increased from 0.55 pairs before (2004–2013) to 1.64 pairs after (2014–2020) following the arrival of the yellow‐legged hornets in 2014. 4 After the hornet colonization, all honey‐buzzard pairs incorporated this invasive exotic species into their diet, making it their second most important prey. 3 , 4 Although it remains unproven that the increase in breeding pairs was directly caused by the hornet's arrival and the subsequent rise in trophic resources, this is the most plausible explanation. 4 This finding highlights the critical role of trophic resources availability in limiting honey‐buzzard densities in the study area. The average annual fledging success per egg‐laying pair (1.2 fledglings, and 1.58 fledglings only considering successful pairs) was higher than the European average reproductive output (0.8–1.0 young per pair per year), according to. 53 It also surpasses the reproductive output that facilitates population expansion, 0.67 young per pair according to Tjernberg and Ryttman 54 or 1.16 young per pair according to Bijlsma et al . 51 These relatively high fledging rates likely contribute to the breeding population, assuming adult survival rates are adequate. Predation by goshawks was a major threat during the breeding season. We estimated that 20.3% of egg‐laying pairs experienced certain or probable predation of their nestlings or fledglings by goshawks, with occasional predation of adult honey‐buzzards also observed ( unpublished data ). Previous studies identify goshawks as major predators of honey‐buzzards across Europe, potentially impacting their breeding performance. 12 , 25 , 51 4.2. Breeding habitat preferences According to random forest analysis (second approach), vegetation and topography were the most influential factors shaping habitat preferences, particularly around nests within a 200 m‐radius. Honey‐buzzards preferred large nest trees within forest patches with high structural complexity and maturity, likely for protection and concealment. 55 Similar behavior was observed in other forest‐dwelling raptors in the study area, 9 supporting their role as indicators of habitat complexity and maturity in Eucalyptus plantations. 28 Results from the GLMs (first approach) also underscored the importance of forest structure near nests. The most parsimonious model explaining the differences between nesting and control points included only two factors: the complexity‐maturity of forest in the nest surroundings (PC1) and the density of Eucalyptus trees at the stand scale. Other authors also reported honey‐buzzards to prefer mature‐like forest patches with high quality site characteristics (more tree height and DBH), less intense silviculture, and greater tree species richness. 13 , 56 , 57 Further discussion of honey‐buzzard forest type preferences can be found in Supporting Information, Text S2 . Random forest analysis also identified influential first‐order factors at the nesting territory scale. Honey‐buzzards selected areas at lower altitude, with less forest coverage, greater field coverage, and longer ecotones between forests and anthropogenic open land uses. A higher coverage of non‐forested land uses in the nesting territories compared to control ones was probably not related to the use of open habitats as hunting grounds, as honey‐buzzards primarily hunt within forest patches (Ziesemer and Meyburg, 2015 and unpublished data ). Forest patches in lowlands have more productive soils and likely support a higher density of vespid nests, the main prey of honey‐buzzards to feed the nestlings. 3 Therefore, although forest coverage was lower at nesting territories compared to control points, the productivity of these forests in terms of prey availability would likely be higher. Unfortunately, we do not have data on vespid nest densities across different forest habitats to confirm this hypothesis. We do have bird census data, the second most important vertebrate in the diet 3 and, probably, a major prey early in the breeding season (May and June) when vespid nests are still small. 56 Consistent with the breeding habitat preferences of honey‐buzzards, bird density is significantly higher in the agroforestry mosaics at low altitudes compared to the extensive forest patches found in midlands and highlands of the study area (Salvador Rebollo et al ., 2017b). Other authors have also noted honey‐buzzard's breeding preferences for more productive areas with fertile soils. 56 , 57 , 58 , 59 The intraspecific NDD was the 7th most important predictor in the random tree analysis and the most influential factor after vegetation and topographic features. Honey‐buzzards are territorial raptors that nest at a distance from other conspecific pairs. 22 We measured a minimum intraspecific NND of 1699 m and a mean NND of 5986 m at nesting points. Thus, despite the low density of breeding pairs in the study area, social interactions among conspecifics played a role in the spatial distribution of pairs. In Supporting Information, Text S3 we further discuss the relationship between intraspecific territoriality, reversed sexual size dimorphism, and diet in raptors. Interspecific interactions within the diurnal raptor guild, estimated through interspecific NNDs, played a modest role in the random forest analysis. The NNDs to active goshawk, sparrowhawk, common‐buzzard nests were the 14th, 16th and 26th most important predictors, respectively. We detected that honey‐buzzard nests tended to be located farther from active goshawk nests. However, given the findings of other researchers 12 and our own data on goshawk predation pressure on honey‐buzzards, we expected distancing from active goshawk nests to play a more significant role. Honey‐buzzard's strategy for managing goshawk pressure in the study Eucalyptus plantations probably involved multiple components. In addition to distancing themselves from active goshawk nests, honey‐buzzards selected forest patches with greater horizontal and vertical vegetation structure likely to better conceal their nests. During the breeding season, adults and nestlings exhibit secretive and elusive behavior, possibly to avoid detection. 12 , 59 Their preferences for nesting territories with less forest cover and more anthropogenic land use might also be part of the strategy to reduce goshawk attacks, as suggested by other authors. 12 , 13 Interestingly, honey‐buzzard nests were found closer than expected to active common‐buzzard nests. This spatial association could provide indirect protection from goshawk predation as no instances of goshawk predation on adult, fledgling, or nestling common‐buzzards were observed in the study area (Salvador Rebollo et al ., 2017b). The mechanism by which common‐buzzards, a medium‐sized raptor similar in size to honey‐buzzards, protect themselves from goshawks remains unclear and warrants further investigation. It is important to note that the spatial relationships between honey‐buzzards, goshawks, and common buzzards are influenced by decisions made by honey‐buzzards, as they breed later and can identify the locations of active nests of the other raptors. 4.3. Relationship between breeding habitat preferences and reproductive performance The relationship between breeding habitat preferences and reproductive performance was weak and generally non‐significant. The only significant model was the one relating fledgling success to the complexity and maturity of the forest in the nest surroundings (PC1). Contrary to our expectations, forest patches with higher complexity and maturity had lower fledgling success. A plausible explanation for this finding is that preferred habitats may act as density traps. 60 As the density of breeding pairs approaches the carrying capacity of the ecosystem, competition among neighboring pairs increases, leading to decreased fledgling success. García‐Salgado et al . 28 noted that negative density‐dependent effects do not necessarily indicate that preferred habitats are of lower quality for raptors. Over the long term, preferred habitats may yield higher reproductive success if they are consistently occupied and produce fledglings regularly. We had predicted that preferred habitats would be occupied first, leading to earlier reproduction, however, any significant models linking laying phenology to habitat preferences was observed. This prediction is particularly applicable to resident raptors, for whom habitat quality before the breeding season likely influences fat reserves and the capacity of females to produce eggs. In contrast, for migrant raptors like honey‐buzzards, conditions on wintering grounds in Africa may have a more significant impact on the energetic status of adults when they arrive at their breeding grounds in Europe. Consistent with this idea, we observe a significant positive relationship between the date of arrival and the date of egg laying (r = 0.868, P &lt; 0.0001, n = 13, unpublished data ), which explained 75.4% of the variance in the date of egg laying. This suggests that the quality of wintering grounds is crucial for an early prenuptial migration and laying phenology, as honey‐buzzards rarely feed during migration. 61 4.4. Implications for forest management to promote honey‐buzzard breeding populations Rebollo et al . 4 found that each breeding pair of honey‐buzzards eliminates at least 15 to 61 yellow‐legged hornet colonies during the raptor's stay in Europe to breed. More recent estimates show that the number of colonies eliminated by honey‐buzzards is even higher ( unpublished data ). Martín‐Ávila et al . 3 found that this predatory pressure has a demographic effect on the invasive hornet and reduces the abundance of workers in the vicinity of raptor nests. One way to promote biological control of this pest would be to increase the density of breeding pairs of honey‐buzzards and improve their spatial distribution. The results of the present study supported that we could promote this regulatory ecosystem service and the applied biocontrol perspective of honey‐buzzards through increasing the coverage of high‐quality habitats capable of supporting breeding populations of honey‐buzzards in the long‐term. The present study identified honey‐buzzard breeding habitat preferences and examined their relationships with pair density and reproductive performance. This information is crucial for guiding forest management decisions aimed at enhancing high‐quality breeding habitats for honey‐buzzards. Our habitat preference model predicted that improving forest structure in breeding habitats could increase pair density (Fig. 2 ). The probability of finding breeding pairs increased from the least favorable scenario (P2, young plantations with low structural complexity) to the most favorable scenario (P0, mixed mature Eucalyptus stands with high structural complexity) across the range of intraspecific NNDs. Given that honey‐buzzards have a radius of action of, at least, 1000 m for managing the abundance of yellow‐legged hornet workers, 5 an intraspecific NND of 3000 m would ensure effective control of the invasive yellow‐legged hornet workers in the study area. The model suggested that such a pair density would have a greater than 50% probability of occurring in the most favorable scenario (P0). Despite the negative effect that increasing the density of pairs may have on the production of fledgling in individual nests of honey‐buzzards, improving breeding habitats is a correct measure of forest management because it will increase the density of breeding pairs, breeding territory occupancy, and regularity in production of fledglings in the medium and long term. In the study area, forests are predominantly dominated by exotic Eucalyptus plantations. Our results suggest that these plantations can offer suitable breeding habitats for honey‐buzzards, if forest management practices to plant or restore forest patches are appropriate. Honey‐buzzard's preferences for specific woody elements were concentrated primarily within a small radius around their nests, including the post‐fledging family area (PFA). Consequently, efforts to improve and restore breeding forest habitats for this raptor should focus on areas with a radius of up to 200 m around nest sites. These intervention areas should situate within larger forest patches. This paper provides insights into the minimum and optimal values for the most relevant first‐, second‐, and third‐order factors within these intervention plots (Supporting Information, Figs S4–S6 ). To achieve the desired mature woody elements, a medium‐ to long‐term planning approach is essential. 62 Depending on local forest productivity, this planning horizon may need to extend for at least 35 years even though Eucalyptus is a fast‐growing tree species. Given the territorial nature of honey‐buzzards, it is important to space intervention plots at least 1699 m apart, corresponding to the minimal intraspecific NND measured between honey‐buzzard pairs in the study area, which would improve the spatial distribution of breeding pairs. 5. CONCLUSIONS Eucalyptus plantations can serve as suitable nesting habitats for the European honey buzzard if managed to meet the reproductive needs of the raptor species. There is a positive correlation between the structural complexity of forest plantations and the density of breeding honey buzzard pairs. Consequently, greater structural complexity enhances the plantations' capacity to provide regulatory services against the yellow‐legged hornet. The honey buzzard showed a preference for mature forest patches characterized by large trees, multiple canopy layers, high tree species richness, and high tree density. The management of forest plantations should promote the presence of these indicators of maturity. Vegetation and topographic features (first‐order factors) were the primary ecological drivers influencing habitat preferences. Secondary factors included intraspecific interactions and territoriality, while interspecific interactions within the guild of forest diurnal raptors (third‐order factors) were relatively less influential. Combining analytical tools such as binomial Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) to identify predictive models, and random forest classification models to determine the relative importance of habitat factors, proved to be an effective approach. This methodology identified habitat preferences of the native avian predator, offering practical applications in forest management aimed at enhancing regulatory ecosystem services.
