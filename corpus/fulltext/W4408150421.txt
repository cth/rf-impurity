Abstract Late-onset Alzheimer’s disease (LOAD) is a progressive and complex neurodegenerative disorder of the aging population. LOAD is characterized by cognitive decline, such as deterioration of memory, loss of intellectual abilities, and other cognitive domains resulting from due to traumatic brain injuries. Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) presents a complex genetic etiology that is still unclear, which limits its early or differential diagnosis. The Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) enable the exploration of individual variants' statistical interactions at candidate loci, but univariate analysis overlooks interactions between variants. Machine learning (ML) algorithms can capture hidden, novel, and significant patterns while considering nonlinear interactions between variants to understand the genetic predisposition for complex genetic disorders. When working on different platforms, majority voting cannot be applied because the attributes differ. Hence, a new post-ML ensemble approach was developed to select significant SNVs via multiple genotyping platforms. We proposed the EnSCAN framework using a new algorithm to ensemble selected variants even from different platforms to prioritize candidate causative loci, which consequently helps improve ML results by combining the prior information captured from each dataset. The proposed ensemble algorithm utilizes the chromosomal locations of SNVs by mapping to cytogenetic bands, along with the proximities between pairs and multimodel Random Forest (RF) validations to prioritize SNVs and candidate causative genes for LOAD. The scoring method is scalable and can be applied to any multiplatform genotyping study. We present how the proposed EnSCAN scoring algorithm prioritizes candidate causative variants related to LOAD among three GWAS datasets. Introduction Identifying the epistatic interactions between multiple genetic variations leading to complex human diseases is essential in developing diagnostic methods and new treatments. Among genetic variations, single nucleotide variations (SNVs) have received much attention in disease prediction. Models that narrow down the prioritized SNVs enriching the causative biomarkers at different loci are promising for discovering the molecular etiology and revealing the phenotypes of complex diseases such as Alzheimer's disease (AD). The late-onset form of AD [ 1 ] develops after age 65, but the causes of this disease are not yet fully understood. However, many single-nucleotide variations (SNVs) have been identified and confirmed to be associated [ 2 – 4 ] with AD. Currently, a diagnosis of late-onset Alzheimer's disease is partially available based on clinical evaluation and imaging; however, many patients cannot be diagnosed at the early stages of AD. Discovering SNVs as biological markers indicating susceptibility to complex diseases can potentially improve early diagnostic accuracy and prevent this disease in clinical decision-making. Additionally, identifying causative SNVs among the set of biomarkers can reveal the molecular etiology, pathogenetic changes, and related phenotypes of complex diseases such as Alzheimer's disease (AD). Straightforward approaches such as genome-wide association studies (GWASs) are based on testing univariate hypotheses. GWASs do not assess the potential interactions of each genetic marker but calculate statistical significance based on statistical data distribution assumptions. Although this standard method provides information about novel loci for complex diseases, it has several limitations. GWASs do not consider the genetic interactions of each biomarker, which are the most influential. Although this approach reveals top-ranked significant correlations associated with a particular disease, it does not provide a prediction model considering statistically significant biomarkers [ 5 ]. At this point, intelligent computational techniques are needed to obtain patterns underlying the etiology of a complex disease. Predictive models that integrate and assess genetic variations and clinical findings have been proposed recently for monitoring the progression of Alzheimer’s disease and diagnosing this disease. Although machine learning (ML) methods for knowledge extraction are widely used in some of these studies, multimodel or hybrid methods have not been implemented, and information gathering from different datasets has not been considered. ML techniques can identify significantly associated biomarkers by prioritizing variables, which leads to the development of prognostic models and decision support systems. Due to the availability of large-scale and complex biological data, modern biology has used advanced machine-learning techniques [ 6 , 7 ]. ML methodologies have also been exploited and adapted for GWASs as preprocessing steps since GWAS data can be considered a classification problem described by thousands of individual genetic variations. The random forest (RF) algorithm, a supervised ML method for classification learning, provides significant improvement in determining classification accuracy by voting on each tree. Hence, significant attribute selection can be performed among the best-split attributes. In addition to building an accurate model, this method can be used as a preprocessing step for incorporating feature selection and interactions in the training process. Moreover, RF analysis produces measures of variable importance [ 8 ] that can be used to rank and select predictor variables. Recent studies have also reviewed the use of RF for genomic data classification, feature selection, pathway analysis, and prediction, association, or detection of the underlying reason for complex diseases. In this study, a meta-analysis of three different LOAD datasets on two different genotyping platforms (Affymetrix and Illumina) was performed with multimodel model building. First, the multimodel classification approaches are handled separately for each dataset, providing the highest classification performance independently. Following ML modeling, different independent models representing each dataset must be assembled to integrate information whose metadata differs among datasets. Since attributes differ on different platforms and majority voting cannot be applied, a novel ensemble approach was developed to select significant SNVs from multiple microarray platforms. We focused on integrating (ensemble) multimodel ML classifiers as the core problem to find a general prediction model from prior knowledge where there are no common representative SNVs within different platforms. We mapped SNVs identified from each dataset to cytogenetic locations to find commonalities, so SNVs are mapped to chromosomal locations adding a new informational dimension. We utilized chromosomal locations, linkage disequilibrium (proximities between pairs), characterization (appearing) with multiplatform databases, and statistical significance captured by ML modeling to add value to the prioritization since variants are selected repetitively in the model while building the scoring of prioritized SNVs. The significant variants proposed by the ensemble model will accelerate new studies focusing on the early diagnosis and prediction of LOAD and the development of new therapeutics. Diagnosing and treating a disease such as Alzheimer's disease with a high burden in the early phases will eventually lead to significant social and economic benefits. Materials and methods Datasets The data used in this study were obtained from the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) database (adni.loni.usc.edu) and the NCBI Database of Genotypes and Phenotypes(dbGaP) ( https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gap ). The dbGaP, developed for storing and sharing genotype and phenotypic data, family history, and other clinical findings of association studies regarding a particular disease, is supported and published by the NCBI. On the other hand, ADNI constitutes an ongoing, longitudinal, multicenter study designed to develop clinical, imaging, genetic, and biochemical biomarkers for the early detection and tracking of Alzheimer's disease (AD). The ADNI collects, validates, stores, and utilizes data such as MRI and PET images; genetic data; cognitive test data; and blood biomarker data biomarkers to predict Alzheimer's disease. The GenADA study [ 9 ] population consists exclusively of Caucasian participants over 65. This includes Caucasian patients diagnosed with probable Alzheimer’s Disease attending memory disorders clinics and ethnically matched Caucasian controls with no history of cognitive problems. The NCRAD Project [ 10 ] recruited elderly community-dwelling individuals aged over 65 from two distinct groups: African Americans residing in Indianapolis and Yoruba individuals living in Ibadan, Nigeria. The study utilized a consistent research design, methodologies, and investigative teams across both populations. ADNI [ 11 ] primarily focuses on older adults, with a specific emphasis on Caucasian populations in the North of the United States and Canada. Through controlled access, GWAS datasets provided by the ADNI (210 controls and 344 cases), GenADA (777 controls and 798 cases), and NCRAD by dbGAP initiatives (1310 controls and 1289 cases) were used and analyzed to construct independent models. The Affymetrix Mapping250K_Nsp and Mapping250K_Sty, Illumina Human610_Quadv1_B 500 K, and Illumina Human610-Quad BeadChip platforms were used for these initiatives, as described in Fig. 1 , and 620,901, 410,907, and 585,295 QC-passed SNVs were included in this study for GenADA, NCRAD and ADNI, respectively. Fig. 1 Workflow for Model Building and EnSCAN: The proposed EnScan ensemble workflow is visualized in three steps a Association analysis, b Feature Selection and Model Building c Ensemble Scoring for Prioritizing Causative Variants Development of the workflow GWAS analysis is employed to discover statistically meaningful SNVs using the PLINK tool for each dataset. PLINK is a widely used tool that employs Chi-Square statistical tests. The chi-square test effectively assesses the relationship between categorical variables, such as genotypes (AA, Aa, aa) and illness status (cases and controls), and is used in GWAS to identify significant SNVs associated with a specific disease. As the GWAS only identifies the independent statistical significances of variations, we used this information to filter the non-informative SNVs that are not directly related to the disease (Fig. 1 a). After the initial dimension reduction analysis for filtering out statistically insignificant variations based on GWAS, the SNVs significantly associated with LOAD were used for further analysis. We have reported that the RF-RF approach outperformed hybrid models for the GenADA dataset [ 12 – 14 ]. As the top-performing modeling approach was GWAS filtering followed by two-step RF, we developed LOAD-RF-RF models for all three datasets (Fig. 1 b). Next, we integrated information from three ML models for each dataset with the proposed ensemble method (Fig. 1 c). Since RF-selected variants from different datasets differ due to the use of sequencing platforms, SNVs are mapped to chromosomal positions to locate and analyze neighboring variants. An ensemble scoring algorithm is proposed to calculate the information provided regarding cytogenetic bands that emphasize disease-related and disease-causing variations, as shown in detail in Fig. 1 . While scoring, a three-row frame slips down within a band containing SNVs. The frame size is selected as three because the information comes from three datasets. Linkage disequilibrium (LD) for each SNV, the incidence of variations in all the databases, and validation with the RF model were vital indicators for assigning scores to prioritize and selecting the most critical variations in AD incidence. Association analysis In the data preprocessing step, our goal was to eliminate noise, clean redundancy and determine the associated SNV subset by feature extraction or dimension reduction methods within the data before modeling. The quality control and exclusion criteria before the analysis with PLINK for GWAS analysis are performed using the initial parameters such as Hardy Weinberg Equilibrium (poor-quality genotyping can result in heterozygotes being called homozygotes), MAF (low power to detect any association to a disease) and call rate (SNV information is not missing). Autosomal SNVs with Hardy Weinberg Equilibrium value &lt; 5e-07, MAF &lt; 0.05, and call rate &lt; 0.99 are excluded. GWAS is used only for prefiltering; the chi-square test is applied with the PLINK tool on each dataset individually, providing p-values for determining the significance of the associated SNVs. Adjusted p -values (e.g., using Bonferroni or FDR correction) in GWAS research are intended to correct for multiple comparisons, which is essential in hypothesis testing and control for false positives but at the cost of filtering out many potentially useful features. When the False Discovery Rate (FDR) threshold for multiple testing with a GWAS significance value of 0.0001 (10 –5 ) is applied, the number of significant SNVs drops down, which is not an adequate input size for s machine learning algorithms (Supplementary Fig. 1). Finding predictive features becomes the new objective when modeling with ML algorithms such as RF, instead of finding statistically significant relationships. Because adjusted p-values place more emphasis on strict statistical significance than on optimizing the model's capacity to catch intricate patterns, they may eliminate potentially informative SNVs. To avoid information loss while considering nonlinear relationships between disease-related SNVs, Type I error (alpha = 0.01) has been used as a threshold value. This allows for the detection of nonlinear patterns associated with the disease through machine learning algorithms. Thus, the contributions of these patterns to the model can be assessed without correction for multiple hypotheses. Feature selection and model building Dimension reduction can be beneficial for computational efficiency and improve the accuracy of the ML model. After GWAS with PLINK, RF is employed in tandem with dimension reduction and supervised learning model implementation [ 15 ]. Since we are considering nonlinear patterns during the dimension reduction by applying ML methods, false positives tend to be eliminated using one of the supervised learning algorithms called Random Forest. The dataset is partitioned by bagging (bootstrap) techniques, which are used to reduce the variance of predictions by assembling the results of multiple classifiers obtained on different subsamples of the same dataset. Sampling was performed on the original data, and new datasets were generated for each tree construction. The data that were out of training were considered out of the bag (OOB) for assessing the accuracy of the trees. First, parameter estimations of the RF model for the number of trees and the number of features that are randomly chosen for each new split are determined. Hence, the value of "the number of trees" is determined by the different sizes of trees. In addition, the "number of randomly selected variables" is also tuned. To detect the optimized RF parameter, the "RANGER" package was used in R (including the "randomForest" package), as the RANGER library has functionality for tuning RF. It automates the fitting of multiple versions of an Ensemble Scoring Model by changing its crucial parameters and tree construction samples. An increase in the "number of trees" requires additional computational effort, but the model's accuracy increases as this increases. Sequential values are given to estimate the tuning parameters. After the parameters are optimized the final RF model is trained. A confusion matrix is formed to assess the general accuracy, specificity, and sensitivity information based on the general majority voting on OOB samples. The best model whose parameters produce the highest accuracy in terms of OOB samples is set as the final model. Finally, to detect the optimized RF parameter and variable importance values using t-test statistics for multi-model, the "RANGER" package is used in R for each dataset (including the "randomForest" package) as the RANGER library has functionality for tuning RF. This multimodel RF is performed to identify significant SNVs before implementing the Ensemble Scoring Algorithm. The most important SNVs are identified using a permutation test on the first run of RF model with optimized parameters that achieved the best accuracy. This approach allows for the identification of variants associated with AD, nonlinearly. The primary objective of the second RF application is to validate the disease-associated variants selected in the first RF model. Ensemble scoring for prioritizing causative variants In the literature, a few ensemble methods that work within the same dataset [ 5 , 16 – 19 ] are proposed. So far, there has been no ensemble methodology for analyzing multi-platforms whose attributes differ. Here, the output of multi-run RF models is used as prior knowledge to merge all information to calculate an importance score for each SNV that gives insight into LOAD susceptibility. As the novelty of the Ensemble Scoring Algorithm, we have utilized the genomic vicinity as an additional dimension where all RF-selected SNVs are mapped to their chromosomal positions on the genome, and cytogenetic bands are set as slots to survey through a sliding window approach for scoring (Fig. 2 ). In this study, we define a "window” as a specific segment of the genome sequence that is being analyzed. This segment can be defined by a certain number of base pairs, and within this window, where variations of interest can be searched. Fig. 2 Mapping of SNVs to their genomic positions (cytogenetic locations) on chromosomes: All RF-selected SNVs are mapped to their chromosomal positions on the genome sequence. The genome mapping reveals associated SNVs that are in close vicinity to each other for scanning prioritized SNVs from different dataset models together Since tagged SNVs do not overlap between different platforms, we developed a framework that maps individual SNVs to chromosomal locations on genes to observe neighboring variants from different datasets. The SNPnexus [ 20 ] was used to determine the annotation of SNVs. SNPnexus is a web-based tool for annotating variants, aimed at facilitating the process of selecting and prioritizing known and novel genomic alterations. We annotated our SNV lists with SNPnexus to search the Genetic Association Studies (GAD) and the NHGRI Catalogue of Published Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) databases to compare genes and related diseases. Selecting GRCh37/hg19 as a human assembly, we used European (non-Finnish) MAF as the population. SNiPA [ 21 ] also determines pairwise linkage disequilibrium (LD). The correlation between the phenotype and the marker allele should indicate the presence of the causal SNV in LD. Capturing all LD blocks in the genomic region provides additional information for detecting variants predisposing patients to the disease [ 22 ]. The LD correlation coefficient was determined during the scoring process. LDs of each SNV are defined and labeled. Finally, all SNVs identified in the LOAD-RF-RF model via three platforms with multistep models were scored following the sliding window algorithm for prioritization. Three factors are used in the Ensemble Scoring Algorithm: (1) the number of platforms in the scoring window showing variants from different datasets is concentrated in the same region, (2) linkage disequilibrium (LD) occurs with neighbors, and (3) whether the SNV is selected after multistep RF is significant. Based on these three variables, each variant is assessed by exploiting the neighboring SNVs in the sliding window, which is a method to define SNVs’ scores. The “sliding window” is a tool for scanning the SNV list that is sorted by the cytogenetic locations. Here the aim is to evaluate each SNV using different aspects as explained in the previous response. Database listing is one of the evaluation aspects whose maximum value is equal to the cardinality of databases used in the study (Fig. 3 ). Fig. 3 Flowchart of the Ensemble Scoring Algorithm: The number of SNVs in each window was set to three to analyze multiple datasets for calculating the importance of SNVs The window size is determined by the number of datasets used in the study and its size is equal to the number of datasets. During the analysis, for some SNVs, the window can be extended if there is a linkage between the target SNV and its successor SNV in the list. To analyze the entire list of SNVs, the sliding window is moved by one unit starting from the top of the list. So, the step size is 1. In this way, each SNV can be listed in several windows at different locations at each time and each SNV can be evaluated multiple times by using its neighboring information in each window. The steps of the algorithm are given in Algorithm 1. All SNVs selected by the RF model are scored using the Ensemble Scoring Algorithm. In Step 6, each SNV is scored three times, as listed in different windows. Algorithm 1 Ensemble scoring algorithm The scoring formula for each SNV has three components: one from database listing, one from LD linkage, and the final component refers to whether the SNV is selected by the RF-RF model. The Ensemble Score of an SNV is calculated with the formula below: 1 S c o r e ( S N V ) = ( 1 * d ) + ( 0.1 * l ) + ( 0.01 * v ) The score is a representation for classifying an SNV using an evaluation based on three aspects: (i) database listing, (ii) LD linkage, and (iii) RF-RF modeling result. Here, there is a hierarchy between those three aspects of evaluation. As the main goal of the study is to ensemble results from independent GWAS studies, database listing has a higher hierarchy (can also be interpreted as importance) than the other two aspects, LD linkage and RF-RF modeling results have an equal hierarchy where LD linkage has priority in evaluation. To represent this evaluation structure, a three-digit system of representation is used. To present the hierarchy the first digit (which denotes the score of the database listing) is separated by (.) notation. Ordering information between LD linkage and RF-RF modeling result is represented by the second and third digits where the second digit denotes precedence in evaluation. When the number of datasets is three, "d" can be 1, 2, or 3. Based on the predefined LD correlation coefficient, considering the neighborhoods in the window, "l" depends on the number of independent SNVs in the defined sliding window; hence, "l" can be 1, 2, or 3. If the post and pre neighbors are in LD, “l” is assigned the lowest score of 1. "v" indicates that an SNV is selected by RF-RF modeling, so the corresponding "v" equals 1 if the SNV is selected via RF-RF. When analyzing three different datasets, the maximum score for an SNV was 3.31, while the minimum score was 1.10. In summary, the score can be an element of S ∈ {1.10, 1.11, 1.20, 1.21, 1.30, 1.31, 2.10, 2.11, 2.20, 2.21, 2.30, 2.31, 3.10, 3.11, 3.20, 3.21, 3.30, 3.31}. A higher score for an SNV first indicates that the locus is associated with the condition according to multiple studies, and multiple unlinked SNVs are associated with that locus. Further selection of an SNV through a multistep RF-RF model revealed the candidate causative variants in that window. Functional enrichment After the GWAS algorithm was run for the ensemble and scores for individual SNVs were calculated, pathway enrichment and visualization of the data were performed following the protocol [ 23 ]. Pathway enrichment analyses were performed with g:Profiler, a public web server for characterizing and manipulating gene lists. Functional enrichment analysis (overrepresentation analysis [ORA] or gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA)) of AD-related single-nucleotide variants (SNVs) was performed to map genes to known functional information sources and detect significantly enriched terms. The GO Molecular Function, GO Cellular Component, GO Biological Process, and Reactome pathways were determined using the g:GOSt component of the g:Profiler tool for the variants reported by the ensemble model. All analyses were performed with default attributions and a significance threshold of 0.05 to obtain the GO molecular function, GO cellular component, GO biological process, and Reactome pathways for the SNVs whose ensemble scores were calculated. The p-value of the enrichment of pathways was computed using Fisher's exact test, and the Bonferroni correction was used for multiple testing corrections. EnrichmentMap, a Cytoscape plugin, was subsequently used for functional enrichment visualization by creating networks from Gene Ontology (GO) annotations and Reactome pathway information. All analyses were completed with a p -value of 0.05, an FDR q-value cutoff of 0.01, and an edge similarity cutoff of 03(Jaccard metric). Multiplatform variant prioritization via the EnSCAN algorithm The ensemble algorithm scores each SNV in an iterated scoring window. Hence, for each SNV, three different scores are calculated, where the location of the SNV within the window changes for each scoring. In this study, as we are ensembling data from three different datasets (the initial number of SNVs in the window is three), all the variants were scored via the Ensemble Scoring Algorithm (EnSCAN) [ 24 ], and the highest-scoring variants were identified. The initial number of SNVs in the window depends on the number of independent datasets studied. If four different datasets were used, the initial window size would be defined as 4, and four scores would be calculated: top, internal, internal, and bottom. The sliding window algorithm creates a framework and can also be used in multi-dataset studies. The distribution of calculated scores is presented in Fig. 8 . Additionally, 13% of the mutations were associated with various neuropsychiatric disorders, and the remaining 14.5% were linked to non-neurological or cancer-related phenotypes. Evaluation of ensemble scores Given that EnSCAN class scores are discrete, representing distinct classes of SNVs rather than continuous values, establishing a rigid threshold is not recommended. Each class corresponds to different characteristics of the selected SNVs, and applying a strict threshold could obscure important data related to disease. Therefore, it is essential to account for the unique features of each class when interpreting the results. For instance, the class with a score of 3.31 represents the highest level of significance or relevance an SNV can achieve, indicating strong support across all datasets and robust associations with the disease. This scoring range reflects the extent to which each SNV is associated with the disease of interest, integrating information from multiple datasets and the results of various analytical models. The EnSCAN class with a score of 3.31 includes SNVs selected across all three datasets from independent linkage disequilibrium (LD) regions, marking them as the top prioritized variants. In contrast, lower-scoring classes indicate a weaker effect of the disease-associated SNVs. For each score, enrichment studies have been performed to identify molecular pathways associated with AD. As with other high-throughput studies, different score classes can be utilized depending on the aim of the downstream applications. The top-scoring variants in the class of 3.31 can be suggested for experimental validation studies with independent case controls, whereas lower-scoring classes such as 2.31 can be utilized for enrichment and predictive analysis as they will provide a wider perspective for the conditions under study. In our study, we highlight the pathways that may be important in the etiology of LOAD and provide insights into how certain scores correspond with the underlying biological mechanisms. Through an analysis of these correlations, a deeper comprehension of the biological background of the results and pinpointing candidate areas for therapeutic intervention can be gained. To present the utility of each EnSCAN class score, where some classes had 100 s of variants we needed an evaluation method that can handle a large query list and visualize the results for easing the understanding of the information. So, an enrichment analysis based on the Gene Ontologies and Reactome Pathway is selected to reveal the hidden to observe relations at the molecular and disease level between the selected variants. We perform functional enrichment analysis, overrepresentation analysis (ORA), and gene set enrichment analysis for the EnSCAN variants with various scoring categories as validation methods. Here, we mapped single-nucleotide variants (SNVs) across genes to access known functional information sources and detected significantly enriched terms for identifying efficacious enrichments related to Alzheimer’s disease. Understanding the genetic underpinnings of complex tissues and individual cell lineages is crucial for developing improved diagnostic methods and therapeutics. Tissue-specific networks provide a new means to generate hypotheses related to the molecular basis of human disease. Neuronal system development pathways are also critical in neurodegenerative diseases, such as AD, strategies to improve the symptoms include different means to stimulate neurogenesis. The regulatory mechanisms of stem cell neurogenesis or functional integration of these neurons have been explored to provide the basis for grafted stem cell therapy. An overview of the pathology of AD involving different neural and glial cell types and a summary of current strategies for experimental stem cell treatments and their putative future use in clinical settings has been reviewed in [ 27 ]. Shirwany et al. [ 28 ] shows that abnormal "ion channels" (especially those showing selectivity for cations such as Ca2 +) might be a reason for the toxic mechanism underlying AD pathology. According to a recent study, "peptides or phosphopeptides" of common plasma proteins are associated with an increased frequency of death according to chi-square tests and/or precursor intensity in AD patients [ 29 ]. Increases in the mean precursor intensity of peptides from common plasma proteins such as DISC1, EXOSC5, UBE2G1, SMIM19, NXNL1, PANO, EIF4G1, KIR3DP1, MED25, MGRN1, OR8B3, MGC24039, POLR1A, SYTL4, RNF111, IREB2, ANKMY2, SGKL, SLC25A5, and CHMP3 were associated with AD. An impairment of the "extracellular matrix" [ 30 ] has been reported to play a role in Alzheimer's disease, as indicated by a focus on synaptic transmission, amyloid-β-plaque generation and degradation, tau protein production, oxidative stress response, and the inflammatory response. The extracellular matrix comprises various macromolecules secreted by cells, namely, collagen, elastin, fibronectin, laminin, and glycoproteins; regulating the expression of individual components is an essential step in stabilizing or improving the course of the disease. When collagen forms as fibrils around vulnerable neurons, it blocks Aβ proteins from binding to the cells. Therefore, its components are potential targets and biomarkers for developing and treating AD. Fig. 13 Enrichment Enrichment Analysis of Variants with EnSCAN Class Score of 3.31 Discussion The development of high-throughput genotyping and next-generation sequencing technologies enables genetic epidemiological analysis of large-scale data. These advances led to the identification of single nucleotide variant (SNV) profiles associated with various complex diseases. Many research centers collect GWAS data and share it for advanced analysis to obtain further meaningful information to determine the genetic etiology of a disease. Integrated meta-analyses of multiple GWAS data from different centers have reported the identification of strongly associated variants, but revealing causative variants is impossible. Our main goal was to develop a methodology for ensemble analysis of GWAS data from different centers and genotyping platforms to reveal associated cytogenetic loci and focus on variants with a higher probability of being causative. To integrate multiplatform data, we investigated ML methods for knowledge extraction. RF-RF models are trained for each dataset of Alzheimer's disease. An assembly of prioritized variants selected through RF-RF models is formed by mapping variants selected in each RF-RF model to their cytogenetic loci. The EnSCAN scoring system is proposed to survey all selected variants and categorize variants according to whether they are on a locus independently selected in all GWAS datasets analyzed. The core of the present study was the ensemble of prior information derived from three different models containing different representative variants, along with the newly proposed EnSCAN algorithm. The enrichment analysis for variants based on their EnSCAN class score showed how biological processes and annotations are narrowed down as the EnSCAN class score increases, allowing researchers to focus their studies on enriched biological processes through a smaller list of associated variants. Here, we present a unique study aiming to reveal hidden patterns by combining the results of different multiplatform genotyping studies. Single-nucleotide variant (SNV) biomarkers associated with the early or differential diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease were determined as proposed by the Ensemble Scoring algorithm after machine learning models were developed to identify significant patterns considering complex interactions between variants. The EnSCAN class score is proposed to prioritize LOAD-RF-RF variants neighboring LD-independent variants in their cytogenetic loci from two or three datasets (EnSCAN class score of 2.31 or 3.31). This approach allowed us to minimize the list of LOAD-associated variants without losing representation of the enriched biological processes. Hence, in future ensemble studies for multiplatform GWASs, EnSCAN class scores of 2.31 and 3.31 can be selected to further investigate the causative variants among the associated SNVs. A total of 85 variants were identified with the LOAD-RF-RF model, with a maximum score of 3.31, and 29 of the variants in protein-coding genes were shown to be associated with known disease phenotypes, including Alzheimer's disease (AD) and other AD-related conditions, such as cholesterol metabolism, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disorders, and immunological issues. In addition to various neuropsychiatric disorders and other disease phenotypes, interestingly, there was a strong representation of tobacco use disorder. The open targets platform score is aggregated across sources such as GWAS, UK Biobank, gene expression, mouse orthology, tissue specificity, and distribution to rank the list of gene-disease associations [ 25 ]. In the platform, 4626 genes are listed as LOAD-associated genes with a score ranging from 0.8 to 0.001. The 11 genes that are cross-listed had low ranking scores ranging from 0.008 to 0.002, except the SLC18A2 gene which scored 0.4. Even though we were able to observe 11 of the EnSCAN-selected genes in the gene-disease association list such as open targets they were not top-prioritized probably due to considering the associations individually while aggregating the score. The main strength of the EnSCAN is ensembling the multi-models of LOAD-RF-RF from different platforms, which reveals non-linear interactions between variants. APOE gene variants are known to increase the predictive power of the TSVR. The results [ 34 ] suggest that the dose of the APOE ε4 or ε2 allele contributes independently to the differential diagnosis of AD, especially in patients with early-onset and familiar forms of the disease. One limitation of our study is that the genotype of the APOE gene was not reported in all three datasets (NCRAD, ADNI, and GenADA). Since the information from APOE alleles was not incorporated into the models, we cannot further discuss the effects of these alleles on LOAD or their interactions with the variants selected in this study. Although we have shown links to AD via enrichment analysis and disease phenotypes, the SNVs detected by the LOAD-RF-RF model should be examined in a clinical context to determine the predictive power of the EnSCAN class score for early or differential LOAD diagnosis. The discovery of new associated and causative variants for AD through LOAD-RF-RF modeling and EnSCAN scoring can lead to the development of cost- and time-effective diagnoses and an understanding of the pathophysiology leading to neurodegeneration. These studies will accelerate the identification of new studies focusing on early and differential diagnosis of AD and the development of new therapeutics. Diagnosing and treating such a high-burden disease in the early phases will eventually bring significant social and economic benefits. Overall, the novelty of our approach is based on the assembly of multiplatform GWAS data along with the proposed EnSCAN scoring of variants. The proposed EnSCAN class score of variants selected with machine learning modeling can capture hidden, novel, and significant patterns considering nonlinear interactions between variants to understand the genetic predisposition for complex genetic disorders, where multiple variants determine risk. Analyzing independent and large data sets using ML methods will enable us to derive more detailed information about the complex genetic background and molecular etiology of Alzheimer’s disease. This framework is a promising approach for post-GWAS analysis of other complex genetic disorders. Supplementary Information Supplementary Material 1: Supplementary Table 1.
