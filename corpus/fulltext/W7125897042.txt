Abstract Field experiments are complex to interpret due to interactions between genotypes, environment, plant development and cultivation practices. This complexity challenges the accurate phenotyping of individual plant traits over the season. Here, we quantified the primary sources of seasonal variation in stomatal conductance (g s ) across 15 grapevine cultivar–rootstock combinations within a large-scale phenotyping platform, comprising over 6000 observations. Environment-related traits and date of measurement accounted for up to 76% of the variance, potentially obscuring cultivar–rootstock effects. Therefore, we integrated machine learning, spatiotemporal normalization of the g s response, and the use of mixed models to disentangle the influences of environmental factors, plant material and crop performance related traits. After spatio-temporal normalization, cultivar and cultivar–rootstock interactions explained over 25% of the variation in g s , and Grenache exhibited the most conservative water-use behavior resulting in high water-use efficiency. Specific rootstock–scion combinations also exhibited smaller, but still significant, differences in g s and water-use efficiency, highlighting the specificity arising from the interaction within each rootstock–scion combination. The high variability in g s indicates that accurate quantification of rootstock–scion contributions to key traits in field studies is complex and requires accounting for spatial heterogeneity driven by the environment. Introduction Plants are essential indicators of global climate diversity ( Kottek et al. 2006 ). Combined with geographical and pedological characteristics, climate diversity has created multiple biomes comprising many plant functional types ( Woodward et al. 2004 ). This interdependence between climate and plant traits reflects the adaptation of specific functional types to their changing environment with the goal of maximizing growth and survival over time ( De Deyn et al. 2008 ). The traits that define particular functional types are critical for maximizing niche adaptation in ecosystems, but can also be leveraged in agriculture to optimize crop performance within a specific agro-ecological context, potentially improving resource-use efficiency (e.g., water-use) at the crop scale ( Martin and Isaac 2015 ). Different water-use strategies are intimately tied to different function types ( Wright et al. 2004 , Brodribb and Feild 2010 , Bloomfield et al. 2019 ) because of their direct impact on growth and survival, and this has resulted in the selection of various structural traits (e.g., leaf morphology, vascular elements and rooting dynamics) and functional characteristics, such as leaf area and osmotic adjustments. Among the numerous traits linked to water-use, stomatal conductance (g s ) is probably one of the most important and most-studied ( Medrano et al. 2002 , Medlyn et al. 2017 ). Stomatal conductance is strongly influenced by environmental factors such as light, temperature and water availability, but because it integrates the dynamics between physiology and hydraulics across the whole soil–plant–atmosphere continuum (SPAC, i.e., from soil to roots to shoots), g s has been employed as a suitable trait for modeling plant efficiency and stress tolerance under water deficit conditions ( Damour et al. 2010 , Medlyn et al. 2011 ). Field observations and modeling studies on g s and carbon assimilation collected from several biomes revealed a gradient of increasingly conservative stomatal behavior, increasing from angiosperm to gymnosperm species, resulting in a parallel increase in intrinsic water-use efficiency ( Lin et al. 2015 , Medlyn et al. 2017 , Bloomfield et al. 2019 ). These differences have been related to several aspects, such as xylem anatomy, wood density and variations in hydraulic safety margins (lower in angiosperms) ( Choat et al. 2012 ). However, an interactive effect between environmental factors (e.g., temperature and humidity) that may influence resource allocation strategies and their trade-offs (e.g., water saving, carbon gains and growth) is also likely to impact the plasticity of stomatal behavior in distinct species and climate regions ( Lin et al. 2015 , Ziegler et al. 2024 ). For agriculture, the goal of maximizing yield while reducing water-use in perennial crops may lead to trade-offs between allocating more assimilates to fruits at the expense of vegetative growth ( Zhang et al. 2016 , Lavoie-Lamoureux et al. 2017 ), and increase vulnerability to cavitation ( Peschiutta et al. 2013 , Edwards et al. 2016 ). Nonetheless, it has been recently observed that warmer, drier seasons adversely affect yield largely due to decreases in g s , net carbon assimilation and lower stomatal density ( Zuffa et al. 2025 ). Accurately estimating how climate change might influence stomatal behavior and plant performance over the longer term is critical in the context of perennial crops. Recent long-term studies in ecosystems demonstrated that drier and warmer scenarios almost universally promote more conservative water-use (i.e., lower g s ) ( Stefanski et al. 2023 ). However, the magnitude of this shift toward a more conservative g s regulation was a result of complex interactions between each species’ innate drought tolerance and its specific environment ( Stefanski et al. 2023 ). In perennial cropping systems, understanding these interactions will likely be critical in adapting specific crops to within and across season variability, where seasonality has a significant influence on growth, productivity and tolerance to abiotic and biotic stresses ( Edwards and Clingeleffer 2013 , Shtein et al. 2021 , Herrera and Hochberg 2024 ). Grapevines are among the most valuable fruit crops worldwide, but current winegrowing regions might be potentially shifting due to climate change, impacting yield and quality ( van Leeuwen et al. 2024 ). Growers have multiple alternatives to prevent productivity losses and manage the vigor of the plant, adapting fruit production to areas where water availability is scarce. Due to the vast diversity of Vitis genotypes, the selection of cultivars has been considered a suitable adaptation strategy to drier and warmer environments ( Levin et al. 2019 , Sinclair et al. 2024 ). In the future, it could also result in the breeding of high-performing ideotypes integrating water-saving traits accounting for growth, ripening and rooting behavior ( Herrera et al. 2021 , Dayer et al. 2022 , Albasha and Bartlett 2024 , Bernardo et al. 2025 ). Moreover, as grapevine is cultivated almost exclusively as a grafted plant, the impact of rootstocks and their interactions with the scion cannot be neglected ( Ollat et al. 2016 , Zhang et al. 2016 , Marín et al. 2021 ). Although most studies have focused on the effects of rootstock on agronomic and water-use traits during drought ( Zhang et al. 2016 , Lavoie-Lamoureux et al. 2017 ), research remains limited on the complex interactions across cultivar and rootstock genotypes, seasonal environmental factors and soil characteristics at the vineyard level. Despite the theoretical-realistic application of predicting water-use efficiency in different eco- and agro-systems, we still need more comprehensive quantifications of stomatal behavior in applied contexts where multiple climatic and edaphic drivers interact ( George and Nissen 2002 , Shtein et al. 2021 , Shapira et al. 2024 ). To date, much of the research on grapevine stomatal behavior and water status has been performed under controlled or semi-controlled conditions, with discussions based on single stress responses or a limited number of observations that may not be upscaled to the parcel level ( Soar et al. 2006 , Tramontini et al. 2013 , Romero et al. 2018 , Villalobos-González et al. 2019 , Sorek et al. 2023 ). On the other hand, research performed under field conditions is equally challenging due to the permanent, yet diverse, influence of the weather and soil conditions interacting with the plant material (i.e., cultivar and rootstock genotypes) ( Tortosa et al. 2019 , Buesa et al. 2023 ). Besides, given the time and labor required to measure carbon assimilation rates, a robust stress response evaluation by infrared gas analyzers and/or water status by pressure chambers in large-scale field trials is impractical due to the rapidity of changes in light and VPD. This study attempts to disentangle the sources of g s variation inherent in large-scale field trials, taking into account the spatiotemporal effects (i.e., the position of the plants on the parcel and seasonal variability) in order to more accurately assess the effects driven by different genotypic combinations. This is one of the most extensive characterizations of stomatal conductance and chlorophyll fluorescence analysis collected over the season (i.e., 10 measurement dates from June to October) across a large cohort of cultivar- rootstock combinations. By integrating the spatial heterogeneity across the season among genotypes, this study proposes a strategy to better understand how different genotypes respond to environmental changes (from the leaf to the plant scale). Materials and methods Experimental design and plant material The study was performed in the 2023 growing season at the experimental vineyard GreffAdapt, located in France (Villenave d’Ornon 44°47′25.2′′N 0°34′26.9′′W), under rainfed conditions ( Marguerit et al. 2019 , Morel et al. 2024 ). The soil is sandy-gravelly. The first organo-mineral horizon (0–35 cm) is composed of ~2% of organic matter. The pH is within the classic range of between 6 and 7. The cation exchange capacity (CEC) of the soil is low (&lt;7 cmol + kg -1 ), with a desiquilibrium in the soil Mg and K ratio (K/Mg ≫ 1), resulting in induced Mg deficiency through an excess of K ( Morel et al. 2024 ). However, no symptoms were observed in 2023. The climate is characterized by moderate summers with cool nights and no dry season ( Bois et al. 2018 ). The water status of grapevines was estimated using the water balance models proposed by Riou et al. (1994) and Lebon et al. (2003) to simulate the daily fraction of transpirable soil water (FTSW) in the plot. The data for monitoring weather were obtained from the INRAE CLIMATIK platform ( https://agroclim.inrae.fr/climatik/ ), managed by the AgroClim laboratory in Avignon, France ( Delannoy et al. 2022 ). Additionally, predawn (Ψ pd ) and stem (Ψ stem ) water potentials were measured periodically throughout the season on 17 July (DOY 198), 23 August (DOY 235) and 8 September (DOY 251), at one studied combination ( Figure S1 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online) to assess the FTSW estimated from the water balance model. During the experimental period, the daily maximum temperature (T max ) ranged from 20.8 °C to 39.7 °C, with total precipitation measuring 226 mm and a mean FTSW of 0.15 ( Figure S1 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online). Eight-year-old Vitis vinifera L. plants were trained using vertical shoot positioning and pruned with the mixed Guyot method. A natural cover crop was planted in each row and mowed according to grass growth, and fungicide treatments were regularly applied to prevent the development of downy and powdery mildew during the vegetative period. Phenotypic monitoring included yield per vine, fertility (number of bunches per shoot), winter pruning weight and the vigor conferred (pruning weight/number of shoots). Among the five scions and 55 rootstocks of GreffAdapt (6250 plants/ha with a spacing of 1.6 m × 1 m), we selected Cabernet-Sauvignon, Grenache and Syrah cultivars, grafted onto the rootstocks 1103P, 140Ru, 110R, 5BB and SO4, amounting to a total of 15 plants distributed randomly in the plot into three groups of five plants. The three groups of five sequential vines per rootstock × scion combination were distributed throughout the vineyard in a completely randomized block design. Ecophysiology measurements Gas exchange and chlorophyll a fluorescence measurements were performed using the porometer/fluorometer LI-600 (LI-COR Biosciences, Inc., Lincoln, NE, USA) across the 15 rootstock–scion combinations. For each plant, three measurements on mature, sun-exposed leaves were made during the morning period (10:00–13:00h) on the following dates across the 2023 growing season: 15 and 26 June, 31 July, 10 and 22 August, 7, 14 and 25 September, and 4 and 11 October. The resulting dataset assembled ~6000 observations over the season. The following parameters were analyzed from the instrument output: stomatal conductance (g s , mol m −2 s −1 ), vapor pressure deficit of the leaf (VPD leaf , kPa), leaf temperature (T leaf , °C), ambient light (PAR, μmol m −2 s −1 ), quantum efficiency of photosystem II (ΦPSII). The linear electron transport rate ( J , μmol m −2 s −1 ) was then obtained following the equation described by Maxwell and Johnson (2000) . Since J was previously found to be positively correlated to carbon assimilation in several species ( Cheng et al. 2001 , Takagi et al. 2022 ), we calculated the ratio J /g s as a proxy for water-use efficiency (WUE J /gs ). The global relationships between ϕPSII and PAR, as well as g s and J , are shown in Figure S2 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online. Data analysis All analyses and data visualizations were performed using R v4.3.3 software ( R Core Team 2024 ). Random forest analysis First, we performed an exploratory analysis of variance on the g s dataset, whose relative effects were primarily attributed to environmental factors, the ‘date’ of measurement and noise (i.e., residual). Therefore, we moved to an alternative data analysis methodology and chose a machine learning approach to better understand which variables were contributing the most to the variation in g s . The following variables were included in the Random Forest ( Breiman 2001 ) regression analysis: VPD leaf , date, T leaf , pruning weight, PAR, cultivar, rootstock, cultivar × rootstock interaction, vigor, yield, number of clusters per vine, fertility and the position of the vines in the field (i.e., row and trellis post). Random Forest is generally insensitive to multicollinearity, due to random features selection at each split reducing dominance of highly correlated predictors. However, despite its robustness, the model requires careful hyperparameter tuning. To prevent overfitting, grid search was performed on 1000 trees over a 10-fold cross validation strategy and 25 values grid for the following hyperparameters: mtry the number of predictors to be sampled at each split, ranging from 2 to 25, and min_n the minimum number of observations required in a node to be split further, ranging from 3 to 40. This optimization helped to control for model complexity by regularizing tree growth and depth. We built a reproducible modeling workflow in R using tidymodels ecosystem ( Kuhn and Wickham 2020 ) and use ranger package ( Wright and Ziegler 2017 ) as the computational engine due to its high performance in handling large datasets and support for variable importance extraction using permutation. The initial dataset was splitted into training and testing datasets with a typical split ratio 80/20 for large datasets to assess trained model performance and reliability. A recipe was built for preprocessing data (date and nominal predictors conversion) and model tuning grid. Model performance was assessed based on Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and R-squared (R 2 ) and the best model hyperparameters combination was picked to fit the model. The last fit was performed on the testing dataset to validate the best trained model on unseen data while controlling for overfitting. To interpret model results and assess feature relevance, we used vip package ( Inglis et al. 2024 ) and quantify both main effect importance and interaction strength ( Greenwell and Boehmke 2023 ). Spatio-temporal g s normalization Following the random forest analysis output, the position of the plant on the field assumed considerable importance to the g s response. Therefore, we decided to address the spatio-temporal variation, which is common in field trials, using smoothing models that consider both the row and the trellis post as sources of spatial dependence with the ‘SpATS’ package ( Rodríguez-Álvarez et al. 2018 ). This method was evaluated in annual crops ( Gilmour et al. 1997 , Taye and Njuho 2008 , Robbins et al. 2012 ), but has recently been applied in phenotypic research on perennial grasses and forage species, adjusting traits to the spatio-temporal variation within experimental fields ( Muktar et al. 2022 , Fernandes Filho et al. 2025 ). For this purpose, we included the effects of the trellis post and row for each cultivar × rootstock combination, and the date as potential sources of spatial variation in the SpATS function to normalize the g s response across the season (see Figure S3 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online). Afterward, we verified the g s trends by plotting the raw and fitted g s values for each combination. Assembling linear mixed models to assess the contribution of plant material to the seasonal g s response We fitted a linear mixed model (LMM) using the ‘lmer’ function from the ‘lme4’ package on both raw and normalized g s data, incorporating the following fixed effects: VPD leaf , T leaf , PAR, pruning weight and yield. All predictors were scaled to facilitate the interpretation of results and improve model convergence. The random effects defined in the g s data prior to SpATS normalization included cultivar, rootstock, their interaction, date of measurement and position of the vines. The latter two effects were excluded from the LMM fitted on the normalized g s data because the spatial–temporal normalization already integrated the impacts of date and plant position. Collinearity among predictors was assessed using the ‘vif’ function from the ‘car’ package. The models were compared using the following metrics: information criterion (AIC), log-likelihood (LogLik) and the conditional coefficient of determination (R 2 ), which integrates both fixed and random effects. Model performance was evaluated using the ‘simulateResiduals’ function from the DHARMa package to identify potential issues such as overdispersion and autocorrelation. The proportions of g s variance explained by the fixed and random effects were extracted from both LMMs. An analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted on the fixed and random effects of the LMM, with significant effects subsequently assessed by Tukey’s post hoc test. The impact of the rootstock × scion combination on J /g s was evaluated using the non-parametric Kruskal–Wallis test followed by Dunn’s post hoc test. Results Exploring the importance of features on seasonal g s We collected field stomatal conductance and chlorophyll fluorescence measurements on ten dates throughout the season ( Figure 1 and Figure S4 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online). This resulted in a substantial dataset from a field trial encompassing three cultivars and five rootstock combinations, yielding ~6000 observations. The seasonal mean values of g s varied across the season from 0.327 mol m −2 s −1 around flowering (June) to 0.112 mol m −2 s −1 at harvest (September), displaying a wide range of values on each date, particularly in the first half of the season ( Figure 1 ). The variability between and within dates was less pronounced in the fluorescence parameter ΦPSII ( Figure S4 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online). We expected this high level of variation, given the inherent variability of the weather, the high number of individuals and plant material interactions analyzed, and the large number of measurements taken per individual plant ( n = 15 per cultivar × rootstock combination). Under these circumstances, exploring potential effects of the plant material (cultivars and rootstocks) on the seasonal response of g s proved challenging. Consequently, we developed a data analysis workflow to disentangle sources of variation. Data ( n = 7038) were filtered to exclude negative values of g s . As a first step we used a machine learning approach to identify the primary contributors to the seasonal g s response within our large-scale experimental design. A random forest model demonstrating the best performance (RMSE = 0.054 and R 2 = 0.76) was validated using the testing portion (20%) of the data and was subsequently applied to the complete dataset to rank the importance and interactions of features affecting the g s response ( Figure 2 ). Environment-related traits, such as VPD leaf , exhibited the highest importance regarding the g s response, followed by the sampling date which agglomerates numerous elements related to plant development (i.e., phenology), the weather and soil characteristics (e.g., evolution of nutrient availability and/or water holding capacity, etc). As one may expect there are spatial effects with the position of the vines (i.e., row and trellis post) having a high importance on seasonal g s . Some agronomic traits such as pruning weight have very high importance, with others being of lesser extent ( Figure 2A ). Regarding plant material, the cultivar × rootstock interaction displayed the highest level of importance, while cultivar and rootstock separately showed a low contribution to g s diversity across the season ( Figure 2A ). Nonetheless, all the variables included in the Random Forest analysis interacted with one another ( Figure 2B ). The variables ‘Date’, VPD leaf , T leaf , pruning weight and PAR exhibited the strongest interactions ( Figure 2B ), followed by the combined variable ‘Cultivar × Rootstock’, and the position of the plants within the field. Addressing spatial and temporal heterogeneity Taking into account the ranking of the features ‘Row’ and ‘Trellis post’ ( Figure 2A ), a significant part of the variation in the g s response was associated with a spatial effect, simply the position of the vines in the field. Besides adding complexity to our study, spatial heterogeneity may obscure the impacts of cultivars and rootstocks when assessing the vine’s water-use diversity in a large experimental design. Consequently, we included the effects of the cultivar × rootstock combination and date as potential sources of spatial variation in a standard mixed model SpATS function ( Rodríguez-Álvarez et al. 2018 ) to normalize the g s response across space and time. The resulting estimated trend plot ( Figure S3 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online) confirmed the spatial heterogeneity and identified areas of g s underestimation (rows 50–60) and overestimation (rows 1–20). Following normalization, a similar pattern of g s distribution was observed between normalized (gs norm ) and raw (g s ) values across cultivars, rootstocks and their combinations ( Figure 3 ). The normalization step integrated spatiotemporal effects, reducing the range of g s variability and extreme values within cultivars and rootstocks, while maintaining the primary trends observed in the raw data. On average, Grenache exhibited the most conservative water-use behavior (0.12 mol m −2 s −1 ), contrasting with Syrah (0.17 mol m −2 s −1 ), and Cabernet-Sauvignon (0.18 mol m −2 s −1 ) ( Figure 3A ). The traditionally classified high drought-tolerant rootstocks 1103P and 110R displayed the lowest stomatal conductance ( Figure 3B ). However, the range of values was relatively small across rootstocks (0.1 mol m −2 s −1 mean g s variation). The influence of the rootstock on Grenache stomatal response was nearly absent ( Figure 3C ), with little variation between different rootstocks. Conversely, stomatal conductance within Syrah and Cabernet-Sauvignon demonstrated higher levels of variation when combined with different rootstocks. Disentangling sources of variability We employed mixed linear models to elucidate g s response by integrating various environmental predictors (VPD leaf , T leaf and PAR) and vigor-related factors (yield and pruning weight) as fixed effects while incorporating the random effect of the plant material (cultivar, rootstock and their interaction). To compare the contributions to g s variation, we ran a model before normalizing the spatial variation of g s , where the position of the vines and the date were also included as random effects. These factors accounted for 27% of the variance in the raw g s dataset, obscuring the impact of the cultivar, rootstock and their interaction (3%) ( Figure 4 , left panel). Following normalization there was a significant increase in the variance explained by the cultivar (26.4%) and the cultivar × rootstock interaction (14.3%), although the rootstock itself (11.1%) showed a low contribution to the variation of the normalized g s ( Figure 4 , right panel). Together, these factors accounted for over 50% of the variance in g s . The environmental and vigor-related variables accounted for 11.5% of the variance, with pruning weight being the only significant parameter influencing g s . The model applied to normalized data exhibited a better fit according to the metrics R 2 (0.63), AIC (−26,164) and LogLik (13093) than the model applied to the raw dataset (R 2 = 0.68; AIC = −15,185; LogLik = 7605). Figure 4 Percentage (%) of variance explaining stomatal conductance by the fixed effects (VPD leaf , T leaf , PAR, pruning weight and yield) and random factors (cultivar, rootstock, cultivar × rootstock interaction and date and vine position) of the linear mixed models. Respectively, the left and right panels represent the % of variance explaining raw g s (fixed effects 38.8%, date 25.5%, residual 31.1%, cultivar 2.7%, position 1.8% and interaction 0.1%; R 2 = 0.68) and normalized g s (cultivar 26.4%, interaction 14.3%, residual 36.7%, rootstock 11.1% and fixed effects 11.5%; R 2 = 0.63). Impact of plant material and vigor-related traits on seasonal water-use diversity Considering the significant interaction between cultivars and rootstocks on seasonal water-use, we paired the different cultivars within each rootstock to explore the extent of g s diversity across combinations ( Figure 5 ). The cultivars Grenache and Syrah displayed a similar pattern of g s response across all rootstocks ( Figure 5A ). Interestingly, the rootstock combinations of this pair, which showed the highest g s values (5BB and 140Ru), also exhibited a greater mean difference between cultivars. A similar pattern was noted for the least transpiring combinations (SO4 and 1103P). The rootstock that contributed to the highest difference (0.083 mol m −2 s −1 ) between cultivars was 1103P in the Gr-CS pair ( Figure 5B ). Conversely, the smallest difference between cultivars (0.006 mol m −2 s −1 ) was recorded in the rootstock 5BB within the Sy-CS pair ( Figure 5C ). To further investigate whether cultivar behavior and the interaction between cultivars and rootstocks could influence seasonal water-use efficiency, we calculated the ratio of the fluorometer parameter J (i.e., linear electron transport rate, μmol m −2 s −1 ), derived from the quantum yield of PSII (ΦPSII, mol e − mol −1 photon) and absorbed light (PAR, μmol photon m −2 s −1 ), against the g s measurements. Consequently, we employed the J /g s ratio to estimate water-use efficiency ( Figure 6 ). A significant cultivar effect was observed, primarily driven by Grenache, which exhibited the highest WUE J /g s values irrespective of the rootstock utilized. In contrast, the cultivar × rootstock interaction was particularly evident in Syrah and Cabernet-Sauvignon, especially with the drought-tolerant rootstocks 1103P and 140Ru, which demonstrated markedly different responses. Nonetheless, both cultivars exhibited high WUE J /g s when grafted onto 110R, another drought-tolerant rootstock. Discussion This study combined environmental and agronomic-related traits to disentangle the sources of g s variation and water-use in a large-scale, field phenotyping platform. Seasonal g s response was primarily influenced by environmental factors such as VPD leaf and T leaf , and to a lesser extent, pruning weight, PAR and the position of the plants in the field. The specific contribution of cultivar and rootstock were clarified through the spatio-temporal normalization of g s . Cultivars showed a much greater contribution to g s variation, accounting for a quarter of the seasonal variance of g s after normalization, and their interaction with specific rootstocks indicated distinct water-use behaviors depending on the rootstock–scion combination. These results highlight that the conservative water-use behavior of Grenache dominated g s variation resulting in a higher WUE J /g s regardless of rootstock. Nevertheless, our results demonstrate the complexity of stomatal responses influenced by rootstock–scion combinations, the environment and their interactions. These results underscore the need to integrate multi-trait phenotyping with state-of-the-art data analysis to disentangle sources of variation in order to isolate the contributions of plant material to traits impacting water-use efficiency in the field. Spatio-temporal heterogeneity of g s Our results show a seasonal decline in g s throughout the season ( Figure 1 ), consistent with previous findings in diverse perennial species ( Radoglou 1996 , Yoshifuji et al. 2020 , Sorek et al. 2021 , Romero et al. 2024 ). In grapevine, seasonality has been shown to significantly impact water-use related traits such as g s and osmotic adjustment (e.g., lower osmotic potential at full turgor), with effects even larger than the differences found between cultivars ( Herrera and Hochberg 2024 , Sinclair et al. 2024 ). A machine learning approach enabled disentangling the sources of variation in g s and results indicated a universal interaction among all variables ( Figure 2 ). In an ecological setting machine learning has been used to robustly estimate g s across a huge breadth of plant functional types and biomes, considering several environmental and physiological predictors throughout the season ( Gaur and Drewry 2024 ). Specifically, the use of Random Forest models incorporating PAR, temperature, VPD, carbon assimilation rates and leaf temperature captured an impressive 87% of the variability across different plant functional types ( Gaur and Drewry 2024 ). Our original model performed almost as well, capturing 76% of the variability in g s using a total of 11 predictors ( Figure 4 ). Our results emphasized the predominant contribution of environment-related variables to stomatal regulation, such as VPD leaf , T leaf and PAR ( Figure 2 ). Similarly, the position of the vines within the field also greatly contributed to g s variation having a greater importance than either cultivar or rootstock alone ( Figure 2 ). This likely reflects the spatial heterogeneity of numerous factors that can include microclimatic conditions, variations in soil structure and composition, and local rainfall distribution ( Bois et al. 2020 , Yu et al. 2020 ). Although we could not comprehensively assess the plant water status in the 15 cultivar × rootstock combinations in such a large-scale design, we did employ a water balance model and some spot measurements of vine water potential which indicated that this season was characterized by low to moderate levels of water stress (Ψ stem varying from −0.95 MPa in July to −1.45 MPa in August, Supplementary Figure S1 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online). The lack of vine water status is an important missing predictor from our analyses. However, our statistical models still performed quite well (R 2 = 0.76), indicating this shortcoming had a minimal impact on our results in this season likely owed to the non-limiting levels of water deficit. The high level of importance of ‘date of measurement’ is more complex because ‘date’ assembles both environmental conditions (e.g., air temperature, precipitation, wind and light intensity) and the dynamics of plant traits (e.g., phenology, leaf/canopy morphological, rooting architecture/behavior). It is important to note that measuring many of these plant traits in a high-throughput manner is almost impossible to achieve in large-scale experiments. For example under field conditions, the heterogeneity of traits such as rooting architecture and behavior limit the continuous and accurate quantification of their impacts on plant water-use ( Bernardo et al. 2025 ). The fact that environmental variables dominate g s variation likely masks potential genotypic effects. To overcome this limitation and more accurately unravel the genotypic effects (i.e., cultivars and rootstocks) we reduced the noise of these environmental variables by using SpATS normalization. This normalization helped reveal the differences arising from cultivar and rootstock–scion interactions, which collectively accounted for over 50% of the variance in normalized g s , while remaining consistent with the general trends observed in the raw data ( Figures 3 and 4 ). Previous research on annual crops has also shown that spatial variation can significantly influence phenotypic data due to fine-grained heterogeneity in the field ( Lado et al. 2013 , Muktar et al. 2022 ). This variation can be due to various factors, such as dependencies between neighboring blocks and soil parameters, highlighting the need to normalize for microenvironmental heterogeneity to reduce noise and improve the accuracy of predictive models ( Muktar et al. 2022 , Fernandes Filho et al. 2025 ). The application of spatial correction models has proven crucial in breeding studies of cereal crops and perennial forages for predicting cultivar performance in the field, identifying markers linked to quantitative trait loci (QTLs) for key traits, and increasing prediction accuracy in genomic selection models ( Lado et al. 2013 , Elias et al. 2018 , Muktar et al. 2022 ). Cultivar and rootstock interactions With regard to genotype, cultivar dominated seasonal g s variation, but notably the contribution of the interaction of cultivar × rootstock was highly significant ( Figure 4 ). In contrast, even after normalization the impact of the rootstock alone was not a significant contributor to g s variation under these field conditions. Most rootstock studies have been conducted in controlled environments using young, potted plants and under water deficit conditions. Among these, many have demonstrated rootstock effects of water-use related traits (e.g., hydraulics, signaling and root architecture) ( Marguerit et al. 2012 , Peccoux et al. 2018 , de Souza et al. 2022 , Buesa et al. 2023 , Flor et al. 2025 ). However, one drought study on potted grapevines found that gas exchange was unaffected by the rootstock ( Barrios-Masias et al. 2019 ). One can speculate that studies using potted plants in controlled environments lead to homogenous root systems that make innate differences in particular traits between rootstocks more apparent. Given the large genotypic diversity of the experimental vineyard (five scions grafted onto 55 rootstocks), agro-ecological interactions may also influence phenotypic variation at both the individual and population levels through ontogenetic variation and shifts in species interactions ( Westerband et al. 2021 ). In this study the cultivar effect was primarily driven by Grenache ( Figure 3A ), which exhibited a conservative, water-saving strategy (mean seasonal gs of 0.121 ± 0.015 mol m –2 s –1 ). The conservative behavior observed in Grenache ( Figure 3A ) has been demonstrated in various studies performed in both field and controlled settings ( Schultz 2003 , Vandeleur et al. 2009 ), being associated with tighter stomatal control, higher sensitivity to fluctuations in leaf water potential, and decreased water transport capacity in the SPAC ( Martorell et al. 2015 , Dayer et al. 2020 ). Even with high leaf temperatures (&gt;30 °C), Grenache still showed relatively lower transpiration levels than the other cultivars ( Figure S5 available as Supplementary Data at Tree Physiology Online), despite converging at higher temperatures. At lower temperature ranges (27–30 °C), these differences became even more pronounced, especially in combination with rootstocks like 110R and 1103P. These observations suggest that this season wasn’t stressful enough to threaten leaf viability or risk defoliation, nor did it trigger latent cooling at the expense of water conservation across cultivars. This suggests there was no significant risk of carbon loss during the growing season. Nonetheless, when crops are exposed to acute high-temperature stress and drought in combination, genotypes with a conservative water-use behavior may reduce negative impacts on fruit size, potentially leading to fewer yield penalties ( Sadok et al. 2021 ). Grenache and Syrah exhibited a similar g s pattern across all rootstocks, with Syrah always exhibiting higher g s ( Figure 5A ). It is noteworthy that the largest differences in g s were observed in 5BB and 140Ru ( Figure 5A ) both of which drive the highest levels of g s regardless of cultivar. This may superficially be considered counterintuitive since these two rootstocks have contrasting traditional classifications regarding their drought resistance (i.e., high resistance for 140Ru and low for 5BB) and different genetic backgrounds ( Serra et al. 2014 ). One could expect a traditionally classified drought resistant rootstock like 140Ru to exhibit lower levels of g s similar to other drought resistant rootstocks sharing the same genetic background such as 110R and 1103P. One plausible explanation is that the stress levels throughout the season were insufficient to homogenize the conservative water-use behavior among the drought-tolerant rootstocks. Alternatively, the expectation that drought resistance would always be characterized by conservative g s behavior may not always hold true since performance under drought results from the emergent interaction of numerous below and above ground hydraulic traits ( Dayer et al. 2020 ). Revealing the variation in important agronomic traits Water-use efficiency (WUE) is an important parameter for understanding cultivar and rootstock impacts on crop water status and production ( Hatfield and Dold 2019 ). At the leaf level, WUE is commonly quantified by single-leaf gas exchange measurements, relating carbon assimilation either to g s (i.e., intrinsic water-use efficiency—WUE i ) or to leaf transpiration ( Tomás et al. 2012 ). Although we did not directly measure net carbon assimilation rates, previous research indicated a positive correlation with the linear electron transport rate ( J ), suggesting that fluorometric methods may be employed to estimate carbon assimilation ( Maxwell and Johnson 2000 , Cheng et al. 2001 , Takagi et al. 2022 ). Consequently, we used the J /g s ratio as a proxy for WUE i . Cultivar was responsible for the greatest differences in WUE J /gs which was highest in Grenache across all rootstocks ( Figure 6 ). On the other hand, WUE J /gs varied to a much lesser extent between rootstocks, although there were still significant differences between some specific rootstock–scion combinations. For example, Grenache grafted onto SO4 and 5BB showed the highest and lowest WUE J /gs , respectively, even though these two rootstocks originate from the same genetic backgrounds ( Vitis riparia × V. berlanderi ). The same is true for certain combinations with Syrah and Cabernet-Sauvignon highlighting the specificity arising from the interaction within each rootstock–scion combination ( Albacete et al. 2015 , Clingeleffer et al. 2019 ). This variation may arise from other traits that include plant hydraulic traits, root system structure and anatomy, rootstock–scion signaling, and numerous others ( Koundouras et al. 2008 , Murray et al. 2019 , Gambetta et al. 2020 , Buesa et al. 2021 , 2023 , Perez-Alvarez et al. 2023 ). Because of the influence of many physiological (e.g., Rubisco activity), environmental and structural parameters (e.g., canopy architecture) that drive carbon assimilation, we recognize that the J /g s ratio might be limited in providing an accurate estimate of WUE ( Douthe et al. 2018 ). Other approaches, such as soil and atmosphere-based methods and IRGAs, could be used to measure WUE at the whole-plant and vineyard levels, although all have limitations ( Tomàs et al. 2014 , Rienth and Scholasch 2019 ). Nonetheless, given the size and robustness of the current dataset, which provided large amounts of g s measurements combined with fluorescence parameters over a relatively short timeframe, it is worth considering J /g s as a viable proxy for WUE estimation in large-scale experimental designs ( Kimura et al. 2025 ). Pruning weight is an indicator of the vegetative growth and vigor, showing a significant positive correlation (r 2 = 0.48) with stomatal conductance ( Figure 7 ). This is consistent with a previous study in grapevine that demonstrated that stomatal conductance and water-use was positively correlated with vegetative and reproductive productivity ( Buesa et al. 2023 ). At the same time Buesa et al. (2023) showed that across a large breadth of rootstock–scion combinations crop level WUE was negatively correlated with WUE i . Our study supports this finding generally speaking, but there are important exceptions. For example, while there are no significant differences between Grenache and Syrah regarding pruning weight in SO4 and 5BB ( Figure 7 , inset), Grenache exhibited much lower levels of g s ( Figure 5A ) and this lower g s translated into significantly higher WUE J /gs ( Figure 6 ). This probably suggests a potential improvement in the coordination between growth and stomatal responses in Grenache, which has been previously associated with the accumulation of non-structural carbohydrates ( Potkay and Feng 2022 ). This strategy has also been suggested to maximize stem growth across seasons, which, coupled with turgor maintenance (i.e less negative osmotic potential), would promote sugar transport, thus improving plant performance in general ( Potkay and Feng 2022 ). Conclusions The intrinsic variability of nature poses challenges for researchers in drawing clear conclusions from field studies. However, crop phenotyping is absolutely essential under these field conditions since this represents the reality within which the crops will perform. Thus disentangling the dynamic influence of the environment across the season from genotypic effects is essential for the more accurate phenotyping of plant functional traits. This study represents the most extensive dataset on grafted grapevine stomatal responses collected under field conditions to date, addressing the complexity of disentangling sources of variation related to environmental and genotypic interactions throughout the season. Our results indicate that the universal interactions between environmental variables and genotypes challenge the idea of any single, or even few, dominant factor(s). Disentangling this complex web of interactions requires large, comprehensive datasets paired with state-of-the-art statistical modeling. These results support the idea that a holistic approach, considering genetic (i.e., rootstock–scion), developmental and environmental factors throughout the season, is likely essential for assessing water-use efficiency and plant performance in large-scale designs.
