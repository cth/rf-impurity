Abstract Understanding the spatial pattern of cropping is crucial for proactive conservation efforts in vulnerable ecosystems. This paper presents a spatially explicit probability layer for the conterminous United States which identifies areas of high likelihood of future cropping (i.e., probability of being tilled) based on key geophysical measures of climate, geomorphology, and soil. This dataset identifies areas with a high propensity for cultivation, offering a critical baseline for prioritizing conservation strategies in remaining natural ecosystems at risk of conversion. Derived using underlying geophysical processes, this spatially explicit output can be combined with socio-economic data for more nuanced and regionally specific conservation planning. This resource provides a valuable tool for researchers, land managers, and policymakers seeking to mitigate the impacts of cropping on these ecosystems. Background &amp; Summary The perennial demand for agricultural goods to feed both the growing population and commodity markets puts untold pressure on the world’s natural resources 1 – 3 . Despite the majority of prime farmlands already being cultivated, significant challenges (e.g., yield gaps, dietary shifts, soil degradation, extreme weather, disease) prevent these lands alone from meeting global demand 4 , 5 . Rising demand has driven agricultural expansion into increasingly less productive, and even marginal lands. Since 2000, millions of acres have been converted to cropland in North America, despite their low crop productivity, to the detriment of myriad ecosystem services 6 , 7 . These lands, while not well-suited for agriculture, typically support grassland and shrubland ecosystems 8 . Even some desert ecosystems are being converted despite their inherent hostility to cultivation. Latest estimates of cropland in North America show the largest relative increase since 2000 occurred in Mexico (48%, 1.69 ± 0.06 million ha), driven largely by irrigation projects in the Chihuahuan Desert 7 . As more land is converted to cropland, the integrity of these ecosystems and the rich diversity of species they harbor is increasingly at risk 9 , 10 . Further, these ecosystems face ongoing threats across the globe, making them in need of targeted conservation efforts 11 – 13 . Losing these vital ecosystems exacerbates ongoing threats to biodiversity, climate, and communities through loss of habitat, release of stored soil carbon, and reduced cultural and economic connections to these landscapes 14 , 15 . The Great Plains of North America exemplifies the global trend of agricultural encroachment into ecologically threatened ecosystems. Just 38% of original grasslands remain in North America with 32 million of those remaining acres tilled for agriculture from 2012-2021 16 , 17 . Mapping past cropland expansion is vital to understanding how much ecosystem loss has occurred and pinpointing where rates are highest to date 8 , 18 , 19 . However, there is a pressing need for assessments beyond past conversion to quantify future risk of conversion. Understanding the fundamental environmental conditions that enable tillage and conversion to agriculture is essential to identifying where future conversion is likely to occur, as cropping is one of the primary drivers of grassland conversion 17 . In shrublands, cultivation risk presents a potentially severe but localized threat to sagebrush ecosystems and the species that rely on those communities 18 , 20 . Without this basic knowledge of which areas are likely to become cropland, conservation efforts are unable to effectively target the most at-risk places to focus conservation efforts 21 . Existing datasets that inform agricultural suitability, such as the Land Capability Class system, primarily evaluate static soil properties like slope, texture, and drainage 22 . Other U.S.-specific datasets, including spatial data on crop expansion and abandonment 23 or yield potential 8 , provide complementary insights but focus on quantifying historical patterns or productivity potential rather than conversion risk. Global-scale models have been developed to estimate agricultural expansion potential 24 . However, the broader spatial scale and coarse resolution (300 m) make them challenging to use at the sub-national level. Our dataset addresses this gap by modeling cropping probability as a process-based measure of conversion risk, offering a spatially explicit foundation for anticipating future agricultural expansion for the Continental United States. We used land use maps of cropland to evaluate the probability of being cropped, which we also refer to as the tillage probability. We note that this includes areas currently under no-till agriculture, but, even where no-till is currently practiced, the conversion from natural areas to cropland is undertaken via tillage. We modeled tillage probability based on historic land use patterns and a combination of biotic and abiotic factors. The resulting dataset has proven valuable for identifying and prioritizing at-risk ecosystems and habitats that support species vulnerable to agricultural conversion. Although initially developed to address conservation-related questions, this dataset serves as a foundation for assessing potential agricultural conversion more broadly. This baseline can be further refined by incorporating social and economic variables to capture more transient drivers, such as market fluctuations. This dataset also offers geographically explicit insights that help prioritize technical and financial resources by identifying areas where agricultural expansion is most likely to occur. Methods Our modelling framework is presented in Fig. 1 . Specifically, we model the probability of tillage, building off the cropland suitability model methodology in previous work 20 . Our tillage model, other than controlling for long-term localized variation by stratifying by county, focuses on geophysical indicators (climate, geomorphometric, soil) to provide a process-oriented tillage probability estimate. We deliberately excluded socioeconomic and infrastructure covariates to avoid the inherent stochasticity associated with such variables and to allow for their inclusion in a post hoc analysis (e.g., heuristically or in a secondary modelling effort). The geographic extent of this model encompasses the Conterminous United States (CONUS). The modeling was performed at the county level, resulting in the development of 3,108 independent models. Models were specified by county to capture local variation and to provide a practical unit of analysis for combination with other datasets. Fig. 1 Workflow diagram for the tillage model. Data collections and layers are indicated by cylinders and ovals, respectively. Relevant analytical steps are represented by rectangles with any binary processes represented by pentagons. Nine soil characteristics were included to capture the physical traits of the soil that influence agricultural productivity and/or enable tilling: available water supply, restrictive depth, saturated hydraulic conductivity, crop productivity index, percent organic matter, percent clay, percent sand, pH, and water capacity. Additional information on these soil attributes can be found in the SSURGO dataset documentation 26 . These attributes were compiled from the Gridded National Soil Survey Geographic Database (gNATSGO) 27 —a composite database that provides complete coverage of the best available soils information for the United States at 30m 2 spatial resolution (available for download at https://nrcs.app.box.com/v/soils ). This dataset includes gSSURGO 28 , which is based on Soil Survey Geographic Database (SSURGO) 29 , State Soil Geographic Database (STATSGO2) 30 , and Raster Soil Survey Databases (RSS) 31 . The data were downloaded from the gNATSGO website (accessed 2020) as an ESRI file geodatabase and extracted and queried using the R libraries terra and sf 32 , 33 . The resulting data were written to LZW compressed GeoTIFF files for the entire CONUS. The ten geomorphometric variables were aimed at representing water balance/distribution and relevant topographic characteristics for agriculture: elevation, topographic wetness 34 , topographic radiation aspect 35 , heat load 36 , slope 37 , roughness 38 , terrain ruggedness 38 , topographic position 38 , eastness 39 , and Martonne’s modified dissection 40 . All metrics were derived from the 30m 2 USGS National Elevation Dataset (NED) programmatically accessed from the AWS on-demand datasets ( https://www.usgs.gov/3d-elevation-program ) 41 . The digital elevation models (DEMs) were downloaded, and metrics derived on a county-by-county basis. The geomorphometric variables were calculated using the R libraries terra 32 , raster 42 , spatialEco 43 , fasterRaster 44 , and rgrass 45 . Nine climatic metrics were included to capture the impact of temperature and precipitation on thermal conditions, water availability and seasonal timing required for crop growth and farming operations. The temperature-based metrics were derived from the TopoWx dataset which applies corrections for bias and artifacts observed in temperature trend data ( https://www.scrim.psu.edu/resources/topowx/ ) 46 . Mean annual temperature was calculated based on a 30-year normal from 1991–2020. Frost-free days (&gt;0 °C) were counted and averaged over the same time-period. Growing degree days, using 5 °C as the base temperature, were derived from monthly minimum and maximum temperature data and averaged for the 30-year normal. The Daymet data were used, based on lack of availability of TopoWx precipitation data, to derive metrics associated with precipitation ( https://daymet.ornl.gov/getdata ) 47 . Precipitation metrics were calculated for the same period as the temperature metrics (1991–2020). In addition to mean annual precipitation, seasonal precipitation amounts were averaged for the spring (April-May), summer (July-August), winter (November-February), and length of the growing season (April-September). We also included the precipitation ratio for summer/spring to capture seasonal precipitation distribution. Rather than simple bilinear resampling to our base 30m 2 resolution, we performed a statistical resampling using our downloaded DEM, [X,Y] coordinates via a Multivariate Adaptive Regression Spline (MARS). This incorporates some additional topographic and spatial variability (inherent in the original gridded model), but we do recognize that no additional information is being added to the spatial process; this approach is taken for the convenience of having a matching spatial resolution allowing for a consistent estimate. Training data For each county, we subset the NASS crop data, across all available years, and calculated the pixel-level frequencies ( f ) of tillage representing any crop type data ( https://www.nass.usda.gov/Research_and_Science/Cropland/Release/index.php ) 48 . Any pixels with f ≥ 2 was assigned a 1, else 0, representing tilled and not tilled. This screening was to account for any potential error in the NASS classification misassigning tilled to an observation. Accounting for variability within soil type was an important consideration so stratification by soil type was implemented, but the discrete soil classes were not considered in the model. As such, in creating our training sample, we first intersected the SSURGO soil type data (i.e., soil map units), to a given county, creating a 5% area (hectare) subsample representing point locations of the 30m 2 pixel centers in each soil type. In areas where there were no classified soil types, a random sample was taken following our 5% area criteria. We extracted tillage classification and corresponding model parameters for each sample at the county-level. We selected spatially continuous variables to minimize missing data, although some variables still contained substantial spatial gaps. Parameters with more than 30% missing values in the training data were excluded from model selection and from all subsequent spatial model estimations. Because no interpolation or imputation was performed, any remaining observations with missing data were dropped from the analysis. The large sample size ensured that this limited data loss did not meaningfully affect model performance. In the spatial raster layers, missing data resulted in NA pixels. Model Because the data represent a binominal process, we utilized Probabilistic Random Forests (PRF) 49 , 50 implemented with the R package ranger 51 , 52 . This method accounts for uncertainties in the parameters as well as in the nominal classes. Using a kernel function, the PRF algorithm treats classes as probability distribution functions, rather than deterministic quantities, thereby removing hard boundary limitations. The probabilistic nature of the model and resulting estimates negate the need for estimate adjustment such as isotonic regression 53 and stabilize log-loss, along with other validation statistics, that are affected by imbalanced data. Counties with minimal tilled acreage were excluded from the modeling process. We also excluded one county (Hamilton County, NY) due to an extremely imbalanced training dataset despite stratifying by soil type (0.2% tilled). In total, 78 counties were excluded. However, to produce a complete wall-to-wall data product, these counties were still represented in the probability layers with probability values of zero. We applied the model selection approach detailed in previous work 54 , 55 using the rfUtilities R package 56 . This optimizes model performance using global and local error along with number of parameters, thus providing a subset of parsimonious parameters with a maximized signal-to-noise ratio. Since techniques like random forests will fit to noise, this step removes spurious variables that may affect performance and impact the spatial estimates. We also implemented a method that evaluates the error within the estimated probabilities. Based on the null class, we refit the model after filtering out identified outliers in the null class based on their log-loss value. This reduces noise in the null class but leaves the sample variation in the tillage class unaltered. In exploratory data analyses, we found that the global and class level errors stabilized between 800–1000 bootstrap replicates. We fixed this model parameter at 1001 (odd number theoretically breaks ties). The mtry parameter was defined as sqrt (number of parameters) because it tends to be a very stable default. We did not want to constrain the partitions in any way so the maxnodes parameter was not given a limit, resulting in unconstrained trees. This leads to a good balance between first and second order variation, robustly representing both global and localized statistical relationships. To account for potential class imbalance, we used a 1/3 rule to evaluate the balance between tilled/not-tilled specific to each county-level model 57 . If there was no imbalance, then a standard model was specified. However, if the classes were imbalanced, we implemented a multi-model approach, where the selected parameter space of the model remains fixed but each iteration a model is fit with a down sampled majority class. We iterated until the pooled covariance of the down-sampled null data matched the covariance of the full data and the ensembles were then combined into a single model. This ensures sample convergence of all data and full representation of global variation. We deployed models on an AWS Windows cloud server with 128 GB RAM and 16 × 3.5 GHz Xeon processors. Processing took approximately 8 days. Data Records Our tillage probability dataset is available for download via Zenodo 58 . The main tillage probability data product is available as a CONUS mosaic and as individual state mosaics. The probability layers for the conterminous US (CONUS_tillage_prob.tif) and states ([State Name]_probs.tif) have a single band ranging from 0-1. The accompanying uncertainty data are also available at the state-level. The uncertainty layers ([State Name]_uc.tif) contain three bands: standard error, lower 95% CI, upper 95% CI. All rasters are provided as GeoTIFFs in the USGS CONUS modified Albers projection (ESPG 5070) with a spatial resolution of 30 m. Development and water are masked out via the NLCD and impervious surface data 59 . To minimize file size, all GeoTIFFs were compressed using lossless DEFLATE compression with a predictor to enhance efficiency. We also provide supplemental tables (tillage_model_results.xlsx) that include tillage, uncertainty, and validation summary statistics for each county model (sheet 2–4) as well as parameter selection frequency (sheet 5). Metadata information is also provided (sheet 1). The results of the cross-validation analysis are also in the repository (cross_val_results.xlsx). Technical Validation Model fit was assessed using a suite of validation statistics calculated from the out-of-bag (OOB) error of the random forest models. To quantify predictive performance, we implemented a bootstrap cross-validation procedure with 99 replicates 42 , 44 . This approach provides a quasi-independent validation by generating error distributions through permutated random withholding of observations. Bootstrap samples were drawn to include 10% of the minority class in each replicate, ensuring balanced representation and mitigating class imbalance during resampling. Cross-validation was performed on a random subset of county-level models, representing approximately 12% of the total modeled area (n = 348). This subset also includes at least one county from each state. We calculated several validation statistics including Percent Correctly Classified (PCC), True Skill Statistic (TSS), Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) and Log Loss (LL) to assess both model fit and performance (Table 2 , Fig. 2 ). Lower values of LL reflect better predictive ability and though the maximum value of 0.65 for the county-level models is not ideal, it does indicate that the model is performing better than random guessing (LL = 0.693 for two balanced classes) 60 . County-level spatial model predictions were conducted using the R terra package 32 with standard error and 95% confidence intervals calculated using an infinitesimal jackknife method 61 . The closest comparable dataset for the United States is the Land Capability Class ( https://websoilsurvey.nrcs.usda.gov/app/ ) 22 . This system categorizes soils into eight classes (I–VIII), ranked from most to least suitable for agriculture based on factors such as slope, soil texture, depth, and drainage. The Land Capability Class system has been in use for decades and remains the most widely adopted framework for evaluating soil suitability. In the absence of a comparable substitute, it is often the default source for assessing the risk of conversion to agriculture, particularly in carbon credit protocols 62 . To examine how our tillage dataset compares to the Land Capability Class system, we calculated the median tillage probability for each class at the state level (Fig. 3 ). The results follow an expected pattern: tillage probability is highest in the most suitable classes and declines as suitability decreases, with greater variability observed in the intermediate classes (IV–V). Arizona is a notable outlier, with a median tillage probability of zero for classes I–IV. This likely reflects the exclusion of counties that have little tilled land but include areas with higher capability classes, which are otherwise rare in Arizona. Usage Notes Our model outputs underscore the persistent threat of further agricultural tillage to natural ecosystems in the United States. Unsurprisingly, current rates of tillage are high across the major agricultural regions of the United States: the Midwest, the Central Valley, and the Mississippi Delta (Fig. 4a ). That being said, substantial regions across the U.S.—notably in the Southwest, Southeast, and northern Great Plains (Fig. 4b )—still possess characteristics suitable for conversion to cropland. These regions were identified by calculating potential tillage expansion as the difference between the current proportion of tilled area and the proportion of land with a tillage probability greater than 0.65. This threshold (0.65) is informed by domain expertise and used here for illustrative purposes. Users should calibrate their own threshold for their specific applications. Tillage expansion is especially relevant in the northern Great Plains, where estimates indicate an annual loss of over 200,000 hectares of rangelands to tillage agriculture, as cropping continues into areas once considered marginal 19 , 63 . Notably, private landowners play a central role in land-use decisions across the Great Plains, where they manage roughly 91% of rangelands 64 . This mirrors a broader national trend, with most rangelands in the continental U.S. under private ownership. The influence of private landholders is further amplified by the fact that vegetation productivity on private lands is twice that of public lands, making them both ecologically valuable while simultaneously more susceptible to conversion pressures 64 . There is notable spatial variation in our tillage model’s uncertainty, with a concentration of counties showing higher model error in the Midwest (Fig. 4c ). A similar pattern emerges in the global log-loss surface, which is higher across much of the eastern United States, particularly around the Great Lakes and Piedmont regions, indicating areas where tillage patterns are more challenging for the model to represent (Fig. 4d ). Counties exhibiting higher standard error also tend to contain a greater proportion of tilled land, a pattern that may reflect the limited representation of no-till observations in the training data and/or the possibility that, in regions with intensive agricultural activity, tillage decisions are shaped more by socioeconomic considerations than by geophysical constraints. More broadly, complex environmental gradients, sparse training data, and substantial management and socioeconomic variability likely all contribute to elevated model uncertainty. Together, these uncertainty surfaces identify regions where predictions warrant additional caution. Complementing these assessments, the spatial distribution of selected model variables highlights where specific predictors exert greater influence, offering qualitative insight into geographic variation in model behavior (Fig. 5 ). The model presented here has already informed grassland and shrubland conservation efforts (Table 3 ). For example, Bedrosian et al . (2024) uses our data alongside measures of human modification and remotely sensed plant composition to identify regions most at risk of conversion across the sagebrush biome 18 . The authors then employed tillage probabilities in the northern Great Plains to illustrate how perpetual easement acquisitions (&gt;93,000 ha) in one of these at-risk regions reduced cultivation risk, and how eliminating this risk is maintaining ecological integrity of adjacent public lands. In the southern Great Plains, Sullins et al . 2021 found that 58% of 13,231 Conservation Reserve Program grasslands that had been planted back from cropland to grass remained in grass cover a decade after federal incentive payments to landowners ended 65 . Then, using our tillage risk data, they found that grassland retention was three times higher in low- versus high-risk landscapes (87 versus 27%). The authors conclude that piecing back together lower-productivity landscapes that are better suited for grazing than farming has the potential to restore and subsequently retain grasslands at unprecedented scales. While our model rigorously identifies the geophysical potential for tillage, it offers an incomplete picture of all the factors influencing tillage. By design, we focus solely on the inherent suitability of the land itself, excluding the complex web of socioeconomic factors that influence farming decisions 66 . The choice to convert grassland to cropland is rarely dictated by soil and slope alone; market forces, policy incentives, personal preferences, and historical context all play critical roles 67 . With that said, our model, trained on previously tilled land, does reflect the outcomes of complex past land-use decisions that extend beyond geophysical characteristics 68 . Consequently, our model may show even poor soils to have a significant probability of being cropped, if that is a common occurrence in that county. In addition, our modeling approach does not predict the amount of agricultural expansion. Rather, if one assumes a certain amount of conversion, this dataset suggests areas where it would be more or less likely to occur. Therefore, while our analysis provides a valuable lens for understanding the potential for conversion, its outputs should not be interpreted as definitive forecasts. While best suited for regional insights, users should anticipate some pixel-level variation due to the inherent limitations of input data. Overall, this model offers significant value for identifying areas more or less likely to become cropland in the future, and the same framework can be extended to evaluate how shifting socioeconomic drivers, policy scenarios, and land use pressures influence conversion and its climate consequences—including potential soil carbon losses—both in the U.S. and in other regions with competing demands for agricultural productivity and environmental integrity.
