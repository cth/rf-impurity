Background: Effective antiretroviral therapy to maintain durable viral suppression is key to ending the HIV epidemic in the United States. We evaluated the ability of machine learning algorithms to predict people with HIV (PWH) at risk of unsuppressed viral load. Setting: Retrospective study among PWH from San Diego County (n = 18,916). The study used reported public health HIV data (2017–2022) to predict the outcome of HIV viral load &gt;200 copies/mL during a year-long prediction window. Methods: The data was partitioned by calendar date into two training and one validation datasets to accurately assess performance for predicting future observations. A random forest model was used to generate outcome predictions for the overall population and stratified by race. Mediation analysis was undertaken to assess underlying causality. Results: The model had an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve of 82.2 (95% CI: 79.3 to 85.0), a sensitivity of 33.8% (95% CI: 28.6 to 39.0), and specificity of 96.9% (95% CI: 95.7 to 97.2) corresponding to a positive predictive value of 55.7% (95% CI: 48.7 to 62.8) and negative predictive value of 91.7% (95% CI: 90.6 to 92.8). The area under the receiver operating characteristic was similar across races. Prior viral load characteristics were identified as the most important variables; however, they partially acted as mediators of underlying demographic (eg, race) and HIV infection risk (eg, injection drug use). Conclusions: Machine learning algorithms using mandatory reported public health HIV data can predict which PWH will have future unsuppressed viral load. Future work will assess its clinical utility compared to existing data-to-care initiatives. Most public health agencies (PHAs) in the U.S. have established retrospective—as opposed to prospective—initiatives, such as “data-to-care” programs whereby individuals who do not have any reported HIV viral loads or CD4 + T-cell counts in a 12-month period are referred for outreach to link the individual to care. 2 However, this strategy results in missed opportunities for providing proactive adherence programs as well as early and more efficacious outreach. Furthermore, it may lead to an unnecessary burden to PHAs as many individuals identified as out of care through this system are actually in care elsewhere (ie, moved to a new area), are receiving care through healthcare agencies that might not report HIV related data, have died, or are incarcerated. 6 Machine learning (ML) algorithms can improve the efficiency and accuracy of healthcare provision and in some disciplines are already in clinical use. 7 , 8 In the field of HIV, ML has the potential to improve the prospective prediction of individuals more likely to dis-engage with care or have unsuppressed viremia, and in the identification of individuals who would benefit from HIV pre-exposure prophylaxis. 9 – 16 Previous studies predicting HIV outcomes have used HIV-clinic level data which provides a rich data source, naturally including variables that are not routinely available to PHAs such as detailed substance use, missed care visits, housing instability and mental health comorbidities. 12 However, studies using these data may not be generalizable to other settings due to variability on what data are available. In this study, we aim to develop a model that accurately predicts individuals at risk of having unsuppressed HIV viremia in the next 12 months using metrics derived from standard public health HIV data (such as viral load testing patterns) to ensure generalizability for PHAs across the U.S. and other settings. ML algorithms, while exhibiting remarkable capabilities, have inherent limitations when tasked with identifying root causes. One challenge is that these algorithms may struggle with complex, interconnected systems where causation is multifaceted and context-dependent, limiting the ability to design targeted interventions. For example, in some settings sex might be highly predictive of leaving care, but there may be an underlying cause such as injection drug use that drives the observed patterns. Understanding these relations are essential to be able to maximize the utility of ML and provide appropriate adherence interventions. We evaluated the predictive capability of machine learning algorithms using HIV data reported to San Diego County public health. San Diego County was selected for analysis as it is a high HIV prevalence region and is one of the 50 high-priority Ending the HIV Epidemic jurisdictions. 1 Our primary interests were developing a ML model to predict unsuppressed viral load as a surrogate marker of being out of care, evaluating how a ML model performs for future populations (ie, out of sample prediction), assessing if the model performance varies by race, and using mediation analyses to investigate potential root causes underpinning our identified predictors. METHODS Study Population The study population consisted of all PWH living in San Diego County between June 2017 and June 2022. Routinely reported individual-level, de-identified, public health HIV data from the Enhanced HIV/AIDS Reporting System (eHARS)—a national application that assists local health departments with data management—were used for the analysis. Data on each PWH included demographic information, transmission risk data, HIV infection characteristics (eg, date of diagnosis and stage), and longitudinal HIV laboratory results (CD4 and viral load). The study was deemed exempt from IRB committee review as data did not contain personally identifiable information (IRB #800152). Primary Outcome The primary outcome was a binary indicator of any unsuppressed viral load (&gt;200 copies/mL) during a future one-year time window called the prediction window. The prediction window for each participant was defined as the one-year period beginning the day after one of their viral load measurements, referred to as the index viral load (see Training, Refinement, and Validation below for specification on how the index viral load was selected for each individual). In cases where no viral loads were observed during the prediction window, suppression status was imputed using the Next Observation Carried Back (NOCB) method, in which the earliest viral load following the time window was used to determine status. The use of NOCB mitigates biases in our analysis due to the possibilities that an individual received care at a non-reporting institution (like the VA or incarcerated) or moved out of San Diego County during the prediction window. Individuals with no available follow up viral load (even using NOCB) were excluded from the analysis. Predictors Predictors included demographic data such as race/ethnicity, sex at birth, age at diagnosis and current age (age at the date of data export). Hispanic PWH, regardless of race, were categorized as “Hispanic, any race.” HIV associated predictors included transmission risk, AIDS at diagnosis, years since diagnosis, CD4 at diagnosis and current CD4. Further predictors were derived using characteristics of HIV viral load data prior to the start of the prediction window. They included the number of viral loads, proportion of viral loads unsuppressed (excluding those within 90 days of diagnosis), mean and variance of the durations of the intervals between viral loads, duration of the interval for the two most recent viral loads, and duration from the earliest viral load to the start of the prediction window. A linear trend in viral loads over time was estimated for each participant by linear regression and the estimates of the intercept and slope were used as predictors; viral loads exceeding 500 copies/mL were truncated to 500 for this purpose. To define the mean of the between-test intervals and the duration of the most recent interval, at least two viral loads are required. The variance of the between-test intervals requires at least three viral loads. When a participant's testing history was insufficient to define these predictors, values were imputed using the average among the other participants. Means were computed separately for the training, refinement, and validation datasets (see below for details on these datasets). Mean imputation was selected to increase clarity and ease of the imputation procedure so the approach can be implemented in various settings. Training, Refinement, and Validation Datasets The data were temporally split into three non-overlapping subsets of participants which we refer to as training, refinement, and validation. Each of the datasets were defined by a distinct time-period with respect to an index viral load which defined the start of the prediction window: training (June 2017–December 2018), refinement (January 2019–December 2019), and validation (January 2020–December 2021). The rationale for stratifying individuals into datasets by chronological time is to ensure accurate assessment of our prediction model for future observations (ie, out-of-sample prediction). Splitting the data at random (ie, not by chronological times) would only assess how well the prediction model performs on current data, which will likely result in an overly optimistic assessment of its performance for future observations. Individuals were assigned to training, refinement, and validation datasets based on a 60%, 20%, and 20% split, respectively. Individuals with viral load measurements available only in a single time-period were assigned to that respective dataset. Individuals with viral load measurements available on multiple time-periods were randomly assigned to a dataset weighted to achieve proportions described above. The index viral load was selected as the most recent measurement within the time-period the individual is assigned. The training dataset was used to develop the initial ML model. The initial model was assessed and refined using the refinement dataset. Once the final ML model was developed, the predictive capability was assessed using the validation dataset. The validation dataset was not analyzed by the authors until the final ML model was selected based on the refinement dataset. As the validation dataset only included later observations not available in the training and refinement datasets, the assessment was designed to provide an accurate assessment of the ML model on future populations. Statistical Analysis Univariate Regression We estimated factors associated with predicting unsuppressed viral load by fitting univariable logistic regression models. The dependent variable in the regression was the primary outcome of unsuppressed viral load during the prediction window and the independent variable was an individual-level characteristic. 17 The analysis was conducted on the training and refinement datasets with the intention of guiding feature selection for the final ML model. We report the estimated odds ratio, 95% confidence interval (CI), and significance level from each model. Machine Learning Methods We investigated the multivariable association between unsuppressed viral load and individual-level demographic and clinical data using a Random Forest (RF) model, which is better able to capture non-linear and hierarchical associations between falling out of care and individual-level characteristics compared to logistic regression. 18 The model included all individual-level covariates listed in Table 1 . The form of the RF model is governed by two hyperparameters: the minimal node size of the trees and the number of randomly selected predictors considered at each split. Values for these parameters were determined by maximizing the cross-validated F1 score on the training set using ten-fold cross-validation with 10 repeats. 19 The Gini index was used as the splitting criterion. 20 We report variable importance using a permutation-based approach, which assesses the contribution of each predictor by randomly shuffling its values and measuring the corresponding decrease in model performance. Specifically, importance scores are computed as the reduction in the F1 score when each predictor is permuted, averaged across all trees in the model. A higher variable importance score indicates a predictor that is more influential in improving the overall predictive power of the model. The scores are normalized to take a maximum value of 100. Prediction Assessment We evaluated the RF model's performance across multiple metrics including sensitivity and specificity, positive (PPV) and negative (NPV) predictive value, accuracy, and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC). We also calculated the prediction metrics for the subpopulations defined by race/ethnicity using the validation dataset. This provides a more detailed understanding of the model's predictive power and helps inform on its clinical suitability by potentially identifying groups that may be inadequately served by its use. Mediation Analysis We conducted a mediation analysis to examine the indirect effects of four independent variables—Black race, history of injection drug use (IDU), AIDS diagnosis, and birth sex—on unsuppressed viral load through three mediators: proportion of unsuppressed viral loads, first CD4 count, and most recent CD4 count. Our approach followed three steps: (1) estimating the relationship between each mediator and the independent variables (mediator model); (2) assessing the association of both the mediators and independent variables with the outcome, unsuppressed viral load (outcome model); and (3) quantifying mediation effects (and associated confidence intervals) for average causal mediated effect (ACME), average direct effect (ADE), and proportion mediated using the causal mediation framework based on potential outcomes; see Imai et al (2010a, 2010b, 2013) for details and statistical assumptions. 24 – 26 The mediation analysis was conducted by specifying the independent variable and each mediator separately. Robust standard errors were used, and statistical inference was based on 500 quasi-Bayesian Monte Carlo simulations. Confidence intervals and P -values were computed using heteroskedasticity-consistent standard errors. All analyses were conducted in R using the mediation package. 27 RESULTS Overall, 18,916 PWH with viral load data between June 2017 and June 2022 were included in the analysis from which 34.4% were Hispanic of any race, 13.6% were non-Hispanic Black, 44.4% were non-Hispanic white, and 7.6% where non-Hispanic other races (Table 1 ). Most participants reported being men who have sex with men (MSM) as the transmission risk (73.8%) with fewer reporting IDU (6.0%), heterosexual contact (10.2%), MSM &amp; IDU (9.1%), or other unspecified risk (0.9%). Prior to the prediction window, the median duration of viral load data was 0.92 years, and the median number of viral load measurements was three. Participants were divided into training (n = 11,350), refinement (n = 3783) and validation (n = 3783) datasets with population characteristics for each dataset shown in Table 1 . Time dependent trends were observed across the datasets in several characteristics. The variation in the distribution of current age was statistically significant ( P &lt;0.001) with later datasets including a greater proportion of younger PWH; notably 17.5% of the training, 23.7% of the refinement, and 24.9% of the validation datasets were under 40 year old. Later datasets also had a higher proportion of non-white PWH ( P = 0.012). The proportion of PWH with unsuppressed viral load during the observation window was 13.1% in the training dataset, 13.0% in the refinement dataset and 12.1% in the validation dataset. Across all datasets, 3013 (15.9%) had no available viral load for outcome assessment. Aside from first CD4 + T-cell measurement, all the selected variables demonstrated an association with having unsuppressed viral load during the prediction window in the training dataset. The proportion of suppressed or unsuppressed viral loads had the greatest proportion of explained variation (McFadden R 2 = 21.8%) in the training dataset, followed by any prior unsuppressed viral load ( R 2 = 16.4%) and most recent CD4 count ( R 2 = 5.5%). Machine Learning Methods Using a RF model, the importance of each variable on predicting unsuppressed viral load was determined using the training dataset (Fig. 1 ). Features that demonstrated high importance in predicting the outcome included viral load patterns (linear trend for intercept of viral load with threshold value of 500, proportion of unsuppressed/suppressed viral loads, slope of viral load measurements, viral load measurements per year, average interval between viral load measurement and variance of viral load measurement intervals); and HIV status related parameters such as most recent CD4 + T-cell result, years since HIV diagnosis and time since first observed viral load. Mediation Analysis Several demographic and transmission risk variables demonstrated mediation through viral trend or measurement parameters. For instance, the model estimated that the effect of race on the outcome (Black vs not Black) was mediated by the proportion unsuppressed viral load predictor and accounted for approximately 39.9% (29.3%–57.2%) of the observed effect. Similarly, the underlying transmission risk variable of IDU vs no-IDU was also mediated by the proportion unsuppressed viral load predictor and accounted for approximately 40.4% (30.2–54.7) of its observed effect. Other causes that demonstrated mediation included AIDS vs no AIDS at diagnosis. In addition, other variables, including most recent CD4 + T-cell measurement, mediated effects. DISCUSSION In this study we applied a machine learning RF model to routinely reported individual-level HIV public health data to predict the risk of developing unsuppressed viremia in the next 12 months. The model identified several viral testing variables that ranked as predictive over and above socio-demographic variables, such as race, transmission risk and age, that are traditionally thought to underlie the risk of developing unsuppressed viral load. 2 In this limited dataset, the model achieved a AUROC of 82.2 (79.3–85.0) with a positive predictive value of 55.7% (48.7–62.8) and negative predictive value of 91.7% (90.6–92.8). While specificity was high (96.5% [95.7–97.2]) the sensitivity of the model remained relatively low [33.8% (48.7–62.8)]. Most existing public health “data-to-care” programs use an algorithm by which PWH who have no reported viral load or CD4 + T-cell count for any 12-month period are referred for outreach to re-link to care. Pertinent issues reported by public health bodies with these programs include issues with staff capacity, difficulty locating individuals, and difficulty identifying if individuals have moved out of the jurisdiction. 28 Some of these issues are inherent in how individuals are selected for outreach. Many individuals identified using the existing algorithm for detecting people ‘out-of-care’ may, in fact, have moved residence/jurisdiction, died, established care at non-reporting institutions or been incarcerated. Published data would suggest that when using these standard algorithms, less than 10% of identified cases are actually out-of-care with anecdotal reports indicating this value could even be as low as &lt;5% in some areas. 6 Improved data linkage and public health-clinic collaboration can improve the accuracy of outreach in some centers though this is labor-intensive. 29 ML algorithms may be able to enhance existing efforts improving efficiency and enabling earlier outreach. Components of our model could be applied in several settings including public health identification of at-risk individuals and in the HIV primary care setting to alert providers to individuals who may require evaluation for retention efforts such as case management, transport assistance, adherence reminders, substance use treatment, specialist psychiatric care and/or financial counseling. Earlier ML enhanced interventions may have a more meaningful impact on individual and community HIV viral load outcomes compared to existing standard of care. 30 , 31 Our model utilized standard reported variables from eHARS. We maximized the impact of these variables by including testing patterns of viral load such as linear trend intercept, frequency of testing, variability in testing frequency and proportion unsuppressed in the predictive window. Using these variables combined with demographic variables, our predictive capacity was comparable or superior to previous studies predicting dis-engagement with care. 9 , 12 The use of variables contained in eHARS improves the generalizability of our method to other jurisdictions. Notably, comparison between studies may be limited as our study used unsuppressed viral load as an outcome over lack of HIV care visits during follow up. Unsuppressed viral load was selected as the preferred outcome measure as ultimately unsuppressed viral load identified individuals who had had a period of being truly out-of-care or at a minimum had gone through a period of limited engagement with care. While not exactly representative of engagement in care, our algorithm will detect individuals who potentially need additional resources or high-intensity interventions. The ML algorithm identified several viral load measurements or trend parameters as having greater importance than “traditional” variables such as age, race, and transmission risk. Using causal inference analyses, it was shown that in several situations, a proportion of the effect of the identified viral measurement/trend parameters were in fact mediating an underlying risk such as transmission risk and/or race. These findings emphasize that while machine learning algorithms can provide predictive capability, the underlying causal network output cannot be used to identify root causes thus limiting the design of the type of interventions that could be used. However, even without identifying root causes, the ML algorithm may provide improved identification of an adherence need , whether it be mental health, substance use, housing instability or other issue. Our study has several limitations. For inclusion in the study, we required that the participant have a follow up viral load during or after the prediction window. Ultimately, this led to the exclusion of over 16% of the dataset. However, extrapolating from prior data-to-care studies, the majority of PWH (∼90%) without any results available are actually in care and so the authors decided it was preferable to exclude these participants as opposed to substantially compromise the outcome ascertainment. Due to limitations in the extent of data available, some individuals did not have enough viral load measurements prior to the prediction window to be able to calculate variance or frequency of testing measures requiring imputation of data. In addition, missing data were encountered in several variables including data on transmission risk. The validation dataset overlapped with the COVID-19 pandemic which may have affected results (though sensitivity/specificity was similar across datasets). Finally, this study used data only from San Diego County and the results may not be generalizable to other settings. Strengths and novel aspects of our study included the large data set, evaluation of predictive capability by race, use of viral load as the outcome and the mediation analysis. Future work will address some of these limitations applying the methods to larger, more diverse, and more comprehensive datasets, for example electronic health records, which have been used to identify candidates for pre-exposure prophylaxis. 32 Our study has demonstrated that it is possible to use novel machine learning algorithms to predict individuals who are at increased risk of having unsuppressed viral load during follow up using public health data alone. It would be reasonable to assume that more than half [PPV of 55.7% (48.7–62.8)] of individuals contacted using the algorithm in this study would have unsuppressed viral load during the next year. Targeting the limited public health resources to these individuals may have greater impact on community and individual viral loads than current data-to-care programs though further comparative studies evaluating the impact of machine learning algorithms are required.
