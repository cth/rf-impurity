Abstract The soil microbiome plays a crucial role in maintaining healthy ecosystems and supporting sustainable agriculture. Studying its biogeographical structure and distribution is essential for understanding the rates and mechanisms of microbially mediated soil ecosystem services. This study aimed to investigate the spatial distribution patterns of Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota across soils in Russia, summarizing data from 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding of topsoils. A machine learning approach (Random Forest) was employed to generate digital distribution maps using climatic, topographic, vegetation, geological, and soil variables. Model interpration was performed using variable importance assessment and Shapley values. According to the error metrics, the Acidobacteriota model achieved a root mean squared error (RMSE) of 6.67% and an R 2 of 0.41, while the Planctomycetota model achieved an RMSE of 2.04% and an R 2 of 0.46. Both phyla exhibited similar spatial distribution patterns, with relative abundance decreasing from North to South. For Acidobacteriota, vegetation cover, surface temperature, and soil pH were significant predictors, whereas the relative abundance of Planctomycetota was mainly influenced by climatic variables. Specifically, Acidobacteriota were more abundant in areas with dense vegetation, stable surface temperatures, and acidic soils. In contrast, Planctomycetota showed reduced abundance in regions with higher levels of precipitable water vapor. These results highlight the potential of machine learning techniques to visualize predictive biogeographic patterns in soil microbial taxa abundance at the phylum level. Despite limitations related to the heterogeneous nature of source data, focusing on higher taxonomic ranks less sensitive to methodological variation enabled to identify preliminary large-scale distribution trends of microbial phyla in soils. Introduction The soil microbiome is essential for soil ecological functions, such as carbon and nutrient cycling, plant growth maintenance, and overall soil health 1 . Recent studies have shown that microbial communities harbor keystone taxa, which drive community composition and function irrespective of their abundance 2 . In soils, Acidobacteriota have been identified through co-occurrence network analysis as microorganisms associated with organic matter transformation 2 . These studies show that microorganisms of sufficiently high taxonomic rank, such as phylum, can exhibit similar physiological patterns and act as keystone taxa associated with specific soil-biological processes. Since members of Acidobacteriota have been identified as structural and functional key elements of soil microbiomes and agroecosystems 3 – 5 , successful manipulation of these soil residents can improve the key ecological process of soil carbon sequestration and enhance crop productivity and performance. Planctomycetota are primarily found in waterlogged soils, marine shelf ecosystems, and geothermal soils, and account for 4–7% of microorganisms in agricultural and forest ecosystems 6 – 8 . They play a key role in anaerobic ammonium oxidation [anammox], regulating the nitrogen cycle 9 . Their unique cell compartmentalization, adaptability to oxygen conditions, and methylotrophy highlight their significance in the study of prokaryotic evolution 8 , 10 . The microbial-mediated functioning of soil ecosystems depends on the structure and distribution of microbial communities, which vary at microscales and both regional levels 11 – 13 and globally 14 , 15 . Therefore, the spatial patterns of such keystone taxa can influence future land use management, and developing agricultural practices that boost crop yields and combat climate change as well 16 , 17 . Accurate mapping of the abundance of microbial taxa requires an understanding of their distribution patterns and the simultaneous analysis of a number of soil ecosystem parameters that determine these patterns. Traditional methods for studying soil microbiomes, such as culture-based techniques, are limited in their ability to capture the vast diversity of microbial life present in soil environments. However, advances in high-throughput sequencing technologies, particularly 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding, have revolutionized the field by enabling the comprehensive profiling of microbial communities directly from environmental samples 18 , 19 . Over the past 15 years, extensive research has been conducted using 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding to fill significant knowledge gaps about the biogeographic principles that govern microbial community structure 20 – 23 . Russia represents vast and ecologically diverse regions characterized by a range of soil types, climatic conditions, and land-use practices. Despite their importance, the soil microbiomes of these regions remain underexplored. This knowledge gap hampers our ability to predict how these ecosystems will respond to environmental changes, such as climate change and anthropogenic disturbances 24 , 25 . Mapping the soil microbiome of the East European Plain and Western Siberia, as has been successfully undertaken in other regions, is necessary. For example, 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding data was used to generate a comprehensive map of bacterial diversity across various soil types in France 26 – 28 , to reveal the biogeographic patterns in bacterial compositions in soils of China 29 and Arctic regions 30 . Similarly, mapping soil fungal communities based on ITS metabarcoding and the soil fungi-to-bacteria ratios has been performed for the territory of France 31 . These studies revealed significant spatial variability in microbial communities, influenced by factors such as soil pH, organic matter content, and land use 26 . These findings underscore the importance of spatially explicit data in understanding soil microbial ecology and highlight the potential for similar approaches to be applied in the East European Plain and Western Siberia. Mapping soil microbiomes poses several challenges. One significant challenge is the high spatial heterogeneity of soils and their properties. Soils can vary widely over short distances, resulting in differences in texture, moisture, organic matter, and other physical and chemical characteristics. This heterogeneity can obscure patterns in microbial distribution and complicates efforts to identify the drivers of microbial community structure. Moreover, soil sampling and processing protocols need to be carefully standardized to ensure that microbial DNA is accurately and consistently captured across different sites. The primary challenge of mapping the soil microbiome across Russia is the vast territory with an extremely wide range of climatic conditions, soil types, and vegetation cover, as well as the difficult accessibility of these regions 32 – 34 . This makes it challenging to collect a sufficient number of soil samples and conduct subsequent analyses. Therefore, it is necessary to combine 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding data with advanced prediction methods when mapping the soil microbiome of these regions. Machine learning (ML) offers powerful tools for analyzing complex and large-scale biological data. By leveraging ML algorithms, researchers can uncover patterns and relationships within microbial community data that might be missed using traditional statistical methods. Recent studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of ML in various aspects of microbiome research, including community classification, functional prediction, and environmental association studies 35 , 36 , 37 . Integrating 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding with ML approaches provides a promising option for mapping soil microbiomes and revealing their global biogeographical patterns under conditions of large territories and acute data deficiency. This study aimed to map the soil Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota of the East European Plain and Western Siberia using 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding and ML techniques. By employing a multiscale approach, we captured the spatial variability of microbial communities across different soil types and environmental gradients. Specifically, this study addressed the following key objectives: (1) to characterize the diversity and composition of soil Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota in the study regions, (2) to identify environmental factors driving Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota structure, and (3) to evaluate the spatial distributions of Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota using ML algorithms. Material and methods Data collection and description Data were collected from published articles focusing on various aspects of the soil microbiome on the Eurasian Plain and in Siberia, with an emphasis on environmental factors affecting the structure and composition of soil prokaryotic communities (Supporting information). The dataset included only upper (organogenic) horizons sampled in anthropogenically undisturbed areas. The number of sampling points for Acidobacteriota was 100 and for Planctomycetia 75 (Fig. 1 ). Sampling was conducted across diverse ecosystems, including arable lands, fallow grasslands, and forests, representing a variety of soil types such as Voronic Chernozems, Albeluvisols, Phaeozems, Kastanozems, Calcisols, Solonets, Retisols, Cryosols, and Histosols. Tundra heathlands were represented by Acrisols and Leptosols, while Solonets and Kastanozems were characteristic of steppe and semi-arid regions. NGS platforms used included the GS Junior (Roche, Switzerland) and Illumina MiSeq, employing the V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene, amplified with primers F515 and R806. Machine learning procedure The Random Forest (RF) algorithm was employed for digital mapping of the soil microbiome community structure. RF is a popular and effective ML algorithm that uses an ensemble of decision trees based on bootstrapped samples from a dataset 38 . It operates by constructing a multitude of decision trees during training, with each tree trained on a randomly sampled subset of the dataset and utilising a subset of features. RF excels in handling high-dimensional data with numerous features and datasets containing a mix of categorical and numerical variables. Its inherent ability to select feature subsets for each tree mitigates the risk of overfitting. Moreover, RF exhibits remarkable resistance to noise and outliers, thanks to the aggregation of predictions from multiple trees, effectively dampening the influence of individual noisy instances. The RF model for each bacterial taxon was tuned using several hyperparameters ( mtry , splitrule and nodesize ) to achieve the highest accuracy. When building individual trees within the RF ensemble, the parameter mtry governs the number of variables randomly sampled as candidates at each split. The splitrule parameter determines the criterion used for splitting nodes during the construction of decision trees. The nodesize parameter specifies the minimum number of observations required to split a node further during the construction of each decision tree. We tested values of mtry calculated as the square root of the number of covariates selected after applying a Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) algorithm, as well as percentages (25%, 33.3%, and 40%) of the total number of selected variables. Two options were tested for splitrule parameter: “variance” and “extratrees”, whereas nodesize values ranged from 1 to 5. The ntree parameter was set to the default value of 500. The RF model with the lowest root mean squared error (RMSE) after a tenfold cross-validation procedure with ten repetitions was selected for modelling all considered bacterial taxa. The final parameters for Acidobacteriota modelling using the RF model were set as mtry = 28, splitrule = extratrees, and min.node.size = 3, whereas for the Planctomycetota model as mtry = 4, splitrule = variance, and min.node.size = 3. Environmental variables Using the RF approach, we incorporated a suite of environmental spatial variables (covariates) as explanatory variables for microbiome mapping. The selection of covariates was based on thoroughly considering abiotic and biotic factors that influence the soil microbiome. We used spatial information that presents climate, relief, vegetation, soil and geology features. The climate variables included precipitation, surface temperature (both day- and night-time), precipitable water vapor, snowfall probability, cloud cover and others, derived from the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer MODIS and Climatologies at high resolution for the Earth’s Land Surface Areas (CHELSA) 39 . The digital elevation model “EarthEnv-DEM90” was integrated 40 . Derived terrain variables such as slope, curvatures, terrain position index, valley depth, SAGA Wetness Index, and multiresolution index of valley bottom flatness were calculated using the System for Automated Geoscientific Analyses—Geographic Information System (SAGA GIS) software, version 9.3.3 ( https://saga-gis.sourceforge.io/ ). Vegetation or organism covariates were presented by Landsat and MODIS remote sensing data, as well as the Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) 41 , 42 . The land use map was derived from the European Space Agency (ESA) 43 . Soil covariates were presented by soil property and class maps. Soil properties map at 0–5 and 5–15 cm depths were derived from the SoilGrids 44 . They included chemical (soil organic carbon (SOC) content and stock, pH levels (H 2 O and KCl), cation exchange capacity (CEC)) and physical (texture, bulk density, available soil water capacity, and coarse fragments) soil properties. Soil type maps were according to the USDA soil taxonomy and World Reference Base (WRB). Geology spatial information was presented by landform and lithology classes 45 , regolith, and sedimentary deposit layers 46 , depth to bedrock, probability of bedrock occurrence, and absolute depth to bedrock 44 . The harmonization of spatial covariates consisted of several steps. Firstly, we harmonized all covariates at a 1 km spatial resolution to the same extent using bilinear and nearest neighbour interpolation methods for numerical and categorical variables, respectively. To mitigate redundancy, we excluded highly correlated variables with Pearson correlation coefficients &gt; 0.85 or &lt; − 0.85, retaining one variable from each pair of correlated covariates. After harmonising all spatial variables, we applied RFE algorithm to eliminate less important features based on their importance scores 47 . The RFE selection technique offers a flexible approach to identifying relevant explanatory variables from a dataset. By iteratively eliminating less important variables based on their importance scores, this technique can improve model performance, reduce overfitting, and enhance interpretability. Variable importance assessment ML algorithms provide variable importance measures, which can be used to identify relevant explanatory variables in a model 48 . During the RF modelling, we estimated the importance of environmental covariates in each model using the permutation method. This method evaluates the impact of each variable on the model’s performance by measuring how much the model’s predictive accuracy decreases when the values of that variable are randomly shuffled 38 . Essentially, it assesses the drop in model performance caused by randomly permuting a particular covariate’s values, thus quantifying that feature’s importance. Additionally, Shapley values 49 from cooperative game theory have been adapted for variable importance assessment in RF models. Shapley values offer a theoretically grounded approach by considering the marginal contribution (positive or negative) of each feature across all subsets of features. A positive SHAP value indicates that the feature increases the model’s prediction. In other words, the presence of that feature makes the model more likely to predict a higher value for the target variable (e.g., phylum abundance). Conversely, negative values indicate that the feature decreases the model’s prediction and the presence of that feature makes the model more likely to predict a lower value for the target variable. Thus, this approach illuminates the importance of each feature by quantifying its impact on the model’s predictions and provides a more nuanced view of variable importance compared to traditional techniques, such as mean decrease in impurity or mean decrease in accuracy 50 . Model validation Standard cross-validation (CV), such as leave-one-out (LOO) CV or k -fold CV, can be biased and overly optimistic in the case of clustered and unevenly distributed data 51 , 52 . We used the Nearest Neighbour Distance Matching (NNDM) LOO CV approach to obtain reliable map accuracy estimates with our clustered dataset. This method involves matching the nearest neighbour distance distribution function between the test and training data during the CV process to the nearest neighbour distance distribution function between the target prediction and training points 52 . Two indicators were used to evaluate the model performance: RMSE and the coefficient of determination (R 2 ). Additionally, to assess the accuracy and reliability of our spatial predictions of phylum abundance, we employed the “Area of Applicability” (AoA) approach 53 . This method evaluates the model’s performance within its intended domain of applicability, identifying areas where predictions are likely to be more reliable. All steps to implement spatial modelling, cross-validation, and statistical analysis were performed using “ranger” (RF implementation), “NNDM” (cross-validation), “fastshap” (Shapley values) and other popular packages in the R programming environment 52 , 54 . According to the error metrics, the values of RMSE and R 2 for the Acidobacteriota model were 6.67% and 0.41, respectively. For Planctomycetota, these values were 2.04% and 0.46, respectively. In the case of Acidobacteriota, lower values of MODIS MIR band 7 covariate, that correspond to more developed vegetation, positively contributed to phylum predictions. Conversely, higher values (indicative of bare soils) of this remote sensing band also contributed positively to the predictions. Among climate variables, higher surface temperatures and lower precipitation levels contributed to lower predicted phylum abundances. Soils with higher SOC content resulted in greater Acidobacteriota abundances, whereas more acidic soils negatively contributed to the prediction. For Planctomycetota, climate covariates, including precipitable water vapor, snowfall probability, and land surface temperature, had a wide range of Shapley values in the RF model. Similarly, high pH (alkaline soils) and precipitable water vapor values negatively contributed to the prediction of Planctomycetota abundance. Thus, both bacterial taxa showed similar trends, where warmer and drier climates were associated with their lower microbial abundances. Consequently, such climatic conditions lead to less fertile, more alkaline soils, which also negatively impacted the predictions. Spatial assessment The generated maps of the phylum are shown in Figs. 6 a and 7 a. For most of the territory investigated, the proportion of the phylum Acidobacteriota in soils ranged from 6 to 24%, tending to decrease from North to South, with the minimum abundance (from 0 to 5%) in the Black Earth (Chernozem) Region. Thus, representatives of Acidobacteriota apparently avoid arid regions with chernozems, solonetz, and solonchaks which are rich in carbonates and easily soluble salts, regardless of the part of the continent (Europe or South Asia). In contrast, Acidobacteriota are likely more abundant and represent a larger proportion of the prokaryotic community in soils of regions with humid cold climates, cryometamorphic, gleyed, peaty, and podzolic soils. Discussion Acidobacteriota is one of the most widespread and diverse phyla, particularly abundant in soils, where they can constitute up to 50–60% of all soil bacteria 55 – 57 . Despite the advent of molecular biological methods identifying many Acidobacteriota species, only 61 have been fully characterized 58 , and culturing remains challenging 59 . These limitations constrain our understanding of their ecological functions, which vary significantly across environmental gradients. Our RF model provided an R 2 value of 0.41 for Acidobacteriota predictions, indicating moderate predictive reliability. Spatial soil models often have limited accuracy, with R 2 values typically below 0.7 44 , 60 , 61 . This is due to various factors, including data quality issues, limited data availability, spatial bias, insufficient explanatory variables, and model limitations 62 . Key predictors, including MODIS MIR band 7, soil pH, SOC, and land surface temperature, explain the phylum’s distribution across diverse soil types. As indicated by Shapley values, well-developed vegetation (moderate MIR band 7 values), acidic soils (pH 4.5–5.5), and regions with higher SOC were positively associated with Acidobacteriota abundance. This aligns with previous findings on their preference for humid, acidic environments with abundant organic matter 54 , 55 . Notably, regions with high carbonate or salinity levels, such as chernozems and solonchaks, showed minimal Acidobacteriota representation, further emphasizing their sensitivity to arid and alkaline conditions. Spatial predictions showed decreasing Acidobacteriota abundance from north to south and from west to east, with the lowest values in arid regions dominated by carbonate-rich soils. The predictive power, along with an RMSE value of 6.67%, suggests that the RF model captured broad ecological patterns. However, the relatively low R 2 value indicates the possible influence of unmeasured or spatially variable factors, as well as substantial error, particularly in regions with more complex environmental conditions. Cultured representatives of Acidobacteriota are heterotrophic, mostly aerobic or microaerophilic, with some classes being more versatile in carbohydrate consumption 59 . Participation of Acidobacteria in the transformation of compounds—products of the decomposition of plant residues, i.e. cellulose, hemicellulose, and xylans 2 , 63 , 64 , determines the influence of plant community composition factors (expressed in MODIS MIR band 7, EVI index and SOC) on the abundance of Acidobacteriota and on its observed spatial distribution (Fig. 4 ). The analysis of the RF model indicates that vegetation cover and soil properties, such as pH, CEC and SOC, were the most influential predictors of Acidobacteriota abundance across the investigated regions (Fig. 4 ). For Planctomycetota, the RF model demonstrated slightly better predictive power, with an R 2 value of 0.46 and a notably lower RMSE of 2.04%, indicating better prediction accuracy for this phylum. Key predictors, including climate variables, soil pH, and water capacity, underlined the phylum’s adaptation to moist, cold environments. Shapley analysis revealed a strong negative impact of high precipitable water vapor levels and alkaline soil pH, while moderate diurnal temperature ranges and water availability positively influenced predictions. These results align with Planctomycetota’s ecological preference for waterlogged soils and coarse-grained soils, particularly in colder high-latitude and aquatic ecosystems 7 , 8 , 56 . The spatial maps generated by the RF model corroborate these ecological preferences. Planctomycetota abundance was minimal in arid, carbonate-rich soils and higher in regions with cold, humid climates and poorly drained soils, such as podzols and gleyed soils. This distribution mirrors their physiological characteristics, including the ability to grow anaerobically and participate in nitrogen cycling through processes like anaerobic ammonium oxidation 64 . The spatial distribution patterns generated by the RF models emphasize the critical role of soil and climatic variables in shaping the abundance of both phyla. Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota both exhibit reduced abundance in arid and carbonate-rich soils, highlighting their sensitivity to environmental gradients. The maps (Figs. 6 and 7 ) reveal strong spatial trends, with Acidobacteriota being more abundant in humid cold climates, and Planctomycetota favoring waterlogged soils and tundra ecosystems. However, it is important to acknowledge that not all potential soil and climatic factors were included in the models, which could have led to unaccounted sources of variability in the predictions. While both models identified climate and soil properties as key drivers of phylum distribution, future studies should consider incorporating additional environmental variables, particularly soil parameters and microbial interactions, as well as more detailed land use data, to refine model predictions of taxa distribution and understand their ecological roles. Limitations and future prospects Despite our achievements, several limitations should be considered when interpreting our results. Firstly, the clustered and uneven nature of our dataset introduced spatial biases, with certain regions oversampled and others underrepresented. Another significant factor is the spatial resolution of our environmental covariates (1 km). While this set of covariates offers a comprehensive view of environmental variables across Russia, its coarse resolution may not adequately capture fine-scale heterogeneity in soil properties and land use patterns. This limitation could potentially obscure localized effects on soil microbiome composition, thereby introducing uncertainties into model predictions, especially in regions characterized by diverse landscapes or abrupt environmental gradients. Addressing these issues through the collection of uniformly distributed datasets in both feature and geographical space, as well as enhancing the spatial resolution of covariates, will be crucial for further research in this field. A major limitation of this study is the heterogeneity of data sources. Our dataset was compiled from multiple independent publications, each employing different sequencing platforms, primer sets, and data processing pipelines. Although focusing on phylum-level taxa helps mitigate methodological biases, as demonstrated in previous studies 65 – 69 , such discrepancies cannot be entirely eliminated. Therefore, the spatial patterns identified here should be consideredas preliminary and subject to further refinement. Another potential source of variability in microbial community composition is the diversity of vegetation types across the sampled sites. Although we primarily categorized vegetation at the biome level (e.g., forests, arable lands, fallow grasslands), detailed plant species composition was not uniformly reported across studies. Existing research suggests that plant species identity and physiological status may strongly influence soil microbial communities at lower taxonomic levels (e.g., genera and species) 70 – 73 . However, at the phylum level, microbial community structure appears to be more closely linked to soil physicochemical properties rather than to vegetation 72 , 74 . Nevertheless, incorporating detailed vegetation data in future studies could help improve predictive accuracy. Given these limitations, we emphasize the need for future research focused on the generation of standardized, high-resolution datasets to enhance the robustness and comparability of microbial biogeographical models. Beyond differences in study designs and sequencing platforms, additional challenges stem from the inherent complexity of sequencing data itself. Issues such as error filtering, abundance normalization, and taxonomic identification and estimation 62 can introduce further variability. Modern sequencers generate hundreds of millions to billions of short reads (primarily from NGS platforms used for metabarcoding), imposing significant computational constraints on analytical algorithms. Moreover, the datasets included in this study originated from research groups that applied different sequencing data processing workflows, quality thresholds, and sequence filtering criteria. This technical diversity, combined with the compositional nature of microbiome data—vectors where all components sum to one 63 —can limit the applicability of conventional statistical approaches, such as multifactor generalized linear models originally developed for continuous absolute data 64 , in exploring the relationships between microbial community composition and environmental factors. Furthermore, challenges arise from the specifics of raw sequencing data, including issues with error filtering, abundance normalization, and taxonomic identification and estimation 75 . Modern sequencers generate hundreds of millions to billions of short reads (primarily NGS platforms used for metabarcoding), imposing computational constraints on analytical algorithms. Moreover, the data used in this study originated from various research groups employing different sequencing data processing algorithms, quality thresholds, and sequence filtering criteria. In addition to these challenges, microbiome data are considered as complex composite vectors of probabilities, where the sum equals to one 76 . This characteristic may limit the applicability of standard methods, such as multifactor generalized linear models, originally designed for continuously varying absolute data 77 , in exploring relationships between microbial composition and the studied factors. Soil, as an object for microbiome analysis, constitutes a multiphase, heterogeneous, and dynamic environment, leading to spatial variations in the soil microbiome composition and structure. This complexity poses two primary challenges: first, the difficulty in comparing data across different research groups due to variations in soil sampling strategies, potentially resulting in the loss of genetic information; and second, the challenge of identifying and combining factors that explain variation in microbiome composition. Often, measured characteristics only partially account for this variation 55 . For example, while organic carbon derived from root exudates may influence the structure of rhizosphere microbial communities, its precise measurement under laboratory conditions remains challenging 78 . These limitations underscore the need for standardized methodologies in data collection and analysis, as well as advancements in sequencing technologies and computational tools, to enhance our understanding of soil microbiome dynamics across diverse ecological contexts. Conclusions This study represents the application of ML techniques to interpret and capture the spatial patterns of microbial 16S rRNA data across different soil types and environmental gradients in Russia. For these tasks, we incorporated environmental covariates as predictors and interpreted their contributions using importance scores and Shapley values. The diversity and composition of Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota in Russian soils were characterized using 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding and ML methods. We used a legacy dataset representing the two phyla—Acidobacteria and Planctomycetes, which are cosmopolitan microbial taxa in soil. The results demonstrated that the combinations of vegetation, climate and soil pH significantly contributed to the prediction of Acidobacteria, whereas climate (precipitable water vapor, snowfall probability, temperature) and soil (pH and water content capacity) variables were key predictors of Planctomycetota. Thus, the combination of abiotic and biotic factors sufficiently explained the spatial shifts in the abundance of these taxa, corresponding to the physiological and environmental strategies employed by the prokaryotes. According to the generated maps, there was a decrease in the proportions of Acidobacteriota and Planctomycetota from North to South, which reflects the influence of different ecoclimatic zones across Russia. Variations in the metabolic processes of individual representatives of these phyla, coupled with the broad functional diversity observed in other dominant soil taxa, such as Pseudomonadota (encompassing Alpha-, Beta-, and Gammaproteobacteria, among others), Firmicutes, Bacteroidota, and Actinomonadota, establish a foundation for using this method to map the abundance of these prokaryotic groups at finer taxonomic levels, such as classes and orders. Future research aimed at studying and analyzing the biogeography of soil prokaryotic taxa may well pursue this goal. Interpreting ML models used to predict bacterial phyla distribution is crucial for understanding the complex relationships between environmental factors and microbial communities, moving beyond simple predictions to gain valuable ecological insights. We acknowledge that the compiled dataset is heterogeneous, encompassing variations in sampling protocols, sequencing platforms, and data processing approaches across different studies. These methodological differences inevitably introduce uncertainty, particularly at finer spatial and taxonomic scales. Nevertheless, by focusing on high-level taxonomic ranks (phyla) and using robust ML techniques, we were able to extract meaningful large-scale patterns. Our results should be considered preliminary and indicative, providing a valuable foundation for future research based on standardized approaches.
