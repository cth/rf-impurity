Abstract Flooding has become a major threat to soybean [ Glycine max (L.) Merr.] production as the frequency and intensity of extreme precipitations have been increasing due to climate change. While advances have been made in identifying soybean genetic resources and genomic regions associated with mid‐season flood tolerance, there is limited understanding of early season flood tolerance at the vegetative growth stages V2/V4. This study aimed to identify genomic regions associated with early season flood tolerance using a diverse panel of 254 soybean accessions, as well as investigate the viability of implementing genomic prediction models for flood tolerance. Field trials were conducted over 2 years, with flooding imposed at the V2/V4 vegetative growth stages. Genome‐wide association studies were performed using the Bayesian‐information and linkage‐disequilibrium iteratively nested keyway and the multiple locus mixed linear model. Forward stepwise genomic prediction models using random forest (RF) were developed to identify the set of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) yielding the highest prediction accuracy while assessing the negative impacts of multicollinearity and overfitting on prediction accuracy. Genomic regions on chromosomes 4, 17, and 20 associated with early season flood tolerance were identified, all distinct from regions previously identified for mid‐season tolerance. The RF model achieved a prediction accuracy of 0.64 with 29 selected SNPs, significantly improving over RF and ridge regression best linear unbiased prediction models with higher SNP counts. These findings provide genomic tools for improving the efficiency of breeding for early season flood tolerance, supporting the need to develop season‐long flood‐tolerant soybean genotypes. Core Ideas Early season and mid‐season flood tolerance have different genetic architecture. Genomic prediction models can accurately identify flood‐tolerant genotypes. Overfitting and multicollinearity can significantly hinder prediction accuracy. Plain Language Summary Flooding is threatening soybean production due to climate change causing more extreme rainfall. While progress has been made in developing soybeans that can tolerate flooding later in the season, less is understood about flood tolerance during the early season. This study aimed to identify genetic markers linked to early season flood tolerance and assess a prediction model to help breeders more efficiently select flood‐tolerant soybeans. Using a diverse group of 254 soybean genotypes, new flood‐tolerance markers on chromosomes 4, 17, and 20 were identified, all different from those associated with later growth stages. A machine learning model selected 29 key markers, achieving high prediction accuracy, significantly outperforming models with larger numbers of markers. These findings provide a foundation for breeding soybeans that are flood‐tolerant throughout the season, providing farmers with more resilient crop options in response to the growing risk of flooding. Globally, flooding affects over 10% of arable land, posing a serious threat to the sustainability of crop production (Kaur et al., 2020 ; Shabala, 2011 ; Voesenek &amp; Sasidharan, 2013 ). The increasing frequency and intensity of extreme precipitations due to climate change further threatens global crop production and overall food security (Pathak et al., 2017 ; Rodell &amp; Li, 2023 ). Soybean [ Glycine max (L.) Merr.] is susceptible to flooding during seed germination, early season vegetative stages (V2–V4) (Fehr &amp; Charles, 1977 ), and mid‐season reproductive growth stages (R1–R4) (Fletcher et al., 2023 ; Ploschuk et al., 2022 ; Rhine et al., 2010 ; Wu, Chen, et al., 2017 ; Wu et al., 2024 ). Insufficient oxygen during flooding leads to hypoxia, causing symptoms such as chlorosis, necrosis of leaves and roots, stunting, and death of rhizobacteria nodules, potentially resulting in yield losses of up to 100% (Grable, 1966 ; Kaur et al., 2020 ; Russell et al., 1990 ; Sullivan et al., 2001 ). This highlights the urgent need to identify flood‐tolerant genetic resources and genomic regions associated with tolerance, as well as to develop improved flood‐tolerant soybean genotypes. Flooding during mid‐season reproductive stages has been the primary focus of research due to its greater impact on yield losses (Fletcher et al., 2023 ; Rhine et al., 2010 ; Scott et al., 1989 ; Wu et al., 2024 ). Substantial progress has been made in identifying flood‐tolerant genetic resources and genomic regions associated with flood tolerance. VanToai et al. ( 1994 ) evaluated 84 soybean cultivars under flooding conditions at the mid‐season reproductive growth stages and reported significant differences in flood tolerance among the entries. Wu et al. ( 2020 ) screened 384 soybean plant introductions (PIs) for flood tolerance at the mid‐season reproductive growth stages. The study identified 29 PIs as highly tolerant to flooding. Genomic regions associated with mid‐season flood tolerance have been identified on chromosomes 1 (Linkage Group [LG] D1a) (Zhang et al., 2022 ), 3 (LG N) (Wu et al., 2020 ; Ye et al., 2018 ), 4 (LG C1) (Wu et al., 2020 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ), 5 (LG A1) (Zhang et al., 2022 ), 7 (LG M) (Wu et al., 2020 ), 11 (LG B1) (Nguyen et al., 2012 ), 13 (LG F) (Nguyen et al., 2012 ; Wu et al., 2020 ; Ye et al., 2018 ), 16 (LG J) (Zhang et al., 2022 ), 18 (LG G) (Zhang et al., 2022 ), and 19 (LG L) (Wu et al., 2020 ). The changes in precipitation patterns in soybean‐producing regions have increased the occurrence of early season flooding conditions (Walthall et al., 2012 ). This study shows that the genetic architecture of early season flood tolerance differs from that of mid‐season reproductive growth stages. Therefore, there is a pressing need to develop season‐long flood‐tolerant genotypes by pyramiding favorable flood‐tolerant alleles from both growth stages. However, knowledge of genetic resources and genomic regions associated with early season flood tolerance remains limited. The objectives of this study were to identify novel genetic resources for early season flood tolerance from a panel of 254 genetically diverse soybean accessions and unveil genomic regions associated with flood tolerance. Given the quantitative nature of the trait, the study also explored the viability of developing and implementing genomic prediction models for accurately identifying and selecting flood‐tolerant genotypes. 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS 2.1 Experimental design 2.1.1 Plant materials and genotypic data A total of 254 genetically diverse accessions were included in this study (Figure S1 ). Accessions originated from 14 countries, including Argentina (two accessions), China (36 accessions), Japan (58 accessions), Mexico (three accessions), Morocco (one accession), North Korea (seven accessions), South Africa (one accession), South Korea (92 accessions), Taiwan (one accession), Turkey (one accession), United States (46 accessions), Vietnam (one accession), Zimbabwe (one accession), and four of unknown origin (Table S1 ). Accessions were equally distributed across maturity groups (MGs) IV (80 accessions), V (91 accessions), and VI (83 accessions) (Table S1 ). Additional agronomic traits are reported in Table S1 . As part of the USDA Soybean Germplasm Collection, genetically diverse accessions were genotyped with the SoySNP50K BeadChip (Song et al., 2013 ) and genotypic data has been graciously made available by the authors (Song et al., 2015 ) at SoyBase Genetics and Genomics Database ( http://soybase.org/snps/download.php ). The 31,410 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were relatively equally distributed across the 20 chromosomes with an average of 1570 SNPs per chromosome, ranging from 995 (chromosome 20, LG I) to 2679 (chromosome 18, LG G). For both genome‐wide association studies (GWAS) and genomic prediction, SNPs were converted to numerical format (0, 1, and 2 for the homozygous minor allele, heterozygous, and homozygous major allele, respectively). 2.1.2 Field trials and data collection Field trials were conducted at the Rice Research &amp; Extension Center in Stuttgart, AR (34°27′56.1″ N, 91°25′20.4″ W) in a DeWitt silt loam soil in 2021 and 2022. Entries were arranged in a three‐replicate randomized complete block design, with plots consisting of a single 1.5 m row spaced 0.76 m apart. Flooding treatment consisted of a 8‐ to 10‐day water immersion (5–10 cm from the soil surface) at the early vegetative stages V2/V4 (Fehr &amp; Charles, 1977 ). Fields had a near‐zero grade slope and were monitored daily for uniform flooding depth. Entries were rated for flood damage score (FDS) on a 1–9 scale 7 days after water removal following established protocol (Wu, Zeng, et al., 2017 ). Briefly, the scale was based on the percentage of plants in a plot showing flooding‐related damage where a score of 1 represents ≤10% of plants showing damage and 9 represents ≥80% of plants showing flooding‐related damage (Wu, Zeng, et al., 2017 ). Overfitting and multicollinearity can significantly hinder prediction accuracy. To increase the within‐genotype consistency of FDS across all observations (three replicates × two locations), a simple three‐step methodology was implemented to identify and adjust outliers for each genotype. The first step consisted of calculating the coefficient of variation (CV, ratio of the standard deviation to the mean) of each genotype including all six observations. To determine the impact of individual observations on the genotype's CV, each observation was sequentially excluded, and the CV was recalculated. The single observation whose exclusion caused the most significant change in the CV was identified as an outlier and replaced with the average FDS of the non‐outlier observations. To assess the consistency of FDS across observations, Cronbach's alpha ( α ) (Cronbach, 1951 ) was calculated using the function " alpha " of the package psych (Revelle, 2021 ) in R (R Core Team, 2024 ). Cronbach's alpha ( α ) is a statistical measure that evaluates the internal consistency of a set of items based on the average inter‐item correlation (Equation 1 ). (1) α = N × c ¯ v ¯ + N − 1 × c ¯ where N is the number of items in the test, represented here as each observation of FDS (three replicates × two locations); c ¯ is the average inter‐item covariance; and v ¯ is the average variance of each item. The adjusted means of FDS for each genotype were used as the response variable for both GWAS and genomic prediction models. The adjusted means were calculated using the function " emmeans " of the R package " emmeans " (Lenth et al., 2024 ) based on a mixed‐linear model fitted using the function " lmer " of the R package " lmerTest " (Bates et al., 2014 ; Kuznetsova et al., 2017 ). The model included the fixed effect of genotype ( i = 1, 2, …, 254), the random interaction between genotype and year ( j = 2021 and 2022), and the random effect of replication ( k = 1, 2, 3) nested within year (Equation 2 ). (2) FD S i j k = μ + Ge n i + Gen × Year i j + Rep | Year k j + ε i j k where FD S i j k represents the FDS for the i th genotype, j th year, and k th replicate; μ represents the overall mean of FDS; Ge n i represents the fixed effect of the i th genotype; ( Gen × Year ) i j is the random effect associated with the interaction between the i th genotype and the j th year; ( Rep | Year ) k j is the random effect of the k th replicate nested within the j th year; and ε i j k is the residual term error with ε ijk ∼ N (0, σ 2 ε ). 2.2 Genomic analysis 2.2.1 Genome‐wide association studies GWAS were conducted to identify significant associations between SNPs and FDS across tested entries. Two different models were implemented using the R package GAPIT (Lipka et al., 2012 ), including the Bayesian‐information and linkage‐disequilibrium iteratively nested keyway (BLINK) (Huang et al., 2019 ) and the multiple locus mixed linear model (MLMM) (Segura et al., 2012 ). In summary, BLINK is an improved version of the fixed and random model circulating probability unification (FarmCPU) model (X. Liu et al., 2016 ) that eliminates the assumption of causal genes being evenly spread across the genome. The approach iteratively updates the model by adding or removing molecular markers, balancing model complexity and accuracy, which enhances statistical power and efficiency in identifying true‐positive associations. It uses the Bayesian information criterion to select the best model from a set of candidate models as opposed to the restricted maximum likelihood in FarmCPU. The MLMM uses a stepwise approach to select a subset of molecular markers fitted as a fixed effect, while the population structure representing the relatedness among individuals through the kinship matrix is fitted as a random effect (Segura et al., 2012 ). This approach allows for the incorporation of multiple molecular markers, which is particularly beneficial for identifying associations in traits with a complex genetic architecture. The significance threshold was obtained using the modified Sidak correction (Sidak, 1967 ) following the protocol established by Li and Ji ( 2005 ) (Equation 3 ). Briefly, the number of tests ( M ) is replaced by the effective number of independent tests ( M eff ), which is calculated based on the eigenvalue decomposition of the correlation matrix of SNPs (Li &amp; Ji, 2005 ). Candidate genes located within 50 kb of the identified SNPs and potentially associated with the trait of interest based on known functions or homology to other species were reported using the genome browser Wm82.a2 ( www.soybase.org ). (3) α p = 1 − 1 − α e 1 / M eff where α p is the corrected significance threshold, α e is the experiment‐wide significance level set at 0.05, and M eff is the effective number of independent tests calculated based on the eigenvalue decomposition of the SNPs correlation matrix. 2.2.2 Genomic prediction models A forward stepwise genomic prediction model was developed using the machine learning algorithm random forest (RF) (Breiman, 2001 ) to identify the combination of independent SNPs that yielded the highest prediction accuracy ( r , Pearson's correlation coefficient between observed and predicted values). RF is a tree‐based supervised learning algorithm that was selected due to its high computational efficiency in high dimensional problems (i.e., number of predictors far greater than the number of samples), as well as a proper balance between the variance‐bias trade‐off (Breiman, 2001 ). While this approach has been previously reported in classification problems (Canella Vieira, Zhou, et al., 2022 ; Canella Vieira et al., 2023 ), this is the first demonstration of its use in a regression context. The model is designed to reduce overfitting and multicollinearity by removing SNPs that are irrelevant to the response variable (i.e., with a variable importance in projection [VIP] &lt; 1.5) and highly correlated with other predictors (with Pearson's correlation coefficients ≥ 0.7). In this study, a partial least square model (Hold, 1966 ) was trained with FDS as the response variable and SNPs as predictors using the “ plsr ” function from the R package “ pls ” (Mevik &amp; Wehrens, 2007 ). The number of components was optimized using a 10‐fold cross‐validation, and the importance of each SNP in the components (VIP) was retrieved using the function “ VIP ” from the R package “ plsVarSel ” (Mehmood et al., 2012 ). Predictors with a VIP &lt; 1.5 were removed from the analysis. For the remaining SNPs, Pearson's correlation coefficients were computed for all pairwise combinations. SNPs with a pairwise correlation of ≥0.7 had the SNP with the lower VIP score discarded, while both SNPs were retained if the pairwise correlation was &lt;0.7. The relevant, noncorrelated SNPs were then used as predictors in the RF model through a forward stepwise procedure to identify the combination of independent SNPs that achieved the highest prediction accuracy. In the first iteration, the model was fitted with the SNP having the highest VIP. In the subsequent iteration, the model tested all remaining SNPs ( n − 1) and selected the combination of two SNPs that provided the highest prediction accuracy. This process was repeated, adding one SNP at a time, until all SNPs were incorporated in the model. As described in Canella Vieira et al. ( 2023 ), all iterations were conducted using a fivefold cross‐validation. The RF model was conducted using the R package “ ranger ” (Wright &amp; Ziegler, 2017 ) with the square root of p predictors (number of predictors) randomly selected at each split. For all iterations, prediction accuracy ( r ) was defined as the average Pearson's correlation coefficient between observed and predicted values across the five cross‐validation folds. In addition to the forward stepwise RF model, a ridge regression best linear unbiased prediction (RR‐BLUP) model (Endelman, 2011 ) was performed with the entire set of non‐filtered SNPs (31,410) as predictors using 10‐fold cross‐validation with the R package “ rrBLUP ” (Endelman, 2011 ). The RR‐BLUP method is known to minimize the “curse of dimensionality” (when the number of predictors far exceeds the number of samples) by shrinking the estimated effects of individual markers, while also simultaneously estimating the effects of all markers by assuming a common variance across them (Endelman, 2011 ). In this study, the RR‐BLUP model was used as the benchmark to assess the potential positive impacts of limiting overfitting and multicollinearity in prediction accuracy using the machine learning algorithm RF. 3 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 3.1 Flooding response The average FDS across all entries was 4.4 over both testing years (4.5 and 4.4 in 2021 and 2022, respectively), with a standard deviation of 1.2 and 1.3 in 2021 and 2022, respectively (Table 1 ). Ratings were consistent across observations with an overall Cronbach's alpha ( α ) score of 0.83 (CI 0.80–0.86), resulting in an estimated error variance of 0.31 (Table 1 ). Trials with α above 0.70 (which corresponds to an error variance of 0.51) are generally regarded as reliable (Amirrudin et al., 2020 ; Bland &amp; Altman, 1997 ; Canella Vieira, Jarquin, et al., 2022 ; Tavakol &amp; Dennick, 2011 ). A total of 14 genotypes were identified as highly tolerant to flooding stress at the V2/V4 growth stages (FDS ≤ 3.0), including PI594567A (FDS = 1.8, China), PI594779 (2.0, China), PI398968 (2.1, South Korea), PI603907 (2.2, North Korea), PI399044 (2.4, South Korea), and nine more (Table S1 ). Conversely, a total of 18 genotypes were identified as highly susceptible to flooding stress at the V2/V4 growth stages (FDS ≥ 6.0), including PI424445 (7.0, South Korea), PI408339 (7.0, South Korea), PI548401 (7.0, Morocco), PI172902 (6.6, Turkey), PI507422 (6.6, Japan), and 13 more (Table S1 ). Given the need to develop genotypes with early season flood tolerance, these accessions may serve as valuable genetic resources for improving soybean tolerance to flooding stress through breeding, as well as genetic studies aimed at mapping additional genomic regions associated with flood tolerance or fine‐mapping previously identified regions. 3.2 Genomic regions of interest The effective number of independent tests ( M eff ) was ≈650, resulting in a significance threshold of ≈4.10 (corrected significance α p = 7.89 × 10 −5 ). This is substantially less stringent as compared to the Bonferroni correction ( α p = 1.59 × 10 −6 , significance threshold of ≈5.80) (Neyman &amp; Pearson, 1967 ). Although a more stringent threshold may minimize Type‐I errors (false positive associations), it comes at the expense of increasing the likelihood of Type‐II errors (false negatives) (Dudbridge &amp; Gusnanto, 2008 ; Johnson et al., 2010 ). Thus, adjusting the significance threshold based on the effective number of independent tests can provide a suitable balance, minimizing false positives while still detecting smaller effect SNPs. This approach offers a more accurate representation of the genetic architecture of quantitative traits, such as early season flood tolerance. Significant marker‐trait associations were identified on chromosomes 4 (LG C1), 17 (LG D2), and 20 (LG I) using the BLINK and MLMM models (Figure 1 ). To the best of our knowledge, most GWAS and quantitative trait loci (QTL) mapping study have been conducted in the mid‐season reproductive growth stages. Significant associations at the reproductive growth stages have been reported on chromosomes 1 (Zhang et al., 2022 ), 3 (Wu et al., 2020 ; Ye et al., 2018 ), 4 (Wu et al., 2020 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ), 5 (Zhang et al., 2022 ), 7 (Wu et al., 2020 ), 11 (Nguyen et al., 2012 ), 13 (Nguyen et al., 2012 ; Wu et al., 2020 ; Ye et al., 2018 ), 16 (Zhang et al., 2022 ), 18 (Zhang et al., 2022 ), and 19 (Wu et al., 2020 ). None of these previously reported associations overlap with the marker‐trait associations identified in this study for flood tolerance at the early season vegetative stages. ( 2019 ) conducted a QTL mapping study for flood tolerance at the early season vegetative stages using a recombinant inbred line population derived from Paldalkong (flood‐tolerant) × NTS1116. The study utilized two secondary traits as proxies for flooding response, including chlorophyll content and dry shoot weight (Dhungana et al., 2019 ). Among the many reported significant associations across nine chromosomes, a region on chromosome 17 (8,741,808–9,008,173 bp) overlapped with our study. Nevertheless, there is strong evidence that the genetic architecture of flood tolerance at the early season vegetative growth stages is distinct from that at the mid‐season reproductive growth stages. Understanding and targeting these growth stage‐specific genomic regions are essential for developing season‐long flood‐tolerant genotypes, which could support and promote the sustainability of soybean production in flooding‐prone regions. The SNP ss715587369 (minor allelic frequency [MAF] of 0.38) located at 2,182,229 bp of chromosome 4 was significant on the BLINK and MLMM models (Table 2 ). A total of seven genes have been reported within 50 kb (25 kb up‐ and downstream) of this SNP, including three with annotations potentially associated with flood tolerance. Glyma.04g027000 encodes a B‐box zinc finger protein similar to STH2 (Salt Tolerance Homolog 2) in Arabidopsis thaliana , which regulates transcription, particularly under stress conditions (Datta et al., 2007 ). Glyma.04g026500 and Glyma.04g026900 encode a predicted hydrolase/acyltransferase from the alpha/beta hydrolase superfamily. These are involved in lipid metabolism and can participate in various stress responses by maintaining cellular membrane stability (Gershater &amp; Edwards, 2007 ; Vijayaraj et al., 2012 ). The SNPs ss715628153 (MAF of 0.21) and ss715628154 (MAF of 0.29), located at 7,207,903 and 7,208,187 bp of chromosome 17, respectively, were found significant on the MLMM model (−log 10 ( p ‐value) of 4.37 and 4.66, respectively), while only ss715628153 was significant on the BLINK model (−log 10 ( p ‐value) of 4.37) (Table 2 ). Five genes have been reported within 50 kb (25 kb up‐ and downstream) of these two SNPs, of which three have annotations potentially associated with flood tolerance. Glyma.17g092300 is a nucleobase‐ascorbate transporter (NAT). With the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) during flooding, ascorbate is an important antioxidant that may contribute to the detoxication of ROS as well as the maintenance of redox homeostasis throughout stress exposure. In maize ( Zea mays ), the differential regulation of NATs under hypoxia‐induced conditions has been reported (Hofmann et al., 2022 ). NAT genes have been differently regulated under plant hormones and stress response, particularly abiotic stresses, including salt and drought in cotton ( Gossypium arboreum L.) (Guo et al., 2022 ) and apple ( Malus domestica ) (Sun et al., 2016 ). Glyma.17g092400 is a spermidine synthase, an enzyme involved in the polyamine biosynthesis catalyzing the formation of spermidine. Spermidine is known to have multiple roles in plants, particularly in stress response due to its antioxidant properties (Hasan et al., 2021 ; Hussain et al., 2011 ; Tsaniklidis et al., 2020 ). Exogenous spermidine has been shown to alleviate oxidative damage and improve flood tolerance in Welsh onion ( Allium fistulosum L.) (Yiu et al., 2009 ), maize (M. Y. Liu et al., 2014 ; Salah et al., 2022 ; Wang et al., 2024 ), rice ( Oryza sativa L.) (M. Liu et al., 2015 ), and cherry tomato ( Solanum lycopersicum L.) (C. Liu et al., 2023 ). Glyma.17g092700 is a calmodulin‐binding protein‐like (CBP‐like), which plays significant roles in plant responses to biotic and abiotic stresses by regulating calcium (Ca 2+ )‐mediated signaling pathways (Ranty et al., 2016 ). In flooding conditions, the differential expression of CBP‐like, including the targeted degradation of signaling proteins, may play a crucial role in redox homeostasis in cells under hypoxia conditions (Ahsan et al., 2007 ; Squier, 2006 ). Lastly, the SNP ss715638723 (MAF of 0.15), located at 46,275,367 bp of chromosome 20, was significant on both BLINK and MLMM models (−log 10 ( p ‐value) of 4.55 and 4.89, respectively). Within 50 kb (25 kb up‐ and downstream) of ss715638723 , eight genes have been reported, and only one whose annotation is potentially associated with flood tolerance (Table 2 ). Glyma.20g228000 has a protein kinase‐like domain, which is known to be crucial in plant response to multiple abiotic stresses (Patil &amp; Senthil‐Kumar, 2020 ). Specific to early vegetative flooding stress in soybeans, proteome analysis revealed kinase‐like proteins as one of 24 proteins with differential expression patterns under stress conditions (Alam et al., 2010 ). The gene ontology analysis of these candidate genes shows predominant association with key stress response pathways, such as response to oxidative stress and lipid metabolic process. This suggests that candidate genes may interact in a broader network of pathways critical for mitigating flooding‐induced damage, although further molecular studies are needed to validate their expression and role in flood tolerance. John Wiley &amp; Sons, Ltd. 3.3 Feature selection and prediction accuracy The average VIP across all SNPs was 0.91, of which 1677 SNPs were identified with VIP ≥ 1.50. After removing the highly correlated SNPs (with Pearson's correlation coefficients ≥ 0.7), a total of 613 SNPs presumably independent (noncorrelated) and associated with the phenotype of interest (VIP ≥ 1.50) were included in the forward stepwise genomic prediction model. These were generally equally distributed across all 20 chromosomes, ranging from 18 SNPs on chromosome 1 to 48 SNPs on chromosome 13, with an average of 30 SNPs per chromosome. The dimension and distribution of selected SNPs across all 20 chromosomes reinforce the quantitative nature of early season flood tolerance. A similar methodology applied to soybean resistance to southern root‐knot nematode [ Meloidogyne incognita (Kofold &amp; White) Chitwood] and off‐target dicamba (3,6‐dichloro‐2‐methoxybenzoic acid) tolerance, both known to possess a less quantitative genetic architecture, resulted in 29 and 41 SNPs after applying similar filtering criteria, respectively (Canella Vieira, Zhou, et al., 2022 ; Canella Vieira et al., 2023 ). As previously described, the first iteration of the RF model was fitted with the SNP having the highest VIP ( ss715628153 ), with subsequent iterations selecting the combination of SNPs yielding the highest prediction accuracy, adding one SNP at a time, until all 613 filtered SNPs were incorporated in the model. The highest prediction accuracy ( r , Pearson's correlation coefficient between observed and predicted values) was 0.64, with a root mean squared error (RMSE) of 0.72. This was obtained with the RF model including 29 SNPs (Figure 2 ). Interestingly, a pronounced decrease in prediction accuracy was observed with the systematic inclusion of SNPs. For instance, the RF model including all 613 filtered SNPs yielded a prediction accuracy of 0.42 with an RMSE of 0.81, while the RF model including all 31,410 non‐filtered SNPs resulted in a prediction accuracy of 0.30 with an RMSE of 0.85 (Figure 2 ). This represents over 52% increase in prediction accuracy (0.42–0.64) between the model including the selected 29 SNPs and the full set of 613 filtered SNPs, and an approximately twofold increase in prediction accuracy (0.30–0.64) compared to the full set of unfiltered 31,410 non‐filtered SNPs. In comparison with the RR‐BLUP model including all 31,410 non‐filtered SNPs, an increase of 39% in prediction accuracy was observed by implementing the stepwise RF model fitting in combination with SNP filtering (0.46–0.64). It is also worth noting that when both models included the entire set of 31,410 non‐filtered SNPs, the RR‐BLUP model outperformed RF by 53% (0.30–0.46). In genomic prediction problems, it is common to encounter the “curse of dimensionality” where the number of predictors (i.e., SNPs) is far greater than the number of observed samples (i.e., genotypes) (Altman &amp; Krzywinski, 2018 ; Ramstein et al., 2019 ). Under such scenario, the abundance of predictors, which often are highly correlated, results in multicollinearity (Daoud, 2017 ; Krzywinski &amp; Altman, 2015 ) and overfitting (Lever et al., 2016 ; Ying, 2019 ), significantly reducing the prediction accuracy and reproducibility of a given model. In recent years, contrasting results have been reported on the prediction accuracy of various soybean traits based on the number of SNPs included in different models. Xavier et al. ( 2016 ) investigated the impact of training population size, density of molecular markers, and various prediction models in the prediction accuracy of six soybean traits (grain yield, days to maturity, plant height, pod number, node number, and pods per node). The study identified the size of the training population as the most relevant property of a genomic prediction model, while the density of molecular markers was the least relevant (Xavier et al., 2016 ). It is worth noting that the study did not conduct an iterative selection of molecular markers based on prediction accuracy and/or significance. The three different SNP panels (100%, 50%, and 25% of SNPs) were formed by sampling all SNPs, every other SNP, and every fourth SNP, respectively. Beche et al. ( 2021 ) reported similar results with minimal differences in prediction accuracy across various SNP densities (250–2250 SNPs, with 250 SNP increments). However, the study also did not conduct an iterative selection of molecular markers based on prediction accuracy and/or significance, rather randomly selecting a subset of SNPs across all 20 chromosomes for each SNP density (Beche et al., 2021 ). Singer et al. ( 2022 ) reported increases in prediction accuracy of soybean methionine content as high as threefold between the model including the entire set of 35,570 SNPs and the model including a subset of 248 SNPs selected based on GWAS significance. Using a similar methodology for feature selection and a forward stepwise genomic prediction model as reported in the present study, Canella Vieira, Zhou, et al. ( 2022 ) reported the highest classification accuracy, precision, and specificity for soybean resistance to southern root‐knot nematode using 21 SNPs as opposed to the entire set of 4974 SNPs. A pronounced decrease in classification accuracy was observed as the number of SNPs included in the model increased (Canella Vieira, Zhou, et al., 2022 ). The same results were observed in a classification problem for off‐target dicamba tolerance. The study reported the highest classification accuracy, precision, and specificity in a RF model including 17 SNPs, followed by a pronounced decrease in classification accuracy with the inclusion of more SNPs (Canella Vieira et al., 2023 ). The proposed methodology aiming to identify the minimum combination of independent SNPs that yields the highest prediction accuracy may be effective at reducing multicollinearity and overfitting, thus increasing the prediction accuracy and overall model reproducibility. It is worth noting that, ideally, to avoid data leakage, this methodology would include a three‐way split—training, validation, and an independent testing dataset. However, due to the limited sample size in this study, an independent testing set was not feasible, which may lead to an overoptimistic model performance. This potential pitfall may occur because the validation set is not entirely independent of the training set during the iterative SNP selection. Nevertheless, this study reinforces the importance of SNP filtering when developing machine learning‐based genomic prediction models and highlights the negative impact of overfitting and multicollinearity in prediction accuracy. 4 CONCLUSIONS The increase in frequency and intensity of precipitation due to climate change is threatening global soybean production. The development and deployment of flood‐tolerant genotypes is essential to sustain production under flooding conditions. While substantial advancements have been made to identify and develop soybean genotypes tolerant to mid‐season flooding stress, knowledge of early season flood tolerance remains limited. Given the growing concern of flooding conditions in the early season, it is imperative to develop season‐long flood‐tolerant genotypes. This research reported that genetic architecture for early season tolerance differs from that of mid‐season tolerance. In addition, many potential genetic resources for breeding flood‐tolerant genotypes were listed. Genomic‐based prediction models have been shown to accurately identify tolerant genotypes, supporting the pressing need to develop season‐long flood‐tolerant genotypes. In addition, higher prediction accuracies were achieved by limiting multicollinearity and overfitting through SNP filtering and iterative model fitting. These findings provide genomic tools for improving the efficiency of breeding for early season flood tolerance, supporting the need to develop season‐long flood‐tolerant soybean genotypes.
