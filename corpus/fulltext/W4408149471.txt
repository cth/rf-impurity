Abstract Background: Evidence suggests the existence of nonlinearity in the relationship between long-term fine particulate matter (PM 2.5 ) and mortality, and the methods to flexibly incorporate nonlinearity can be improved. To heuristically evaluate the necessity of incorporating machine-learning algorithms, we compared the benefit of reducing long-term PM 2.5 on mortality estimated from three analytical methods with varying flexibility and complexity. Methods: Using a cohort of the Canadian Community Health Survey respondents (followed from 2005 until 2014), we obtained consented respondents’ baseline characteristics, time-varying annual average PM 2.5 in the previous 3 years, yearly income and neighborhood characteristics, and vital status. We estimated the 10-year cumulative mortality rate under both a natural-course exposure and a hypothetical dynamic intervention, which would set the respondent’s exposure to 8.8 μg/m 3 (current Canadian annual PM 2.5 standard) if higher. We compared estimates of three analytical methods and mean squared errors under a range of hypothetical true values. Results: Among 62,365 participants, the 10-year cumulative mortality rate differences per 1000 participants were −0.23 (95% confidence intervals: −0.46, 0.00), −0.83 (−1.24, −0.43), and −0.67 (−1.27, −0.06) for parametric g-computation, targeted minimum loss-based estimator using parametric models, and targeted minimum loss-based estimator with SuperLearner and six candidate algorithms of high flexibility, respectively. Changing the hyperparameters did not meaningful change estimates or algorithm weights. Conclusions: All three methods of reducing long-term exposure to PM 2.5 yielded tangible public health benefits in Canada where PM 2.5 levels are among the lowest worldwide. However, the advantage of employing machine-learning algorithms with a doubly robust estimator remains minimal, especially considering the variance-bias tradeoff. What this study adds: Accurate estimation of the health benefits of reducing fine particulate matter is important for evidence-based policy. There is evidence of nonlinearity in the chronic health impact of fine particulate matter, but the evidence considering more flexibility in estimating the health benefits of PM 2.5 intervention and in low-concentration settings is limited. In this short communication, we compared the benefit of an intervention reducing long-term fine particulate matter on mortality estimated using three novel causal estimators with varying flexibility and complexity in a Canadian national cohort. We conclude that the advantage of employing more complex machine-learning algorithms with a doubly robust estimator in this low-concentration example remains minimal. Introduction To further reduce the public health burden attributable to air pollution, government agencies around the world have progressively strengthened air quality standards, especially for fine particulate matter (PM 2.5 ). For example, the Canadian Council of Ministers of the Environment reduced the Canadian Air Ambient Quality Standards for annual PM 2.5 from 10.0 μg/m 3 to 8.8 μg/m 3 in 2020, 1 while the World Health Organization and the US Environmental Protection Agency employed similar reductions in 2021 and 2014, respectively. 2 , 3 The identification of detrimental health effects from long-term exposure to PM 2.5 in low concentrations contributed to such updates. 4 Indeed, previous studies linking long-term PM 2.5 to health outcomes suggested that adverse health impacts exist even at lower levels of exposure than existing standards and guidelines, with potential supra-linear relationships. 4 The previous characterization of nonlinearity in such relationships was often done by modeling the concentration-response function in multivariable outcome regressions (e.g., Cox proportional hazards model) with spline functions and interactions across covariates. Whether adopting more flexible methods to incorporate such nonlinear patterns in evaluating the health benefits of PM 2.5 intervention, especially in low-exposure environments (e.g., Canada), has not been explored. 4 In recent years, data-adaptive algorithms (i.e., machine-learning algorithms) have been increasingly utilized to incorporate nonlinearity considerations. 5 , 6 Such algorithms are employed within marginal estimators, which can directly estimate the health benefits of hypothetical interventions. 7 Machine-learning (ML) algorithms avoid imposing overly restrictive parametric model assumptions and reduce bias due to model misspecification, especially in high-dimensional settings. The nonlinear relationships are usually incorporated through modeling nonlinearity including interactions across covariates in nuisance functions of these marginal estimators (e.g., outcome model in the g-computation), leading to easier interpretation than directly reported coefficients of multivariable outcome regressions. However, using ML algorithms with singly robust marginal estimators (e.g., g-computation) may lead to bias and incorrect confidence intervals. 8 Instead, doubly robust estimators (e.g., targeted minimum loss-based estimator [TMLE]) constitute the preferred option to incorporate ML algorithms. 8 To evaluate the necessity of incorporating flexible ML algorithms in estimating the benefit of long-term PM 2.5 intervention on mortality, we compared three analytical methods with varying flexibility and complexity by calculating the differences in effect estimates and quantifying the bias-variance tradeoff across a range of plausible true effect estimates. Methods As a case study, we used a national cohort of respondents to the 2000/2001 cycle of the Canadian Community Health Survey with linkage to administrative datasets. We obtained consented respondents’ baseline characteristics (i.e., time-fixed confounders including sex, age, body mass index, marital status, immigration status, visible minority status, indigenous status, smoking status, diet, physical activity, working status, and education attainment) and individual and community-level characteristics varying across the year (i.e., time-varying confounders including annual income and annual neighborhood characteristics). We restricted the cohort to participants who were alive on 1 January 2005 to avoid potential differential participation bias and followed them till 2014. We calculated respondents’ moving average PM 2.5 in the previous 3 years at residential postal codes as the time-varying exposure for each year and utilized yearly nonaccidental death as the outcome. Details of cohort creation, exposure assessment, and covariate obtainment are included in eAppendix 1; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 . To calculate the health benefits of an intervention reducing long-term PM 2.5 on mortality with varying flexibility and complexity, we used three analytical methods (i.e., combinations of estimators and algorithms) to estimate the 10-year cumulative mortality rate (till the end of follow-up) under both a no intervention scenario and a dynamic hypothetical intervention scenario: (1) g-computation using parametric generalized linear regression models (singly robust estimator with the gfoRmula package), 9 (2) TMLE using parametric generalized linear regression models (doubly robust estimator with the lmtp package), 10 and (3) TMLE using SuperLearner with six candidate algorithms with default hyperparameters (doubly robust estimator with the lmtp package). 11 We utilized both the g-computation and the TMLE because g-computation was previously utilized in evaluating health benefits of PM 2.5 intervention and can serve as a comparison, 7 , 12 while TMLE is the more appropriate estimator to incorporate ML algorithms. 8 Instead of reporting the concentration-response function (e.g., hazard ratio) estimated with traditional methods like Cox proportional hazards model, both marginal estimators calculated the 10-year cumulative mortality rate standardized to the distribution of confounders and exposure in the study population under no intervention and a hypothetical intervention. We then reported the difference in the 10-year cumulative mortality rate across interventions (i.e., benefit of the intervention), a directly interpretable and policy-relevant effect measure that is easy to compare across analytical methods. These marginal estimators also incorporated the counterfactual framework implicitly. The algorithms (parametric generalized linear regression models and SuperLearner) were used to estimate nuisance functions in the estimators, including outcome and time-varying covariate models in g-computation, as well as exposure and outcome mechanisms in TMLE. The first two methods did not utilize ML algorithms while the third one did. Although the nuisance functions estimated are not directly reported, they are essential in calculating the 10-year cumulative mortality rate and more flexibility in their estimation algorithms corresponds to fewer restrictions in the relationships among exposure, confounders, and outcome. In the hypothetical intervention, we reduced the participant’s annual average PM 2.5 exposure in the previous 3 years to 8.8 μg/m 3 (i.e., 2020 Canadian Air Ambient Quality Standards for annual PM 2.5 ) if it was higher. This hypothetical intervention is dynamic in the sense that each individual year would experience an intervention that depends on their previous year’s exposure and confounder values. We then calculated the difference in 10-year cumulative mortality rates under this intervention and no intervention as the final effect measure for each analytical method because additive-scale estimates are more relevant to public health policies. Estimates at a multiplicative scale can also be calculated. To evaluate the influence of flexible algorithms, we used Wald tests to evaluate whether the effect estimates across the analytical methods were statistically different. 13 To evaluate the bias-variance tradeoff in these estimates, we calculated the mean squared error as the sum of squared bias plus variance of the estimates. The bias is equal to the difference between a range of hypothetical true values and the effect estimates (i.e., difference in 10-year cumulative mortality rates under intervention and no intervention). In the first two analytical methods using parametric models (i.e., g-computation and TMLE), we only employed the main terms of confounders in the models and considered historical measurements of time-varying covariates (confounder and exposure) from the previous year. Besides, both analytical methods allowed the relationship between PM 2.5 and probability of death to change across years, by including interactions between year and PM 2.5 in the outcome model for g-computation and by estimating the outcome mechanisms at each time point separately for TMLE. In the third analytical method, we included six candidate algorithms for SuperLearner in TMLE to allow more flexibility in the relationship among exposure, confounders, and outcome, which is consistent with previous studies using the lmpt package: 14 , 15 simple mean, generalized linear regression with main terms, extreme gradient boosting, 16 random forest, 17 multivariate adaptive regression splines, 18 and elastic net regularized regression. 19 Including these algorithms in SuperLearner also accounts for complex interactions among covariates. We used the ensemble process (default nonnegative least squares meta-learner) of SuperLearner to combine the predictions from each candidate algorithm with the weights for each based on cross-validated performance of those algorithms. 20 Since previous studies using CCHS data found a supra-linear PM 2.5 –mortality association, 21 , 22 we transformed the exposure with natural logarithm. We utilized bootstrapping to estimate confidence intervals for g-computation. For the two TMLE-based methods, we obtained valid asymptotic standard errors and Wald-type confidence intervals by solving the efficient influence function in TMLE. 9 , 14 We conducted two sensitivity analyses. First, as hyperparameter specifications can affect the results of ML algorithms dramatically, we ran a sensitivity analysis using 28 different specifications of hyperparameters in the six candidate algorithms (12 for extreme gradient boosting, four for random forest, nine for elastic net regularized regression, and one for the other three). Second, we included PM 2.5 on the original scale in the most flexible combination of estimator and algorithm, TMLE and SuperLearner (i.e., assuming a log-linear PM 2.5 –mortality association). More details of the model specifications for main analyses and sensitivity analyses are in eAppendix 2; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 . All analyses were done in R version 4.0.5 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). 23 Results and discussion Among 62,365 participants, the 3-year average PM 2.5 concentrations at baseline were 6.4 μg/m 3 (16% exceeding the 8.8 μg/m 3 standard) and we observed 5770 (9.3%) nonaccidental deaths till the end of follow-up (eTable 1; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 ). Participants with baseline PM 2.5 above and below or equal to 8.8 μg/m 3 demonstrated similar characteristics, except for community-level characteristics like community size, urban form, and airshed, as urban and highly populated areas tend to have higher levels of PM 2.5 (eTable 1; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 ). We removed 5.21% of participant-years with missing data in covariates. The 10-year cumulative mortality rate decreased under the intervention of setting exposure above 8.8 μg/m 3 to 8.8 μg/m 3 in all analyses, with rate differences of −0.23 (95% confidence interval [CI]: −0.46, 0.00), −0.83 (95% CI: −1.24, −0.43), and −0.67 (95% CI: −1.27, −0.06) per 1000 participants from the no intervention for g-computation, TMLE with parametric models, and TMLE with SuperLearner, respectively (Table 1 ). We did not identify any meaningful difference among estimates of TMLE methods, but the estimate of parametric g-computation was heterogeneous from estimates of TMLE with a parametric model ( P value &lt;0.01) and TMLE with SuperLearner ( P value = 0.09). As the estimators utilized in this study were rarely employed in studies of long-term PM 2.5 and mortality, we cannot compare our results to previous studies directly but the direction of health benefits is consistent with the literature. 4 Table 1. The analytical method with the lowest mean squared error (i.e., best performance regarding bias-variance tradeoff) varies by the true value: parametric g-computation performed best if the true value is above −0.55, TMLE with parametric model performed best if the true value is below −0.58, and TMLE with SuperLearner performed best only when the true value falls between −0.55 and −0.58 (Figure 1 ). In other words, employing ML algorithms with a doubly robust estimator did not meaningfully change the effect estimate when compared with estimates using a less flexible algorithm in our example. Employing ML algorithms also leads to so large a loss in precision that its performance only exceeds the other simpler methods when the true value falls into a small range. Figure 1. Mean squared errors (MSEs) for each analytical method across a range of hypothetical true values for the estimate regarding benefits of reducing PM 2.5 levels (10-year cumulative mortality rate in the intervention minus no intervention per 1000 participants). Dotted gray lines marked the boundaries for the ranges of true values where each analytical method performed best (with the lowest MSE). Our sensitivity analysis using TMLE with SuperLearner and 28 hyperparameter specification and algorithm combinations demonstrated similar results with wider confidence intervals (−0.59 [95% CI: −1.21, 0.32]) than the TMLE-SuperLearner method using the default hyperparameters (eTable 2; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 ). Candidate algorithms assigned the highest weights by SuperLearner in estimating the outcome mechanism are similar in main and sensitivity analysis with and without intervention, including multivariate adaptive regression splines, extreme gradient boosting, and random forest, with slightly higher weights in some hyperparameter specifications other than default hyperparameters in the sensitivity analysis (eFigure 1; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 ). Candidate algorithms assigned the highest weights in estimating the density ratio or exposure mechanism are similar for main and sensitivity analysis but different with and without intervention (eFigure 1; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 ). Results with original PM 2.5 as input also showed no difference from the main result with natural log-transformed PM 2.5 (eTable 2; http://links.lww.com/EE/A333 ). In conclusion, all three methods demonstrated tangible public health benefits from reducing long-term exposure to PM 2.5 in Canada where PM 2.5 levels are among the lowest worldwide. However, we observed that the advantage of employing ML algorithms with a doubly robust estimator to estimate the health benefits of PM 2.5 intervention in this low-PM 2.5 environment was relatively small. As the composition of PM 2.5 and population susceptibility changes across space and time, the true relationship among PM 2.5 concentration, confounders, and mortality risk might vary across populations. The necessity of employing ML algorithms in evaluating the health benefits of PM 2.5 intervention with a doubly robust estimator needs to be explored separately in other geographical contexts, as well as under other hypothetical intervention scenarios (e.g., more stringent interventions).
