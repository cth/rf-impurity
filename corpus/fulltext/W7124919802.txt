Abstract Introduction Blood donors may face deferral due to low hemoglobin (Hb) levels, posing a key challenge for blood management. Most prediction studies are based on European data, and their accuracy can be improved, while research on Chinese donors remains limited. Objective establish and evaluate the applicability of existing low Hb delay prediction methods to Chinese data and introduce new machine learning models and hyperparameter adjustment methods to optimize prediction schemes. Methods This study used 26,796 whole blood donation records from Hangzhou (Jan 2023–Oct 2025) to build a machine learning classification model. Seven algorithms, including Logistic Regression and LightGBM, were evaluated using SMOTE for imbalance correction and Hyperband for tuning. Logistic regression and SHAP analysis were then applied to identify key donor characteristics influencing prediction performance. Results Under the premise of 90% specificity, the LightGBM model had the highest prediction accuracy for blood donation delay due to low Hb. At the same time, the historical average Hb test value of blood donors plays the most important role in the prediction performance of the model, followed by gender, occupation, and type of blood donors (whether to donate blood in groups). In addition, ethnic group also had a significant impact on the prediction of delayed blood donation in Hangzhou area of China. Conclusions Using historical pre-donation test data and donor personal information enables reliable prediction of low-Hb deferral. The feature importance results based on Hangzhou data were consistent with previous studies, suggesting shared mechanisms and cross-regional model transferability. Moreover, hyperparameter optimization further enhances model performance. Blood management agencies use this indicator to evaluate the blood’s oxygen-carrying capacity and potential anemia risks, ensuring donors do not experience severe anemia after donation. If Hb levels or other indicators do not meet health standards, donors will be temporarily deferred and must wait until the indicators are within the acceptable range to participate again. This delay may affect willingness to donate blood and reduce the efficiency of blood management. Therefore, it is of great practical significance to study how to develop a prediction model for low-Hb level. This issue has been studied for a long time in Europe. Based on the data of blood donors in the Netherlands, Baart et al. [ 1 ] screen the predictive variables through backward selection, and use the Logistic model to evaluate the impact of the predictive variables (such as the Hb level of the last visit of the blood donor, gender, time interval, total number of blood donations, etc.) on the delayed blood donation due to low Hb, and constructed a prediction model. Early studies achieve an area under ROC curve (AUROC) of 0.7–0.9, but this is not an ideal result for a classification task with a severe imbalance in sample quantity between categories [ 1 , 2 ]. Subsequent studies have attempted to introduce the previous status of individual donors or interindividual differences by using transfer models and linear mixed-effects models. This attempt improved the prediction performance of the model to a limited extent, but it was still unsatisfactory [ 3 , 4 ]. In 2022 and beyond, scholars have introduced machine learning methods to the study of this problem. In the Hb prediction problem of Finnish blood donors, Toivonen et al. [ 5 ] applied decision tree and random forest models, and hyperparameter optimization of the model was performed under resampling with four-fold cross-validation. The results show that the random forest model has achieved great improvement in prediction accuracy compared with linear and nonlinear statistical models, but the AUPR is lower than 0.5 and the F1-score is lower than 0.4, and the comprehensive prediction accuracy is still not ideal. In the study of Meulenbeld et al. [ 6 ] support vector machine (SVM) and random forest were introduced, but they still could not achieve higher accuracy, and it was often necessary to take the non-delayed class as the positive class to obtain better precision and recall results [ 7 , 8 ]. In addition to machine learning models, studies have focused on identifying key factors influencing low hemoglobin (Hb) deferral. Mast identifies predictors such as gender, age, donation frequency, smoking habits, and laboratory test results (e.g., ferritin and hepcidin). Frequent donors and premenopausal women are especially at risk due to iron deficiency caused by menstruation, pregnancy, and insufficient recovery intervals [ 9 ]. Urbina et al. [ 10 ] found that baseline Hb concentration, Hb changes, type of donation, and donation interval are key predictors in repeat female donors, and increasing the minimum acceptance criterion for females by 0.5 g/dL could reduce the risk by 50%. Kim et al. [ 11 ] showed that individualized donation intervals based on post-donation Hb test results significantly reduced the delay rate, especially among women. This method takes into account the recovery of Hb and donor characteristics (such as age, blood type, and ethnicity). Browne et al. [ 12 ] confirmed that factors such as female sex, ethnicity, age, weight, seasonality, donation intensity, and historical Hb levels influence deferral rates, with women being significantly more likely to be deferred regardless of Hb threshold. In terms of cross-country comparison of low Hb delay prediction, Baart et al. [ 13 ] extended the research scope to Ireland, and recently expanded the scope of this problem to a cross-country comparison involving Australia, Belgium, the Netherlands, Finland, and South Africa. This series of comparative studies found that when the same model was applied to the blood donation data of different countries, the model’s performance varies, but the important variables affecting prediction were similar [ 7 , 6 ]. Although studies on this issue have been ongoing for decades, it is still of great significance to conduct relevant migration research in Hangzhou, China. This is mainly due to the following three reasons: there are significant differences between China and Europe in the Hb threshold requirements for blood donation. For example, China stipulates that men must have an Hb value of ≥120 g/L and women ≥115 g/L. In contrast, the Dutch standards are higher, with men needing ≥135 g/L and women ≥125 g/L. These threshold differences can result in variations in the feature learning of the prediction model for the target information. Second, the regulations regarding the interval between whole blood donations differ, with China enforcing a 180-day interval and the Netherlands allowing a 56-day interval. This systemic difference may affect the incidence of deferral due to low Hb levels and the characteristics of low-Hb deferral, thereby causing an imbalance issue in the blood donation data sample categories from the perspective of the binary classification (delayed and not delayed) prediction problem. For example, in the blood donation record dataset from Hangzhou (2019 to 2024) used in this paper, the deferral rate due to low Hb levels is 0.2% for men and 3.5% for women. In contrast, a study of blood donors in the Netherlands between 2006 and 2013 showed that the deferral rate for whole blood donors increased by 1.22 times in 2013 compared to 2006, with a significant gender difference – female donors had a higher deferral rate (OR 1.76, 95% CI: 1.75–1.78) [ 14 ]. More recently, from 2019 to 2023, the deferral rate in the Netherlands was 2.9% for men and 5.4% for women [ 7 ]. Compared to the Netherlands, the Chinese data encounters a more severe category imbalance issue: most samples fall under “not deferred due to low Hb,” and the minority samples of “deferred due to low Hb” are relatively scarce, increasing the challenge of category prediction tasks. Third, previous research has been relatively simple in the use and adjustment of prediction models, and there has been little involvement in the details of model hyperparameter adjustment. With the development of machine learning technology, it is necessary to introduce new machine learning models and hyperparameter adjustment methods to improve the prediction scheme for deferral due to low Hb levels. Based on the basic information of blood donors and pre-donation test data from Zhejiang Blood Center, this paper reformulated the research problem as a machine learning binary problem of whether blood donors will delay blood donation because of low Hb and then evaluated the performance of seven statistical and machine learning methods in predicting blood donation delay due to low Hb. The Logistic regression model and Shapley Value Analysis were used to analyze the importance of blood donor characteristics on the prediction performance of the model. Through this study, this paper realizes the transfer study of the prediction method of delayed blood donation with low Hb in Hangzhou area of China, which is helpful promote the relevant research results in Europe to China from the perspective of regional research, and has certain reference value. Materials and Methods Data Overview Raw Data Overview The original data in this paper were based on the blood donation records and Hb test data of whole blood donation from January 1, 2019, to October 11, 2025, provided by Zhejiang Blood Center. The original data contained 703,605 whole blood donation records, including 14 variables: donor ID, gender, ethnic, occupation, date of blood donation, age, weight, ABO blood group, RhD blood group, pre-collection Hb value, delay due to Hb substandard defer blood donation, volume of blood donation, team donation, or not. Among them, donor ID was used to identify the blood donor anonymously, delayed blood donation due to Hb substandard was used as the predictive target variable, and other variables are used directly as predictor variables or indirectly through the calculation of derived variables. The Health examination Requirements for Blood Donors, promulgated by the National Health Commission of China and implemented officially on July 1, 2012, clearly stipulates that Hb should meet the standard of ≥120 g/L for men and ≥115 g/L for women to meet the standard of blood donation, and the interval between two whole blood donations should not be less than 180 days. Therefore, Zhejiang Blood Center will qualitatively determine whether the Hb level of blood donors meets the blood donation requirements by the copper sulfate specific gravity method before 2023. After 2023, the method of Hb quantitative examination before blood collection will be implemented, and the Hb value of blood donors will be recorded. At the same time, there are a lot of missing variables, such as body weight, pre-collection Hb value and blood donation volume in the original data from 2019 to 2023. Considering the data integrity and information completion of the sample set, this paper uses the blood donation records from 2023 to 2025 for the training and prediction of the prediction model. The blood donation records of blood donors from 2019 to 2023 were used for the completion of the historical characteristic’s variables of blood donors. Description of the Sample Set Construction Process First, this paper referred to the study of Baart et al. [ 2 ], and screened blood donors according to the following conditions: gender information was not missing, there was no delay in blood donation for other reasons, and at least 2 blood donations were required after 2023, and Hb values were recorded. Second, this study aimed to predict whether a blood donor would be deferred from donation at a specific future time point (T) due to low Hb levels, using the donor’s historical testing data. However, the Hb and weight measurements at time T could not be directly used as predictor variables. Therefore, following the approach of Baart et al. [ 13 ], we used the raw dataset collected since January 1, 2019. After manually handling outliers in the weight variable, we calculated historical features for each eligible donor using the 2 years preceding the record date as the historical period. These features included the number of donations (Hist_donate_n), average donation interval (Hist_donate_gap), mean donation volume (Hist_donate_vol_mean), total donation volume (Hist_donate_vol_total), mean Hb value (Hist_hb_mean), and mean weight (Hist_weight_mean). In addition, the interval in days between two donations was computed to replace the original donation date variable. All predictor variables were derived from past data and excluded current-period information to avoid data leakage that could affect model training and prediction. Third, in order to apply the study data to the prediction model, we applied integer floating-point and character variable factorization to all the variables, respectively. The factor variables were encoded by category cardinality: dummy variables were encoded when category cardinality was 2; Effect coding was used when the category cardinality was 3 or more. Fourth, in order to ensure that this study has enough characteristic variable information for prediction, this paper first deleted donors with missing historical average Hb value and historical average blood donation volume in blood donation records and second, taking 2023 to October 2025 as the observation window, screened out blood donation records with at least two times (including delayed cases) and the blood donors with complete information on all variable characteristics. Finally, the dataset used for this study included 26,796 donation records from 11,952 donors. The training set included the historical blood donation records of each blood donor, with a total of 14,844 samples. The prediction set included the last donation record, with a total of 11,952 samples. Descriptive Statistical Analysis of Variables A total of 26,796 blood donation records were included in this study and grouped according to whether the donors were deferred due to low Hb levels (defer). As shown in Table 1 , there were 405 cases of Hb-deferral (defer = Yes, 1.5%) and 26,391 cases without deferral (defer = No, 98.5%). The distribution of donors by low-Hb deferral status exhibited a clear imbalance between the two groups. The Hb level was the most prominent difference between the two groups ( p &lt; 0.001). The average-Hb level of the delayed group was only 108.0 ± 6.3 g/L (median 110.0 g/L), which was significantly lower than that of the non-delayed group 149.0 ± 14.6 g/L (median 149.0 g/L). This indicates that low-Hb level may be the core related factor of blood donation delay. Age, gender, and team also had a significant impact on blood donation delay ( p &lt; 0.001). The proportion of women in the delayed group was as high as 94.0% (only 39.0% in the non-delayed group). The delay rate of non-team donors (64.0%) was significantly higher than that of team donors (36.0%). In addition, in the delayed group, there were significant differences in weight (mean 59.2 vs. 67.3 kg), total blood donation volume in the past 2 years (mean 456 vs. 601 mL), and the interval between the last blood donation (Hist_donate_gap). The above key features provide a key basis for feature screening of the subsequent blood donation delay prediction model and help prioritize the high correlation variables to improve the prediction performance of the model. Due to the large number of occupation and ethnicity categories among blood donors in the sample, the present study simplified these variables to facilitate model grouping. Occupation and ethnicity were each divided into two categories: (1) Han and other ethnic groups, and (2) reported and unreported occupations. The results showed that there were no significant differences in the distribution of ABO blood type (ABO), RhD blood type (RhD), ethnic and occupation between the two groups (all p &gt; 0.05). Although the statistical test did not reflect significant associations between ABO blood group, RhD blood group, ethnic group, and occupation and delayed blood donation, it may be related to the distribution characteristics of the samples, so we kept these characteristics as variables in the subsequent study. Study Design In the field of machine learning, this problem can be interpreted as predicting the risk probability of blood donation delay due to low Hb according to the historical information characteristics of blood donors, and then we can transform this problem into a binary prediction problem in the field of machine learning. This problem includes prediction target setting, prediction model selection, model performance comparison, and feature importance analysis. The framework of the methodology for delayed blood donation in this paper is presented in Figure 1 . Fig. 1. Framework of methodology for delayed blood donation. This figure presents the whole framework of methodology for delayed blood donation in this paper. Firstly, the framework introduces different forecasting models such as linear model, kernel model, and tree-based models. Secondly, the important two parts as data extraction and analysis as well as modeling and optimization are presented. Finally, the parameters setting and model evaluation are organized to obtain the forecast results. Forecast Target Setting First, we included the event of whether a donor delayed donating blood due to an Hb disqualification as a predictive target (denoted as defer). Among them, donors who delayed donating blood due to Hb disqualification were positive samples (Defer = Yes) and vice versa were negative samples (Defer = No). Therefore, for each sample of blood donors, we will use their historical blood donation data to predict the value of the defer variable recorded last time. Prediction Model Selection Second, in order to systematically examine the linear and nonlinear relationship between blood donor characteristics variables and delayed donation with low Hb, seven representative prediction models were used in this study: logistic regression, decision tree [ 15 ], random forest [ 16 ], SVM [ 17 ], XGBoost [ 18 ], LightGBM [ 19 ], and CatBoost [ 20 ]. From the perspective of modeling ideas, these models cover two main types of relationship patterns: (1) the first type, linear models. Logistic regression is a traditional statistical method, which assumes that there is a linear relationship between the risk of Hb delay and various predictor variables (such as age, weight, and previous Hb level). The model has good interpretability and can be used as a baseline model to compare the performance of different methods. (2) The second type, nonlinear model. The physiological state and Hb recovery process of blood donors are often affected by the interaction of many factors, such as the difference in iron metabolism caused by gender differences [ 10 ], the decline in iron reserve caused by repeated blood donation [ 12 ], and the interaction between body weight and blood donation volume [ 1 ]. This kind of complex nonlinear relationship is difficult to be captured by linear models. Therefore, six machine learning models were further introduced in this study. Among them, the Decision Tree model forms nonlinear boundaries between variables through a series of rules, which can be used to identify whether donors will delay blood donation due to low Hb. By integrating the results of multiple decision trees and using the voting method to make decisions, Random Forest improves the stability and generalization ability of the model. SVM constructs the optimal classification hyperplane and uses the kernel function to describe the complex nonlinear boundary. XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost belong to the gradient Boosting tree series models, which improve the prediction accuracy through continuous iteration and error correction, and are especially suitable for handling high-dimensional, unbalanced, and missing medical data. Among them, LightGBM and CatBoost have higher efficiency and robustness in processing categorical variables and missing values, which are suitable for the real blood donation data scenario of this study. Training, Optimization and Evaluation of the Model To ensure the stability and generalization ability of the model, in this study, comprehensive strategies such as five-fold cross-validation, SMOTE oversampling [ 21 ], threshold optimization and Hyperband hyperparameter optimization [ 22 , 23 ] were adopted in model training and optimization. The specific reasons include four points. 1. Five-fold cross-validation can maximize the utilization efficiency and stability of the model under limited samples. The dataset is randomly divided into five subsets, of which four subsets are used for training and one subset is used for verification in each training, which can effectively reduce the accidental bias caused by the difference in sample division [ 24 ]. 2. Considering that there are very few low-Hb delay samples in this study, and the proportion of categories is highly unbalanced (1.51%), direct training will lead to a bias of the model to the majority of classes (no delay). Therefore, we adopted Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) to improve the class balance by generating synthetic samples of minority classes in the feature space, so as to enhance the ability of the model to identify individuals with low Hb delay. 3. In the model output stage, the prediction result is usually the probability value. In order to better balance “Sensitivity” and “Precision,” this study further adjusted the classification boundary by threshold optimization method to improve the recognition effect of minority groups (delayed blood donors). Compared with a fixed threshold (such as 0.5), this method can better reflect the clinical practicability of the model in actual decision-making scenarios. 4. For gradient boosting algorithms with many model parameters and complex interactions (such as LightGBM and CatBoost), this study uses the Hyperband algorithm for hyperparameter tuning, and some models refer to the parameter optimization search space of previous studies [ 24 , 25 ]. Based on the principle of multi-arm gambling machine, Hyperband can quickly identify the parameter combinations with excellent performance under the limitation of computing resources, and improve the search efficiency through the early stopping mechanism, so as to achieve the balance between performance and computing cost [ 26 ]. In terms of model evaluation, this study comprehensively uses area under the ROC curve (AUC), sensitivity, specificity, and F-beta index (beta = 5) to measure performance. Among them, AUC reflects the overall discriminant ability of the model. Sensitivity was used to measure the ability of the model to identify individuals with low Hb delay. Specificity measures its ability to distinguish between normal blood donors; the F-beta index strengthens the attention to recall rate by weighted method (when beta = 5, the recall weight is greater), which is more in line with the defensive principle of “prefer more identification and less missed identification” in blood donation safety management. Feature Importance Analysis According to the benchmark comparison of predictive performance between models, the model with the best performance was selected to analyze the importance of blood donor characteristics on predictive performance. In conclusion, the five-fold training, SMOTE balance, threshold optimization, and hyperband tuning constitute the complete modeling system of this study, which not only ensures the robustness and interpretability of the model, but also takes into account the sensitive detection ability of minority samples, providing a prediction tool of practical significance for blood centers. In addition, three R toolkits of tidyverse [ 27 ], mlr3verse [ 28 ], and mlr3hyperband [ 29 ] were used to complete all the data processing and analysis work. Results Model Benchmark Test In this part, we trained seven prediction models using the historical data of blood donors and extrapolated the prediction on the last blood donation record of the blood donor to verify the discriminant ability of the models in the real scenario. In the process of model training, data via SMOTE sampling to balance type distribution, and fifty percent using cross-validation to ensure the performance of model robustness. Integrated model assessment using AUC, sensitivity, specificity, and F-beta (beta = 5) indicators, to measure model of comprehensive discriminant ability, overall recognition effect of low Hb delay samples and its balance between the recall rate and precision rate. The results are shown in Table 2 and Figure 2 . Overall, the AUC of all models except the decision tree model was above 0.91, indicating that the discrimination ability of different algorithms for low Hb delay was strong as a whole. Among them, Logistic regression in the linear model showed the most stable performance in AUC (0.931) and specificity (0.918). With about 90% accuracy of negative samples, the Logistic model correctly identified 10,847 non-delayed donors (TN) and accurately identified 106 true individuals with low Hb delayed samples (TP, sensitivity = 0.779). This stability in overall understanding and the results show that Logistic model has a good balance, but slightly lower than on identify individual number of delayed sample performance of the optimal model of the tree. The SVM with the overall performance of the CatBoost center, although AUC were higher than 90%, but after the threshold optimization recall rate is below 80%, weaker than XGBoost and LightGBM model. From the ROC curve ( Fig. 2 ), it can be seen that the curves of XGBoost and LightGBM are closer to the upper left corner of the coordinate in the area of low false-positive rate (1 – specificity &lt;0.1), indicating that the two models can still maintain high sensitivity under the condition of controlling the false-positive rate of about 10%, which is consistent with the results in Table 2 . In summary, under the constraint of “ensuring the accuracy of about 90% negative samples,” the Logistic model performed best among the linear methods, with stable results and strong explanatory power. XGBoost and LightGBM have the best performance in the tree model and have obvious advantages in identifying more true delay individuals. Therefore, the subsequent feature importance analysis will be mainly carried out based on these two types of representative models: Logistic regression is used to reveal the direction and strength of linear influence of each variable, and LightGBM is used to identify the nonlinear feature contribution and variable interaction mechanism. Feature Importance Analysis Feature Importance Analysis Based on Logistic Regression In the step of feature importance analysis, a Logistic regression model was constructed based on multiple blood donation records with “whether blood donation was delayed due to low hemoglobin” as the dependent variable. The sample was limited to individuals who had donated blood at least twice, and the model used their historical records (excluding the latest one) to extract features to ensure the consistency of prediction direction. Categorical variables including gender, type of blood donor, RhD blood type, and whether the last blood donation was postponed were treated as binary factors. Age was divided into three groups according to the distribution characteristics: “≤30 years old,” “31–50 years old,” and “≥51 years old” to reflect the differences in risk among different age groups. Impact coding was used for variables with many category levels and uneven sample distribution – occupation, ethnic group, and ABO blood group. This approach converts each level into the difference between the conditional log-likelihood and the population log-likelihood of its target value (risk of delay) and is used to express the overall effect of category on the dependent variable. This phenomenon may reflect better recovery or improved health management (e.g., iron supplementation, longer intervals) before subsequent donation, thus reducing the probability of further postponement. Some variables did not achieve significance level (5% significance level) in this model, including ABO blood group (OR = 3.08, p = 0.063), RhD (OR = 0.87, p = 0.847), historical mean body weight (OR = 0.98, p = 0.859), and historical number of blood donation (OR = 1.08, p = 0.117). These variables did not show stable main effects under the linear assumption of the Logistic model, but their potential effects in nonlinear or interaction scenarios could not be excluded. LightGBM-Based Feature Importance Analysis In order to further verify the robustness of feature importance and explore the possible nonlinearity and interaction effects, we established LightGBM model based on the same training set and used Shapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) for explanatory analysis. SHAP importance reflects the global average effect of donor characteristics on whether blood donation is delayed due to low Hb and does not rely on the linear assumption. The results of the analysis (see Table 4 ; Fig. 3 ) showed that there was a clear stratification in the contribution of individual characteristics to the prediction of donation delay due to low Hb. To facilitate the alignment with the Logistic regression results, we adopted the “group importance” dimension: coding columns under the same concept were combined (such as ABO, ethnic, and job), and the SHAP of each group was averaged (Mean_abs_shap) as the global importance measure. Higher values in Table 4 indicate a stronger average influence of the feature on the model output. The results of group importance showed that the historical Hb mean was the dominant characteristic (Mean_abs_shap = 1.493), and its contribution was more than 2 times that of the second ABO blood group, highlighting its core explanatory power for low Hb delay risk. The individual attribute variables gender (0.464), occupation (0.384), and type of blood donor (0.384) were in a medium contribution gradient, and their average SHAP values were all higher than 0.37. The individual historical characteristic variables were in the third gradient, including the mean historical weight (0.147), the mean historical total blood donation (0.143), and the mean historical blood donation (0.135). In addition, the importance of history of blood donation, blood donation interval, and ethnic group were close to 0, indicating that these variables were not effectively used in the tree model. Summary of Feature Importance Results Based on the results of Logistic regression and LightGBM-SHAP analysis, the two methods showed high consistency in feature ranking and directionality. The historical Hb mean value was always the most explanatory feature, highlighting its central role in predicting the risk of delayed blood donation with low Hb levels. The three variables of gender, occupation, and type of blood donor showed significant effects in the two models, indicating the stable contribution of physiological characteristics, job attributes, and blood donation organization form to the risk distribution. The mean value of historical blood donation still had moderate importance in both models, supporting its physiological significance in relation to the recovery of iron stores. Unlike Baart et al. [ 2 ], variables such as body weight and number of blood donations showed low or no contribution to the overall risk prediction, indicating that their role in the overall risk prediction was limited. Ethnicity and history of blood donation delay showed overall significant effects in the Logistic model but had less significance in LightGBM, suggesting that although these two variables had direct linear effects on blood donation delay, their independent predictive value was overlaid by the nonlinear interactions of other characteristics. ABO showed a high importance in LightGBM but was not significant in the Logistic model, indicating that the effect of ABO blood group on blood donation delay was nonlinear and depended on the interaction with other characteristics. In general, the Logistic model revealed the directionality and statistical significance of the main characteristics, while LightGBM-SHAP further confirmed the stability and global contribution of these characteristics in the nonlinear framework, providing complementary evidence for the subsequent model interpretation and practical application. In general, historical average Hb levels, gender, occupation, and type of blood donors (group donation or not) were the most important characteristic variables for predicting low Hb levels, followed by blood donation interval, whether the last time to donate blood was delayed due to low Hb level, and historical average blood donation volume. These variables showed significant predictive effects in the linear model. This is consistent with the results of existing studies [ 1 – 3 , 8 ]. The commonality of feature importance indicates that the key features predicting low-Hb levels are applicable to blood donors in Hangzhou, China, further validating the applicability of the prediction model in different regions and contexts. In addition, ethnic group had a significant effect on the Hangzhou sample, suggesting that although it was not a major factor in the European study, it may reflect differences at the level of social or health habits in the context of a specific Chinese population. Discussion In this study, using the data of blood donors and whole blood donation records in 2023–2025 in Hangzhou, China, a prediction scheme for delayed blood donation due to low Hb was proposed. The concise conclusion content is described in Figure 4 . Through benchmarking multiple statistical and machine learning models and hyperparameter optimization by Hyperband method, it was found that LightGBM and XGBoost model had the best performance. LightGBM achieved an AUC of 0.928 and an F-beta of 0.614. The XGBoost model achieved an AUC of 0.924 and an F-beta of 0.604, which was significantly better than the performance of similar studies. In addition, the feature importance analysis showed that the historical average Hb test value, gender, occupation, and type of blood donors (group donation or not) had important effects on the performance of the prediction model, which was consistent with the results of previous studies. Fig. 4. Discussion, conclusion, and prospect of delayed blood donation. The Figure 4 describes the final discussion, conclusion and prospect of delayed blood donation. The Figure 4 presents the core of research through three parts, including Data and Methodology, Key findings, Innovation and Prospect. The innovation of this study is reflected in three aspects. First, this study is the first to carry out a migration study on the prediction of delayed blood donation with low-Hb level in the context of China, which is of great value considering the differences in blood donation standards and population characteristics between China and Europe. Second, compared with previous studies, this study introduced an ensemble learning model and used hyperparameter optimization methods such as Hyperband to significantly improve the prediction accuracy. Third, regarding the importance of predictive features, this study not only verified the findings of previous studies (such as historical Hb value, gender, etc.), but also found that the type of blood donor (whether it was a group donation) and ethnicity had less attention but had important predictive functions, which provided new reference for future research. However, this study has some limitations. Due to the Zhejiang Blood Center only starting systematic collection of Hb test data in recent years, the number of predictive variables that can be studied is relatively limited. Simultaneously, future research could consider collecting more data from various regions in China and abroad to further investigate the generalizability of prediction methods.
