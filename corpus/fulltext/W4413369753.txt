Abstract Introduction Type 2 diabetes (T2D) significantly increases dementia risk, yet the molecular mechanisms underlying this association remain unclear. Objectives This study aimed to identify protein signatures that distinguish dementia risk in T2D patients, develop a proteomic prediction model, and elucidate biological pathways connecting T2D and dementia. Methods We analyzed 2,920 plasma proteins from 52,958 participants (including 3,292 with T2D) in the UK Biobank Pharma Proteomics Project with a median follow-up of 14.6 years. Cox regression models with interaction terms identified T2D-specific protein associations with dementia risk. Machine learning models were developed to predict dementia in T2D patients. Pathway analysis and weighted gene co-expression network analysis identified biological mechanisms linking T2D and dementia. Results We identified 471 proteins with significant interaction effects between T2D and dementia risk. In non-T2D individuals, elevated levels of neuronal pentraxin receptor (NPTXR, HR = 0.74, 95 %CI:0.66–0.83) and carbonic anhydrase 14 (CA14, HR = 0.67, 95 %CI:0.60–0.75) were exclusively associated with decreased dementia risk. Conversely, in T2D patients, elevated rho guanine nucleotide exchange factor 12 (ARHGEF12, HR = 1.45, 95 %CI:1.10–1.91) was specifically associated with increased dementia risk. A 51-protein model accurately predicted 15-year dementia risk in T2D patients (AUC = 0.835, C-index = 0.829), outperforming conventional clinical risk scores and maintaining high accuracy for Alzheimer’s disease and vascular dementia. Pathway analysis revealed enrichment of IL6-JAK-STAT3 signaling in T2D-related dementia, while dysregulation of fatty acid metabolism was specific to T2D-associated Alzheimer’s disease. Conclusions This large-scale proteomic analysis identifies specific molecular signatures that differentiate dementia risk in diabetic and non-diabetic populations, with potential applications for early risk stratification and targeted interventions. Introduction Diabetes poses a significant global health challenge, with an estimated prevalence of 6.1 % worldwide in 2021, affecting 529 million people [ 1 ], of whom 96 % have type 2 diabetes (T2D). The demographic shift toward an aging population has resulted in a growing proportion of older adults (≥65 years) with diabetes, placing this population at substantially elevated risk for adverse health outcomes associated with both metabolic dysregulation and age-related pathophysiological processes [ 2 ]. Among these complications, dementia occurs with nearly twice the prevalence in individuals with T2D compared to normoglycemic counterparts [ 3 , 4 ]. Despite this pronounced epidemiological association, early detection of cognitive decline in diabetic populations remains a significant clinical challenge. Current diagnostic paradigms, including standard neuropsychological assessments, exhibit limited utility as they predominantly identify neurocognitive symptoms only after significant pathological progression has occurred [ 5 , 6 ], thereby failing to identify at-risk individuals during the crucial preclinical phase when intervention might be most effective. The pathophysiological processes between T2D and dementia likely involves multiple converging mechanisms extending beyond classical amyloid and tau pathology [ 7 , 8 ]. These include cerebrovascular dysfunction, accelerated neurodegeneration, chronic neuroinflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and aberrant central insulin signaling [ 9 , 10 ]. Consequently, established biomarkers for Alzheimer's disease, such as amyloid-β and tau markers, may have limited applicability or different interpretative significance in T2D populations [ 11 ]. Several clinical predictive algorithms have been developed, including two diabetes-specific dementia risk models incorporating metabolic parameters, diabetes-related complications, comorbidities, glucose-lowering pharmacotherapy, and glycemic control metrics [ 12 , 13 ]. While these models demonstrate moderate predictive capacity for 10-year dementia risk in T2D patients, they inadequately capture the dynamic molecular processes underlying diabetes-associated cognitive deterioration. Proteomics offers a powerful systems biology approach to address the challenge of predicting dementia risk in T2D patients. Blood proteomic signatures integrate multiple physiological dimensions, including health behaviors, genetic predisposition, environmental exposures, comorbid conditions, and pharmacological interventions, all of which modulate disease susceptibility [ [14] , [15] , [16] ]. Previous proteomic study have yielded translatable, cost-effective risk stratification models for T2D patients [ [17] , [18] , [19] ], significantly enhancing the identification of individuals at elevated risk for cardiovascular and renal complications who might otherwise escape detection through conventional screening strategies [ [20] , [21] , [22] ]. Similarly, proteomic-based predictive models for dementia have demonstrated excellent prognostic performance over extended temporal horizons [ 23 , 24 ]. However, there exists a critical gap in the literature regarding proteomic signature specifically tailored to assess dementia risk in T2D populations. Furthermore, high-throughput proteomic profiling enables the elucidation of underlying biological mechanisms at a population scale [ 25 ], providing unprecedented opportunities to explore the complex bidirectional relationship between metabolic dysfunction and neurodegeneration. Such analyses may yield clinically actionable mechanistic insights into this relationship. In this study, we leverage data from the UK Biobank Pharma Proteomics Project (UKB-PPP)—the largest proteomic investigation conducted to date, measuring nearly 3,000 distinct plasma proteins in approximately 50,000 individuals [ 26 ]—to address four primary objectives: (1) identify specific protein signatures associated with dementia in both T2D and non-T2D participants; (2) develop and validate a robust protein-based predictive model for dementia risk stratification in T2D patients; (3) characterize the proteomic signatures reflecting the modulatory effects of antidiabetic pharmacotherapy on dementia risk; and (4) elucidate the fundamental biological pathways connecting T2D and dementia through proteomics-based weighted correlation network analysis. Material and methods Study design and population All participants in this study were included from the UK Biobank, a comprehensive biomedical database and research resource encompassing detailed genetic and health information from over 500,000 UK adults aged 37 to 72 (details available at https://www.ukbiobank.ac.uk ). Participants attended one of 22 centers across England, Scotland, or Wales and provided health-related information through questionnaires, interviews, physical measurements, and biological samples between 2006 and 2010 (initial assessment visit). Ethical approval for the UK Biobank was obtained from the North West Multicenter Research Ethics Committee (reference number: 11/NW/0382). Each participant provided written informed consent. This study was conducted under the project with application number 150493. Initially, over 50,000 participants involved in the UKB-PPP were recruited for this study. Among them, participants with T2D at baseline were identified and included in the primary analysis through data from multiple sources. Firstly, T2D cases were identified based on hospital inpatient records with 9th and 10th Revision of the International Classification of Disease (ICD) diagnostic codes. These records included admission dates and disease diagnostic codes, obtained by linking records to Health Episode Statistics (England and Wales) and the Scottish Morbidity Records (Scotland) (details available at https://content.digital.nhs.uk/services ). Participants with a T2D death registration code based on ICD-10 were also identified. Additionally, self-reported medical history and medication information were collected through touchscreen questionnaires or oral interviews conducted by trained experts at baseline. Participants were considered to have T2D if they reported a history of diabetes with onset age over 35 years or any prescriptions for hypoglycemic medications, including insulin and oral medications such as biguanides, sulfonylureas, meglitinides, and thiazolidinediones. These self-report methods have been described in previous studies [ 27 ]. Furthermore, according to the standards set by the American Diabetes Association (ADA), participants with a random glucose level ≥ 11.1 mmol/L or a glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) level ≥ 48 mmol/mol (6.5 %) were also considered to have T2D. Participants were excluded from the T2D cohort if they had specific primary care, death registration, or hospital inpatient records indicative of type 1 diabetes. Additionally, those who received insulin prescriptions within one year of T2D diagnosis or had prescriptions for multiple glycemic control drugs at the time of diagnosis were also excluded. Participants with diagnosed dementia at baseline were also excluded (see 'Dementia Outcome' section). Finally, 3,292 T2D participants and 49,666 non-T2D participants were included in the primary analysis. The ICD10 codes used in this study are shown in Table S1 . Blood proteomics Participants in the UKB-PPP, a consortium of 13 biopharmaceutical companies, provided blood samples at 22 assessment centers during the initial baseline phase (2006–2010) (details shown at https://biobank.ndph.ox.ac.uk/ukb/refer.cgi?id = 4654). Blood samples were collected, transferred, processed, and analyzed using standardized procedures. Blood was collected in EDTA tubes and centrifuged at 2500g for 10 min at 4 °C to separate plasma, which was then immediately stored at −80 °C. These samples were subsequently transported on dry ice to the Olink Analysis Service in Sweden for uniform quantification using the antibody-based Olink Explore™ Proximity Extension Assay. A total of 2,923 unique proteins were detected and categorized into cardiometabolic, inflammation, neurology, and oncology classifications. All results underwent rigorous quality control (QC) measures, including the removal of outlier samples, data flagged by Olink with QC or assay warnings during data generation, or potential sample swaps (details available at https://biobank.ndph.ox.ac.uk/ukb/refer.cgi?id = 4658). The reliability of the Olink proteomics method has been validated in previous studies [ 26 ]. Olink also conducted an investigation of batch effects, with all assays showing inter- and intra-assay coefficients of variation below 20 % and 10 %, respectively. Normalized Protein eXpression (NPX) values were generated by dividing the counts for each sample and each assay by the extension control counts and further log-transforming the ratio (details at https://biobank.ndph.ox.ac.uk/ukb/refer.cgi?id = 4656). Variations within and between batches were minimized by accounting for the median of extension control-normalized counts, batch-specific median NPX values, and the differences in assay-specific median NPX values of each batch compared to the reference batch. For missing data, due to the values of three proteins (PCOLCE, NPM1, GLIPR1) being missing more than 30 %, these proteins were excluded from this study, leaving 2,920 unique proteins. Missing values for the remaining proteins were processed using the missRanger algorithm, a non-parametric imputation method based on random forests that is particularly suitable for high dimensional data [ 28 ]. The imputation process assumed that missing data occurred at random conditional on the observed variables in the dataset. A single complete dataset was generated and used for all statistical analyses in this study. No additional processing steps were applied to these NPX values in this study. Dementia outcomes All-cause dementia was considered the primary outcome of this study. The occurrence of all-cause dementia was identified using 3-character ICD-10 codes extracted from UK Biobank health-related outcomes datasets (category 100091), specifically the 'first occurrences' (category 1712) and 'algorithmically-defined outcomes' (category 42). These datasets include diagnostic data from primary care, hospital inpatient records, death register records, and self-reported medical conditions. The diagnostic criteria for Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia were consistent with those for all-cause dementia, with specific fields detailed in Table S1 . The follow-up period began on the date participants first visited the assessment center (field 53). The end of follow-up was determined by the earliest date of any of the following events: diagnosis of the outcome, date of death (field 40000), loss of follow-up (field 191), or the date of censoring (September 2023). The follow-up duration was calculated as the interval between the initial visit and the earliest of these events. Glycemic control drugs The self-reported prescription data used in this study were collected at baseline and obtained from the UK Biobank through field 20003, 2986, 6153, and 6177. During the initial assessment visit, participants are asked about the medications they are currently taking and trained staff record information directly about these medications, including prescription medications, over-the-counter medications, and supplements. We extract relevant prescription records based on drug names and the corresponding codes. For this study, we focused on glucose-lowering medications, including insulin, biguanides, sulfonylureas, meglitinides, and thiazolidinediones. These medications were used to identify individuals with T2D, as being prescribed a T2D medication is part of the diagnostic criteria. Additionally, the use of these medications is part of the exclusion criteria (see Study design and population). Due to the limited number of participants reporting the use of sulfonylureas, meglitinides, and thiazolidinediones, our analysis focused on insulin and biguanides. In the analysis, participants who did not report using any glucose-lowering medications were set as controls, while those who reported using only insulin or only biguanides were categorized accordingly as insulin users or biguanides users. Cognition The cognitive function of participants was assessed at baseline using touchscreen tests in UK Biobank. This study included results from two tests: pairs matching (field 20023) and reaction time (field 399). The pairs matching test required participants to remember the locations of as many paired cards as possible. The cards were then placed face down on the screen, and participants were asked to touch as many matching pairs as possible in the fewest number of attempts. Two rounds were conducted; the first round used 3 pairs of cards, and the second used 6 pairs. The number of incorrect matches made by participants in each round was recorded (details available at https://biobank.ndph.ox.ac.uk/ukb/refer.cgi?id = 100239). Reaction time was assessed using 12 rounds of the card game “Snap.” Participants saw two cards at a time and, if the cards were identical, pressed a button on the table in front of them as quickly as possible. For rounds where the cards matched, the sum of the durations of the first button presses was collected for analysis (details available at https://biobank.ndph.ox.ac.uk/ukb/refer.cgi?id = 100245). Blood biochemistry During the baseline period (2006–2010), the UK Biobank collected peripheral blood samples from all participants. Measurements were conducted using 10 immunoassay analyzers (6 DiaSorin Liaison XL and 4 Beckman Coulter DXI 800) and 4 clinical chemistry analyzers (2 Beckman Coulter AU5800 and 2 Siemens Advia 1800). The project analyzed 30 key serum biochemical markers (category 17518), applying rigorous quality control and correction for outliers (details available at https://biobank.ctsu.ox.ac.uk/crystal/crystal/docs/serum_biochemistry.pdf ). In this study, we analyzed 12 biochemical markers, categorized into lipid metabolism, diabetes, renal function, and inflammation groups as follows: 1) Lipid metabolism: Apolipoprotein A (APOA, field 30630), Apolipoprotein B (APOB, field 30630), Cholesterol (field 30640), High-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol (field 30760), Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol (field 30770), and Triglycerides (field 30870); 2) Diabetes: Glucose (field 30740), Glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c, field 30750), and Insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF1, field 30770); 3) Renal function: Cystatin C (field 30720) and Creatinine (field 30700); 4) Inflammation: C-reactive protein (CRP, field 30720). Demographics and APOE ε4 gene This study incorporated a range of potential confounders measured at baseline, including demographics and genetic susceptibility to dementia. Demographic data included age at baseline (field 21022), sex (field 31), and education (field 6138). APOE ε4 allele status was determined based on the genotypes of two single nucleotide polymorphisms (rs7412 and rs429358). Participants were categorized according to the number of APOE ε4 alleles they carried: none, one, or two. Statistical analyses We employed the Cox regression model to evaluate the association between proteins and dementia, considering interactions between T2D status and proteins as well as between medication use and proteins. Primary analyses used unadjusted models to avoid over-adjustment bias. Further analyses adjusted for age and sex as established confounders to assess robustness of findings. Proportional hazards assumptions were verified using Schoenfeld residuals, with no violations detected. For all interaction analyses, we used raw p-values rather than FDR adjusted p-values due to the inherently lower statistical power of interaction terms compared to main effects. Interaction analyses test “differences of differences,” resulting in larger standard errors. In our high-dimensional proteomic dataset (∼3,000 proteins), applying stringent multiple testing correction would substantially increase Type II error risk, potentially obscuring biologically meaningful interactions. This approach follows established methodological precedents in proteomic interaction studies [ 29 ] and was applied consistently across all protein-disease and protein-medication interaction analyses. To compare protein levels between T2D patients and non-T2D individuals, as well as categorical variables, we utilized the Mann-Whitney U test for two-group comparisons. Correlations between proteins and blood biochemical markers were analyzed using the Spearman correlation analysis. We performed enrichment analysis using the KEGG pathway database and Gene Ontology (GO) classifications for molecular function (MF), biological process (BP), and cellular component (CC) to understand protein functions. The top five enriched pathways in each module were selected to summarize their biological functions. Samples were randomly divided into a training set and a test set in an 8:2 ratio. For feature selection, least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) was applied to 100 subsets, with each subset randomly extracting 80 % of the training set. Within each training set model, dementia outcomes were binarized, hyperparameter lambda was optimized through grid search with ten-repeated ten-fold cross-validation, and ROSE synthetic sampling [ 30 ] was implemented to address class imbalance using the R package 'caret' [ 31 ]. Protein selection frequency was calculated across 100 iterations, retaining features appearing in ≥ 75 iterations for final model construction. For model construction, five different machine learning models—random forest (RF), Cox regression, LASSO, generalized linear models (GLM), and support vector machines (SVM)—were developed with a ten-fold cross-validation procedure applied to the training set, using Python modules 'sklearn' and 'lifelines'. Hyperparameter optimization was performed for each model using grid search on the training set. Key hyperparameters tuned included: n_estimators, min_samples_split, and max_depth for RF; C and kernel type for SVM; regularization strength (C) for LASSO and GLM; and penalizer and l1_ratio for Cox regression. The optimal hyperparameters were selected based on the highest mean area under the curve (AUC) (or C-index for Cox regression) across cross-validation folds. Final model performance was evaluated on the test set using AUC and C-index to assess accuracy. Model calibration was determined by comparing predicted versus actual dementia risk. Decision Curve Analysis (DCA) was used to evaluate the clinical net benefit of the model using the R package 'dcurves'. To assess model robustness given the relatively low event-per-variable ratio in the final model, we conducted sensitivity analyses using reduced feature sets. Based on protein importance rankings from the final model, we constructed simplified models incorporating the top proteins. These reduced models were developed using identical methodology as the final model, with performance compared using AUC and C-index metrics to evaluate stability across different feature dimensions. Weighted Gene Co-Expression Network Analysis (WGCNA) was used to identify key protein modules from 2,900 proteins in T2D patients, using the R package 'WGCNA' [ 32 ]. Based on the fit of the scale-free topology model, a power parameter of 12 was selected. Through the blockwiseModules subroutine, we identified 17 protein modules, covering 1,758 proteins, with a minimum module size set to 13. Functional annotations for each module were performed using GO annotations with the R package 'clusterProfiler' [ 33 ]. We created three-dimensional force-directed layout visualizations of the 17 functional modules using the R package 'igraph' [ 34 ]. Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) assessed the enrichment scores of modules and diseases at the protein level using the R package 'clusterProfiler'. The ranked proteins in dementia and modules were determined by the HR derived from Cox regression and by the correlation derived from Spearman's correlation analysis, respectively. Additionally, the top 50 proteins in each module were visualized in two-dimensional force-directed layouts. For the statistical analysis, significance was determined using a two-sided P &lt; 0.05. All statistical analyses were conducted using R (version 4.3.1) or Python software (version 3.11). Results Sample characteristics This study employed an aptamer-based assay to analyze 2,920 distinct plasma proteins in 52,958 participants without dementia at the baseline visit (2006–2010) ( Fig. 1 ). The median age of the participants was 56.8 years, with 46.1 % being female, 90 % of White ancestry, and 26.4 % carrying the APOE ε4 allele ( Table S2 ). At baseline, 3,292 participants had T2D. Over a median follow-up period of 14.6 years, 1,473 participants developed dementia, including 713 with Alzheimer's disease and 291 with vascular dementia. In the subset participants with T2D, the mean age was 59.92 years, with 62.2 % being female ( Table S3 ). During the follow-up period, 203 participants in this group developed dementia, including 101 with Alzheimer’s disease and 52 with vascular dementia. Fig. 1 Study design. For subsequent analysis, we focused on the top 20 proteins with the most significant HR changes based on interaction term coefficients. In T2D patients, the top 3 proteins associated with the greatest decrease in HR were tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily member 27 (EDA2R) (HR decrease 1.33), neurofilament light polypeptide (NEFL) (HR decrease 1.26), and lysosome membrane protein 2 (SCARB2) (HR decrease 1.21) compared to non-T2D individuals ( Fig. 2 B ). Conversely, the most significant HR increases in non-T2D individuals were observed for receptor-type tyrosine-protein phosphatase S (PTPRS) (HR increase 0.98), paraoxonase/lactonase 3 (PON3) (HR increase 0.76), and carbonic anhydrase 14 (CA14) (HR increase 0.51). After adjusting for age and sex in multivariable Cox models, NEFL emerged as the protein with the highest decrease in HR in T2D patients (HR decrease 0.81), while PTPRS continued to show the most significant HR increase (HR increase 0.68) ( Fig. 2 C ). To examine differential protein expression between diabetic and non-diabetic individuals, we conducted Mann-Whitney U tests. Among the 20 proteins analyzed, 18 showed significant differences ( P &lt; 0.05), with the majority (12/18) exhibiting higher levels in T2D patients ( Fig. 2 D ). Moreover, we stratified these 20 proteins into high and low expression groups based on their median NPX levels and examined their association with dementia risk using separate Cox regression models for T2D and non-T2D individuals ( Fig. 2 E-F ). NEFL showed the highest HR in non-T2D individuals (HR 5.108, 95 % CI 4.401–5.928, P &lt; 0.001), while EDA2R had the highest HR in T2D patients (HR 2.792, 95 % CI 2.042–3.811, P &lt; 0.001). NPTXR (HR 0.742, 95 % CI 0.664–0.829, P &lt; 0.001) and CA14 (HR 0.666, 95 % CI 0.595–0.745, P &lt; 0.001) were associated with incident dementia only in non-T2D individuals. Conversely, rho guanine nucleotide exchange factor 12 (ARHGEF12) was associated with increased dementia risk only in T2D participants (HR 1.448, 95 % CI 1.095–1.914, P = 0.009), but showed non-significant association in non-T2D individuals (HR 0.93, 95 % CI 0.8333–1.039, P = 0.199). The remaining 15 proteins demonstrated consistent risk patterns across both groups, as visualized in supplementary materials ( Fig. S1 and Fig. S2 ). Development and validation of a protein model for dementia in type 2 diabetes For feature selection, we applied least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) to 100 random subsets (each containing 80 % of the training set), identifying 54 proteins that appeared in &gt; 75 iterations for initial model construction. Among the five machine learning algorithms evaluated (random forest, Cox regression, LASSO, generalized linear models, and support vector machines), the LASSO-based model demonstrated superior predictive performance ( Fig. S3 A-B ). During coefficient regularization, three protein features were shrunk to zero, resulting in 51 proteins being included in the final model. The LASSO-based protein model showed superior predictive accuracy for incident dementia (AUC = 0.835, C-index = 0.829) compared to models based solely on demographics or cognitive assessment variables ( Fig. 3 A and Fig. S3 C ). Notably, the addition of demographic or cognitive variables did not significantly enhance the model's discriminative performance, suggesting that the protein signature may already encompass the predictive information captured by cognitive assessments or contain independent molecular signals. Both the protein model independently and in combination with demographics or cognition exhibited good calibration between predicted and observed risk probabilities ( Fig. 3 B ). To evaluate the clinical utility and net benefit of the model, we conducted DCA for the protein model both independently and in combination with demographics or cognition ( Fig. 3 C ). The results indicated that the protein model's discriminative capabilities translated into meaningful clinical utility across a wide range of threshold probabilities, with incremental improvements observed when demographics or cognitive assessment data were incorporated. Moreover, the protein model maintained high accuracy in predicting not only all-cause dementia but also specific subtypes such as Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia (both with AUC and C-index &gt; 0.8) ( Fig. S3 D-E ). When patients were stratified based on the median predictive risk scores derived from the protein model, those with scores above the median demonstrated a significantly increased risk of developing dementia, including its major subtypes Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia (HR range, 5.995–9.452) ( Fig. 3 D ). Among the 51 proteins retained in the final LASSO model, latent-transforming growth factor beta-binding protein 2 (LTBP2), glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP), and apolipoprotein E (APOE) emerged as the most significant independent predictors, with their expression levels strongly correlated with dementia risk in Cox regression analysis (LTBP2, HR = 3.00; GFAP, HR = 2.69; APOE, HR = 0.60; all P &lt; 0.001) ( Fig. 3 E-F ). Through correlation analysis, we found that 37 proteins were significantly associated with demographic factors, 16 with cognitive assessment scores, and only GFAP and APOE showed significant associations with the APOE ε4 genotype (all P &lt; 0.05). Correlation analysis revealed that the majority of these predictive proteins were closely related to glycometabolism, lipometabolism, renal function, and inflammatory pathways. Given the relatively low event-per-variable ratio (EPV = 4.0 [203 events/51 proteins]) in the 51-protein model, we performed sensitivity analyses using reduced feature sets to assess model robustness under improved EPV conditions ( Table S4 ). We developed simplified predictive models incorporating the most important proteins from the final LASSO model: a 20-protein model (EPV = 10.2 [203/20]) and a 13-protein model (EPV = 15.6 [203/13]). The sensitivity analyses demonstrated consistent model performance across varying feature dimensions. The 20-protein model achieved an AUC of 0.746 and C-index of 0.743, corresponding to 89 % and 90 % of the original 51-protein model's discriminative capacity, respectively. The most conservative 13-protein model maintained robust predictive performance (AUC = 0.718, C-index = 0.715), retaining 86 % of the full model's accuracy. These results indicate that our proteomic approach exhibits stability against overfitting, despite the inherent limitations imposed by the relatively low EPV ratio, and suggest that the identified protein signatures capture genuine biological signals rather than spurious associations. Association between blood proteins, glycemic control drugs, and dementia risk in type 2 diabetes Using Cox regression models with drug-protein interaction terms, we identified 164 proteins that significantly interacted with insulin use in modulating dementia risk in T2D patients (raw P &lt; 0.05) ( Fig. 4 A ). When examining dementia subtypes, only ME5 was associated with a decreased risk of Alzheimer's disease (HR 0.639, 95 % CI 0.506–0.807, P &lt; 0.001), whereas for vascular dementia, both ME1 (HR 1.503, 95 % CI 1.217–1.857, P &lt; 0.001) and ME4 (HR 1.405, 95 % CI 1.098–1.798, P = 0.007) were linked to an increased risk. The results revealed that ME1 and ME5 were significantly enriched for IL-6/JAK-STAT3 signaling pathway, inflammatory response, and epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) pathways, which were also enriched in all-cause dementia (normalized enrichment score &gt; 1.5, P &lt; 0.05). In vascular dementia, only the IL-6/JAK-STAT3 signaling and EMT pathways showed significant enrichment. Notably, ME5 and Alzheimer's disease were both uniquely enriched in the fatty acid metabolism pathway, a signature not observed in vascular dementia. Using force-directed layout visualization, we constructed protein–protein interaction networks to identify key hub proteins within these modules ( Fig. 5 E ). For ME4, ACRBP, AMOTL2, and ADAMTSL2 were identified as central nodes, while in ME5, ACY1, ADH4, and AGXT were identified as key hub proteins. Discussion In this study, we analyzed approximately 3,000 plasma proteins in a cohort of 52,958 dementia-free participants, including 3,292 individuals with T2D, who were followed for more than 10 years. Our findings show the potential of specific protein signatures to predict dementia risk in T2D patients and offer important insights into the underlying pathophysiological process that connects dementia and T2D. We identified proteins that serve as strong predictors of dementia, demonstrating specific pathways that may contribute to dementia risk in both individuals with and without T2D. For example, we found that up to 10 years before diagnosis, elevated plasma levels of EDA2R were strongly associated with an increased risk of dementia in non-T2D individuals, while this predictive power was diminished in T2D patients. EDA2R, a protein involved in immune regulation and apoptosis [ 35 ], has been previously linked to increased risks of both dementia and diabetes [ [35] , [36] , [37] ]. Moreover, elevated plasma levels of NPTXR and CA14 were predictive of a higher risk of dementia specifically in non-T2D individuals but not in T2D patients. NPTXR, a synaptic protein, has been associated with frontotemporal dementia [ 38 , 39 ], but its role in T2D has been less explored. CA14, part of the carbonic anhydrase family, has been linked to cognitive function and Alzheimer's disease [ 40 , 41 ], and it is also underreported in the context of T2D. Our results suggest that these two markers may not be suitable for assessing dementia risk in T2D patients. Conversely, high plasma levels of ARHGEF12 were associated with a higher risk of dementia only in T2D patients. ARHGEF12, a protein involved in vascular smooth muscle regulation, has been connected to Alzheimer's disease hallmarks and insulin metabolism [ 42 , 43 ], suggesting that vascular factors may play a crucial role in the relationship between dementia and T2D. Furthermore, our study found that NEFL, a well-established synaptic marker for cognitive impairment, was a stronger predictor of dementia risk in non-T2D individuals compared to those with T2D. This finding aligns with previous research indicating that diabetes can accelerate the progression of markers such as NEFL and GFAP [ 44 ], the latter being a non-specific marker for dementia that can predict dementia risk over a 10-year period [ 23 ]. However, we did not observe a differential association between GFAP and dementia in T2D versus non-T2D individuals. This may be because T2D itself affects GFAP levels without significantly impacting the T2D-dementia pathway, as GFAP was associated with glucose, lipid, and renal metabolism—all pathological characteristics of diabetes—found in our study. Overall, these findings underscore the importance of accounting for diabetes when using dementia-related predictive proteins to identify individuals at high risk of dementia among T2D patients. We developed a proteomic prediction model with robust prognostic accuracy for evaluating dementia risk among patients with T2D. Notably, the model also provided precise predictions for dementia subtypes, including Alzheimer’s disease and vascular dementia, and demonstrated excellent calibration with observed dementia incidence over a 15-year follow-up period. The accuracy of our protein model (C statistic: 0.829) surpasses traditional diabetes-specific dementia prediction models based solely on clinical variables, which typically report C statistics in the range of 0.733–0.744 over follow-up periods exceeding 10 years [ 12 , 13 , 45 ]. The superior performance of our proteomic model can be attributed to the integration of 51 blood proteins that capture the heterogeneous pathophysiological processes linking T2D and dementia. These biomarkers encompass key biological pathways involved in lipid metabolism, glucose homeostasis, renal function, and inflammatory responses. Importantly, proteins such as LTBP2, GFAP, and APOE emerged as particularly robust predictors of dementia risk, underscoring the potential of a proteomics-based approach to elucidate complex disease phenotypes. Although previous studies have proposed proteomic-based dementia prediction models [ 23 , 46 , 47 ], these efforts targeted broader populations and did not specifically address the T2D cohort. Our protein model holds significant promise for application in older adults with T2D who are at elevated risk for dementia, thereby facilitating early detection and enabling timely intervention strategies to mitigate future dementia onset [ [48] , [49] , [50] ]. Our proteomic analysis revealed that glycemic control drugs, particularly insulin and biguanides, exert bidirectional effects on dementia risk through distinct protein interactions. This molecular heterogeneity helps explain previous conflicting clinical observations [ [51] , [52] , [53] , [54] , [55] , [56] , [57] , [58] ]. Specifically, we identified 164 proteins interacting with insulin and 90 with biguanides, with minimal overlap between these sets (only DSG2 showed significant interactions with both medications). This protein-specific interaction pattern indicates that antidiabetic medications may simultaneously activate both neuroprotective and neurodegenerative pathways. For insulin, proteins associated with increased dementia risk were enriched in immune regulation and cell signaling pathways, while those associated with decreased risk implicated metabolic regulation. Similarly, biguanides showed differential pathway engagement, with protease activity and inflammatory response linked to increased risk, and lipid metabolism pathways associated with decreased risk. These findings align with previous study showing that when metformin exposure was accounted for in the models, T2D demonstrated a potentially protective effect against Alzheimer's disease [ 51 ]. The contrasting results suggest that glycemic control medications may confound the relationship between T2D and dementia by modulating key biological mediators. Clinically, these findings highlight the need for personalized approaches to T2D management in patients at risk for cognitive decline. Patient-specific proteomic profiling might eventually guide selection of antidiabetic therapies to maximize neuroprotective effects while minimizing pathways associated with increased dementia risk. Our findings support that multiple biological pathways, beyond insulin resistance, may contribute to the association between T2D and dementia, though these represent correlative observations requiring experimental validation. The inflammatory response pathway, particularly the IL6-JAK-STAT3 signaling, emerged as a key mechanism enriched in the association between T2D and dementia. This signaling pathway is known to promote vascular damage and insulin resistance [ [59] , [60] , [61] ], as well as aberrant astrocyte reactivity [ 62 ]. Our results suggest that altered inflammatory responses in T2D may be associated with neuronal dysfunction, potentially contributing to cognitive decline and the development of dementia, though direct causal relationships remain to be established. Additionally, we found EMT as a potentially important process connecting T2D and dementia. EMT, which is implicated in the most major complications of T2D, may be associated with chronic inflammation, vascular dysfunction, and renal abnormalities, all of which contribute to an increased risk of dementia in T2D patients [ 63 , 64 ]. Moreover, our results demonstrated an enrichment of viral processes in the connection between T2D and dementia. The relationship between viral infections and dementia has been previously reported [ [65] , [66] , [67] ], but our findings suggests that viral infections may represent a critical, yet underexplored, pathway linking these two conditions, though this requires further mechanistic investigation. We also found that fatty acid metabolism were more strongly enriched in Alzheimer’s disease compared to vascular dementia in T2D patients, indicating a potentially unique role for this metabolic pathway in the pathophysiology of diabetes-specific Alzheimer's disease [ 68 , 69 ]. This result underscores the heterogeneity of the mechanisms connecting T2D to dementia and its subtypes, highlighting the need for subtype-specific prevention and treatment strategies in T2D patients. However, these pathway associations should be interpreted as hypothesis-generating correlative findings rather than definitive mechanistic evidence, warranting future experimental validation to establish causal relationships. This study's strengths include its large sample size, comprehensive blood proteomics profiling, and long follow-up period, which collectively facilitated an in-depth exploration of the relationship between T2D and dementia. Nonetheless, several limitations warrant consideration. First, the comparatively small number of T2D patients relative to non-T2D individuals may have diminished the statistical power for certain comparisons, potentially obscuring subtle effects. Moreover, the relatively young age of our cohort could influence the assessment of dementia risk, as the risk trajectory in younger diabetic patients may differ from that in older populations [ 70 ], thereby limiting the direct applicability of our findings to older adults. Additionally, the study was not originally conceived as a dedicated cohort for dementia or diabetes research. This design limitation resulted in a modest number of dementia cases, which may introduce selection bias and limit the generalizability of our findings to broader T2D populations. Moreover, the predominantly white demographic further restricts the generalizability of the results to other racial and ethnic groups. Another pertinent limitation is the study's inclusion period, which predated the widespread use of contemporary diabetes medications, such as glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonists[ 71 ] and sodium-glucose co-transporter protein 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors [ 72 ]. The potential modulatory effects of these agents on dementia risk were not accounted for, possibly limiting our observed associations. Additionally, we acknowledge that medication data were collected only at baseline, and medication changes during the follow-up period could not be assessed, potentially affecting the evaluation of long-term medication effects on protein expression. The absence of diabetes severity indicators, such as disease duration and complications, may confound the observed protein-medication interactions, as disease severity could independently influence both prescribing patterns and proteomic profiles. Furthermore, our proteomic interaction analyses were conducted using raw p-values rather than FDR-adjusted values. This methodological choice was necessitated by the inherently lower statistical power of interaction tests—often characterized by larger standard errors due to the “differences of differences” framework. However, this approach may elevate the risk of Type I errors, and as such, these findings should be interpreted as exploratory until validated in independent cohorts. Future research should aim to address these limitations by enrolling larger, more diverse populations, incorporating the effects of modern diabetes therapies, and employing rigorous multiple testing correction where feasible. Additionally, complementary animal and in vivo studies are essential to further elucidate the mechanistic underpinnings of diabetes-specific dementia proteomic signatures. In summary, our study demonstrates that proteomic signatures may offer improved prediction over standard clinical strategies for assessing individuals at high risk of dementia in T2D patients, and may be useful for identification of the potential targets for possible drug therapy to reduce or delay the onset of dementia in T2D patients.
