Although many adolescents and young adults experiment with drugs, a subset may develop a drug use disorder (DUD). Few studies have used machine learning to identify risk and protective factors associated with DUDs, and to the best of our knowledge, none have been conducted with a high-risk youth sample or incorporated explanatory analyses to understand how such factors influence model predictions. We sought to address these gaps by estimating a random forest to identify salient risk and protective factors that differentiate youth with a DUD from their peers who use drugs but do not meet diagnostic criteria for a DUD. We extend traditional supervised learning methods by conducting explanatory model analyses to unpack what the model learned from the data, allowing for better interpretation of how influential factors may affect DUD risk at both the sample and case levels. Cross-sectional data were analyzed from a sample of 600 youth aged 14–24 with past 6-month illicit drug use (59 % male, 58 % Black; 57 % meeting DUD criteria). Global assessments revealed that risk factors aligned with deviance proneness and stress/negative affect were associated with higher likelihoods of a DUD, whereas academic achievement and later drug use initiation were associated with lower likelihoods. Local assessment methods highlighted how these broad predictive patterns can be applied to individual risk and protective factor profiles to inform precision-based preventive strategies. Overall, these analytic approaches and findings may help inform the development of indicated preventive interventions for DUDs in adolescent and young adult populations. Highlights • Explanatory model analyses improved interpretability of a “black box” model. • Global/local assessments elucidate how factors influence DUD classification. • Deviance proneness characteristics were most influential in distinguishing a DUD. • Stress/negative affect predictors were also implicated in the classification process. • Protective factors included later drug use initiation and academic achievement. In a recent U.S. national survey, 14.3 % of youth aged 12–17 and 40.9 % of youth aged 18–25 reported illicit drug use within the past year, but only 7.0 % and 18.6 % of these age groups met diagnostic criteria for a DUD, respectively ( Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration, 2023 ). A DUD is characterized by compulsive use that leads to clinically significant impairment and disruptions to daily life ( American Psychiatric Association, 2013 ) and is associated with adverse health and psychosocial outcomes that can persist through adulthood ( McCabe et al., 2022 , Newcomb and Locke, 2005 ). Given these deleterious effects, identifying salient risk and protective factors among youth at elevated risk for developing a DUD can inform the development of indicated prevention strategies. This study examines a comprehensive set of risk and protective factors in a sample of youth with illicit drug use, with the aim of distinguishing youth with a DUD from youth who use drugs but do not meet criteria for a DUD. Theoretical models of substance use collectively implicate a myriad of influential factors across social-ecological levels. Substance use prevention models have accordingly evolved to incorporate broader ecological and developmental perspectives ( Scheier, 2015 ). One commonly utilized framework is that of risk and protective factors ( Hawkins et al., 1992 ), which recognizes certain factors can elevate or reduce the likelihood of engaging in substance use. Through exposure to varying levels and combinations of risk and protective factors, different likelihoods of engaging in substance use are conferred. Although some risk factors are fixed (e.g., genetics, prenatal exposure), many risk and protective factors are malleable and thus can be modified through preventive interventions ( Cicchetti and Rogosch, 1999 , Stone et al., 2012 ). As such, identifying risk and protective factors associated with DUDs among youth who are at elevated risk for such a disorder may be particularly useful for informing which factors to target in interventions designed to reduce susceptibility and enhance resilience. Cumulative exposure to various risk and/or protective factors leads to countless intertwined pathways through which drug use can progress into a DUD; however, some risk and protective factors may be more salient than others. For instance, there is strong empirical support for a deviance-proneness pathway, which suggests DUDs occur within a cluster of externalizing and problematic behaviors, such as criminal activity, aggression, and conduct or antisocial personality disorder ( Sher and Slutske, 2003 ). Prior research also suggests genetically inherited traits, poor parenting, and high-risk childhood environments may set the stage for developing cognitive and executive functioning deficits that hinder self-regulation, placing youth at elevated risk of affiliating with deviant and substance-using peers, delinquency, academic disengagement, and, ultimately, problematic drug use ( Chassin and Bountress, 2014 , Lee and McDowell, 2019 ). A DUD may also be influenced by a stress and negative affect pathway, whereby youth use drugs as a maladaptive coping mechanism to alleviate negative emotional states ( Chassin and Bountress, 2014 , Lee and McDowell, 2019 ). However, because drug use often occurs in conjunction with impulsivity and emotion dysregulation, there is conflicting evidence of whether this is a unique pathway or an extension of deviance-proneness ( Hussong et al., 2017 ). While limited research has been conducted with high-risk youth to identify protective factors that may disrupt these pathways, prospective studies have implicated family cohesion/harmony ( Hoffmann and Cerbone, 2002 , Zhou et al., 2006 ) and parental support ( Measelle et al., 2006 ). Machine learning approaches are increasingly being used to examine constellations of numerous risk and protective factors because they can effectively handle high dimensional data among many correlated variables ( Kuhn and Johnson, 2013 ). Recent studies with adolescent and young adult samples have used supervised learning algorithms (e.g., random forest, regularized regression) to predict drug use and misuse ( Han, 2020 , Henry et al., 2024 , Vázquez et al., 2020 ) as well as substance use disorders inclusive of both drugs and alcohol ( Jing et al., 2020 , Wadekar, 2020 ). But to date, few studies have used such algorithms to predict the presence of a DUD, and to the best of our knowledge, none have examined risk and protective factors associated with DUDs among samples of youth who are already at elevated risk for such disorders. One recent study examined the risk of a DUD in a community-based sample of Brazilian youth aged 18–24 ( Rakovski et al., 2023 ) and identified alcohol use disorder, tobacco use disorder, major depression, lifetime history of a manic episode, and suicide risk as the most important factors. However, the sample was not limited to youth who used drugs (i.e., included those who were abstinent), and thus the study was unable to discriminate young adults with a DUD from those who used drugs but did not meet criteria for a DUD. Whereas some prior studies conducted specifically with adults who reported using drugs allowed for such a distinction ( Rajapaksha et al., 2020 , Rajapaksha et al., 2022 ), this remains a gap in research on DUDs among youth. Despite the potential benefits of supervised learning algorithms for examining constellations of risk and protective factors, they are often critiqued for their lack of transparency and interpretability compared to traditional statistical modeling approaches. Indeed, they are often described as “black box” models due to their opaque decision-making ( Apley and Zhu, 2020 , Biecek and Burzykowski, 2021 ). Studies using these algorithms to predict drug use among youth typically report model performance and which independent variables were most important in generating accurate predictions. However, those metrics alone do not convey how those variables affect the predictions. Explanatory methods are therefore needed to supplement those metrics with (1) global assessments of how specific variables influence the predicted probability across the analytic sample and (2) local assessments of how the model arrived at a prediction for a single case ( Biecek and Burzykowski, 2021 , Molnar, 2023 ). When supplemented with these global and local assessments, supervised learning algorithms can provide deeper insight into the influence of important risk and protective factors in predicting the presence of a DUD for both aggregate samples of youth as well as individual cases. Additionally, identifying modifiable risk and protective factors that differentiate high-risk youth who have a DUD vs. those who use drugs but do not have a DUD may help inform the development of indicated preventive interventions. Leveraging a comprehensive set of risk and protective factors collected from a high-risk sample of youth, the objectives of this study are to: (1) differentiate youth with a DUD from their peers who use drugs but do not meet criteria for a DUD; (2) identify specific risk and protective factors that are most important in the classification process; and (3) apply explanatory model analyses to unpack what the model learned from the data and better interpret how key risk and protective factors affect DUD predictions at the global level (i.e., the analytic sample) and local level (i.e., an individual case). 2 Methods 2.1 Dataset and analytic sample The present study analyzed publicly available data from the Flint Youth Injury Study, which compared medical outcomes and service trajectories between youth with illicit drug use who presented to an emergency department with an assault-related injury and youth with illicit drug use who presented with another condition ( Cunningham, 2017 ). Youth aged 14–24 seeking treatment at an emergency department in Flint, Michigan were screened for the study’s eligibility criteria; those who reported using cannabis, cocaine, methamphetamine, inhalants, hallucinogens, opioids, or misusing prescription drugs via any route of administration during the past six months were recruited for the full study, which consisted of a baseline assessment and four follow-up assessments. Institutional Review Boards at the University of Michigan and Hurley Medical Center approved the recruitment and data collection procedures (see Bohnert et al. 2015 and Cunningham et al. 2014 for details). The analytic sample for the present study consists of 600 individuals who completed the baseline assessment. This secondary data analysis, conducted by an independent research team, only analyzed baseline data because follow-up data were not included in the publicly available dataset. Additional Institutional Review Board approval was not required because it exclusively used deidentified, publicly available data. Approximately 57 % of youth met diagnostic criteria for a DUD, with a cannabis use disorder being the most prevalent (93.6 %). Participants ranged in age from 14 to 24 (14–17 = 14.8 %, 18–20 = 42.7 %, and 21–24 = 42.5 %); 58.8 % identified as male and 41.2 % as female. The racial composition of the sample was 58.2 % Black, 32.5 % White, 7.5 % Multiple Races, and 1.8 % Asian, American Indian, or Native Hawaiian. Most reported they or their parents/guardians were receiving public assistance (73.0 %), and over half sought treatment at the emergency department for an assault-related injury (58.3 %). A binary variable was created to indicate whether participants met the diagnostic criteria for any of these drugs, which was used as the outcome of interest (0 = drug use without disorder, 1 = a DUD). 2.2.2 Independent variables Table 1 presents the variables that were extracted from the parent study and used as predictors in the models. We organized these independent variables into the following categories: (1) demographics and personal characteristics; (2) parent and family characteristics; (3) social influences; (4) violence exposure and involvement; (5) legal system and criminal involvement; (6) mental health; and (7) substance use behavior/attitudes. Two clinical measures of alcohol use (alcohol use disorder and binge drinking) were included to capture potential comorbidity and underlying risk processes; comparable clinical measures for tobacco and nicotine use (e.g., tobacco use disorder) were not collected by the parent study and thus could not be included. Guided by prior research (e.g., Hawkins et al., 1992 ; Stone et al., 2012 ), independent variables were coded as risk factors, protective factors, or demographics and personal characteristics (see Fig. 1 ). Notably, because these variables were assessed at the same time as the outcome, these should not be interpreted as causal risk and protective factors, but rather as simply risk and protective factors associated with the presence of a DUD. Missing data were present in three independent variables, affecting 6.5 % of participants; bagged trees were used to impute the missing values ( Kuhn and Johnson, 2013 ). Bivariate comparisons were conducted to compare the drug use ( n = 257) and DUD ( n = 343) groups on the independent variables. Unadjusted group differences were assessed using independent t tests for continuous variables and Pearson’s chi-square tests or Fisher’s exact tests for categorical variables. Cohen’s d and odds ratio effect sizes were calculated to quantify the magnitude of these differences. To address the study’s first objective, we estimated a random forest model using the ranger package ( Wright and Ziegler, 2017 ). A random forest is a bootstrapped supervised learning algorithm that aggregates decision trees constructed with randomly selected subsets of cases and independent variables ( Breiman, 2001 , Hastie et al., 2009 ), and the results provide the predicted probability of a DUD for each participant. A participant was classified as having a DUD if their predicted probability was ≥ 0.50. While growing each tree, the model sampled 80 % of cases with replacement to reduce variance and improve generalization. To optimize performance, we iteratively tuned the number of trees, terminal node size, tree depth, and number of variables considered at each split. Model performance was evaluated using 10-fold cross-validation. Prediction accuracy, area under the curve, sensitivity, specificity, and precision were calculated in held-out folds, ensuring complete separation between training and testing data. Full details on data pre-processing, hyperparameter tuning, and cross-validation are provided in Supplemental Material 2 . We applied global and local methods to comprehensively assess the model and its predictions using the ranger ( Wright and Ziegler, 2017 ), DALEX ( Biecek, 2018 ), and iBreakDown ( Staniak and Biecek, 2019 ) packages. To address the study’s second objective, we used permutation importance to identify which variables were most influential in the classification process, with larger positive permutation values indicating greater importance for predictive accuracy ( Breiman, 2001 , Wright and Ziegler, 2017 ). Permutation importance answers which variables matter most for accuracy, but it does not convey the directionality or magnitude of their effects on the predictions. Therefore, to improve interpretability of the model (third objective), we calculated accumulated local effects to evaluate how changes in the most important variables affected the predicted probability of a DUD, on average, across the sample ( Apley and Zhu, 2020 , Biecek, 2018 ). Thus, accumulated local effects characterize the functional relationship between an independent variable and the model’s prediction. Additionally, we used local methods to better understand how different risk and protective factors influenced the predicted probability of a DUD for a simulated, representative case, providing individualized insights that complement the sample-level patterns identified by the global methods. To do so, the fitted random forest model was applied to the simulated case, and the predictions were decomposed to identify which variables contributed most to the case’s predicted probability ( Biecek and Burzykowski, 2021 , Staniak and Biecek, 2019 ). We then estimated a ceteris-paribus profile for the case to examine how changes in malleable risk and protective factors would affect their predicted probability of a DUD while holding all other variables constant ( Biecek, 2018 ). 3 Results 3.1 Bivariate comparisons There were statistically significant differences between the drug use and DUD groups on all but the following variables: age, race, public assistance, study condition, living with parents, previous sexual intercourse, male mentorship, female mentorship, parent support, and posttraumatic stress disorder. The full results are reported in Table 1 . 3.2 Random forest The model performed well in differentiating the presence or absence of a DUD (area under curve = 0.77): 74 % of the total sample was correctly classified as either having a DUD (positive) or using drugs without meeting DUD criteria (negative). The model correctly classified 82 % of cases with a DUD and 64 % of cases who used drugs but did not meet criteria for a DUD. Among cases classified as having a DUD, 75 % were true positives. Variability in performance metrics is reported in Supplemental Material 3 . 3.2.1 Global assessments Fig. 1 displays scaled permutation importance for the independent variables. Overall, the variables that were generally identified as the most important were measures of substance use-related behaviors and attitudes, peer influence, criminal history, academic performance, and mental health conditions. Consistent with the bivariate comparisons, biological sex was the only demographic variable with high importance in the classification process. Fig. 2 displays the accumulated local effects for the 12 most important variables. Intoxicated driving was associated with a higher likelihood of a DUD ( Fig. 2 A), with the average predicted probability increasing from 0.52 at the minimum score to 0.82 at the maximum score. The slope suggests even a single instance of intoxicated driving was highly predictive of a DUD. Exposure to negative peer influence was also associated with a higher likelihood of a DUD: the average predicted probability increased from 0.45 to 0.73 across the score range ( Fig. 2 B). The slope was steepest between scores of 1.0 and 3.0, indicating even lower to moderate levels of negative peer affiliation had a considerable effect on the predicted probability of a DUD. Meeting criteria for alcohol use disorder increased the average predicted probability from 0.50 to 0.82 ( Fig. 2 C). Temptation to use drugs when experiencing self-disappointment increased the average predicted probability from 0.50 (1 = “not at all”) to 0.76 (4 = “very” and 5 = “extremely”; Fig. 2 D). Meeting criteria for a conduct or anti-social personality disorder increased the average predicted probability from 0.51 to 0.76 ( Fig. 2 E). Later drug use initiation was associated with a lower likelihood of a DUD, decreasing from 0.65 to 0.40 at ages 8 and 22, respectively ( Fig. 2 F). The decline was steepest between ages 14 and 17, highlighting the potential importance of early drug use prevention efforts. The average predicted probability linearly increased across criminal history scores from 0.51 to 0.77 ( Fig. 2 G). Temptation to use drugs when experiencing family problems increased the likelihood of a DUD, with the average predicted probability increasing from 0.50 to 0.78 across the score range ( Fig. 2 H). The average predicted probability increased from 0.51 for female to 0.61 for male ( Fig. 2 I). Better academic performance reduced the average predicted probability, decreasing from 0.62 at the lowest achievement level to 0.54 at the highest level ( Fig. 2 J). Temptation to use drugs when experiencing friend problems increased the average predicted probability from 0.53 to 0.78 across the score range ( Fig. 2 K). Higher anxiety increased the likelihood of a DUD, with the average predicted probability linearly increasing from 0.52 to 0.70 ( Fig. 2 L). In particular, substance use-related behaviors and attitudes, peer influence, criminal history, conduct or anti-social personality disorder, sex, academic performance, and anxiety were among the most important differentiating features. Several indicators of violence and aggressive behaviors were also identified as important but to a lesser extent. To overcome the limited transparency and interpretability of supervised learning models, we conducted explanatory model analyses to provide insight as to what the model learned from the data and how it influenced the classification of participants. The results of these global and local assessments provided findings that might be used to better inform future indicated prevention strategies to reduce the likelihood of a DUD among youth who use drugs. Findings from global assessments implicated factors aligned with both deviance proneness as well as stress and negative affect. First, several externalizing behaviors were identified as having high importance in the classification process. Accumulated local effects revealed youth who reported intoxicated driving, engaged in criminal activity, had an alcohol use disorder, or met criteria for conduct or antisocial personality disorder had substantially higher predicted probabilities of a DUD. Similarly, even limited exposure to negative peer influence was associated with a higher predicted probability of a DUD, highlighting the salience of peers in the lives of adolescents and young adults. Early initiation to drugs and low academic achievement were also associated with higher predicted probabilities, both of which may be influenced by externalizing behaviors. Notably, these results align with those reported by Rajapaksha et al. (2022) , which identified delinquency and peer cannabis use during adolescence as key predictors of cannabis use disorder in adulthood. Second, indicators of negative affect and stress were also identified as important predictors in the classification process. Accumulated local effects revealed substantially higher predicted probabilities of a DUD among youth who reported greater temptation to use drugs when experiencing self-disappointment and family/friend problems as well as youth with higher levels of anxiety. These findings suggest that using drugs to alleviate stress and negative affect may also be an underlying drug use motive among youth with a DUD. Given the various pathways through which a DUD may develop among youth, precision medicine approaches targeting specific risk and protective factors known to be associated with the likelihood of a DUD are particularly useful for guiding preventive interventions ( August and Gewirtz, 2019 ). The local explanatory methods used in this study may be particularly useful for such efforts, permitting examination of how the unique ecologies and circumstances of an individual may relate to the likelihood of a DUD. For example, when tailoring a skill-building module for such an intervention, this study’s simulated case might benefit more from program components targeting behavior management and decision-making, whereas another case with higher stress and negative affect might benefit more from components targeting affect regulation and distress tolerance. Such a precision-based approach is supported by recent evaluations of a personality-tailored substance use prevention program, which found that at-risk youth enrolled in the program had long-term beneficial reductions in alcohol and illicit drug use relative to control groups ( Edalati and Conrod, 2019 , Lawler et al., 2024 ). 4.1 Limitations Nonetheless, it is important to acknowledge the limitations of the current study. First and foremost, the cross-sectional design of this study precludes any causal conclusions. Indeed, our results should only be interpreted as characteristics associated with the presence of a DUD rather than the development of a DUD. Future studies using longitudinal and causal research designs will be needed to further elucidate issues of causal ordering. Second, although the fitted model had higher accuracy rates than similar studies conducted exclusively with adults who used drugs ( Rajapaksha et al., 2020 , Rajapaksha et al., 2022 ), there is still room for improvement. Utilizing other advanced algorithms and features not available in the public dataset may increase predictive accuracy. Third, many DUDs were combined into a single binary outcome, treating a diverse set of drugs as a single category. It is possible this broad approach obscures distinct risk and protective factors that may be uniquely associated with specific DUDs (e.g., cannabis use disorder, opioid use disorder). Thus, future research should address this limitation by examining different DUDs separately. Fourth, some variables may represent unmeasured factors, such as shared genetic and environmental influences underlying conduct disorder and substance use disorders. Last, the sample is comprised of youth from one city presenting to the emergency department (58 % with an assault-related injury), potentially limiting generalizability of the results. Future studies should attempt to replicate these findings in other samples of high-risk youth. Despite these limitations, this study contributes to the literature by: (1) advancing our understanding of the complex risk and protective factors that may be worth addressing in indicated, youth-focused preventive interventions targeting DUDs; and (2) highlighting the interpretive value of conducting explanatory model analyses in future substance use disorder studies that utilize supervised learning algorithms. 4.2 Conclusion This study examined risk and protective factors that differentiate profiles of youth with a DUD from their peers who use drugs but do not meet criteria for a DUD. Results implicated factors consistent with both deviance proneness (e.g., intoxicated driving, negative peer influence, conduct or anti-social personality disorder) as well as stress and negative affect (e.g., temptation to use drugs when experiencing self-disappointment and family/friend problems, anxiety) among the variables with the highest importance in predicting the likelihood of a DUD. Applying global and local explanatory model analyses enabled a deeper understanding of how the included risk and protective factors influenced predictions at the sample and individual levels, respectively, reducing the opaqueness of a “black box” model. Taken together, these analytic approaches and findings may provide useful insights for the development of indicated preventive interventions for DUDs in adolescent and young adult populations.
