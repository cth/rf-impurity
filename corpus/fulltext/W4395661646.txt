Abstract We aimed to identify serum biomarkers that predict knee osteoarthritis (OA) before the appearance of radiographic abnormalities in a cohort of 200 women. As few as six serum peptides, corresponding to six proteins, reached AUC 77% probability to distinguish those who developed OA from age-matched individuals who did not develop OA up to 8 years later. Prediction based on these blood biomarkers was superior to traditional prediction based on age and BMI (AUC 51%) or knee pain (AUC 57%). These results identify a prolonged molecular derangement of joint tissue before the onset of radiographic OA abnormalities consistent with an unresolved acute phase response. Among all 24 protein biomarkers predicting incident knee OA, the majority (58%) also predicted knee OA progression, revealing the existence of a pathophysiological “OA continuum” based on considerable similarity in the molecular pathophysiology of the progression to incident OA and the progression of established OA. INTRODUCTION There has been a 9% global increase in the age standardized osteoarthritis (OA) incidence rate over the 28-year period from 1990 to 2017 ( 1 ). Given the growing OA population globally and magnitude of the economic and societal burden ( 2 ), there is a growing interest in a new paradigm—switching from palliation, the traditional focus in OA, to early prevention. Early-stage OA could provide a “window of opportunity” in which to arrest the disease process and restore joint homeostasis ( 3 ). Efforts are ongoing to establish classification criteria for early OA ( 3 – 5 ). The current goal of classification criteria for early-stage knee OA is to discriminate those with joint symptoms at increased risk of OA structural progression, from those with symptoms due to other causes. However, OA has a long prodromal phase, possibly in part asymptomatic, most certainly preradiographic ( 6 – 8 ). The identification of sensitive biomarkers to identify early OA, before development of overt structural damage, has the potential to greatly reduce the medical burden of disease. This is posited because disease-modifying drugs, once available, are expected to have the greatest favorable impact on OA if administered early in the disease course before irreversible radiographic abnormalities ( 7 ). However, the majority of biomarker research has focused on established radiographic knee OA (RKOA) ( 9 – 11 ); the task of developing measures with utility in individual patients is arduous ( 12 ). There have been some molecular biomarkers identified to predict incident RKOA, but results are conflicting, for instance, for serum Cartilage Oligomeric Matrix Protein (COMP) [positively ( 13 – 17 ) and negatively ( 18 ) associated], and urinary C-terminal cross-linked telopeptides of type II collagen (CTXII) [positively ( 16 , 17 ) and negatively ( 18 ) associated]. Therefore, the identification of robust biochemical predictors of RKOA incidence is a challenge and remains an unmet need. By definition, all individuals who develop incident RKOA have had progression of a pathological process from a subclinical to a clinically detectable stage with joint symptoms, imaging abnormality, or both. We are interested in determining whether the molecular pathophysiology in the decade before incident RKOA bears any similarity to the molecular pathophysiology of progression of “established” RKOA. We hypothesize that molecular entities predicting incident RKOA are similar to those predicting progression of RKOA because of a similar underlying molecular pathophysiology, thereby supporting the concept of a biological OA continuum. To test this hypothesis, we evaluated a serum proteomic biomarker panel that we showed predicted RKOA progression with initial discovery in the Foundation for National Institutes of Health (FNIH) cohort [a subset of individuals from the Osteoarthritis Initiative (OAI) with established RKOA] and with validation in the Biomarker Factory cohort ( 19 ). The current study was designed to evaluate the proteomic panel for its ability to predict incident RKOA in the Chingford 1000 Women study, a longitudinal cohort of middle-aged women with RKOA data available over 10 years. In our prior study of 649 women in the Chingford study, we identified knee level radiographic factors, added to clinical factors, as important for prediction of incident RKOA ( 20 ). In the current study, we aimed to use serum proteomic biomarkers to predict incident RKOA in a case-control cohort of Chingford study participants selected for low risk of incident RKOA based on traditional risk factors [excluded prior joint injury and any baseline knee OA of Kellgren-Lawrence (KL) grade &gt; 1]. We quantified serum peptides using targeted multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mass spectrometry. Machine learning techniques were used to select proteomic biomarkers in sera (obtained years 2 and 6) that best predicted RKOA incidence at year 10. RESULTS Participant characteristics In total, 200 white female participants from the UK were studied ( Fig. 1 , study design), consisting of 100 incident RKOA cases and 100 age- and body mass index (BMI)–matched controls; their baseline characteristics included mean (SD): age 54 (6) years and BMI 26 (4) kg/m 2 ( Table 1 ). As expected, because of the selection criteria, there were no statistically significant group differences based on baseline age or BMI. There were no discernible clinical differences related to baseline KL knee OA severity score 0 versus 1 (table S1). Few participants (16%) reported any baseline pain on walking; however, excluding those with missing data, more incident cases reported baseline pain on walking compared with controls ( P = 0.03, Table 1 ). At baseline, 12 individuals (six controls and six RKOA) reported some use of an analgesic (majority ibuprofen); no individuals reported osteoporosis medication use, and only one control individual reported hormone replacement therapy use. Stable biomarkers To identify serum peptides predicting incident RKOA, we used a validated, targeted, MRM assay consisting of 165 peptides (104 proteins) ( 19 ) to analyze nondepleted serum samples obtained from the 200 study participants in y2 and y6, totaling 400 samples ( Fig. 2 ). A total of 115 peptides in 393 samples passed quality control (QC) measures for subsequent statistical analyses (196 from y2 and 197 from y6). A total of 16, 10, and 16 peptides were chosen most frequently by elastic net regression as predictors of incident RKOA at y10 based on serum biomarker concentrations from y2, y6, and y2/6–time-integrated concentrations (TICs), respectively (peptide amino acid designations provided in subscripts, listed in table S2). After reducing the predictor sets to one representative peptide per protein, the final number of predictors was 14, 9, and 14 peptides at each time point; these were termed the “stable set” biomarkers ( Fig. 3 ). In total, from the three analyses, 27 peptides (24 proteins) were identified as stable set predictors of incident RKOA. Three peptides, COMP (652–665) , CRAC1 (101–108) , and ZPI (438–444) , were selected as part of all three stable biomarker sets. Fig. 2. Schematic of serum peptide selection process. Essential biomarkers and model performance for predicting incident RKOA Full models, composed of 14, 9, and 14 biomarkers in the stable sets for y2, y6, and y2/6-TIC models, respectively, all yielded bootstrap area under the curve (AUC) of 0.83. After backward elimination to remove the peptides that contributed least to the overall AUCs ( Fig. 4 ), there were 11, 6, and 9 biomarkers in the three datasets corresponding to y2, y6, and y2/6-TIC predictors, respectively, with AUCs of 0.76 to 0.77 ( Table 2 ); these were termed the “essential set” biomarkers. CRAC1 (101–108) and COMP (652–665) appeared in all three essential sets ( Fig. 5 ). The y2 biomarkers of the primary analysis also predicted y5 RKOA (AUC 0.71, Table 2 ). Secondary analysis of y2 biomarkers (excluding the y5 n = 41 incident cases and n = 3 with missing RKOA status) and reselecting biomarkers with elastic net yielded six essential biomarkers achieving a similar AUC (0.77) as the primary y2 analysis using all cases (0.76) but requiring only half the number of predictors (6 versus 12 peptides); five of these essential biomarkers overlapped with the original y2 biomarker essential set ( Table 2 ). Sensitivity analyses demonstrated no impact of the baseline Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Arthritis Index (WOMAC) pain on walking, baseline knee KL grade 1, or y2 radiographic hip OA (RHOA) on y2 biomarker predictivity of y10 RKOA ( Table 2 ). The y6 biomarker only model yielded AUC [95% confidence interval (CI)] of 0.77 (0.67 to 0.86); this was similar to results using y2 and y2/6-TIC data but required only six peptides, the fewest number among all three main analyses ( Table 2 ). The addition of age and BMI did not improve the discriminative capability (no change of AUCs) of any model; this was also consistent with the lack of discrimination by age and BMI only, AUC 0.51 (with AUC 0.50 reflecting no different than chance alone), which likely reflects the age and BMI matching between cases and controls. WOMAC pain and RHOA individually were slightly better discriminators of incident OA (AUCs, 0.57 to 0.59). Adding all clinical variables to essential biomarkers yielded a maximum AUC 0.83 (for y2 and y2/6-TIC) but with CIs overlapping those of models of essential biomarkers only ( Table 2 ). We also explored the combination of a few biomarkers, CRAC1 (101–108) and COMP (652–665) with and without ZPI (438–444) , the three biomarkers in all stable sets. These fewer biomarkers were still statistically significant predictors (lower AUC 95% CI &gt; 0.5) in yr2; the yr2 essential set AUC 0.76 fell within the upper CI of the AUC of the 3 peptide predictors of 0.80, so the 11 essential versus the 3 predictors were statistically indistinguishable. However, the two or three peptide predictors in yr6 were not statistically significant (lower CI of the AUC equaled or was below 0.5) so this combination was inferior to the full yr6 essential set of six peptides. The maximum AUCs (0.76) were achieved with yr2 or yr6 predictors, with baseline age, BMI, WOMAC A1, and RHOA (table S3). Among the essential peptides, the highest odds ratio (ORs [95% CI]) for independently predicting incident RKOA in logistic regression models were achieved by y2 HABP2 (186–203) (4.09 [1.60, 4.97]) and HRG (1.05–2.87) (2.49 [1.05, 2.87]) and y6 CRAC1 (101–108) (2.58, [0.97,6.88]) and ZPI (438–444) (2.52 [1.12, 5.68]) ( Fig. 5 ). For example, the OR 2.58 for CRAC1 (101–108) means that when other biomarker concentrations remain unchanged, for every one-unit (equal to 1 SD) increase in CRAC1 (101–108) , we expect an 158% increased risk for incident RKOA in the subsequent 4 years. Some peptides were positively associated with incident RKOA [ACTG (96–113) , APOB (133–139) , APOE (270–278) , COMP (652–665) , CRAC1 (101–108) , HABP2 (186–203) , HPT (216–227) , HRG (140–153) , HRG (188–201) , and ZPI (438–444) ; OR ranges: 1.36 to 4.09; 95% CIs provided in Fig. 5 ], while some were negatively associated [C1S (516–523) , CO5 (509–515) , FBLN1 (386–395) , FBLN3 (363–376) , LYAM1 (71–78) , PLF4 (82–92) , PRG4 (1185–1201) , RET4 (185–195) , and VTNC (331–337) ; OR ranges, 0.35 to 0.64; 95% CIs provided in Fig. 5 ]. Single-cell gene expression and pathway analysis Single-cell gene expression corresponding to proteins in the stable sets was available from our prior analyses of knee OA cartilage and matched synovium ( 21 ). Genes corresponding to 18 of the 24 stable protein biomarkers were expressed in knee joint cartilage and/or synovium ( Fig. 6 ) including ACTG1 , C1R , C1S , COMP , CRTAC1 , EFEMP1 (FBLN3), and FN1 (cartilage and synovium); RBP4 (RET4) (chondrocytes from lesioned and nonlesioned cartilage); and FBLN1 and PRG4 (both chondrocytes and most strongly synoviocytes). A2AP , HABP2 , HEMO (HPX) , HRG , PLF4 , and ZPI were not found in the joint tissue transcriptome data. Fig. 6. Joint tissue gene expression profile corresponding to essential biomarkers. As previously described ( 21 ), single-cell RNA sequencing of medial tibial (MT) lesioned knee OA articular knee cartilage, outer lateral tibial (OLT) nonlesioned knee OA articular cartilage, and matched knee OA synovium (SY) were evaluated for number of cells expressing each of the essential biomarkers. Of 24 stable protein biomarkers, 18 were found in the transcriptome data and are plotted. Those highly expressed in both lesioned (medial tibial) and nonlesioned (outer lateral tibial) cartilage and synovium of OA joints included ACTG1 , C1R , C1S , COMP , CRTAC1 , EFEMP1 (FBLN3), and FN1 . RBP4 (RET4) was highly expressed by chondrocytes from both lesioned and nonlesioned cartilage. FBLN1 and PRG4 , although expressed in both chondrocytes and synoviocytes, were more strongly expressed in synoviocytes. A2AP , HABP2 , HEMO ( HPX ), HRG , PLF4 , and ZPI were not found in the joint tissue transcriptome data. The stable biomarker sets in both yr2 and yr6 strongly implicate an unresolved acute phase response (APR) as the underlying pathophysiology in the years leading up to RKOA as they include proteins functioning in hemostasis with components related to coagulation, fibrin clot formation, and fibrinolysis [Platelet factor 4 (PLF4), Fibulin-1 (FBLN1), Histidine-rich glycoprotein (HRG), Protein Z-dependent protease inhibitor (ZPI), Fibronectin (FINC), Alpha-2-antiplasmin (A2AP), Hyaluronan-binding protein 2 (HABP2), Haptoglobin (HPT) and C4b-binding protein beta chain (C4BPB)] and proteins functioning in the complement cascade [Complement C1r subcomponent (C1R), Complement C1s subcomponent (C1S), Complement C4-B (CO4B), Complement C5 (CO5), Vitronectin (VTNC), Hemopexin (HEMO) and C4BPB]. Search Tool for the Retrieval of Interacting Genes/Proteins (STRING) network (fig. S1) and Pathways Common analyses of the non-overlapping stable biomarkers identified blood coagulation, hemostasis regulation, negative wound healing, and regulation of the complement cascade for yr2 and hemostasis-related fibrin clot formation of a fibronectin (FINC)–centered network for yr6; extension of the yr2 network to include interactors also demonstrated the involvement of fibronectin. Notably, the fibronectin peptide selected as essential in this study resides in a C-terminal fibrin binding domain of fibronectin consistent with the pathway results. Consistent with these results, the top canonical pathways identified by Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA network diagrams in fig. S2) of the stable biomarker sets included APR signaling, LXR/RXR activation [recently shown to be the most up-regulated pathway in early OA based on synovial fluid proteomics ( 22 )], FXR/RXR activation, the complement system, and clathrin-mediated endocytosis signaling. Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) receptor, LDL and LDL-cholesterol, were frequent connectors in the networks. Immunoglobulin G linked many nodes in the network, which may signify additional interactions with the immune system and consistent with the known association of OA with innate immunity. DISCUSSION Using elastic net regression, we identified a small and parsimonious number of “essential” proteomic biomarkers that could discriminate, as much as 8 years prior, incident RKOA (based on change in KL grade) from non-RKOA in a subset of middle-aged women of the Chingford cohort. All models (y2, y6, and y2/6-TIC) based solely on the proteomic biomarker sets yielded cross-validated AUCs ≥ 0.76 for predicting incident RKOA. As few as six essential predictors (y2 secondary analysis and y6; AUCs, 0.77) predicted incident RKOA; this suggested that a select few biomarkers can be optimal for identifying the risk of incident RKOA occurring anytime up to 8 years later. The secondary analysis yielded an AUC 0.77, thereby demonstrating the ability of y2 biomarkers to also predict incident RKOA in the relatively distant future (i.e., RKOA incidence 3 or more years after the biomarker measures). Although it has been noted that the predictive value of risk factors often increases when the time to the outcome is smaller ( 23 ), in this study, the strength of prediction based on y2 and y6 essential biomarkers was equivalent, suggesting that it may be possible to predict incident RKOA even earlier than observed here. Individually, clinical characteristics (age, BMI, WOMAC pain on walking, and RHOA) were less predictive (AUCs, 0.51 to 0.59) than peptide biomarkers of incident y10 RKOA. Our study is relatively unique for selection of a cohort without enrichment for specific clinical or other risk factors, such as contralateral knee OA ( 24 ), self-reported knee injury, prior knee surgery, contralateral total knee replacement, baseline knee symptoms, or Heberden’s nodes ( 25 ), as performed in other studies to increase the likelihood of incident RKOA. Two peptides, COMP (652–665) and CRAC1 (101–108) , were selected in all stable and essential biomarker sets; ZPI (438–444) was also selected in all stable biomarker sets. These peptides alone, measured in yr2, significantly discriminated incident RKOA and were statistically indistinguishable from the full yr2 essential set predictors. COMP is a well-known OA-related biomarker indicating cartilage degeneration ( 26 ), OA disease status, incidence, and progression ( 15 , 27 – 30 ). COMP fragments released from the cartilage extracellular matrix function as disease-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) that activate joint inflammation ( 31 ). Our current results confirm that COMP is useful for identifying incident RKOA and agree with several prior studies that similarly identified COMP as a predictor of incident RKOA based on KL grade including the Chingford cohort ( 15 ), a meta-analysis of four cohorts ( 16 ), and the Johnston County Osteoarthritis Project ( 14 ), in contrast to one prior study in which it was reported to be a negative predictor of incident RKOA ( 18 ). CRAC1 functions as a cartilage degradation marker; it is a cartilage extracellular matrix protein, enriched in the upper layer of articular cartilage ( 32 ), that can distinguish chondrocytes from osteoblast-like cells and mesenchymal stem cells in culture ( 33 , 34 ), predict severity and progression of RKOA ( 35 ), and risk of joint replacement ( 36 ). ZPI, protein Z-dependent protease inhibitor, is an anticoagulant protein of the serpin family that can rapidly inhibit membrane-associated coagulation factors Xa and Xia ( 37 ). It is intriguing to note that despite the absence of overt injury in this cohort, nearly all of the stable set biomarkers predicting incident RKOA directly implicate an unresolved, perpetual APR ( 38 ), that leads to poor tissue repair and that has been identified previously as playing a pathogenic role in rheumatoid arthritis. The APR is made up of interacting and co-evolved processes ( 39 ) now known to be intimately involved in innate immunity, including hemostasis involving coagulation fibrin clot formation and fibrinolysis and the complement cascade. In addition, several of the biomarkers are known DAMPs that directly activate innate immunity, including fragments of fibronectin, PRG4 (lubricin), and COMP ( 31 ). COMP, fibronectin, and collagen II exposed on the articular cartilage surface bind lubricin (PRG4) responsible for boundary lubrication of the joint surfaces ( 40 ). Thus, in addition to activation of inflammatory pathways by fibronectin and COMP, the appearance of components of important extracellular matrix components—fibronectin, COMP, and PRG4—by y6 as essential and/or stable biomarkers, may signify the evolution to the loss of essential boundary lubrication of articular cartilage. To our knowledge, no literature yet reported three of the essential peptides identified in this study [CRAC1 (101–108) , LYAM1 (71–78) , and ZPI (438–444) ], as predictors of incident RKOA, although we previously identified CRAC1 and ZPI as predictors of progression of established radiographic OA ( 19 ). LYAM1 ( l -selectin) is a major regulator of leukocyte adhesion, migration, and signaling ( 41 ); serum concentrations are increased in patients with rheumatoid arthritis ( 42 ). Although some of the proteins corresponding to this APR-related response were not found to be expressed in the joint tissue transcriptome data, including A2AP ( SERPINF2 ), HABP2 ( HABP2 ), HEMO ( HPX ), HRG ( HRG ), PLF4 ( PF4 ), and ZPI ( SERPINA10 ), they can all be found in synovial fluid because it is a dialysate of plasma that contains bioactive molecules from the circulation, in addition to those from joint tissues, that can mediate the OA process. Several of these APR-related biomarkers demonstrated previously to be in arthritic synovial fluid include HABP2 ( 43 ), HRG (early OA marker) ( 22 ), and ZPI ( 44 ), all positively associated with incident RKOA in our study, and PLF4 ( 45 ), negatively associated with incident RKOA in our study. The ability of serum proteomic biomarkers to discriminate incident RKOA 4 to 8 years later in the Chingford cohort (AUCs, 0.76 to 0.77) was unexpectedly greater than discrimination of incident RKOA up to 1 to 5 years later achieved by magnetic resonance imaging features in other cohorts (AUC ranges of 0.59 to 0.68) ( 46 – 48 ). This could denote a molecular phase of OA that is best predicted by molecular measures. The first study to evaluate prediction of incident RKOA by different risk factors (clinical, genetic, and biochemical) identified baseline KL grade alone (AUCs, 0.70 to 0.84) as a predictor in two cohorts ( 23 ); the biochemical marker urinary CTXII alone was less predictive (AUCs, 0.60 to 0.61). Our results using proteomic predictors alone also compare favorably with results from a larger subsample (649 women) of the Chingford 1000 women cohort that achieved AUC 0.80 with baseline knee pain, KL grade, hip alpha angle, spine bone mineral density, and the presence of contralateral knee OA. A prior proteomic study of the OAI cohort (AUC 0.79 in replication cohort) ( 25 ) yielded panels of peptides that alone could well discriminate incident RKOA. However, the cohort was enriched for incident OA risk factors; their clinical model (age, BMI, previous knee injury, and WOMAC pain) was also highly predictive (AUC, 0.75) of incident RKOA ( 25 ). Our cohort was not selected for incident RKOA risk factors, and cases and controls were matched for age and BMI. In our study, individual clinical variables yielded AUCs 0.51 to 0.59 in contrast to the OAI study in which the clinical model (baseline age, BMI, WOMAC pain, and RHOA) yielded AUC 0.68. In our study, the combination of essential biomarkers with clinical variables boosted the y2 and y2/6-TIC biomarker AUCs to 0.83. The imbalance due to lack of matching on WOMAC pain and RHOA status at baseline likely contributed to this result and suggest a potential benefit of matching on these additional variables in future incident RKOA studies and perhaps further accounting for OA status at other joint sites not accounted for in these analyses. In contrast to the OAI cohort in which ITIH1 alone was predictive of incident RKOA ( 25 ), in our study, five essential peptides, three positive [HABP2 (186–203) , HRG (140–153) , and ZPI (438–444) ] and two negative [C1S (516–523) and FBLN3 (363–376) ] predictors were statistically significant in multivariable models of incident RKOA. Moreover, as described above, statistically significant discrimination of incident RKOA could be achieved with as few as two to three yr2 predictors, COMP (652–665) , CRAC1 (101–108) , and/or ZPI (438–444) . It remains to be determined whether hip OA incidence and/or progression could be predicted by this panel of biomarkers. The limited literature on the subject of inflammation of hip OA would suggest that the inflammasome, a key component of innate immunity, plays a role ( 49 ). Thus, we would speculate that there may be a generalized preradiographic OA dysregulated innate immunological state leading to an unresolved APR contributing to development of incident hip as well as knee OA. Our candidate biomarkers were derived from a panel developed to identify prognostic markers of clinically relevant knee OA (radiographic and pain) progression ( 19 ). Although risk factors for incident OA have been postulated to differ from those for OA progression ( 50 ), our results show that there is considerable overlap, based on proteomic biomarkers, in their molecular pathophysiology. By comparing the stable sets of both analyses at the protein level, we found that among all 24 protein biomarkers predicting incident RKOA, 14 (ACTG, APOE, C1R , C1S , CO4B , CO5 , CRAC1, HABP2 , HEMO , HRG , PLF4 , PRG4, RET4 , and ZPI ) of them were also prognostic for RKOA progression with those in bold representing an APR ( 19 ), which means that more than half (58%) of the biomarkers predicting incident RKOA overlapped with biomarkers predicting RKOA progression and a majority of these reflect a dysregulated innate immune response and unresolved APR. Moreover, 10 of these ( C1R , C1S , CO4B , CRAC1, HABP2 , HEMO , HRG , PLF4 , RET4 , and ZPI ), corresponding to 42%, overlapped with biomarkers that predicted clinically relevant OA (pain and radiographic) progression ( 19 ), again with nearly all reflecting dysregulated innate immunity with an unresolved APR. As Felson ( 51 ) has aptly pointed out, the distinction between incidence and progression is less clear-cut than is optimal, therefore defining these stages is challenging. These results are important as they suggest overlap in biomarker predictors, and therefore considerable similarity in the molecular pathophysiology of the progression to incident RKOA and the progression of established RKOA. The ability of both y2 and y6 biomarkers to predict incident RKOA at y10 supports the existence of a prolonged molecular derangement of joint tissue before the onset of radiographic abnormality. This supports the OA iceberg concept ( 8 ) that an OA disease process begins long before a radiographic change and may even include an asymptomatic serological stage reflected only in molecular derangement discernible by tissue components released into the joint fluid or systemic circulation ( 6 ). Although we cannot conclude that the OA process is precisely the same throughout this time period (from preradiographic through to radiographic progression), we can confidently say that there are many similarities based on shared predictors (58%). The earliest OA continuum biomarkers (i.e., predicting incident RKOA more than 3 years in the future) may be APOE, CRAC1, CO5, HABP2, and HRG as they appeared in the y2 original and the y2 secondary analysis (excluding y5 RKOA cases). Further support for this is provided by a recent proteomic study showing that HRG was the strongest up-regulated synovial fluid protein comparing early OA (meniscal injury) to control ( 22 ). There were several strengths of this study. Compared to traditional statistical approaches, such as univariate analysis followed by a multivariable logistic regression, machine learning approaches (elastic net is one) have greater predictive power because they are focused on making predictions as accurate as possible although this predictive power may come at the expense of other factors (overfitting, as one limitation) that can affect accuracy, while traditional statistical models are aimed at inferring relationships between variables ( 52 ). Moreover, elastic net is a method of choice for this study for several reasons: We could compare to prior results using the same method in an OA cohort ( 19 ), it deals with highly correlated variables, and it is appropriate for our situation wherein the number of predictors is larger or close to the sample size of the smallest class. The backward elimination process is also prediction oriented, aiming for fewer variables and the least AUC loss. Therefore, regarding prediction accuracy, it performs better than other predictive performance procedures such as conventional stepwise backward elimination based on AIC or P values or choosing unrelated biomarkers based on correlation coefficients. Since the starting point for backward selection was the stable sets, already a small set from feature selection, the computational cost was low. In addition, the biomarker panel evaluated in this study is well established as it has already been validated, in three independent cohorts, to contain biomarkers that predict progression of established RKOA ( 19 , 32 ). Last, knee radiographic structural change and not symptoms was used as the outcome variable; so these biomarkers may have utility for selecting study participants for early OA clinical trials designed to develop and test disease-modifying OA drugs capable of benefiting joint structure. There were several limitations of this study. First, the study was only conducted in women, however, none of the 17 essential predictors of incident OA in the Chingford cohort were significantly associated with sex in the FNIH cohort ( 19 ); moreover, none of these essential biomarkers were identified as sex-associated based on plasma proteomic analysis of 99 healthy adults that included 51 women ( 53 ). Second, sample size was relatively small. Therefore, we did not follow the regular training-testing procedure for AUC calculations as the separation would have further reduced the sample size. Instead, we used full samples to do the selection and fit the selected biomarkers to the same dataset, which will introduce bias and inflate the AUC. To adjust for this, we performed bootstrapping and deducted the bias of the bootstrapping estimate from the original AUC to yield a bias-corrected estimate. The sample size could also affect the biomarkers selected for the essential set. Third, there is no external validation for this study due to the difficulty of finding a similar longitudinal cohort with enough years of follow-up. Fourth, although imputation may have affected the peptide selection process, the overall missing rate was low (75% of the peptides had a missing rate less than 0.1% and half of the peptides had a missing rate of 0%); there were no missing data for y2 or y6 selected peptides and only three missing values for y2/6-TIC [related to FBLN3 (363–376) ] . Last, although the combination of individual clinical factors (age, BMI, WOMAC pain, and RHOA) yielded AUC 0.68, added to y2 molecular biomarkers the predictivity was boosted (to AUC 0.83); their combined contribution might prove significant in a larger sample. Further validation will be needed in independent and larger cohorts, preferably prospectively collected and including male participants and the combination of incident radiographic and symptomatic OA. In summary, the present study identifies a small number of serum peptides that parsimoniously predicts incident RKOA up to 8 years before the appearance of radiographic abnormality. This is important as it provides more evidence for a preradiographic phase of disease. Our results also provide valuable information for understanding the molecular events of early disease that could inform strategies to develop disease-modifying drugs for preclinical OA. The value of our study is a panel that, in the absence of clinical factors indicative of high risk of knee OA, has the potential to discriminate individuals at risk of incident RKOA from those not at risk. MATERIALS AND METHODS Study design The Chingford study is a population-based longitudinal cohort of 1003 women from a general practice in Chingford, North-East London, UK ( 54 , 55 ). Since 1989, participants were assessed annually for osteoporosis and OA. A subset of women was identified for this case-control cohort based on available sera from y2 and y6 for biomarker analyses; they were matched on baseline age within 2 years and BMI within 3 kg/m 2 ( Fig. 1 ). There were two main outcome groups—incident case ( n = 100) and non-incident control ( n = 100) groups. All participants had KL grade ≤ 1 radiographic scores at baseline of both knees. Participants had no history at baseline of rheumatoid arthritis, gout, or previous joint injury (answered “no” to “Have you ever injured your knees enough to rest for a week?”). Incident RKOA was defined as KL grade &gt; 1 of either knee over the 10-year follow-up. Hip radiographic data were available for y2. Of the 100 incident RKOA cases, 16 had RHOA of either side (KL grade ≥ 2), and 2 had missing data; controls had no RHOA of either hip. Participants were assessed at baseline for knee pain based on WOMAC question A1, “How much pain have you had when walking on a flat surface?” All participants provided informed written consent. The study was approved by the Research Ethics Committee of the UK National Research Ethics Service Committee South Central Oxford A (reference numbers LREC R&amp;WF96 and REC84131 ). Sample preparation for LC-MRM analysis Nondepleted serum samples from y2 and y6 were analyzed blindly and in random order for the proteomic panel using a liquid chromatography–multiple reaction monitoring (LC-MRM) methodology that we previously described ( 19 ). Briefly, samples were processed in randomized groups of 60 samples, protein was quantified by a Bradford assay in duplicate, and three types of QC samples were prepared to provide a global reference standard, to assess differences in digestion efficiencies between sets, and to quantify intra/interplate variation of the targeted analytes. For sample digestions, 5 μl of each sample was diluted 1:40 with a 5.13% deoxycholate (DOC) surfactant such that the starting protein concentration was 1.25 μg/μl. Samples were reduced with 10 mM dithiolthreitol at 80°C for 20 min and then alkylated with 20 mM iodoacetamide at room temperature for 40 min. Sequencing grade trypsin was added (1:10 enzyme to protein ratio to each sample) for 2 hours at 37°C. Samples were acidified with 1% trifluoroacetic acid and centrifuged to remove residual DOC before LC-MRM analysis. An exogenous stable isotope-labeled (SIL) peptide mixture (10 fmol) of 165 C13/N15-labeled peptides was added corresponding to 104 endogenous human proteins. Targeted LC-MRM analysis Quantitative LC-MRM was performed, blinded to the clinical data, on 1 μg of protein digest spiked with SIL peptides corresponding to 165 peptides (104 proteins) using a nanoAcquity Ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC) system (Waters Corp) coupled to a Waters Xevo TQ-XS triple quadrupole mass spectrometer via a nanoelectrospray ionization source. Briefly, the sample was first trapped on a Symmetry C18 300 mm by 180 mm trapping column [5 μl/min at 99.9/0.1 (v/v) water/acetonitrile], after which the analytical separation was performed with a 1.8-μm Acquity HSS T3 C18 75 μm by 150 mm column (Waters Corp) using a 55-min gradient of 5 to 40% acetonitrile with 0.1% formic acid at a flow rate of 400 nl/min and column temperature of 55°C. Data collection on the Xevo TQ-XS mass spectrometer was performed in a targeted mode following a method created in Skyline (MacCoss Laboratory, University of Washington). Data collected using Skyline were expressed as a ratio of peak areas of the light endogenous signal divided by the heavy SIL peptide. QC procedures Several QC sample types were processed. (i) To assess differences in digestion efficiencies between sets, a digestion QC standard was created by pooling 50 μl from the first set of 60 samples, which was then mixed and sub-aliquoted (at the intact protein level). All digestion QC standards were then frozen. Three random aliquots were thawed and digested with each sample block to assess differences in digestion efficiency between sets 1 and 10. The average intraplate digestion QC across 131 peptides was 10.1%; the average interplate digestion QC across 131 peptides was 22.9%. (ii) To assess intra/interplate variation of the targeted analytes, a sample pool QC (SPQC) sample was created from the initial pooled sample (used for digestion controls), digested using the same protocol as the patient samples, and then sub-aliquoted and frozen at −80°C. An aliquot was removed for each of the 10 plates and run periodically throughout the acquisition window with the same liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry method as the rest of the study. The average intraplate SPQC across 131 peptides was 9.9%; the average interplate SPQC across 131 peptides was 15.5%. Samples were reanalyzed when interplate digestion variation was &gt;30% and/or interplate SPQC sample variation was &gt;20%. Data cleaning/QC The quality of each MRM assay was determined by the dot product of the transition ratios in the heavy channel compared to the transition ratios in the light channel; the dot product is a strong indicator of interference in either channel and can identify potential targeted peptides below the limit of quantification. A total of 115 peptides with dot products ≥ 0.90 were retained for statistical analysis. After filtering by dot product, seven samples were excluded: four from y2 and three from y6; five of these were from the case group and two were from the control group; this resulted in a final analysis of 196, 197, and 196 samples for y2, y6, and y2/y6-TIC, respectively. Samples were excluded on the basis of:a peptide missing rate greater than 15%, insufficient amount of starting material, outliers in mean ratio of all peptides for this sample, and outliers detected by the first two principal components of a scatterplot. We also manually verified and/or adjusted peak retention times and integrations within Skyline. Any signals not of sufficient quality based on peak shape, interference, or low signal-to-noise ratio were deemed unreliable and values set to missing. Missing data imputation There were no missing not at random (i.e., below limit of quantification) values; there were missing at random values verified by manually checking the Skyline data. The statistical analysis requires complete data; therefore, for retained peptides (&lt;15% missing at random values), missing values were imputed by random forest ( 56 ) using the missRanger ( 57 ) R package. This nonparametric method of imputation is superior to other common imputation approaches for mass spectrometry–based missing at random values ( 58 ). To account for variability introduced by imputation, imputation was repeated 10 times to generate 10 complete datasets; Rubin’s rule was used for pooling the 10 datasets ( 59 ). Because of the wide range of raw peptide values, imputed data were scaled to z -scores for further analysis. Statistical analysis Study design The statistical analysis focused on testing each individual peptide represented by the ratio of the endogenous to exogenous SIL peptide; protein level summation of peptides was not performed. Using incident RKOA status of one knee at y10 as the outcome, we analyzed three sets of predictors: biomarkers measured at y2, y6, and TICs of biomarkers over y2/6-TIC derived from the AUC of biomarker measures across the specific time interval. After data cleaning, a total of 115 peptides were retained for statistical analysis in a final 196, 197, and 196 samples for y2, y6, and y2/y6-TIC, respectively. Peptides with missing rate &lt; 15% were imputed by random forest, and 10 imputed complete datasets were generated. Selection of biomarker set We used the same variable selection strategy that we implemented in Zhou et al. ( 19 ) ( Fig. 2 ). Considering the number of samples (~200) and peptides (115) retained for statistical analysis and correlation between peptides, we performed bootstrapped elastic net logistic regression ( 60 ) for variable selection, as we previously implemented ( 19 ). Briefly, the standard elastic net modeling is to optimize the two tuning parameters, α and λ, which was done by 10-fold cross-validation for a given α across a grid search of α from 0.1 to 0.9 with an increment of 0.1. Our elastic net approach incorporated a bootstrapping strategy; that is, we repeated the elastic net modeling of each α for 100 sets of bootstrap samples and computed the average misclassification rate. Optimal α was determined on the basis of the smallest average misclassification rate across different α values. Next, the optimal α and λ were used to perform elastic net variable selection in 100 sets of bootstrap samples, and the frequency of a peptide being selected (selection frequency) was computed. We ranked peptides based on their selection frequency and chose up to 16 top peptides. The decision to use 16 as the maximum number of peptides was based on the consideration of the average maximum number of variables permissible in the three models using a 5:1 rule [ratio of min(events, non-events) to number of candidate parameters in the model] ( 61 ). For instance, as y6 had only 50 case participants without prevalent RKOA, the maximum number of variables chosen for backward selection for this model was 10; after filtering out any peptides in the same protein, nine peptides remained in the model. This procedure resulted in a final 14, 9, and 14 peptides subjected to backward elimination for y2, y6, and y2/y6-TIC models with one peptide per protein within each model. Across models, this resulted in two different peptide representatives for ApoB and HRG, while the remaining proteins had only one peptide representative. We denoted these selected peptides as the “stable biomarker” set; they totaled 26 peptides (24 proteins). However, the stable biomarkers could contain correlated peptides with interference in models due to collinearity. Therefore, using our previously published protocol ( 19 ), we used a backward elimination procedure as previously described ( 19 ) to further reduce the number of peptides from the stable sets. Here, we defined “AUC bias” based on the difference between areas under receiver operating characteristic curves (AUC) from the original samples and the average AUC from 1000 bootstrap samples. The initial AUC bias was based on all stable peptides in the model. A single stable peptide that contributed the least to AUC was eliminated subsequently in each backward selection step with AUC bias calculated for each step. The final model chosen was based on a rapid drop in AUC bias after peptide removal. The peptides left in the final pool were denoted the essential set of biomarkers consisting of 11, 6, and 9 biomarkers for y2, y6, and y2/6-TIC, respectively. Risk models Three multivariable logistic regressions were fit: (i) essential biomarker sets only; (ii) essential biomarker sets with demographic variables (age and BMI); and (iii) essential biomarker sets with all clinical variables (age, BMI, WOMAC A1 pain, and RHOA). We assessed the models’ predictive performance using AUCs ( 62 , 63 ); AUC ranges between 0.5 and 1, with higher values indicating better discrimination between individuals who are high risk and low risk. A total of 1000 bootstrap samples were used to obtain bias-corrected estimates and the confidence interval of the model AUC as well as model coefficients. The model fitting was applied independently to each imputed dataset with results combined by Rubin’s rule ( 59 ). Because of development of cases of RKOA by y5, we also performed a secondary analysis excluding RKOA cases at y5 to test the predictivity of biomarkers in y2 for RKOA development in years 6 to 10; for this analysis, the biomarkers were selected using the same variable selection method. To address the group imbalance in clinical variables (WOMAC A1, baseline knee KL grades, and y2 RHOA), we performed four sensitivity analyses testing the predictivity of y2 biomarkers for (i) RKOA in y5, (ii) RKOA in y10 excluding those with baseline WOMAC pain on walking or missing WOMAC data, (iii) RKOA in y10 excluding those with baseline RKOA (KL = 1) of either knee, (iv) RKOA in y10 excluding those with y2 RHOA (KL ≥ 2). Pathway analysis and network visualization We explored protein networks and canonical pathways associated with the selected stable protein sets using STRING version 12.0, Pathway Commons (2019 update), and QIAGEN’s IPA (IPA, QIAGEN Redwood City, www.qiagen.com/ingenuity ).
