For commercial re-use, please contact journals.permissions@oup.com Abstract Background While intergenerational caregiving is increasingly prevalent among older individuals, longitudinal evidence on its associations with multidimensional health outcomes remains limited. Purpose This study examined the associations between childcare engagement in older adults and various health and behavioral outcomes at follow-up. Methods We analyzed data from a cohort of &gt;12 000 (range: 12 124–12 896) community-dwelling adults aged 70+ years, categorizing childcare engagement as never , &lt;weekly , and ≥ weekly . Using an outcome-wide approach, we assessed 42 outcomes across physical, cognitive/major health events, psychological, social, and behavioral domains. Follow-up assessments occurred at ∼2 years for most outcomes, with extended follow-up (median 6–9 years) for time-to-event outcomes. We performed gender-disaggregated regressions, adjusting for multiple covariates. Results Participants were aged 70–95 years (mean: 75.2 ± 4.3) at baseline, and 54.5% were women. Childcare engagement was more common among women (46% vs. 40%). Key findings included: (1) social domain : both genders showed increased social contacts and community participation, with women additionally demonstrating reduced social isolation; (2) mortality : lower mortality was observed in men with a dose-response pattern, while only &lt;weekly childminding was associated with lower mortality in women; (3) physical domain : men showed increased moderate-to-vigorous physical activity while women had higher pain reports and slower gait speed; and (4) null associations : most psychological outcomes, as well as certain physical and health events, showed no significant relationships. Conclusion Childcare engagement during older adulthood was linked to not only selected health outcomes, including notable social benefits and lower mortality, but also some physical trade-offs in women. These findings support considering intergenerational engagement in healthy ageing initiatives. Introduction The global ageing population is increasing rapidly, with projections indicating that by 2050, one in 6 people worldwide will be over age 65. 1 This demographic shift challenges healthcare systems to address the complex physical, psychological, and social needs of older individuals. In response, the World Health Organization (WHO) reframed ageing as a dynamic opportunity through its 2015 “healthy ageing” framework, advocating for interventions that optimize functional ability and wellbeing across the life course. 2 Achieving healthy ageing requires extending health and wellbeing support beyond primary care and hospital settings to incorporate community-based interventions that actively engage older adults. 3 Such strategies are particularly advantageous as they minimize the risks associated with hospitalizations while supporting wellbeing in nonclinical environments. 4–8 The Activity Theory of Ageing provides a theoretical foundation for such interventions, proposing that continued participation in meaningful activities preserves identity, purpose, and functional capacity in later life. 9 In this context, childcare engagement (eg, babysitting, mentoring, or caring for grandchildren) has emerged as a promising activity, with the potential to create opportunities for social interaction, cognitive stimulation, and physical activity. Unlike spousal or partner caregiving, which can sometimes lead to stress, loss of autonomy, or unhealthy lifestyle behaviors, 10 childcare roles are typically voluntary, reciprocal, and embedded in intergenerational relationships. Growing evidence suggests that grandparenting (or childcare engagement during older adulthood) is generally associated with positive health outcomes. 11 , 12 However, the literature remains fragmented, with most studies focused on a narrow range of health outcomes, leaving gaps in understanding its broader impact. A systematic review of studies published up to 2019 found that most research centered on psychological health, with some addressing self-rated health and cognitive function. 12 Furthermore, gender-specific findings are limited, despite differences in caregiving patterns and health trajectories between older men and women. 12–14 To further explain, older women are more likely to take on intensive, daily childcare roles due to societal norms, while men’s involvement is often sporadic and discretionary. 15 These gendered dynamics may lead to different health outcomes. For instance, women’s childcare engagement may not only strengthen social connections but also increase physical strain, whereas men’s selective engagement may offer cognitive benefits with fewer physical risks. Yet, few studies have tested these hypotheses. To address these gaps, this study uses an outcome-wide epidemiological approach to investigate longitudinal associations between childcare engagement and various health and behavioral outcomes in a cohort of relatively healthy, community-dwelling Australians aged 70+ years. Analyses are stratified by gender to explore potential differences in these associations. The purpose of this research is: (1) to identify both the benefits and potential risks of childcare engagement in older adults, and (2) to inform community programs that use intergenerational engagement as an innovative strategy to promote healthy ageing through a gender-sensitive approach. Methods Participants This study used data from the ASPirin in Reducing Events in the Elderly (ASPREE) trial, 16 its substudy the ASPREE Longitudinal Study of Older Persons (ALSOP), 17 and the post-trial follow-up ASPREE-eXTension (ASPREE-XT) observational cohort study. 18 ASPREE was a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial that examined the effects of daily 100 mg aspirin on disability-free survival. Between March 2010 and December 2014 ( t −2 ), the trial enrolled 19 114 participants from Australia (87%) and the United States (13%), with participants in Australia recruited mainly through primary healthcare providers. Participants were generally healthy individuals aged 70+ years (or 65+ years for African American or Hispanic participants in the United States), without major cognitive impairment, dementia, cardiovascular diseases, or independence-limiting physical disability upon enrolment (see Table S1 for eligibility criteria). At the conclusion of the ASPREE intervention phase (median 4.7 years), participants were invited to join the ongoing ASPREE-XT study, continuing the annual in-person follow-up visits, telephone contact, and/or medical record reviews. 18 The ALSOP substudy invited only Australian participants from the ASPREE trial. Data collection began with Wave 1 medical questionnaires ( t −1 : 2012-2014), followed by Wave 1 social questionnaires ( t 0 : 2012-2014), which were typically administered within the first year of enrolment into ASPREE. Approximately 2 years later, Wave 2 medical and social questionnaires were administered ( t 1 : 2013-2017). These questionnaires collected data on a broad range of self-reported general medical, lifestyle, behavioral, and psychosocial factors. For the present study, we analysed data from the most recent ASPREE-XT04 dataset, which includes post-trial outcomes collected between 2018 and 2022 (i.e., up to the fourth annual visit of ASPREE-XT; t 2 ) ( Figure 1A ). Measures Childcare engagement The frequency of childcare engagement was assessed at ALSOP Wave 1 with the question, “During the past year, have you undertaken babysitting or childminding?” Participants chose from 4 response options: never , occasionally (&lt;once a week) , often (≥once a week) , and every/most days . For analysis, the often and every/most days groups were combined due to small sample size in the latter group. Outcomes A total of 42 health and behavioral outcomes were assessed, comprising 36 outcomes at t 1 (median follow-up: 2.0-2.4 years) and 6 time-to-event outcomes at t 2 (median follow-up: &gt;7 years for most) (see Figure 1 footnotes for details). These outcomes were grouped into 5 domains based on a prior review. 19 Physical health : poor vision, poor hearing, poor oral health, pain, falls, grip strength, gait speed, body mass index (BMI), waist circumference, systolic and diastolic blood pressure (SBP and DBP), and physical component scores of health-related quality of life (HRQoL). Cognitive and major health events : modified mini-mental state (3MS) test scores, cognitive impairment, dementia, cardiovascular diseases, disability, and all-cause mortality. Psychological wellbeing : life satisfaction, happiness, hopefulness, depression, depressive symptoms, and mental component scores of HRQoL. Social wellbeing and altruistic behaviors : friends see or hear from, friends to talk about private matters, friends to call for help, loneliness, social isolation, low social support, social entertainment, community group participation, volunteering, and skill-sharing. Health behaviors : fruit and vegetable intake, snack and convenience food intake, moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA), strength exercise, smoking, excessive drinking, sufficient sleep duration, and flu vaccination. Outcome measures were derived from self-reports, performance-based assessments, and adjudicated clinical data. Most physical health, psychological wellbeing, social wellbeing, and health behavior variables were self-reported. Objective measures (eg, grip strength, gait speed, BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, and 3MS scores) were collected by trained assessors using standardized protocols. Major health events (eg, dementia, cardiovascular disease, disability, and mortality) were identified through structured interviews and clinical record review, with adjudication by study clinicians where applicable. Further details on the measurement of each outcome are available in Table S2 . Covariates Covariates included: (1) sociodemographics: age, race, education, socioeconomic indexes for areas, urban/regional residence, current marital status, number of children participants have, and household gross annual income; (2) intervention arm assignment; (3) health behaviors: smoking, alcohol consumption, and physical activity; (4) social relationships: number of friends seen or heard from, number of friends to talk about private matters, and number of friends to call for help; and (5) preexisting health conditions: hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, depressive symptoms, and frailty. Covariates were measured at prebaseline ( t −2 or t −1 ), except for social relationships, which were assessed at baseline ( t 0 ). Statistical analysis Using an outcome-wide analytic approach, 20 , 21 which assesses the influence of a single exposure on multiple outcomes, we examined the prospective associations between frequency of childcare engagement in later life and various health and behavioral outcomes. The type of regression was determined by the outcomes: (1) generalized linear models with Poisson distribution using robust variance error estimation for binary outcomes 22 ; (2) linear regression for continuous outcomes; and (3) Cox proportional hazards regression for time-to-event outcomes. Continuous outcomes were standardized (mean = 0, SD = 1) to enhance comparability across different scales, with the resulting β coefficients interpreted as the effect of a 1-SD change on the outcome. In Cox regressions, participants were censored at the time of death, withdrawal/loss to follow-up, or upon reaching the data cutoff date. All models were adjusted for the covariates described above, as well as prior values of the outcome variables (where available) to reduce potential reverse causation. Two sensitivity analyses were performed. First, we calculated E -values to evaluate the robustness of the observed associations to potential residual confounding. 23 The E -value represents the minimum strength of association on the risk ratio scale that an unmeasured confounder would need to have with both the exposure and outcome, above and beyond the adjusted covariates, to nullify the observed exposure-outcome associations. Second, to address concerns regarding potential collinearity/bias from controlling for prior outcomes, we repeated adjusted regression models excluding prior outcome values. To minimize selection bias and preserve statistical power, missing data (ranging from 0 to around 10%) were imputed, as the assumption of data being missing completely at random was unlikely. Imputation was performed using the missRanger algorithm, which combines chained random forests and predictive mean matching. 24 , 25 All statistical analyses were gender-disaggregated (men and women, as no other gender identities were recorded) and conducted using Stata v.17 and R v.4.2.0. Statistical significance was set at a 2-tailed P -value of &lt;.05, with results interpreted without applying corrections. However, P -values corrected for multiple comparisons using the Šidák method were also reported for researchers from various disciplines. Physical health . Childcare engagement was associated with a slightly lower risk of poor hearing and a reduction in SBP among men. For women, there was a reduced risk of falls and slower gait speed, increased physical component scores of HRQoL, and a higher risk of pain. Hearing, falls, pain, and HRQoL were self-reported; SBP and gait speed were measured by trained assessors. Cognitive health and major health events. In men, engaging in childcare less than once a week was associated with a reduced risk of dementia (adjudicated), but no association was observed for at least once a week. Additionally, a dose-response relationship was observed between frequency of childcare and lower all-cause mortality (adjudicated) in men. In women, it was similarly associated with lower all-cause mortality, albeit without evidence of a dose-response relationship. Psychological wellbeing . In men, childcare engagement had minimal to no association on most psychological outcomes. In women, however, it was modestly associated with increased life satisfaction and happiness (both self-reported). Social wellbeing and altruistic behaviors. There were several beneficial associations on self-reported social wellbeing and altruistic behaviors in both genders. These included increased social relationships with friends, greater engagement in social entertainment, and higher participation in altruistic activities such as volunteering and skill-sharing. Notably, in women, childcare engagement was associated with a reduced risk of social isolation. Health behaviors. Childcare engagement influenced self-reported health behaviors in both men and women. A dose-response pattern was observed for increased intake of fruits and vegetables as well as snacks and convenience foods. Among men, childcare was further associated with higher MVPA and greater uptake of influenza vaccination. Overall, while many associations were small to modest in magnitude, the inverse association with mortality in men (HR = 0.63) and reduced social isolation in women (RR = 0.42) represented moderate to large effect sizes. Sensitivity analyses Evidence of robustness against unmeasured confounding, assessed using E -values, ranged from 1.21 for influenza vaccination to 4.26 for snack and convenience food consumption in men, and from 1.27 for pain to 4.15 for social isolation in women, focusing on significant associations observed in the primary analysis ( Table S5 ). To interpret this, for example, in the association between &lt;once-a-week childcaring and social isolation in women, an unmeasured confounder would need to be associated with both the exposure and the outcome by 4.15-fold each on the risk ratio scale, above and beyond the measured covariates, to fully explain away the observed association, and by 1.75-fold each to shift the lower limit of CI to include the null value. Re-evaluating the fully adjusted models after removing (pre)baseline outcomes showed that the associations remained mostly consistent, with slightly larger effect estimates observed for certain outcomes ( Table S6 ). Discussion Main findings This study provides evidence of the relationship between engagement in childcare activities and multiple health and behavioral outcomes over a follow-up period of ∼2 years (or up to median 8 years for time-to-event outcomes) in a cohort of older adults aged 70+ years. Five key findings were identified, with notable gender differences. First , engagement in childcare activities was associated with several positive social wellbeing outcomes 2 years later, in both men and women. These included increased contact and support from friends, engagement in social entertainment, and altruistic behaviors such as volunteering and skill-sharing within the community. Additionally, there was evidence of a lower risk of social isolation in women. Second , childcare engagement was associated with lower mortality in both genders over a median follow-up of 7.9 years, with men showing a dose-response relationship. Third, engagement in childcare activities influenced some health behaviors over the 2-year follow-up, especially in men. It was associated with increased physical activity and preventive health behaviors, such as receiving flu vaccination. Additionally, there was evidence of higher fruit and vegetable intake, as well as increased consumption of snacks and convenience foods in both genders. Fourth, most associations with physical health outcomes were not significant, with some exceptions. In men, modest beneficial associations were found for hearing and SBP after 2-year follow-up. In women, there were not only improvements in the physical component score of HRQoL but also an increased risk of pain and slower gait speed. Fifth, no associations were found with most psychological wellbeing outcomes. However, in women, a small increase in life satisfaction and happiness was observed at the end of 2-year follow-up. Interpretation Prevalence Our discussion was informed by literature on general childcare activities or grandparenting, although our study could not distinguish whether participants were caring for their grandchildren or providing nonkin childcare. Grandparenting prevalence varies widely, ranging from 24% to 60% across European nations. 26 In Australia, a nationally representative sample reported a prevalence of 51%, 27 while our sample had a slightly lower proportion (40%-46%). This difference may reflect variations in sample characteristics, regional differences, or temporal changes in caregiving patterns. However, the trend of older women providing childcare more frequently than men remains consistent, with a stable gender difference of 6%-7%. 27 Social wellbeing Engaging in childcare activities at later life is recognized as both social engagement and altruistic behavior, 13 , 28 likely explaining the observed benefits for social wellbeing. Social engagement and prosocial behaviors often reinforce each other, with one altruistic act encouraging further similar behaviors. 29 However, findings are not always consistent. For example, European survey data suggest that regular grandchild care may reduce participation in other community activities, such as volunteering, particularly among women. 30 This may reflect cultural and structural differences, as well as variations in caregiving intensity, 31 which our study could not assess. In certain contexts, childcare demands may be so high that they limit opportunities for other forms of social engagement, highlighting the need to consider these factors when interpreting our findings. The evidence for reduced social isolation in women warrants attention. This finding aligns with cross-sectional analyses from Germany, 32 but we extend the literature by demonstrating a longitudinal association, indicating that these benefits persist over time. Moreover, the greater protective benefits against social isolation observed in women compared to men may stem from their traditionally central roles in family caregiving and social networking, which increase opportunities for meaningful interactions and support. 33 This observation can also be interpreted through the lens of role enhancement theory , 34 which suggests that occupying multiple roles, such as caregiver and community member, strengthens social networks, self-esteem, and overall wellbeing. Mortality We found lower mortality in both men and women who engaged in childcare activities, with a clear dose-response association observed in men. This pattern lends stronger support for a potential causal relationship, consistent with Bradford Hill’s criterion for biological gradient. 35 In contrast, the absence of a dose-response in women weakens the strength of causal inference. This could also suggest a threshold effect, where even occasional engagement provides benefit, or reflect differences in the nature of childcare involvement between women and men. Alternatively, it may be due to a selection effect, whereby women who are healthier or more functionally able are more likely to participate in childcare, even at low frequencies, rather than childcare itself conferring a direct health benefit. Despite this, the consistency of our findings with prior studies 36–38 continues to support the possibility of a protective association between childcare during older adulthood and survival, alongside biologically plausible mechanisms such as increased physical activity (discussed in the next section) and social engagement. Health behaviors The prevalence of self-reported MVPA in our cohort was 73.8% in men and 60.5% in women. These were higher than national estimates for Australians aged 65+, where 51.3% met physical activity guidelines in 2021. 39 Recent trends show increasing activity levels among older individuals, particularly those aged 75+ and men. 39 We also found that caregiving was associated with higher self-reported MVPA in men. This may help explain the gendered mortality pattern noted above, as physical activity is a well-established protective factor. Meta-analyses involving older adults have shown that, compared to least active individuals, higher MVPA was associated with a 22%-48% reduction in all-cause mortality, depending on the intensity and method of measurement. 40 , 41 Null association between caregiving and MVPA among women in our study may be due to differences in the nature or intensity of caregiving activities. Childcare engagement was also associated with dietary behaviors, although the relationship was mixed. While it was associated with higher fruit and vegetable intake, it was also linked to increased consumption of snacks and convenience foods in both genders. This suggests a trade-off between health-conscious eating and the practical demands of caregiving. These findings align with research among Greek adults aged 65+, where those with more grandchildren and greater weekly caregiving hours had poorer dietary habits. 42 Therefore, nutritional support for older adults providing care should focus on promoting health-conscious food choices while also addressing the conditions that may contribute to less optimal dietary behaviors. Physical health While the observed association is modest and warrants limited emphasis, engagement in childcare may benefit sensory and cardiovascular health. This is supported by its association with improved hearing and lower SBP in men. A prior study involving Chinese grandparents found that those providing part-time grandchild care had reduced odds of prehypertension among men and women, although these findings were not statistically significant. 43 However, the evidence remains mixed, as the same study reported that part-time dual caregiving (simultaneously caring for both grandchildren and great-grandparents) was associated with significantly increased odds of prehypertension in women. 43 The gender-specific association observed in our study may be partly explained by differences in physical activity. Men engaging in childcare also showed higher MVPA, which is known to lower blood pressure, 44 whereas no similar association was observed in women. Furthermore, the improved physical component score of HRQoL seen in women aligns with research conducted among older Korean and Vietnamese women (but not men), where those engaged in grandparenting reported better self-rated health. 45 , 46 Conversely, childcare engagement was associated with increased pain and slower gait speed in women but not men. This may result from women’s greater exposure to caregiving tasks (eg, lifting, prolonged standing) compounded by age-related physical decline. 47 In contrast, men’s childcare activities may involve less biomechanical strain due to intermittent engagement patterns and physiological advantages. 48 For instance, older men tend to preserve higher levels of skeletal muscle mass and strength, which are linked to better physical functioning and reduced risk of musculoskeletal strain. 48 , 49 Systematic reviews have shown that reduced muscle mass and strength are associated with increased risks of functional decline and disability in older adults. 50 , 51 Psychological wellbeing Overall, most psychological outcomes showed no associations, except for increased life satisfaction and happiness in women. This aligns with previous research in Vietnam and China, which found that grandparents (particularly grandmothers) reported greater life satisfaction and better self-rated health when engaged in childcare. 46 , 52 This association could be attributed to the sense of purpose and fulfillment that childminding provides. 13 Additionally, the reciprocal nature of grandparenting, where grandparents help care for grandchildren and, in return, receive financial or practical support, could indirectly enhance psychosocial wellbeing. 53 , 54 However, our study lacked data to investigate this mechanism further. The null findings for depressive symptoms, despite observed improvements in social engagement (eg, increased social contacts and community participation) and psychological wellbeing (eg, higher life satisfaction in women), deserve attention. While prior work associates grandparenting with reduced depression risk through enhanced social roles, 55 , 56 our cohort’s high baseline psychological wellbeing 57 may have created a ceiling effect, limiting the ability to detect any significant association with depressive symptoms. Dementia The association between childcare engagement and reduced dementia risk in men, but not women, may indicate gender differences in the cognitive effects of childminding and sociocultural roles. Notably, this reduced risk was found only in men providing care less than once per week. This could be that occasional engagement may give cognitive benefits through social interaction and purposeful activity without the chronic stress of regular childminding. 58 More frequent involvement (≥once weekly) could introduce sustained responsibilities that counteract these benefits. This association may also be attributable to the novel cognitive stimulation and sense of purpose it provides in later life, especially for older men with limited child-rearing experience, as noted in our prior work. 13 Besides, a Chinese cohort study also found that nonintensive caregivers (1-39 hours per week) had a 24% lower odds of developing dementia over 5 years compared to noncaregivers. 59 Although that study did not report gender-specific results, 59 the consistency of findings across cohorts suggests that occasional childcare may be associated with cognitive benefits in later life. Overall interpretation across domains While many associations were small to modest in magnitude, the overall direction was generally positive across multiple domains, including social wellbeing, health behaviors, and mortality. This consistency could indicate a cumulative benefit associated with childcare engagement in later life. The presence of relatively moderate to large effect sizes for some outcomes (reduced social isolation in women and lower mortality risk in men) also suggests potentially more meaningful effects. Our findings should also be interpreted alongside null associations observed across multiple domains, particularly physical, psychological, and major health events. Such null results could remain clinically meaningful. Implications This research further supports the WHO’s functioning-based healthy ageing framework, which emphasizes building and maintaining the ability to “be” and “do” the things people have reason to value in later life. More specifically, functional ability in older age is determined by the interaction between intrinsic capacity (across cognitive, psychological, sensory, locomotor, and vitality domains) and the environment in which they live. 2 , 60 The social environment is one important component for healthy ageing, with adverse social determinants such as loneliness, social isolation, and insufficient social support, being associated with poorer cognitive and cardiovascular health outcomes, among others. 61–64 The positive associations observed in this study, even with occasional childcare engagement, suggest that intergenerational caregiving could serve as an intervention strategy. Such interventions could take 2 forms: those involving grandparents and their own grandchildren, and those connecting older adults with nonkin children. Existing evidence demonstrates that structured intergenerational programs (eg, shared daycare centers, mentorship initiatives, and community gardening) provide mutual benefits for both older participants and children. 11 , 65 The Australian television series Old People’s Home for 4 Year Olds exemplify the cultural relevance and widespread appeal of structured intergenerational engagement. 66 Independent evaluations of these programs found that they improved public attitudes toward ageing, inspired new community initiatives, and catalyzed research and funding opportunities. 66 However, we noted an equity concern: childcare engagement was more common among socioeconomically advantaged individuals, even within this already-advantaged cohort. This suggests that without deliberate efforts to improve access, such as subsidized programs, transportation support, and targeted outreach, intergenerational initiatives may inadvertently widen health disparities. We therefore recommend that policymakers: (1) prioritize funding for community-based intergenerational programs in underserved areas, and (2) implement inclusive recruitment strategies to ensure equitable access. Additionally, these programs should be assessed for their potential usability within social prescribing framework, an emerging healthcare model in which healthcare workers prescribe social participation to community resources and monitor associated health outcomes. 67 Limitations and strengths There are several limitations. First, the lack of detailed information on childcare activities is a major limitation. We were unable to distinguish between: (1) residential versus nonresidential caregiving, (2) multigenerational versus skipped-generation households, (3) care for grandchildren versus nonkin children, or (4) paid versus unpaid caregiving. These distinctions might be important as prior research suggests that nonresidential grandparents experienced better mental health outcomes. 68 Second, information on the intensity and duration of caregiving, as well as distinctions between long-term and short-term caregiving was also unavailable. These factors may modify the observed associations and warrant further exploration. Third, a further analytical approach would be to examine participants who newly began caregiving between waves and compare them to those who remained noncaregivers. While valuable, such transition-based analyses across multiple outcomes were beyond the scope of this study but warrant future investigation. Fourth, although we controlled for prior values and multiple covariates, some bias may remain. Individuals who take on caregiving may already be healthier and more capable, so caregiving could reflect underlying wellbeing, contributing to the observed associations. However, E -values indicate that several findings are relatively robust against potential unmeasured confounding. Fifth, regarding family structure, while one might hypothesize that childless participants (7.6% of our sample) could differentially affect results, our models adjusted for number of children. Thus, sensitivity analyses excluding childless participants would have minimal impact given their small proportion, suggesting negligible impact on overall findings. Finally, the transition from a clinical trial to an observational study may have introduced a healthy cohort effect. One example of this is the higher-than-average MVPA levels reported by our participants. This could also be due to selection bias or over-reporting. Moreover, our study population predominantly consisted of White and socioeconomically advantaged older Australians. This skewed representation may limit the generalizability. However, the observed associations could plausibly be even stronger in more heterogeneous populations with greater variability in wellbeing and social needs. While the hypothesis has previously been examined, our study is among the first peer-reviewed investigations to comprehensively assess the relationship between childcare engagement and various outcomes within a large cohort of older adults. The simultaneous examination and reporting of multiple outcomes reduce publication bias. Standardized assessments conducted by trained staff adhering to rigorous protocols, along with adjudication of health events, minimize the likelihood of misclassification and information biases. Conclusion Our study provides evidence of the association between childcare engagement and various health and behavioral outcomes among community-dwelling adults aged 70+ years. Some significant associations suggested potential benefits, such as positive associations on social wellbeing and lower mortality risk. However, adverse associations were also observed, such as an increased risk of pain and slower gait speed in women. Nonetheless, these findings support the WHO’s healthy ageing framework by demonstrating how intergenerational engagement can improve functional ability through social-environmental interactions.
