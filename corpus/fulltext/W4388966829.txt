Red snow caused by blooms of microalgae darkens the surface of summer snowfields, increasing snowmelt. To assess the contribution of red snow to supraglacial snowmelt in northwestern North America, we systematically mapped the 2019–2022 distribution of blooms by applying supervised classification to 6158 satellite images. Blooms occurred on 5% of the total glaciated area, heavily affecting many glaciers in years of prolonged snow cover duration. Individual glaciers had up to 65% of their surface area affected by bloom in one melt season, which we estimate caused as much as 3 cm of snow meltwater equivalent averaged across the glacier surface. These results demonstrate appreciable snowmelt caused by red snow albedo over vast areas of North American glaciers. The rate of snowmelt is important because snowpack provides water supplies for one-sixth of Earth’s population ( 6 ), and the duration of snow cover affects ecosystems, regional climate, and planetary albedo ( 7 – 9 ). On some glaciers, mineral dust and black carbon deposited on the snow surface drive earlier loss of snow cover, resulting in extended exposure of bare ice and consequent loss of glacial mass ( 8 , 10 , 11 , 12 ). Glacier mass loss in northwestern North America has strongly accelerated in recent decades ( 13 – 15 ). While extensive red snow algal blooms have been reported on glaciers in this region ( 2 , 4 , 16 , 17 ), studies of red snow albedo and snowmelt to date have been restricted to isolated patches of snow algae ( 3 , 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 ) or individual snow and ice fields ( 1 , 4 , 5 , 17 , 22 ). To assess the large-scale impact of red snow on glaciers and icefields in northwestern North America, we applied a random forest classifier to Sentinel-2 satellite images ( Fig. 1 ) and built a spatiotemporal map of blooms. Although blooms occur on snowfields overlying tundra, rock, lake ice, and sea ice ( 23 , 24 ), we focused the present study on glaciers because they have relatively simple surfaces with minimal vegetation, have well-defined boundaries, and support vast blooms identifiable in medium-resolution satellite images ( 2 , 4 , 17 ). Only individual glaciers with surface area greater than 1 km 2 were included in the current study. To estimate the impact of snow algae on snowmelt, we applied an empirical model of snow-algae albedo ( 17 ) to the remotely sensed distribution. Fig. 1. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION Spatiotemporal distribution Supervised classification of Sentinel-2 images revealed that red snow occurred over an area of 4214 km 2 (95% confidence interval (CI): 3719 to 4604 km 2 ) at least once within the 4-year study period (2019–2022). We refer to this as the occurrence range. This is equivalent to 4.5% of total glaciated study area (defined as all glaciers greater than 1 km 2 in northwestern North America; table S1). Results are depicted in Fig. 2 and are viewable in full 10-m resolution alongside true-color Sentinel-2 images at https://caseyengstrom.users.earthengine.app/view/watermelon-snow . Fig. 2. Red snow percent cover and snowmelt derived from red snow albedo on North American glaciers. Red color scale indicates percentage of glaciated area affected by bloom; blue shading indicates estimated bloom-albedo snowmelt, in units of millimeters snow water equivalent ( SWE ) averaged across the glaciated study area. Circle size is proportional to glaciated area per 40-km grid cell. Color scales capped for clarity at 20% and 40 mm, respectively. See fig. S16 for uncapped version of this figure. Blooms were widespread on glaciers throughout northwestern North America, except in locations such as the northern Alaska Range, the Wrangell and St. Elias Mountains, and coastal side of the Pacific Coast Ranges ( Fig. 2 and fig. S1). In the Alaska Range, most red snow was located on the range’s south side, where deeper snow persists later into the summer ( 25 ). The sparsity of snow algae in the Wrangell and St. Elias Mountains was likely due to below-freezing average summer air temperatures (fig. S1), meaning meltwater was not available for snow algal growth. The notable concentration of snow algae on the inland side of the Pacific Coast Ranges (fig. S2) could be due to the climate contrast between interior and coast, such as lower cloud cover or higher summer air temperatures inland (fig. S1A) ( 26 ). The red snow distribution was skewed toward low-angle slopes (fig. S3), possibly due to slower drainage of liquid water at lower angles. Percent cover was similar across all aspects. Red snow extent was generally highest at median glacier elevations (fig. S3), where air temperatures are cold enough for snow cover to persist well into summer, yet warm enough to provide liquid meltwater. The presence of liquid meltwater in the snowpack is likely a key limiting factor for snow algal growth ( 4 , 16 , 27 ). Red snow occurred on more than half of the glaciers (4552 of 8700; table S2). One-year percent cover per glacier was as high as 65%, recorded on the Bow Glacier, Alberta in 2020. Small glaciers less than 10 km 2 had the highest algal cover relative to their surface area (fig. S4). Although the percent cover was smaller, large icefields and glaciers hosted notable blooms. For example, Black Rapids Glacier in the eastern Alaska Range had 20% of its 235-km 2 surface area covered by bloom in 2020. Blooms first appeared in late June or early July in most regions and years (fig. S5 and data S1 to S3), consistent with previous studies using direct observations ( 4 ) and simulations ( 27 ). The Sentinel-2 images reveal that the blooms moved upslope throughout the season, as expected due to rising snowline and melt of snow at higher elevation (fig. S6), in line with ground observations made by Takeuchi ( 16 ) and Lutz et al. ( 28 ). Red snow was visible as late as September, at which point it was either covered by new snowfall or had melted away. While blooms appeared in the same general location each summer on many glaciers ( Fig. 1 and data S1 to S3), year-to-year algal extent and biomass was highly variable (figs. S5 and S7). In the Alaska Range, bloom extent was four times higher in 2020 than any other year, likely due to persistent summer snowpack in this year (data S3). In southern British Columbia, blooms were widespread in all years except 2021. The Pacific Northwest heat wave of 2021 resulted in early loss of snow cover throughout much of the bloom range (fig. S8) ( 29 ), resulting in a truncated snow algal growth season. Snowmelt derived from bloom-albedo On the basis of the remotely sensed spatiotemporal distribution, we estimated bloom-albedo and radiative forcing across each summer to provide a first-order approximation of bloom-albedo snowmelt (fig. S9). As expected, the snowmelt was proportional to percent cover, with some variation due to differences in bloom duration and intensity ( Fig. 2 ). Regionally, the highest bloom-albedo melt occurred in southeastern British Columbia in 2020 and northwestern British Columbia in 2022. Estimated annual bloom-albedo snowmelt per individual glacier was as high as 3.1 ± 1.2–cm snow water equivalent ( SWE ), which equates to a layer of snow approximately 5.5 cm deep over the 2.5-km 2 unnamed glacier in southeastern British Columbia, assuming a typical summer snow density ( 30 ). Although we do not have winter accumulation data for this unnamed glacier, the Gulkana Glacier in the eastern Alaska Range has historical mass balance records ( 31 , 32 ) and, in recent years, was the site of extensive snow algal blooms ( 16 , 33 , 34 ). On the basis of our analysis, snow algae affected one quarter of the Gulkana Glacier surface in 2020 and contributed 2.4 ± 0.9–cm SWE where present ( Fig. 1 ). This equates to approximately 1.6% of the previous winter’s accumulation (winter mass balance). For comparison with a previous study, in a nonglaciated basin in Washington State, USA, bloom-albedo meltwater in July was equivalent to 2% of the month’s total snowmelt within the bloom area ( 5 ). In conclusion, red snow was extensive on many North American glaciers and icefields. On heavily affected glaciers, bloom-albedo contributed substantially to total summer snowmelt. Blooms covered the most area in years with persistent summer snowpack, suggesting that while occasional years of deep, persistent snowpack may slow the rate of glacier mass loss in North America, this mitigating effect could be counteracted by the darkening effect of snow algal blooms. MATERIALS AND METHODS Overview of classifier training and map validation We trained a random forest pixel classifier to differentiate between snow algae and other snow surface classes such as mineral dust (fig. S10). Random forest classifiers are widely used in remote sensing due to their ability to be deployed in parallel on large high-dimensional datasets and high predictive performance across a range of applications ( 35 , 36 ). As with all machine-learning algorithms, random forest is limited by the quality and quantity of training data, the similarity between classes, and proper implementation of hyperparameter and threshold tuning ( 37 , 38 ). Training data were collected by visual interpretation of true-color images (described below), following the approach of previous global land cover classification studies ( 36 ). Originally, we set out to map snow algae globally; therefore, we collected training data across all major glaciated regions excluding interior Greenland and Antarctica. However, the highest classifier accuracy was achieved in northwestern North America; therefore, the current manuscript is limited to this region. Nevertheless, red snow was similar in all global regions, so we took advantage of the larger global dataset to train the model that was implemented here to map snow algae in North America. The trained classifier was applied to four summers of Sentinel-2 data to generate a 2019–2022 North American occurrence range map at 10-m resolution. To validate the range map, we viewed true-color Sentinel-2 images at randomly sampled grid cells to verify the presence or absence of red snow (fig. S11). Ground truthing red snow sites in true-color satellite images Red snow was identifiable in Sentinel-2 true-color images and was distinct from other snow surface contaminants such as mineral dust. We found red snow in the field corresponding to the red snow distribution in true-color satellite images at eight of eight field validation visits in southern British Columbia (table S3). These remote field sites were accessed within 10 days of satellite image acquisition. At the Illecillewaet field site, we confirmed the location of several large patches of snow algae and mineral dust by walking around the bloom and dust perimeters with a handheld GPS and found that the GPS perimeters correctly encircled the red and brown snow in the corresponding true-color image. However, this was not possible at all field sites due to the safety concerns with crevasses. At six sites, we walked a transect along the length of the bloom, collecting surface samples at 100 m intervals. Light microscopy confirmed the predominance of spherical red algal cells in all samples. Training data collection To train the algorithm to recognize pixels dominated by blooms, we labeled segments of Sentinel-2 images by visual inspection. Segments with an ambiguous identity were not included in the training data. The training data was collected over an area was defined using Global Land Ice Measurements from Space (GLIMS) which provides global estimated glacier and icefield boundaries as they were on September 14, 2021 ( 39 , 40 ). In addition, we included training images from a 100-km-wide strip along the edge of the Greenland Ice Sheet south of the 75th parallel, calculated using Copernicus Global Land Cover CGLS-LC100 Collection 3 ( 41 ). Sentinel-2 was used for the current study because it has a 5-day flyover period and 10-m spatial resolution, making this platform well suited to monitor transient and ephemeral blooms ( 42 ). Sentinel-2 surface-reflectance L2A satellite images were processed in Google Earth Engine ( 43 ). Sentinel-2 images from 2019 to 2022 were included in the analysis—surface-reflectance L2A data before 2019 was not available in the Google Earth Engine data catalog at the time of analysis. Red snow is frequently observed on North American snowfields throughout July and August ( 1 , 2 , 4 , 5 , 16 , 17 ); therefore, we filtered the Sentinel-2 image collection to these months. To reduce the number of cloudy and low-solar-angle images, images with cloudy_pixel_percentage greater than 40 and mean_solar_zenith_angle greater than 65 were discarded. The resulting Sentinel-2 images were aggregated into 5-day low-cloud mosaics and masked using the precomputed msk_cldprb MSK_CLDPRB band with a threshold of 10%. Following the approach of our previous study ( 17 ), we masked pixels with aerosol optical thickness greater than 200; any band value greater than 11,000 (masking saturated pixels); B3 &lt; 5000 and normalized difference snow index ( NDSI ) &lt; 0.65, where N D S I = B 3 − B 11 B 3 + B 11 (masking bare ice); green blue normalized difference ( GBND ) &gt; 0.03, where G B N D = B 3 − B 2 B 3 + B 2 (masking rockfall on snow); snow redness index ( SRI ) &lt; 0, where S R I = B 4 − B 3 B 4 + B 3 . We labeled segments of true-color Sentinel-2 L2A images by manually drawing polygons around segments we identified as either “bloom” or “not bloom” (fig. S12). Images were selected by visually browsing the filtered Sentinel-2 image collection in the Google Earth Engine code editor, striving to include images throughout all global glacier regions in all years and seasons and including a diversity of snow surface types. On the basis of visual interpretation, segments represented various features including snow algae, mineral dust on snow, wildfire ash on snow, clouds that were missed by our cloud mask, reddish glare on snow, the red portions of rainbows projected onto snow, haze, bare glacial ice, crevasses, and white snow. We did not attempt to draw polygons around pixels that appeared to contain a mixture of bloom and not-bloom (e.g., mixed algae and mineral dust). To account for varying polygon size, a maximum of 25 pixels were sampled at native 10-m resolution per class using the ee.Image.stratifiedSample function. Only pixels that passed the masking criteria described in the previous paragraph were included. The final training dataset consisted of 9312 pixels from 225 images, evenly distributed by global region, year, and date. Twelve percent of training pixels were labeled bloom and the remaining 88% were not bloom. Random Forest classifier training Following a typical supervised classification workflow, a 75% random split of the global dataset was used for training and the remaining 25% was reserved for classifier testing (fig. S10). To avoid the problematic scenario of geographically adjacent pixels being present in both training and testing data, the split was grouped by image such that the training testing sets contained different images. The random forest classifier was trained in R using the ranger and tidymodels packages ( 44 – 47 ). Classifier inputs included the 12 raw satellite band values ( B1 to B12 , including B8A and excluding B10 ) plus 19 derived variables calculated from the normalized differences of raw band combinations, with the rationale that normalized differences are more robust to varying light intensity across different images. As expected, the strongest predictors of red snow were bands in red and near-infrared wavelengths and normalized differences containing those bands. Classifier performance was optimized by tuning random forest hyperparameters to minimize both false discovery rate ( FDR ) and false-negative rate ( FNR ), where FDR (also known as commission error) is the fraction of predicted bloom area that is truly not bloom, and FNR (also known as omission error) is the fraction of the true bloom area that is missed by the classifier. FDR is the complement of precision, also known as “producer’s accuracy,” i.e. FDR = 1 − precision, and FNR is the complement of recall, also known as “user’s accuracy.” Classifier hyperparameters mtry and min_n were tuned within the 75% training split using a standard 10-fold cross-validation grid search to maximize precision-recall area under the curve ( PR AUC ). As above, to avoid the situation of geographically adjacent pixels being present in multiple cross-validation folds, the folds were grouped by image such that no images were present in multiple folds. PR AUC remained between 0.85 and 0.86 for all mtry between 2 and 12 and min_n between 2 and 5, indicating that the model was robust to a range of hyperparameter values. Threshold tuning (“thresholding”) is often used to further improve classifier performance and can be tuned using cross-validation same as any other hyperparameter. However, PR AUC cannot be used for thresholding, because by definition, PR AUC is estimated across a range of threshold values, so instead we used a related metric, the harmonic mean of precision and recall ( F 1 ). The North American subset of the training data (25 images, 854 pixels) was evaluated across a range of thresholds using the threshold_perf function from the probably library ( 46 ). The maximum F 1 was 0.27; therefore, this value was used as a probability threshold in our analysis. We evaluated the tuned classifier on the 25% split (the “test split”) of the global dataset (55 images, 2231 pixels). The random forest classifier had precision of 86% and a recall of 72% on this global dataset (fig. S13). Classifier implementation and occurrence range map assembly The classifier was implemented to identify blooms of red snow algae on glaciers with surface area greater than 1 km 2 . Masked, 5-day low-cloud mosaics were classified using the smile.RandomForest function in Earth Engine to generate 10-m resolution probability images based on the per-tree vote count of the random forest. Probability images were flattened into 1-year and 4-year maximum-probability composites, and the threshold of 0.27 was applied to generate annual distributions and the 4-year occurrence range map. Bloom area per region was calculated using the Earth Engine reduceRegions function (fig. S7). Occurrence range map validation While the test split provided a measure of classifier performance globally, we sought to quantify error in the North American 4-year occurrence range map. Following the approach of Murray et al. ( 36 ), we undertook an independent map validation by sampling across the predicted distribution and viewing true-color images overlying the selected grid cells (fig. S11). True-color images were labeled on the basis of visual interpretation, following an approach similar to our training data collection. All map validation points, labels, and corresponding true-color images can be browsed interactively at https://caseyengstrom.users.earthengine.app/view/watermelon-snow . Calculation of map error requires adequate representation of the red snow occurrence range, but red snow area represents a small fraction of the total glacier area. To solve this “needle in a haystack” problem, sampling was stratified by probability decile. That is, an equal number of grid cells were sampled from the area with 0 to10% bloom probability as from the area with 90 to 100% bloom probability, in which bloom probability is the value from the 4-year maximum probability composite image. Each grid cell contained, on average, 25 Sentinel-2 pixels, a large enough area to allow for visual interpretation of the image segment in the context of the surrounding image. We labeled the grid cells as either “1: bloom predominant on at least one date”, “0: no bloom present at any time”, or “N/A: unsure or mixed bloom and dust on at least one date” following visual interpretation of true-color Sentinel-2 images in a custom Google Earth Engine web application (fig. S14). Because each grid cell of the occurrence range map represents a 4-year time interval, map validation grid cells were labeled on the basis of multiple images (fig. S11). Because it would not be practicable to view all images across the entire 4-year study period, only the top three images with the highest predicted probability of bloom within the grid cell were viewed. We found that lower-ranked images generally did not yield additional information. In high-probability deciles, a single image was enough to assign a label of “1.” Grid cells labeled as “N/A: mixed/unsure” were discarded from further analysis. The final North American map validation dataset contained 385 rows, each corresponding to a different manually labeled grid cell (fig. S15). Bootstrapping (1000 replicates) of the map validation dataset was used to estimate 95% CIs around the occurrence range map precision and recall (fig. S15). Because blooms covered a relatively small fraction of the total glaciated surface, each validation grid cell was weighted proportional to decile area, e.g., a grid cell from decile 0 (0 to 10%) would be assigned a much higher weight than one from decile 9 (90 to 100% probability) because it represents a much larger area. Following ( 36 ), precision CI lower limit was scaled to yield the bloom-area CI lower limit, and recall CI lower limit was scaled to estimate bloom-area CI upper limit (fig. S15 and table S1). The map had an FDR of 0.09 and an FNR of 0.06. Two-thirds of the false-positive grid cells were located on volcanos in the Alaska Peninsula and Cascades (fig. S15) and appeared to be caused by volcanic ash on snow. These volcanic false-positive grid cells had minimal impact on the total North American error rate because the Alaska Peninsula and Cascades composed a small fraction of the total area. The remaining false-positive points were scattered throughout the study area and appeared to be caused by mineral dust on snow or mixtures of mineral dust and snow algae. Bloom elevation, slope, aspect, and snow cover duration The elevation, slope, and aspect of the 4-year snow algae occurrence range (fig. S3) was assessed by applying the ee.Terrain.products function in Earth Engine to a digital elevation model ( 48 ). Aspect was masked to exclude slopes less than 15° because the impact of the sun’s direction, or aspect, on these low-angle slopes is minimal. In addition, we re-ran the aspect analysis masking slopes less than 10° and 5°. Snow cover duration (fig. S8) was estimated using the Sentinel-2 NDSI with a threshold of 0.65. The last day of snow cover between 1 July and 15 September was assessed, creating day-of-year images, masking by snow cover, and making a maximum value composite. Biomass and snowmelt estimates We estimated red snow biomass and albedo using the SRI , which we define as the normalized difference between the red and green Sentinel-2 satellite bands, S R I = B 4 − B 3 B 4 + B 3 . Previous studies calibrated SRI as a proxy for red snow biomass and albedo in Alaska ( 4 ) and southern British Columbia ( 17 ), supporting its application in the current study. Cloud-masked SRI was calculated for each 5-day mosaic, with “not bloom” areas assumed to have zero biomass. We refer to these random forest–classified SRI images as “ bloom-SRI .” To estimate yearly biomass (fig. S7), we created maximum annual composite bloom-SRI images, scaled to units of biomass using the linear regression from ( 17 ). Biomass error was likewise derived by scaling the linear regression error of ( 17 ). Snowmelt derived from bloom-albedo ( Fig. 2 ) was estimated following the approach of previous studies ( 17 , 49 ). First, the decrease in albedo caused by snow algae (D α ) was estimated from bloom-SRI images. Second, the additional solar energy absorbed by the snowpack was estimated as the product of D α and incident solar irradiance (“insolation,” I* ). Third, the additional absorbed solar energy was converted into units of potential snowmelt using the latent heat of fusion for water ( L w ). These three steps are summarized in the following equation: snowmelt algae = I* D α L w −1 . To fill in the missing dates between 5-day mosaics, temporal gaps were interpolated using the smooth.spline function in R. Splines were visually inspected to verify that the curve did not contain biologically or meteorologically implausible day-to-day fluctuations ( 17 ). Splines were weighted by clear-sky fraction over the predicted bloom area, where clear-sky fraction was calculated from the cloud mask in each bloom-SRI image (fig. S5). The decrease in albedo due to snow algae (D α ) was estimated by multiplying the spline-interpolated bloom-SRI by the albedo coefficient from albedo-SRI model of ( 17 ). Albedo is defined here as the ratio of reflected to incident shortwave solar radiance. Error was likewise derived from the SE in the albedo-SRI model ( 17 ). Insolation ( I* ) was derived from DAYMET V4 gridded climate reanalysis data ( 50 ). DAYMET insolation is interpolated from North American weather station data, taking into account empirical and theoretical relationships between insolation and elevation, temperature, and precipitation ( 51 , 52 ). At 1-km resolution, DAYMET likely misses microscale weather such as convective thunderstorms but nevertheless provides a reasonable estimate of the average insolation in a region. The effect of aspect and mountain shadow on insolation are not considered in this simple model, but as most bloom locations were on expansive, relatively flat glaciers and icefields (fig. S3), this potential bias was likely minimal. Our estimate of snowmelt algae does not consider the impact of meltwater evaporation ( 53 ), nor snow grain sublimation, which was minimal relative to snowmelt for glaciers in Europe and North America ( 54 , 55 ), nor the impact of meltwater refreezing at the surface, e.g., due to diurnal air temperature fluctuations ( 56 ). In Greenland, ice algae used 1 to 2.5% of incident solar irradiance ( I* ) for photosynthesis ( 57 ), which is not considered in our first-order approximation of bloom-albedo snowmelt.
