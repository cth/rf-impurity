Abstract Background Alcohol is a commonly used substance associated with significant public health consequences. Treatment is often stigmatized and limited with regard to both access and affordability, demonstrating the need for innovations in alcohol treatment. Accelerometer sensors can detect drinking without user input and are widely incorporated into wearable devices, increasing accessibility and affordability. Method s We compared a distributional and random forest classification approach to detect and evaluate sensor‐based drinking data. Data were collected at a local state fair ( n = 194), where participants drank water at specified intervals interspersed with confounding behaviors (e.g., touching nose, rubbing forehead, or yawning) while wearing an Android‐based smartwatch for 10 min. Participants were randomized to receive one of three drinking container shapes: pint, martini, or wine. Results The random forest model achieved an overall testing accuracy of 93% (sensitivity = 0.32; specificity = 0.99; positive predictive value = 0.74). The distributional algorithm achieved an overall accuracy of 95% (sensitivity = 0.76; specificity = 0.97; positive predictive value = 0.72). The distributional algorithm had a significantly greater accuracy ( t (193) = 7.73, p &lt; 0.001, d = 0.56) and sensitivity ( t (193) = 24.5, p &lt; 0.001, d = 1.76). Equivalency testing demonstrated significant equivalency to the ground truth for sip duration ( t lower (193) = 16.92, p &lt; 0.001; t upper (193) = −9.85, p &lt; 0.001) and between‐sip interval ( t lower (193) = 1.72, p = 0.044; t higher (193) = −3.96, p &lt; 0.001). However, the random forest did not have significant equivalency to the ground truth for between‐sip interval ( t lower (193) = 1.98, p = 0.025; t higher (193) = 0.160, p = 0.564). Conclusions Overall, the results indicated that consumer‐grade smartwatches can be utilized to detect and measure alcohol use behavior using machine learning and distributional algorithms. This work provides the methodological foundation for future research to analyze the behavioral pharmacology of alcohol use and develop accessible just‐in‐time clinical interventions. Keywords: accelerometer, alcohol, classification, machine learning Our study develops and validates a novel framework for detecting and analyzing drinking behavior using smartwatch sensors. We compared a distributional and machine learning algorithm and found that both performed effectively in identifying sips and analyzing drinking patterns comparable to manual coding without extensive data preprocessing. However, the distributional algorithm was less sensitive to differences in container shape. INTRODUCTION Alcohol use is one of the most widely used substances in the United States, with about 85.6 percent of individuals over the age of 18 reporting lifetime use. Meta‐analyses suggest alcohol use and binge drinking have increased over the past 10–15 years (Grucza et al., 2018 ). Alcohol‐related health consequences have also risen, with an estimated 62% increase in the rate of alcohol‐related emergency department visits over 2006–2014 (White et al., 2018 ). Alcohol use is associated with 95,158 deaths and 2.8 million years of potential life lost each year and has been shown to account for 27% of the socioeconomic inequalities in mortality (Esser et al., 2024 ; Probst et al., 2020 ; Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration [SAMHSA], 2024 ). However, despite the prevalence of alcohol use and its health‐related consequences, the development of accessible treatments has stalled over the past two decades (Heilig et al., 2016 , 2019 ; Ray et al., 2020 ). One potential reason for the lack of progress in developing new treatments is the difficulty of accurately measuring alcohol use behavior. One approach to measuring alcohol use behavior is ecological momentary assessment (EMA). EMA is a methodology that uses repeated measurement in naturalistic environments to track behavior in real time with minimal participant recall (Shiffman et al., 2008 ). While this approach has many advantages over traditional recall data collection, it has inherent limitations that challenge effective implementation, including user error, forgetting to log drinks, and participant fatigue (Doherty et al., 2020 ; Dzubur, 2017 ). Other research on continuous alcohol monitoring in day‐to‐day life has relied primarily on transdermal alcohol content (TAC) sensors or estimating the level of intoxication through gait abnormalities (Suffoletto et al., 2020 ; van Egmond et al., 2020 ). A drawback of these approaches is that TAC and gait abnormalities lag behind actual drinking by 1–4 h (Fairbairn et al., 2020 ; Yu et al., 2022 ). This temporal delay makes detecting and analyzing drinking instances in real‐time difficult. Alternatively, other approaches rely on broad measurements of alcohol use, such as asking about typical alcohol use patterns over the past year. While these approaches are validated to estimate the quantity and frequency of alcohol use, they are unable to capture information on typical use behavior, such as rate of consumption. Recent research has demonstrated that the rate of consumption has associations with alcohol‐related consequences beyond just the number of drinks consumed. Richards et al. ( 2024 ) showed that transdermal alcohol concentration features associated with rate of alcohol consumption were independently associated with increased odds of alcohol‐induced blackouts. Russell et al. ( 2024 ) demonstrated that faster transdermal rise and fall rates were predictive of next‐day alcohol‐related consequences. Other research has implicated the rate of consumption being correlated with a steeper slope in blood alcohol concentration (BAC) curves and a higher overall peak BAC (Carpenter et al., 2019 ). An alternative approach to measuring alcohol use is to examine the microstructure of drinking episodes, commonly referred to as drinking topography (Caudill &amp; Marlatt, 1975 ; Collins et al., 1985 ; Schneider et al., 2022 ). Drinking topography quantifies the behavioral patterns of alcohol consumption such as sip duration, sip interval (i.e., the length of time between sips), and the number of sips taken throughout a drinking event. Prior research has shown that a faster rate of intake of alcohol is associated with a variety of negative consequences, such as blackout and nausea (Carpenter &amp; Merrill, 2021 ). It also has been shown to be associated with differential physiological effects. Faster drinking rate has been shown to correlate with a steeper slope in BAC curves, higher overall peak BAC, and enhanced cardiac effects (Carpenter et al., 2019 ; Conrod et al., 1997 ). In addition, faster drinking results in subjective intoxication ratings that peak earlier than BAC (Martin &amp; Earleywine, 1990 ). This is important because greater subjective intoxication during a drinking episode is associated with negative alcohol‐related consequences, such as risky sexual behavior and illicit substance use (Quinn &amp; Fromme, 2011 ). Past year drinking behavior has also been shown to be associated with laboratory drinking topography outcomes, such as greater binge episodes are associated with more consistent drinking behavior (Schneider et al., 2023 ). Greater Alcohol Use Disorder Identification Test (AUDIT) scores have also been shown to be associated with faster between‐sip intervals (Schneider et al., 2023 ). Drinking topography outcomes are also sensitive to external manipulations. Schneider et al. ( 2022 ) found that participants had greater sip volumes and faster sip intervals when a virtual person in the bar environment drank quickly. In addition, Gilmour et al. ( 2023 ) demonstrated a sex‐dependent effect of pain on between‐sip intervals such that men drank faster when experiencing pain, but not women. One past limitation of drinking topography has been that previous studies often rely on manual coding by multiple researchers to observations or video recordings (Collins et al., 1985 ). However, manual coding via video recordings is often prone to error and requires significant time and personnel effort. It also cannot characterize fine‐grained temporal data due to being limited to its resolution (the number of frames per second of the video recording device). Due to these limitations, conducting these studies outside of laboratory settings is often infeasible. As such, this limits the ability to assess drinking in different contexts and environments. A more efficient and potentially more accurate alternative to manual coding is utilizing sensors with high temporal resolution, such as accelerometers. Accelerometer sensors measure the movement speed of a device and are built into common wearable smart devices, including smartwatches and fitness trackers. These sensors can read and transmit data with millisecond‐level accuracy. Consumer‐grade accelerometers in smartphones and smartwatches are easy to use, comfortable, unobtrusive, and suitable for long‐term use and do not interfere with daily activities (Auepanwiriyakul et al., 2020 ). These sensors have also been found to correspond strongly with research‐grade accelerometer devices (Auepanwiriyakul et al., 2020 ; Davoudi et al., 2019 ). This is important because wearable devices are one of the fastest growing sectors in the technology industry. A large online survey found that 59% of respondents owned a wearable device and the primary reasons for owning one was to track fitness and health (Shandhi et al., 2024 ). Wearable devices also exist across a variety of price points, from $49.99 for an entry‐level device and up to $799 for a high‐end device. However, the accelerometer sensor is present in the majority of wearable devices regardless of price point. This makes wearable devices a potential target for affordable research and clinical interventions as there are a variety of prices to choose from and the devices can be utilized for other applications. While there is little research on using accelerometers to measure alcohol‐drinking behavior, a body of research aims to detect sips to track water consumption. Gomes and Sousa ( 2019 ) used a wrist‐based accelerometer to measure hand‐to‐mouth movement to predict hydration status. They found that using random forest machine learning could accurately detect the start of a sip (sensitivity = 0.97 and positive predictive value = 0.97; Gomes &amp; Sousa, 2019 ). Research has also found that wrist‐based accelerometer devices can accurately detect fluid intake using machine learning models (Hsieh et al., 2024 ; Huang et al., 2020 ). However, most of these approaches rely on applying a sliding window, which is an approach that combines data across a prespecified time window. Then, researchers can extract features during this time window, such as the mean of the signal in the time window. While this may be useful for assessing if a sip occurs, measuring important aspects of drinking topography (e.g., sip duration) is difficult because the sliding window may blend parts of a sip with nonsips. It also increases the computational time needed to analyze the data, limiting its utility in real‐time contexts. Taken together, a substantial body of research demonstrates that alcohol use is highly prevalent, and its associated health costs and public health burden have been increasing over the past decade. However, alcohol use treatment is marked by several barriers to access, resulting in only a fraction of individuals receiving treatment. Current mobile harm reduction‐focused interventions aimed at increasing accessibility rely on manual input after drinking episodes, which are prone to error and cannot facilitate real‐time modifications to drinking behavior. Secondly, current automatic drinking detection relies on ethanol in sweat (i.e., TAC sensors), which may not be ideal for characterizing drinking episodes in real time due to individual variability and lagged BAC recording. No study has used sensors in widely available mobile, wearable technology to detect and analyze drinking behavior. Thus, this study aimed to develop the methodological framework for automatic drinking detection and behavioral analysis using wearable technology. In addition, due to the variety of container shapes that alcohol can be served in, we aimed to compare the classification outcomes between three container shapes: pint, martini, and wine glass. We hypothesized that there would be no significant classification outcome differences between the three container shapes. METHODS This present study aimed to develop and test a smartwatch algorithm capable of identifying drinking behavior and discriminating drinking from nondrinking‐related behaviors. The study was approved by the University of Minnesota Institutional Review Board (IRB #00020287). All participants provided informed consent prior to data collection. Participants Data were collected at the University of Minnesota's Driven to Discover (D2D) research facility during the Minnesota State Fair from August 22, 2023, to September 2, 2023. Research staff members recruited participants by engaging with fairgoers at the D2D facility. Interested participants provided written consent prior to data collection. Participant's eligibility was assessed via screening questionnaires. Eligibility criteria included: (1) 21 years of age or older, (2) reported having consumed alcohol on at least one occasion in the past year, and (3) being able to read, speak, and comprehend the English language. Participant's age was confirmed via self‐report and via a driver's license or other state‐issued identification. All other criteria were assessed via self‐report on the screening questionnaires. Sensor data collection All accelerometer and gyroscope data were collected via the Android‐based Mobovi TicWatch Pro 3 smartwatch. Each smartwatch was paired to a Samsung A03s smartphone connected via a Dell laptop computer. A proprietary smartwatch app called “AlcWear” was downloaded on the smartwatch. AlcWear was developed by the lead author (NJB) in the Android Software Development Kit (Google, 2023 ) to record triaxial accelerometer data from the Android‐based smartwatch. The app consisted of a black screen with updated text with accelerometer sensor values and two buttons to start or stop data recording. Participants pressed a button labeled “Start” when instructed, which began the sensor data collection. Data were transmitted via Bluetooth debugging to the paired smartphone, which was logged into the Android Studio Logcat. The log file was then saved as a comma‐separated file. Randomization procedure Research staff randomly assigned an 8‐digit identification number using the R language (version 4.3.1; The R Development Core Team, 2012 ) package “ids” (FitzJohn, 2017 ). Participants were randomly assigned to one of three container shapes: pint, martini, or wine. Study procedures Eligible participants were randomized to their beverage container shape, which was then filled to a standard level with plain water. A laptop was placed in front of the participant and were instructed to follow the behavior that was presented in text on the laptop screen. Participants were also instructed to place their beverage back down on the table after taking a sip. Participants then drank water from their assigned container at a controlled pace of one sip and one confound behavior every 30 s. There were three possible confound behaviors: (1) touching the nose, (2) touching the forehead, and (3) covering the mouth while yawning. The three behaviors were selected due to their similarities to drinking behavior. The order of confounds throughout the study was randomized. Participants alternated between sips and confounded behaviors for 5 min, thus resulting in 10 sets of sips and 10 sets of confound behaviors. Measures Demographics Participants were asked to report their age, sex, gender identity, race, and ethnicity. Ground truth Two members of the research team (HA and MS) manually classified each observation (i.e., every recorded data point) of participant data into one of the following three sets of behaviors: points of no interest (e.g., the data points where neither a sip nor a confound behavior is occurring), confound behaviors, and sip behaviors. The lead author resolved discrepancies between the two manual coders based on visual inspection of the data and the research assistant's codes. Thus, each observation for each participant was manually classified into one of the three behaviors. Interrater reliability analysis revealed substantial agreement between the three raters (Cohen's kappa = 0.867). Sip classification approach Sip classification was assessed by first classifying every observation into 1 (sip) or 0 (not a sip) utilizing either the distributional or the random forest algorithms. After each observation was classified, they were separated into groups based on whether there was a time difference in 200 milliseconds or greater between observations. Afterward, the sinusoidal regression approach was used to classify each grouping as either a sip or not a sip. This approach was utilized to reduce the number of false positives and to provide a method to extract sip‐level data. Leave‐one‐out cross‐validation Training and testing datasets were created via the leave‐one‐out cross‐validation approach (LOOCV). For each participant, we iteratively separated their data from the entire dataset. With the remaining data ( n −1), we calculated the distributions for the three behaviors (e.g., sip, confound, and points of no interest). The training dataset was comprised of the n −1 datasets and the testing dataset was comprised of the selected n participant. Distributional algorithm The distributional algorithm uses the multivariate probability density function (MPDF). We chose to use this approach because the accelerometer records in three axes (i.e., x , y , and z ) and each of the axes are correlated with each other. As such, the multivariate distribution would be an appropriate method to quantify the likelihood and probability of values occurring together. First, the mean of each accelerometer axis and the covariance matrix were defined for each of the three behaviors (i.e., points of no interest, confound, and sip) using a training dataset. Then the MPDF was calculated for each observation in the testing dataset for each of the behaviors using the following equation: p x μ ϵ = 1 2 π d ∣ ϵ ∣ * exp − 1 2 x − μ T ϵ − 1 x − μ , where x is vector of observations ( x ‐, y ‐, z ‐axis values), μ is the mean of the behavior, ϵ is the covariance matrix, d is the dimensionality of the distribution, | | is the determinant of the matrix and T represents the transpose of a matrix. Second, we summed the MPDF for all three behaviors together to get a total likelihood value. Third, we divided each of the behaviors' MPDF values by the total sum to derive a probability value that the observation exists in that behavior's distribution. Every observation was then classified as either 1 (sip) or 0 (not a sip) if the probability of being in the drinking distribution was greater than the probability of belonging to the no‐interest distribution. Random Forest algorithm Random forest is an ensemble machine learning approach consisting of a collection of decision tree models. Each tree model is trained using a bootstrap sample comprised of the training data. Then, each tree makes a prediction on the data, and the random forest ensemble model aggregates the predictions and selects the best prediction based on a majority vote. The random forest model was generated via R's “ranger” package (Wright &amp; Ziegler, 2017 ). The random forest classification algorithm was selected due to its resistance to overfitting and robustness to outliers and noise (Wright &amp; Ziegler, 2017 ). In addition, the random forest model is commonly used in wrist‐based fluid detection studies (Chun et al., 2019 ; Gomes &amp; Sousa, 2019 ; Huang et al., 2020 ). The random forest model also has been shown to perform the best on the balance between sensitivity and positive predictive value compared with other machine learning models (Huang et al., 2020 ). The model features were the x ‐, y ‐, and z ‐axis accelerometer values, which were trained using the manually labeled sip data. The default parameters of 500 trees, unlimited tree depth, 1 for minimal node size, and 1 for the number of randomly selected predictors (defined by the following equation: ⌊ n variables ⌋) were implemented. We opted for an approach that classified each observation of data rather than a sliding window to aid in preprocessing time and to reduce the arbitrary decision making needed to specify time windows. Sinusoidal sip classification For both the distributional and random forest algorithms, a complete sip was assessed via sinusoidal regression using the sinreg function from the “ShellChron” package in R (formula: I = a ; A = b 2 + c 2 and ψ = P ∙ 0.25 − cos − 1 b A 2 π ; de Winter, 2022 ), where a, b, and c are the coefficients of the linearized form of the sinusoidal function, A is the amplitude of the sinusoid, P is the period of the sinusoid, and ψ is the phase of the sinusoid. This approach was utilized to reduce the number of false positives and to create a standard equation of a line for each sip. Time was defined as the x variable and y ‐axis accelerometer value was defined as the y variable. The y ‐axis of the accelerometer was chosen because it was observed to display a consistent sinusoidal shape during a sip. A group of observations was classified as a sip if the R 2 value was greater than 0.5 (see Figure 1 , e.g., sip and sinusoidal regression curve). R 2 values less than 0.5 were observed to be more sensitive (i.e., capture more actually classified sips), but were more prone to false positives. R 2 values greater than 0.5 were observed to be more accurate in detected sips, but were more prone to false negatives. As such, we chose the threshold of ≥0.5 as a middle ground between false negatives and false positives. FIGURE 1 Scatter plots of accelerometer values for defined behaviors. Dotted lines represent the manually coded start and end times of the sip behavior. Data analysis The dataset was divided based on handedness (e.g., left or right). Due to differences in accelerometer axis values depending on handedness, we made separate models for left‐handed and right‐handed individuals. A classification table comparing the predicted and manually coded values was then computed and the following classification outcomes were calculated: accuracy True Positives + True Negatives True Positives + True Negatives + False Positives + False Negatives * 100 , sensitivity True Positives True Positives + False Negatives , specificity True Negatives True Negatives + False Positives , positive predictive value True Positives True Positives + False Positives , negative predictive value True Negatives True Negatives + False Negatives , and F 1 ‐score, which is the harmonic mean of the sensitivity and positive predictive value 2 * Sensitivity * Positive Predictive Value Sensitivity + Positive Predictive Value . This process was then repeated for all participants. We chose to use F 1 ‐score because there are more data points where no sip occurs than data points where a sip occurs. This results in a heavily imbalanced dataset. As such, accuracy may be artificially inflated due to a greater proportion of nonsips than sips. However, F 1 ‐score is only calculated via data from the classification of sips and thus cannot be influenced by the nonsip classification. This results in a metric that is more interpretable as to how well the model correctly classifies sips compared with accuracy. This is also a commonly recommended metric for datasets with imbalanced outcomes (Jeni et al., 2013 ). All statistics were calculated in Jamovi (The Jamovi Project, 2024 ). Sip duration and sip interval (i.e., the amount of time between sips) were calculated from the manually coded data and the automatically coded data from the algorithm. Paired t ‐tests were used to compare mean differences of sip duration, sip interval, and total sip number between the manual, quadratic, and sinusoidal automatic classifications. Equivalence paired samples t ‐tests were performed to determine the equivalency of sip duration, sip interval, and total number of sips between the manual classification and the predicted algorithm's classification. The two one‐sided tests (TOST) method was utilized due to its high interpretability and frequent use in equivalency research (Lakens, 2017 ; Schuirmann, 1987 ). The TOST method relies on a prespecified lower and upper‐bound equivalency zone. TOST defines the null hypothesis as the mean difference outside the equivalency zone: Mean 1 – Mean 2 ≤ Lower Bound or Mean 1 – Mean 2 ≥ Upper Bound. Thus, a significant result suggests that the mean difference is significantly within the equivalency zone (H a : Mean 1 – Mean 2 ≥ Lower Bound and Mean 1 – Mean 2 ≤ Upper Bound). The equivalency threshold for the sip duration was set at between ±1 standard deviation of the ground truth sip duration (0.765 s), and the equivalency threshold for the number of sips was set between ±1 sip. The 1 sip threshold was set since it is the minimum meaningful difference (i.e., a sip can only recorded as a whole number). The equivalency threshold for the between‐sip interval was set to be within 5% of the expected between‐sip interval based on experimental design (e.g., one sip every 30 s). Thus, the equivalency threshold was set at ±1.5 s. Lastly, we used univariate analysis of variance (ANOVA) tests to assess for differences according to container shape and handedness on classification outcomes. Exclusions and missing data A total of 255 participants were screened for eligibility, and 252 were deemed eligible. Of the eligible participants, 23 participants were excluded due to a technical programming error ( n = 229), 21 were excluded for a loss of watch sensor data ( n = 208), 11 ( n = 197) were excluded due to file saving errors, and three were excluded due to questionnaire response quality issues ( n = 194). Thus, the final sample used for the present study included 194 participants. RESULTS Participants The sample average age was 37.1 (SD = 14.3, range = 21–72). The sample consisted mostly of cisgender (98.1%) women (66.7%) and non‐Hispanic/Latino/a/x/é (92.9%) individuals. Most individuals identified as White (83.4%), followed by Asian (10.7%), Black (1.8%), Native American or American Indian (1.2%), more than one race (0.6%) and not specified (2.4%). The majority identified their right hand as the hand they most commonly drink with (90.1%). 37.3% of the sample drank out of a wine‐shaped container, 33.7% out of a pint‐shaped container, and 29% out of a martini‐shaped container. Overall, we found that the distributional and the random forest algorithms performed well at correctly classifying each observation as a sip. In addition, the distributional algorithm was significantly equivalent in its ability to analyze sip duration and sip interval compared with the manually coded ground truth. We also compared the distributional algorithm to a random forest machine learning model and found that the distributional algorithm had significantly greater true positive detection. The random forest algorithm was also sensitive to the container shape, such that the pint container generally performed the best. The distributional algorithm had no significant differences between containers on any classification outcomes. Our distributional algorithm performed well, with a good harmonic mean of sensitivity (F 1 ‐score) and positive predictive value. When sip‐level data was analyzed, it performed equivalently to the manually coded data on sip duration, sip interval, and number of predicted sips. This suggests that while the algorithm missed some manually coded observations, the overall sip‐level data was not meaningfully impacted. In addition, the distributional algorithm required little preprocessing to accurately classify the data, suggesting it may be suitable for real‐time assessment of drinking behavior. The classification outcome of our random forest model was generally lower than reported in other studies. Huang et al. ( 2020 )'s random forest model achieved a 0.95 for sensitivity and 0.95 for positive predictive value. Other research has found sensitivity and positive predictive values ranging from 0.85 to 0.97 (Chun et al., 2019 ; Gomes &amp; Sousa, 2019 ). A potential discrepancy between our model and previous works may be due to the features in the study. Other studies focused on using a sliding window method. Sliding window is a method that segments sequential data into overlapping segments and extracts features from within the window (e.g., minimum and maximum values, mean). Our study focused on using each data observation rather than a sliding window. This was done to maximize our ability to accurately capture the entirety of the sip behavior, which could overlap with nonsip behaviors when using a sliding window. In addition, we prioritized reducing the amount of processing required to correctly classify the data to help extend this research to real‐time assessments. Overall, these findings provide a promising methodological framework for detecting and analyzing drinking behavior using smartwatch sensors. Acute laboratory alcohol administration paradigms could benefit from utilizing this technology as it would provide additional high‐resolution temporal data about a participant's behavior during the session. In addition, this approach could be combined with TAC sensors, which could provide information regarding drinking behavior and physiological alcohol processing and further improve our understanding of alcohol use behavior and related consequences. Future research should utilize this combined approach to investigate if tracking alcohol use topography could improve the variable delay time of the TAC sensor. In addition, this technology has the potential to be useful in real‐world research settings and for the development of just‐in‐time interventions. The automatic detection and limited processing needed to classify data make this technology easily implementable into existing mobile interventions. Future research is needed to analyze how well the distributional algorithm works in a free drinking environment (i.e., participants drinking at their own pace). Further, given that in real‐world drinking settings individuals may change drinks throughout a drinking event, more research is needed to assess how well the algorithm performs when participants switch drinking containers during a session. Limitations While our study findings contribute important knowledge regarding drinking gesture recognition using distributional and machine learning approaches, the study should be considered within its limitations. First, this study was based on controlled drinking behaviors, which limits its ability to generalize to naturalistic drinking. Future research should test these approaches as participants drink freely to address this. Second, the participants in our study drank only water. It is possible that consuming alcohol may produce subtle motor changes that could alter the ability to detect drinking accurately. Future studies should aim to replicate these findings in a sample consuming alcohol. Third, we aimed to minimize the preprocessing needed to run our models. However, previous research using random forest models relied on sliding window features and data cleaning methods such as adding pass filters and outlier removal. It is possible that the random forest model would perform better using this approach. Fourth, in real‐world settings individuals may drink both alcoholic and nonalcoholic beverages. This technology currently cannot detect differences by beverage type. Future research should consider testing with additional sensors, such as TAC, or utilize EMA approaches to gain additional information into a person's drinking. Lastly, we tested only one machine learning classification model. There are a variety of other methods that could be used, such as support vector machines and recurrent neural networks. Other methods may perform better without the additional preprocessing methods. Future research should compare multiple machine learning models. Our results provide the initial development and validation for a promising methodological framework for detecting and analyzing drinking behavior using smartwatch sensors. The results demonstrate that the distributional approach algorithm can be equivalent to a manually coded ground truth when detecting sips and analyzing behavioral characteristics (i.e., sip duration/sip interval). In addition, our results are promising for in‐real‐time drinking detection as we did not apply distributionally expensive data preprocessing steps. This methodology may be useful for just‐in‐time clinical interventions. It would allow researchers and clinicians to assess the dynamics of individual drinking patterns in an accessible, affordable, and efficient manner. Furthermore, this technology could be integrated into existing mobile clinical interventions, which could help to develop interventions to moderate an individual's alcohol use during a real‐time drinking event.
