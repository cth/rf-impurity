Abstract The prevalence of synthetic associations in GWAS, where non-causal variants become significant by tagging multiple undetected causal variants and not necessarily in strong linkage disequilibrium with any single one, remains unexplored. We introduce a novel machine-learning approach using only genotype data to infer such associations in human GWAS. Our analysis reveals that 3–5% of GWAS Catalog peaks may represent potential synthetic associations, often arising from epistatic interactions between common variants rather than multiple rare variants acting independently. Our findings highlight the need for multi-locus models and emphasize careful GWAS interpretation and follow-up analyses like fine-mapping and trait prediction. Background The standard GWAS analyses employ a single-locus approach, implicitly assuming that the presence of other causal variants does not affect marginal associations [ 1 ]. While effective for identifying common variants with large effects, this method is less suited for complex situations involving multiple causal variants [ 2 , 3 ]. Attempting to map multiple causal variants using single-locus models will generally decrease power and can bias estimates, particularly generating so-called synthetic associations [ 4 , 5 ]. Importantly, here we use “synthetic” not to imply a completely spurious GWAS peak, or that the top SNP is not causal as is already commonly accepted, but rather to indicate that the peak may arise by tagging the combined effect of multiple causal variants (either nearby or distal), even without strong linkage disequilibrium (LD) to any single one. While there is currently less evidence for synthetic associations in human genetics, examples do exist, such as the association between NOD2 and Crohn’s disease [ 6 ]. Debate regarding their relevance, primarily focused on rare variants, occurred over a decade ago [ 4 , 7 – 9 ]. However, whether synthetic associations matter in contemporary, much larger GWAS [ 10 ] remains untested, despite growing evidence for multiple causal variants [ 11 ]. We have introduced a novel interpretative machine-learning approach (GARFIELD) to identify multiple functional variants at a given locus or among distal loci. GARFIELD leverages the strengths of random forests and logic gates for variable selection and prediction. It generates interpretable pseudo-genotypes that capture complex interactions by using the non-linear output of logic gates from multiple variants as input (see Additional file 1: Supplemental Notes and Additional file 1: Fig. S1 for its rationale and implementation). While alternative methods for detecting allelic heterogeneity exist [ 3 , 12 – 14 ], particularly various collapsing tests for capturing cumulative effects of many rare variants [ 15 , 16 ], we employed GARFIELD as a flexible tool that produces results easy to interpret, since our primary focus is not on methodology, but rather on exploring the evidence of synthetic associations in published human GWAS data. Results and discussion Given that individual genotype–phenotype data is generally unavailable, especially for large-scale assessments, we developed a strategy to infer potential synthetic associations without phenotypic data. Briefly, we identified variants within 100 kb of each GWAS peak that were individually not strongly correlated with the peak SNP ( r 2 &lt; 0 . 3, thus also not strongly associated with the GWAS phenotype), and then used GARFIELD to search for combinations of these variants that were highly correlated with the genotype of the peak SNP ( r 2 ≥ 0 . 8, potentially associating the phenotype as well; Fig. 1 a). This approach was applied to human GWAS peaks from the NHGRI-EBI GWAS Catalog [ 10 ] and the deep variant profiles from the 1000 Genomes Project [ 17 ] to identify potential synthetic associations. The boxes represent the interquartile range, and significance was assessed using Student’s t -test. d Distribution of minor allele frequencies (MAFs) for different variant categories Our procedure found no evidence of synthetic association in most GWAS results, but 3–5% matched our criteria depending on population (Fig. 1 b; Additional file 1: Fig. S2a). These numbers greatly exceeded those from random variant controls (matched for allele frequency; Additional file 1: Fig. S3a; Additional file 1: Fig. S2b), suggesting the pattern is unlikely to be due to background haplotype structure. In total, we identified 45,082 peaks as potential synthetic associations (Additional file 2: Table S1) in at least one population. Figure 2 a–b shows an example of an association with smoking and drinking behavior [ 18 , 19 ]. GWAS on these traits identified three intergenic SNPs around 11.88 Mb on chromosome 20. Our analysis showed these peaks are strongly associated with a pair of SNPs 40 kb away in the coding region of BTBD3 , a gene influencing neural circuit formation [ 20 ] and behavior in mice [ 21 ]. The right panel illustrates the relationship between p -values and LD with the peak within the region. d Detailed haplotype structure and allelic segregation within the APOE region Another example of an intergenic peak that might be a synthetic association caused by variants in good candidate genes is 2:85315595, associated with high-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels. This peak, located between TCF7L1 and TGOLN2 , exhibits strong correlation ( r 2 = 0 . 81) with a missense and a 3′UTR variant within TGOLN2 jointly (Additional file 1: Fig. S4), suggesting TGOLN2 as a more plausible causal gene. This is further supported by TGOLN2 ’s role in high-density lipoprotein transport and its increased expression upon EPA treatment [ 22 ], contrasting with the T-cell-specific expression of TCF7L1 . Another compelling example involves the APOE locus and its association with Alzheimer’s disease. An intergenic SNP (rs1081105), recently found to be independently associated with Alzheimer’s disease and over 40 other metabolic traits ( r 2 &lt; 0 . 2 to rs429358), exhibits near-perfect correlation with both the well-known APOE missense mutation (rs429358) and a novel variant (rs72654473) located within a potential enhancer (E1957019) (Fig. 2 c–d). Notably, the latter variant shows no association with Alzheimer’s disease, making it difficult to be involved in further standard statistical or functional fine-mapping approaches. Further examples include associations between TUT7 and cortical surface area (Additional file 1: Fig. S5), ZKSCAN1 and body height (Additional file 1: Fig. S6; Additional file 2: Table S1). Furthermore, GWAS peaks with suspected synthetic associations demonstrate notably lower conservation scores compared to others, and all the multiple variants we identified as potentially driving synthetic associations showed greater evolutionary conservation compared to the original GWAS lead SNPs (Fig. 1 c), suggesting a higher likelihood of true causality in general. The uneven distribution of synthetic associations across different phenotypes (Phenotype Ontology enrichment; Additional file 2: Table S2 and Additional file 1: Supplemental Notes) additionally supports the presence of genuine biological signals, which aligns with the understanding that genetic architecture varies across traits, with some genes and pathways being more prone to heterogeneity than others. Importantly, we note that the multi-variants we identified as potentially causing synthetic associations are generally more common than the corresponding peaks (Fig. 1 d; Additional file 1: Fig. S7), suggesting that synthetic associations may frequently arise from epistatic interactions between relatively common variants, where multiple mutations are required for phenotypic expression. This contrasts with the previous notion of common SNPs tagging multiple rare, independent causal mutations [ 4 ]. Instead, our results suggest a scenario where the lead SNP tags a rarer multi-mutation haplotype rather than rare alleles, reflecting an “AND” relationship other than an “OR” relationship between causal variants. In this study, we utilize a flexible, random forest-based algorithm to identify combinations of genetic variants that jointly explain traits, focusing on potential synthetic associations where the lead variant tags multiple causal variants without strong linkage disequilibrium. Our findings suggest that considering multi-variant models can refine GWAS results and identify more plausible causal variants, which is crucial for understanding both specific molecular mechanisms and broader genetic architecture. Notably, we find numerous examples where intergenic GWAS hits can be explained by multiple SNPs within genes. Indeed, this study was motivated by the first GWAS in plants showing a suspicious tendency for associations to be located near, but not near enough to, a priori candidates [ 23 , 24 ]. Uncritically interpreting GWAS peaks as causal, without considering synthetic associations, could therefore introduce systemic bias against the regions of the genome that matter. Furthermore, our results suggest that synthetic associations in humans may often arise from epistatic (synergistic) interactions between relatively common variants, rather than multiple rare variants acting independently (in the molecular, not the statistical) sense. This is an interesting point that has not received sufficient attention previously, “AND” rather than “OR” in other words. While our approach offers valuable insights, we do not claim to have a general solution to this problem, nor do we propose that our results are unbiased. Significant potential for algorithm improvement does exist, but we hope to have demonstrated that much can be learned even with relatively simple methods. Applying other high-dimensional interaction models could improve power and remains an active research area. Nevertheless, our findings highlight the importance of considering multi-variant epistatic models in GWAS to gain a more accurate and comprehensive understanding of genetic architecture. Further studies with phenotypic data are highly needed to validate these predictions and determine whether the identified SNP combinations indeed exhibit stronger phenotypic associations than the lead SNPs. Conclusions Our study introduces a novel machine-learning framework that, using only genotype data, infers potential synthetic associations across the human GWAS Catalog. We provide evidence that a non-trivial fraction of GWAS peaks may not tag a single causal variant, but rather the combined effect of multiple underlying variants. Critically, our findings challenge the prevailing notion that such associations arise from tagging multiple independent rare variations. Instead, we propose that these associations often result from epistatic interactions between common variants. In this case, the lead SNP effectively tags a rarer, functionally distinct, multi-locus haplotype. This genetic architecture is governed by “AND” rather than “OR” logic. This realization is a crucial step forward, which would have profound implications for interpreting GWAS results, particularly for the numerous intergenic signals that may in fact point to complex allelic interactions within plausible candidate genes. While our approach offers a powerful screening tool, these findings are statistical inferences that underscore the urgent need for multi-locus models in fine-mapping and trait prediction. Future validation with individual-level phenotype data is essential to confirm these epistatic relationships and fully unravel the complex genetic architecture of human traits. Methods GARFIELD GARFIELD leverages the strengths of both random forests and logic gates to efficiently identify and interpret complex interactions between genetic variants. Random forests excel at capturing complex interactions [ 25 ] but lack interpretability, while logic gates offer clear relationships between variables but are less efficient for high-dimensional data [ 26 ]. GARFIELD utilizes random forests for initial variant selection, followed by logic gate analysis to elucidate the relationships between selected variants and their combined effect on the outcome. Another key innovation of GARFIELD is its use of predicted outcomes from either method as “pseudo-genotypes”. These pseudo-genotypes, representing the combined effect of complex interactions between variants, can then be directly used for association mapping. A detailed description of the GARFIELD pipeline, implementation, and simulations demonstrating its power to identify multiple causal variants is provided in Additional file 1: Supplemental Notes (Additional file 1: Fig. S1). The “ghost” mode in GARFIELD was used to investigate synthetic associations in the present study. Collection and processing of human public data The published human GWAS results were obtained from the NHGRI-EBI GWAS Catalog [ 10 , 27 ]. We excluded peaks reporting SNP-SNP interactions, multi-SNP haplotypes, or those not represented in the 1000 Genomes Project genotype data (chromosomes 1–22 only). Additionally, peaks with multiple alleles or MAF below 2% were removed. This resulted in 206,609 unique variants and 382,504 genotype–phenotype associations. Summary statistics were also downloaded from the GWAS Catalog [ 28 ], except for human height, which was obtained from the GIANT Consortium [ 29 , 30 ]. Phased genotype data from the high-coverage whole-genome sequencing of the 1000 Genomes Project were downloaded from [ 31 ]. We further filtered this dataset to include only biallelic variants on chromosomes 1–22 with MAF ≥ 2%. This resulted in a dataset with no missing genotypes, eliminating the need for imputation. Inference of potential synthetic associations To maintain consistency with the binary nature of logic gate analysis, and the phased information is often disregarded during linkage disequilibrium analysis, we transformed diploid phased genotypes (0 | 0, 0 | 1, 1 | 0, 1 | 1) into two haploid individuals (0 or 1). GWAS peak genotypes were processed in the same way. Notably, GARFIELD-generated pseudo-genotypes and haploid genotypes can be easily merged back into diploid phased genotypes. Indeed, linkage disequilibrium estimates from haploid genotypes showed high correlation with those obtained after merging back to diploid genotypes (Additional file 1: Fig. S3b), demonstrating the robustness of findings from haploid analysis. For each GWAS peak, we analyzed variants within a 100 kb flanking region that were not in strong LD with the lead SNP ( r 2 &lt; 0 . 3). To improve variant selection during random forest analysis, we excluded variants in strong LD ( r 2 ≥ 0 . 9) with each other. This prevented shared importance scores from masking potentially important variants. Treating each peak variant’s genotype as a “phenotype”, we applied GARFIELD to identify associations with other filtered variants and categorized the strongest associations as: Single variant, whose LD r 2 &lt; 0 . 3 with the peak (by design). Multi-variant, moderate association, with r 2 ≤ 0 . 8. Multi-variant, strong association, with r 2 &gt; 0 . 8. This analysis was performed for the full sample of 2,504 individuals (“human” group) and within each of the five 1000 Genomes Project subpopulations (AFR, AMR, EAS, EUR, SAS). Cases with indistinguishable association levels between single- and multi-variant categories (e.g., “snp_A” or “snp_A &amp; snp_B”, mixed with individual and multiple variants) were excluded. To assess synthetic associations, we repeated the analysis using randomly selected control variants (matched to GWAS peaks for MAF and number; Additional file 1: Fig. S3a) as “phenotypes”. These control variants were selected using PLINK with two rounds of pruning for LD. We observed instances where identical haplotype combinations from multiple selected variants yielded different pseudo-genotype alleles (Additional file 1: Fig. S3c), potentially due to other hidden variants or unaccounted factors. We implemented a filtering step to remove such cases for both GWAS peaks and control variants. Complex DNF expressions (e.g., snp_A &amp; snp_B &amp; snp_C || snp_A &amp; snp_B &amp; snp_D, where snp_D is in high LD with snp_C) were simplified (e.g., to snp_A &amp; snp_B &amp; snp_C) for further analysis of allele frequencies and LD (Additional file 1: Fig. S7). Evaluation of variant effects To assess the ability of different methods to identify causal variants, we evaluated predicted variant effects obtained from the 1000 Genomes Project [ 32 ] with two conservation metrics (higher scores indicate a greater degree of conservation): phastCons20way_mammalian: phastCons conservation score (range: 0–1) based on multiple alignments of 20 mammalian genomes; GERP + + _RS score: evolutionary conservation score (range: − 12.3 to 6.17 in this dataset) based on nucleotide substitution patterns. It is worth noting that our LD-pruning approach during genotype processing, while enhancing power, may influence variant effect analysis due to the random selection of highly linked variants.
