Abstract Background Understanding early predictors of treatment outcomes allows better outcome prediction and resource allocation for efficient tuberculosis (TB) management. Objectives This study aimed to predict treatment outcomes of TB patients from a real-world population-wide health record dataset with a significant rate of incomplete observations. In addition, potential risk factors associated with death during TB treatment were investigated. Methods We exploited the upweighting approach and multiple imputation analysis (MIA) to address the extreme imbalance in responses and missing data. Three algorithms were employed for TB treatment outcome prediction, including logistic regression (LOGIT), random forest, and stochastic gradient boosting. The three models exhibited similar performance in predicting the treatment outcomes. Moreover, an interpretation of LOGIT was conducted, adjusted odds ratios (aORs) were computed, and the interpretation results were compared between MIA and complete case analysis (CCA). Results MIA was an appropriate method for coping with missing data. In addition, compared to CCA, the interpretation results of the MIA-derived LOGIT showed more statistically significant covariates associated with TB treatment outcomes. In MIA, factors such as TB clinical form involving both pulmonary TB and extrapulmonary TB [aOR = 3.077, 95% confidence interval (CI) = 2.994–3.163], retreatment after abandonment (aOR = 2.272, 95% CI = 2.209–2.338), and the absence of isoniazid (aOR = 2.072, 95% CI = 1.892–2.269) or rifampicin (aOR = 1.968, 95% CI = 1.746–2.218) in the treatment regimen were associated with increased odds of death. Conclusion In conclusion, our results shed light on the potential risk factors for death during TB treatment and suggest the use of simple yet interpretable LOGIT for the prediction of TB treatment outcomes. Background Tuberculosis (TB) is a global health issue that accounts for 1.4 million deaths annually. Notably, there is an increasing trend in the TB burden in Latin American countries after a prolonged decrease. This may be driven by changes in demographic, socioeconomic, climatic, and healthcare characteristics, which increase population vulnerability [ 1 , 2 ]. These data highlight the need to improve TB management. Furthermore, the TB pharmacological treatment is complicated, lasting six months with standard therapy for drug-susceptible TB and 9–24 months for drug-resistant TB [ 3 , 4 ]. The prolonged duration of TB treatment may increase the nonadherence of patients, loss to follow-up, and severe adverse drug reactions, which lead to unfavorable outcomes, such as treatment failure, relapse, drug resistance, and potential TB outbreaks [ 5 , 6 ]. Therefore, accurate and early prediction of TB treatment outcomes is crucial for timely interventions for patients with a higher risk of death. In addition, being aware of relevant risk factors for death during TB treatment would be advantageous for treatment optimization and resource allocation, which ultimately contributes to an efficient TB management strategy [ 5 , 7 ]. Although current attention is focused on the use of transcriptomics-based signatures to predict TB treatment outcomes, the available signatures still have not achieved a breakthrough [ 8 , 9 ]. The typical challenge of an omics study is the small sample size due to substantial technical and resource requirements, which limits the reliability and generalizability of the findings. On the other hand, electronic health records (EHRs), which contain routinely collected clinical data, may be up to multiple million and more accessible. Owing to its cost-effectiveness, the EHR is an attractive data source for predicting treatment outcomes and identifying outcome-associated risk factors [ 10 ]. Nonetheless, a robust analysis pipeline with proper data preprocessing and treatment is needed to utilize this source of data effectively. Statistical learning (SL) and machine learning (ML) show great promise as valuable tools for predicting TB treatment outcomes, with various published models that have acceptable performance. However, a critical concern lies in their high risk of bias due to inadequate reporting, improper data preparation, lack of calibration assessment, small sample size, and poor cross and external validation [ 7 , 11 – 13 ]. Addressing these challenges is essential to harness the full potential of SL and ML for accurate and reliable TB outcome prediction. Furthermore, most published models have been built in low-burden TB settings, and a limited number of models have been developed for populations from regions with high burden and resource constraints, such as the Latin American region [ 7 ]. The Brazilian Information System for Notifiable Diseases (Sistema de Informação de Agravo de Notificação or SINAN) database opens a window of opportunity to establish a TB outcome prediction model tailored for populations with similar characteristics. The dataset derived from the SINAN database comprises 964,099 records with sociodemographic, clinical, laboratory, and outcome data of TB cases [ 14 ]. Nevertheless, the values of laboratory test results are largely unavailable. The other variables, such as sociodemographic and clinical information, have a higher degree of completeness. These high-completeness variables have shown their potential usefulness for TB outcome prediction in previous studies [ 7 ]. In addition, they might be informative regarding the missing pattern of the low-completeness variables. Therefore, a workflow that can efficiently leverage these “easy-to-collect” sociodemographic and clinical variables for the modeling process is needed. Several attempts have been made to develop models using SINAN data for predicting TB treatment outcomes, and these models demonstrated excellent performance (with an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve ≥ 0.9 in most cases) [ 15 , 16 ]. However, it is worth mentioning that in these studies, the established models included the “DIAS_EM_TRATAMENTO” (“DIAS”) variable, representing the duration of TB treatment for each patient. Since this variable is self-explanatory to the outcome variable, its inclusion in the models might potentially inflate their performance. Collectively, it is crucial to employ a robust modeling methodology, coupled with a profound understanding of factors associated with treatment outcomes and an efficient imputation strategy, to effectively mitigate and overcome the potential bias. This study aimed to predict TB treatment outcomes and explore the various factors associated with the outcome of TB treatment. For this purpose, we employed SL and ML methods to provide deeper insight into the influence of the available covariates on TB treatment outcomes. Moreover, we used a multiple imputation strategy to overcome the potential bias caused by the large percentage of missing values in the dataset used. The workflow of this study is depicted in Fig. 1 . Our strategy elucidated the potential risk factors for death during TB treatment, potentially contributing to timely intervention, better resource allocation, and the development of personalized therapy. Fig. 1 The study workflow. Following the data cleaning process, the revised dataset was subjected to either complete case analysis (CCA), involving the removal of all samples with missing values, or multiple imputation analysis (MIA), involving the imputation of missing values. An upweighting method was used to handle the imbalance of labels. In MIA, three algorithms, including logistic regression, random forest, and stochastic gradient boosting, were used to establish prediction models, and the performances of the models were evaluated on the basis of the confusion matrix. In CCA, only logistic regression was used to build the prediction model. The results of the logistic regression models between the CCA and MIA were compared Materials &amp; methods Data collection and cleaning The processed dataset of patients diagnosed with TB in Brazil, derived from a retrospective cohort study using the SINAN database, was acquired through the Mendeley Data repository [ 17 ]. The collection time of the dataset spans from January 2001 to April 2020. The original dataset comprises 36 variables with 964,099 observations. Even though it was filtered as extensively described in the previous report [ 14 ], we developed a pipeline for further data cleaning to obtain a more suitable dataset for modeling (Fig. 2 ). In brief, the records of patients with only extrapulmonary TB were excluded. Next, observations with undefined sex were also removed. The records were then removed if the entry situation of patients was unclear (i.e., “Don’t know” and “Transfer”) or if the intervention could not be made at the time of entry (“Post-death”). Cases whose chest X-rays were not accomplished or whose results indicated pathology other than TB were excluded. Subsequently, if examining whether the patients were associated with diabetes at the time of notification or whether patients got TB from working conditions/situations was not performed, the record was removed. The variables that were self-explanatory to the dependent variable (“DIAS_EM_TRATAMENTO”) or considered not helpful in clinical settings (“DT_NOTIFIC” and “ESTREPTOMI”) were excluded. With respect to the age-of-patients variable (“IDADE”), we categorized it (“IDADE_c”) by classifying ages of patients according to The National Institutes of Health guidelines [ 18 ]. All remaining data with “Ignored,” “In progress,” and “Not performed” labels were transformed into “NA” to present missing values. Finally, we removed variables with more than 40% missing values from the dataset. Fig. 2 The data cleaning strategy. The final dataset comprised 665,883 cases with 19 variables Imputation strategies We used the “Hmisc” R package [ 19 ] to impute missing values. This package fills in missing values through the predictive mean matching, where predicted values are obtained from an additive model fitted over bootstrapped samples. The additive model is based on a multiple linear regression where the target variable to be imputed is regressed on the others. In the “Hmisc” package, categorical variables are transformed into a continuous scale by Fisher’s optimum scoring method and regressed using the additive model. To verify the generalizability of our results reported in the main manuscript, we have tried to split the full dataset multiple times and repeated the evaluation. Fig. S1 shows that the results remained very similar across different runs. Moreover, the multiple results are not easy to interpret, whereas interpretability is a key objective of this study. Based on these considerations, we chose to report the results from a single split for consistency. It is generally recommended to include in imputation models all variables that will be used in subsequent analyses after imputation [ 20 ]. We used all variables except “UF” (geographical information) in the imputation model, because some of its categories have too few observations (e.g., AP and RR have less than 2000; see Table S1 ) to be sampled in a bootstrap sample. Following the conventions of the missing data literature [ 21 , 22 ], the number of multiple imputations was set to 10; that is, 10 pseudo-complete datasets were produced. The other settings were not specific to this study, so we adopted the default options in the “Hmisc” package, including the predictive mean matching method as described previously, 10 imputations, and 3 burn-in steps, among others. We also tried more burn-in steps (e.g., 50 steps), which turned out to be not helpful. Our imputation strategy is illustrated in Fig. 3 a. The level of confidence is 0.95 and non-significant results (including 0) are blurred Statistical modeling Our statistical analysis consists of two parts: assessment of predictive performance and interpretation. Firstly, we compared different classification methods to determine how difficult it is to decide whether subjects are dead or cured from TB treatment. Secondly, we provided an interpretation of the results based on logistic regression (LOGIT), which exhibits transparency, since we observed from the first part that its performance was comparable to that of the other nonlinear methods. To predict the binary state (cured (0) / died (1)) of each subject, we considered three classification methods, ranging from the basic one to the one that performs well in general: LOGIT, stochastic gradient boosting (GB), and random forests (RF). These three algorithms used in our paper are popular and known to work well in diverse situations, especially with tabular data. When these statistical models are used, special care has to be taken because the data are imbalanced; the ratio of subjects with target labels “cured” and “died” is approximately 26:1. Unless appropriately treated, the imbalanced data would guide a model to learn only characteristics of the majority class, that is, cured samples in our context. Moreover, the trained model will produce too many false negative decisions, i.e., mistakenly predict that people dying from the illness will be cured soon. To address this problem, people have commonly used downsampling or upweighting techniques. The former randomly extracts some subjects from the majority class and combines them with those from the minority class. By repeating this process across all subjects in the majority class, one can obtain multiple balanced datasets on which prediction models are fitted before aggregating the multiple results. This approach works well in prediction but is unsuitable for the inference we aim at in this paper. Another technique assigns new class weights to subjects so that a model treats subjects in the minority class more importantly. We adopted this upweighting method because it enables us to perform statistical inference in a much simpler way. The class weights are set inversely proportional to the ratio of the classes, meaning that the weight for the “died” class is approximately 26 times greater than that of the “cured” class. With multiply imputed datasets ( is the number of imputations), we trained a classification model for each pseudo-complete dataset. Given the covariate information , we predicted the probability of being cured and the status from the individual fitted models. We finally aggregated the decisions by majority votes . Based on the final decisions, we can construct a confusion matrix and evaluate prediction models by different performance measures for classification, such as sensitivity, specificity, and precision. Note that GB and RF have a set of tuning parameters to be specified by users or to be tuned adaptively to data. For example, the number of variables used in each split and the number of trees to grow are typically tuned to find the optimal RF model; for GB, the number of trees to grow and the degree of shrinkage (or learning rate) are to be tuned. However, we fixed them in this study at the default values suggested in R packages (“ranger” [ 23 ] for RF and “gbm” [ 24 ] for GB) because the models optimally tuned by the cross-validation (not reported in this paper) showed negligible differences from those with default values. To assess the generalization error of classification methods, we set aside 30% of completely observed samples (48,940 subjects) and used the remaining samples for training models. Note that multiple imputation analysis (MIA) has additional training samples filled by imputation compared to complete case analysis (CCA) (Fig. 3 b). It is common to repeat splitting training and test datasets due to random sampling. However, we reported results from a single split because the results did not change substantially, and the computation was prohibitive. Other conditions associated with an increase in the odds of death during TB treatment, which have aORs &lt; 2 in both the CCA and the MIA, were patients with relapse TB, being male, having no supervision in TB treatment, and having negative sputum smear results in the 1st sample (Table 3 ). In contrast, non-adult patients were associated with lower odds of death in TB treatment compared to adults for both CCA (aOR = 0.464, 95% CI = 0.437–0.492) and MIA (aOR = 0.483, 95% CI = 0.470–0.497). At the time of notification, if patients had a self-report of having no alcohol consumption, then the odds of death decreased with an aOR = 0.487 (95% CI = 0.474–0.501) in the CCA and an aOR = 0.507 (95% CI = 0.498–0.516) in the MIA. In the same vein, patients who were reported with no mental illness at the time of notification had a lower odds of death during TB treatment (CCA, aOR = 0.501, 95% CI = 0.469–0.535; MIA, aOR = 0.600, 95% CI = 0.575–0.626). In addition, the findings from both the CCA and MIA indicated that patients who had a normal chest X-ray result, belonging to the Caucasian or Asian races, and the absence of other drugs used in the regimen were negatively associated with the death outcome of TB treatment (Table 3 ). Of note, the associations of ethambutol drug use with the odds of death during TB treatment were opposite for the CCA (aOR = 1.023, 95% CI = 1.001–1.047) and the MIA (aOR = 0.512, 95% CI = 0.498–0.527). Moreover, there were three conditions that only statistically significantly increased the odds of death in the MIA, including the absence of pyrazinamide (PZA) drugs from the treatment regimen (aOR = 1.060, 95% CI = 1.029–1.092), patients of African descent (aOR = 0.944, 95% CI = 0.922–0.966), and the lack of association between diabetes with TB (aOR = 1.313; 95% CI = 1.225–1.408). This comparison confirms again that a naive approach to deleting samples containing missing values may lead to different scientific conclusions. Discussion In TB management, blood RNA signature appears to be the cutting-edge technology and has been the focus of interest in recent decades [ 26 ]. Even though blood RNA signatures have been reported to be able to distinguish TB from non-TB conditions, most studies were conducted on a small scale and lack proper validation. In addition, current evidence suggests that blood RNA signatures are not promising biomarkers for indicating persistent sputum culture positivity [ 8 ] or for prediction of treatment outcome (e.g., area under receiver operating characteristic curve ~ 0.745 in small-scale studies [ 9 ]). Common issues in omics-related studies are high cost and stringent technical requirements, which often result in small sample-size cohorts and have yet to achieve an innovative method [ 27 ]. Therefore, a national record with conventional information and a large sample size still provides a valuable source to assist clinicians in their decision-making process, especially when considering cost-effectiveness in resource- and budget-strained environments [ 10 ]. According to a recent systematic review of TB outcome prediction, 33 studies (37 prediction models) were developed, many of which reported acceptable performance [ 7 ]. Nonetheless, the developed models were at high risk of analysis and modeling, and the nature of the studies was heterogeneous, with disparate definitions of outcomes. Most of these studies lack proper validation strategies [ 7 , 28 ]. More rigorous and large-scale studies with proper analyses are needed to close the gap between research and clinical practice. In addition, the used datasets must be carefully inspected and further processed to make them eligible for subsequent analysis and modeling. This study highlighted the importance of a proper method for knowledge-based predictor selection and missing value handling. Of note, we identified and characterized the effect of a self-explanatory variable (Additional Table S2 - S3 ), the duration of TB treatment for each patient, which can inflate the performance of models and lead to overoptimistic results in previous studies [ 15 , 16 ]. We also demonstrated that a simple LOGIT modeling is sufficient with comparable performance to two popular ML algorithms with this specific task. Noticeably, the data used in our models consist of routinely collected information that is usually available and inexpensive. The simple yet efficient computational pipeline, which requires only affordable resources, is advantageous in promoting its application in clinical settings for countries with low and middle incomes [ 10 ]. Moreover, our models were established based on 665,883 records, which is a far larger sample size than most of the prior works, strengthening the precision of our findings [ 7 ]. An essential strength of our study is the rigorous knowledge-based data cleaning strategy, enabling the identification and exclusion of self-explanatory variables. The exclusion of these variables is crucial to avoid misleading results by ensuring a realistic assessment of the predictive capacities of the developed model. One such variable, “DIAS_EM_TRATAMENTO,” representing the duration of the treatment period (from diagnosis to the end of treatment), is inherently self-explanatory and can be derived from the treatment outcome. This phenomenon arises because cured patients typically complete the treatment on an expected timeline, e.g., 6 months for conventional cases, whereas patients who died during TB treatment may experience other lengths of treatment periods, such as from below 5 months in most cases. Nevertheless, the information on this variable can be obtained only after the treatment outcome is known, limiting its value in real-life applications. Additionally, incorporating such self-explanatory variables into the models can result in an overly optimistic assessment of their performance. Indeed, TB prognosis models developed using SINAN data in a recent investigation achieved over 90% accuracy, specificity, and precision by training with GB and RF [ 15 ], seemingly outperforming the methods used in this paper. Noticeably, the results were obtained using 17 covariates out of 38, including “DIAS_EM_TRATAMENTO.” In another study, TB treatment outcome prediction models were established with a multinomial naive Bayes algorithm using SINAN data [ 16 ]. The accuracy, specificity, and precision of these models were over 90%, which also gives more optimistic results than ours. However, similar to the study of Barros et al., the “DIAS_EM_TRATAMENTO” variable was included in these models. For reference, we also checked that including “DIAS_EM_TRATAMENTO” significantly inflated the model performance while maintaining the modeling pipeline we used (accuracy and specificity &gt; 0.9 and sensitivity &gt; 0.8 for LOGIT, RF, and GB) (Additional Table S2 - S3 ). Therefore, despite the loss in model performance, removing this self-explanatory variable from the final dataset is necessary to avoid overestimating model capacity. Nonetheless, we acknowledge that the classification performance was not very satisfactory. To address this issue, we tested two more methods, neural networks and extreme gradient boosting, and performed a grid search to find optimal hyperparameters. However, these attempts did not lead to distinctive improvements in classification performance. While the current results appear reasonably good given the characteristics of the dataset, exploring better predictive models remains an important direction of future work. In terms of handling missing data, it is known that MIA yields a valid inference under the missing at random mechanism, which is not the case for CCA [ 29 ]. Since it is inherently impossible to verify this assumption, it is generally advised to include as many available variables as possible [ 30 , 31 ] in the imputation model. In this study, some of the covariates (e.g., BACILOSC_6 and BACILOSC_1) were excluded from the missing imputation because we believe that they might not be informative due to having high percentages of missing values. Therefore, future studies are needed to examine the trade-off relationship between including variables with high missing rates in the imputation model and discarding them from it. Moreover, monitoring and diagnosing the quality of imputed datasets is important. However, related tools for diagnostics are relatively limited in literature, as all variables in our dataset are categorical. We believe that this direction of research is important in the missing data analysis. The classification performance of our models displayed a low precision reflecting a higher false positive rate. In our context, a false positive occurs when a patient who will be cured (negative case) is (wrongly) predicted to die (positive case). However, due to the nature of the current decision problem, we believe that identifying accurately patients at risk of death is more important than the other. From a clinical point of view, such false alarms may lead to more cautious behaviors from patients and/or clinicians, which is not harmful in the context of TB treatment. This is why we used the upweighting strategy (i.e., putting more weights on the died class) during model training. Although this approach may lower precision, it is aligned with the primary goal of predictive medicine, to minimize missed cases of adverse outcomes. In this study, the conventional LOGIT showed comparable performance with advanced ML methods, i.e., RF and GB. Other factors contributing to TB treatment outcomes that were not captured in the SINAN database may explain this finding. More sophisticated algorithms may help improve the model performance, but this deviates from our objectives, which aim to use easy-to-employ methods that may be more accessible and require fewer resources. Despite the weak prediction power for TB treatment outcomes, the interpretability of LOGIT allows a more straightforward investigation of the covariates that significantly influence the prediction of outcomes. It is essential to examine the clinical significance of these covariates to understand how patient characteristics contribute to treatment outcomes. Furthermore, the plausibility of the covariates should also be assessed, considering potential confounding factors. Male patients were reported to have a higher mortality rate of TB compared to females [ 32 ]. Indeed, being male was found to be positively associated with death during TB treatment in the current study. This could possibly be explained by the nonadherence behavior as well as high rates of smoking, alcohol consumption, and HIV in males [ 33 – 35 ]. Males also commonly work in places with poor ventilation and sanitary conditions [ 33 ]. The mortality incidence of TB was reported to increase with age, which is consistent with our findings. The higher mortality rate in the group of aged individuals is due to complex clinical situations (comorbidities), physiological changes (drug metabolism), and polypharmacy [ 36 , 37 ]. In our study, patients who were retreatment after abandonment or relapsing TB cases had higher odds of death compared to patients who were new cases. Retreatment after abandonment can be considered as a treatment after dropping out (one month of TB pharmacological treatment and at least one month to discontinue the treatment) [ 38 ]. It is associated with increasing nonadherence to TB treatment, treatment failure, multidrug-resistant tuberculosis cases, and death [ 39 – 41 ]. A previous study that also used the SINAN data found that re-entry after abandonment can increase the risk of mortality, indicating the possibility of adverse drug reactions and difficult healthcare access [ 42 ]. Besides, TB patients with relapse have the potential of treatment failure and/or a higher risk of mortality [ 43 , 44 ]. Clinical forms with both pulmonary and extrapulmonary TB require a more extended treatment duration and encounter many difficulties in the evaluation [ 45 ]. Unlike pulmonary TB, which can be evaluated via culture conversion or acid-fast bacillus (AFB) staining, extrapulmonary TB has no gold standard for determining the successful treatment [ 46 ]. Additionally, certain types of extrapulmonary TB can cause a high incidence rate of mortality [ 47 ]. Poor compliance of the patients links to TB treatment failure [ 48 ]. Supervised treatment, which can improve patient adherence to TB therapy, was reported to be associated with a reduction in the mortality of TB [ 49 ]. Accordingly, the current study showed that the odds of death during TB treatment were higher in patients for whom supervised treatment were not indicated for. We found that the absences of INH, RIF, or PZA in the TB treatment regimen were associated with increased mortality during TB treatment. RIF/INH/PZA has been used as the backbone of the anti-TB treatment [ 50 – 52 ]. RIF has excellent bacterial sterilizing activity [ 50 , 51 ]. Patients without administration of RIF have a high risk of longer treatment duration, treatment failure, and poor outcome [ 50 , 52 ]. Regarding INH, it has an early bactericidal activity [ 50 , 51 ]. Several lines of evidence show that patients without the administration of INH are at high risk of treatment failure and poor outcomes [ 50 , 52 , 53 ]. Our finding of elevated odds of death in TB patients whose treatment regimen lacked INH is consistent with these previous findings. However, in the SINAN database, whether INH was absent from the initial standard regimen or from the modified regimens during treatment remains unclear. In addition, INH could also be removed because of the alteration of the regimen during TB treatment. A possible explanation for the absence of INH in the TB treatment regimen might be due to INH monoresistance. INH monoresistance is also associated with a higher incidence of poor TB treatment outcomes for TB cases reported in the SINAN database [ 53 ]. The role of PZA in TB treatment might explain the association between its absence in the regimen and higher odds of mortality. PZA is the only drug that can work under acidic conditions during inflammation, and adding PZA to the regimen shortens the treatment duration [ 51 , 54 , 55 ]. Other drugs might not be included in TB treatment unless there are resistance conditions of the four first-line anti-TB drugs or if there are intolerance conditions of these first-line drugs that require them to be withdrawn. Those complicated conditions are often challenging and are associated with a prolonged treatment period and poor treatment outcomes, which might explain the decrease in the odds of death during TB treatment when other drugs are not used in the regimen [ 53 , 56 – 58 ]. In our study, a contrast between CCA and MIA was observed when estimating the effect of ethambutol use on TB-related mortality. One potential explanation is the substantial difference in sample sizes: 163,136 for CCA versus 665,883 for MIA. The larger sample size in the MIA may allow the detection of hidden associations that were not apparent with the CCA. Additionally, if the prevalence of less severe TB cases is higher among records with missing ethambutol data [ 59 , 60 ], possibly due to a reduced risk of ethambutol-induced toxicity, simply excluding these cases in the CCA may introduce selection bias. This can result in a nonrepresentative dataset and distort the aOR estimates in the CCA. The sputum smear for AFB is used to diagnose TB. During TB diagnosis, two times of sputum sampling are supposed to be taken in one microbiology test [ 61 ]. Nonetheless, only a few bodies of evidence suggest the association between first or second sputum smear test results and the mortality rate. Besides the sputum smear AFB, the clinical examination and radiological findings will also be considered for TB diagnosis. If the patients produce no sputum, then symptoms and radiological improvement will be the evaluation standards. If the patients have had normal radiological findings since the beginning of diagnosis, this may indicate that the lung is not severely damaged by the bacterial growth and the immune response [ 62 ]. Nonetheless, when this variable is considered for inclusion, symptoms and sputum tests should be clarified. Patients with issues related to mental health have three times higher odds of mortality, twice the odds of loss to follow-up, and twice the odds of compliance issues for TB [ 63 ]. Furthermore, the drugs that are commonly used to control this condition exhibit drug‒drug interactions with INH and RIF [ 64 ]. These findings are in accordance with our findings of a reduction in the odds of death when there was a lack of association between mental health and TB. Similarly, we found that no alcohol consumption was associated with lower odds of mortality. A plausible rationale is that alcohol consumption, particularly alcohol abuse, may promote several changes in behavior (e.g., lowering medical adherence), increase the potency of hepatotoxicity, alter the clearance of anti-TB drugs, and affect the immune response [ 65 – 67 ]. This study had several limitations that should be assessed. Firstly, we excluded the extrapulmonary cases from our analysis when cleaning the dataset, which can reduce the generalizability of our study to extrapulmonary TB. Nevertheless, this strategy can improve our models’ interpretability and clinical applicability by focusing on a homogeneous and epidemiologically significant subgroup of TB. Additionally, several types of variables were excluded from our analysis: (1) variables that were self-explanatory to the dependent variable, (2) variables deemed not clinically relevant, and (3) variables with high rates of missingness (&gt; 40%). This approach is consistent with recommended practices for reliable imputation and modeling, and helps to improve the clinical utility of our model. However, the exclusion of those variables, particularly those related to comorbidities and concomitant medications, may introduce bias or unmeasured confounding and could influence the observed associations with TB treatment outcomes. Examples are HIV-related variables, which are well-known for increasing the mortality rate of TB treatment. Future studies that incorporate more comprehensive data on these potential confounders are needed to improve adjustment for them and enhance the model interpretation. Thirdly, outliers were not detected and removed from our dataset, which may negatively impact the interpretability of the models. However, a considerable amount of our reports aligns with current evidence from the literature, ensuring the validity of the findings and supporting the novel findings. Next, the type of missing data is not generally verifiable, as the underlying data generation process is unknown in the case of electronic health records data. Since multiple imputation methods are effective for handling missing completely at random (MCAR) and missing at random (MAR) scenarios, and many of these methods have demonstrated robustness against assumption violations [ 68 ], multiple imputation can still be considered an acceptable approach for data imputation in this study. Fifthly, a systematic evaluation of whether imputing missing values improved the reliability of model interpretation was not conducted due to a lack of essential clinical information. Future studies may be performed to investigate further how imputing missing values affects the model interpretation. Finally, given the characteristics of the data (retrospective with highly incomplete observations) and the unstatisfactory performance of the LOGIT model, the causal relationship between the significant risk factors could not be fully established and cautions should be taken when interpreting the association between identified risk factors and TB treatment outcome. There is a need for better data quality in future research to help overcome this limitation. Conclusion We applied a workflow that integrates a straightforward and interpretable modeling approach with a multiple imputation strategy for analyzing complex medical record data. The importance of data preparation with domain expertise was highlighted. Additionally, we found that the outcome prediction of TB treatment was a formidable challenge, and advanced ML methods, i.e., RF and GB, were not significantly better than the conventional LOGIT in predicting outcomes of TB treatment. Notwithstanding, the interpretation of LOGIT highlighted potential risk factors associated with higher odds of death during TB treatment in high-burden settings. They included various factors such as patients whose ages surpass that of the adult, TB clinical forms involving both pulmonary and extrapulmonary TB, retreatment after abandonment, and the absence of INH or RIF in the TB treatment regimen. These identified factors emphasize the importance of detecting specific patient profiles to facilitate better and more informed decision-making in patient care and resource allocation. Our results also demonstrated the usefulness of the simple yet interpretable LOGIT in identifying potential risk factors for TB treatment outcomes, suggesting that more studies should explore its applicability in diverse clinical settings and patient populations.
